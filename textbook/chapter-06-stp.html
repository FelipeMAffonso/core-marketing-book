    Why STP exists at all

    1. Sears at its peak mailed 75 million catalogs to an undifferentiated middle-class buyer. Once that buyer split into needs-based niches, Sears lost ground to Spiegel and L.L. Bean. The everybody trap, in one firm.

    Segmentation: how to cut the market

    Once you decide to cut the market into slices, the next question is what to cut it on. The textbook tradition gives four standard answers, and it calls them the four segmentation bases.     What makes a segment usable

    Cutting the market hands you a list of slices, and not every slice is worth serving. A five-part filter, built on the foundational methods work, decides which ones you can use: a segment has to be measurable, accessible, substantial, differentiable, and actionable. A slice that fails any one of the five is not yet a usable segment, it is research.

    Measurable: the firm can estimate the segment's size and buying power from data it can get. Accessible: it can reach the segment with a marketing program. Substantial: the segment is large and profitable enough to repay the cost of serving it. Differentiable: the segment responds differently than others to a different program, which is the whole point of cutting. Actionable: the firm has the resources to run a program for it, and a slice that fails this last test is research, not a target.

    Measurable The segment's size, purchasing power, and characteristics can be estimated from data the firm can get. “Buyers who feel anxious on Tuesdays” fails; “US households earning $75,000 to $120,000 with at least one child under 18” passes because the Census Bureau publishes it.
    Accessible The firm can reach the segment with a marketing program. 2026 college freshmen are reachable through TikTok, campus visits, university partnerships. Off-grid homesteaders in rural Montana are harder, and that difficulty has to be priced into the targeting decision.
    Substantial The segment is large and profitable enough to repay the cost of serving it. 2,000 households is substantial for Wagner Custom Skis, not for a national brand. Niche marketers make a virtue of a threshold every other competitor decided was too small.
    Differentiable The segment responds differently than others would to a different marketing program. If men and women respond identically to the same shaving-cream ad, gender is not a usable base for that program. Differential response is the point.
    Actionable The firm can design and execute a program for the segment. A small firm that identifies a global luxury-watch segment may lack the distribution, budget, and retail presence to act. A segment failing this test is research, not a target.

    There is one more honesty check before you trust a cut. If a team cannot describe a segment in one plain English sentence, it is probably a statistical artifact the next data refresh will rearrange, not a real type of buyer. Fit between two and six segments, then keep whichever count still holds up on next quarter's data. And before building anything, ask whether segmenting even beats one offer for everyone, because sometimes it does not, and a cheap pretest on past experiment data tells you which case you are in.

    03 // targeting

    Targeting: which segment to serve

    Once the analysis has cut the market into real segments, you reach the question the firm is actually paying you to answer: of all the slices the data named, which one will you go after? That choice has a trap worth seeing before you score anything. A segment can be big, growing, and full of profitable buyers and still be wrong for you, because if a stronger rival already serves those buyers better than you could, you are walking into a fight you will lose, and the size of the prize is what makes losing it so expensive. So targeting hunts for the segment you can win, not the biggest one.

    Once the profiles are scored, a second question follows: how many segments should you chase at all? The answer is a bet on what your resources can carry.     When targeting crosses a line

    The same machinery that finds the buyer you want can find buyers you have no right to single out. Target learned this in 2012, when its model read a teenager's shift toward unscented lotion and vitamins as a pregnancy signal and mailed her baby coupons before she had told her father, who complained to the store and later had to apologize. The lesson is not that the model was wrong; it was that it was right, and acting on it was the violation. Three risks recur. First, targeting on proxies for a protected class (a pregnancy score, a health inference, a religion read off purchases) is a legal and reputational hazard even when each variable you used is lawful on its own, because the combination reconstructs the trait the law protects. Second, targeting children triggers separate rules under COPPA, so a segment that is fine for adults can be illegal the moment it includes minors. Third, charging different buyers different prices off the same behavioral read crosses fast into manipulation: Orbitz was caught in 2012 steering Mac users toward pricier hotels because they spent more, and the backlash was immediate. Since 2018, the GDPR in Europe and a wave of US state laws have put hard fences around exactly this kind of real-time personalization on sensitive data, so the question a brand team now asks is not only can we target this, but are we allowed to.

    The targeting ethics check

    Before you ship a micro-targeted campaign, run it past three questions. They take a minute and they are the ones a brand team actually gets asked.

    1. Does the cut reconstruct a protected trait? If your descriptors, combined, let you guess someone's health, pregnancy, religion, or race, you are targeting on that trait even if you never named it. Each variable being lawful does not make the combination lawful.
    2. Could the audience include children? If minors can fall inside the segment, COPPA and platform rules apply, and a cut that was fine for adults is now a separate legal question.
    3. Would the buyer feel watched if you told them how you found them? If explaining the targeting out loud would read as creepy or as price discrimination, that is the Orbitz problem, and the reputational cost lands whether or not the law does.

    Positioning: the work in the buyer's head

    4. The term positioning entered marketing in 1972 (Advertising Age), collected in book form in 1981.

The mind is too small to hold the flood of claims aimed at it every day, so it screens almost all of them out and keeps room for only a handful of brands per category, ranked in an order the buyer can recite without thinking. Your job in positioning is to win one of those slots and the meaning that comes with it. Avis is the proof: it changed how buyers ranked it without touching the cars, the prices, or the staff.

    05 // POP and POD

    Points of parity, points of difference

    Two questions decide where a brand stands. What does every buyer expect any brand in this category to deliver, and what does this one brand offer that none of the rivals can match? The first answer is the brand's points of parity. The second is its points of difference. Think of two overlapping circles.     Each side gets its own checklist next. A point of difference has to clear three tests before a brand can build on it, and parity itself comes in three kinds the brand has to cover.

    Points of difference (POD) Attributes or benefits buyers strongly link to your brand, like, and believe they cannot get at the same level anywhere else in the category. The reason a buyer picks you. Apple's design; Nike's performance technology; Southwest's value with personality.
    Points of parity (POP) Attributes not unique to your brand but needed to count as a real option in the category. A coffee shop has to serve coffee. POPs do not win the choice; their absence loses it.

    Three tests every POD has to pass

    Before you build a position on a point of difference, run it through three tests. Desirable asks whether buyers in your target actually want the attribute, the test being whether they name it on their own when you ask what matters about the category. Deliverable asks whether the firm can prove the claim and keep proving it, because a claim you cannot back loses more buyers than no claim at all. Differentiating asks whether rivals can already credibly say the same thing, since a difference any rival could copy by next quarter is really a point of parity that has not commoditized yet.

The brand-equity tradition adds a second triad, feasibility, communicability, and sustainability, asking whether the firm can produce the claim at a price its margin supports, convey it in the media it can buy, and defend it across the planning horizon.

    Three kinds of POP

    The parity side is easy to underrate, because a manager who only audits points of difference learns about a missing point of parity after the campaign is already live. Parity comes in three kinds. A category POP is the price of admission, like a coffee shop serving coffee or a campus food truck taking cards, and buyers treat its absence as disqualification before any brand comparison starts. A correlational POP defends against a bad assumption a buyer would otherwise draw from one of your own strengths, since buyers assume some things trade off (cheap means low quality, fast means careless), so a brand that wins on one side has to say out loud it has not lost on the other. Trader Joe's quality-at-a-low-price claim is the textbook case, and the cell managers miss most often. A competitive POP matches a rival's point of difference just enough that buyers stop treating it as decisive, the way Visa's "accepted anywhere" neutralized AmEx's prestige for buyers who did not want their card refused.

    When the brand competes in two frames at once

    07 // narrow vs broad

    Narrow targeting versus broad penetration

    Everything up to here has told you to pick a small slice and serve it well. A second body of evidence says the opposite, and a marketer who does not know about it can pour money into the wrong move. The first side is the positioning tradition you have been reading since the start of the chapter, which says to find the smallest group you can credibly own, serve it deeply, and let the rest go. The second comes from forty years of watching what households actually buy, scanner by scanner, across roughly fifty categories, and the record there runs the other way: brands grow by reaching more of the category's buyers, not by serving a small loyal group harder. Two findings carry the weight.

A rival cannot match forty years of compounded giving by starting today. On top of that sits the Worn Wear repair-and-resale channel, launched in 2017, which reaches a cohort about ten years younger: a buyer who cannot afford a new $300 jacket can afford the same one repaired and resold, and the values-based cut reads them as the segment the new-product channel had filtered out on price.

    13. Worn Wear at scale (fiscal 2025): more than 110 repair centers worldwide handle Patagonia garments, with roughly 92,000 repairs in North America and over 35,000 more in Europe, and the channel's revenue grew about 40 percent year over year. The firm's stated goal is 100,000 repairs a year by 2028.

    Most brands write off repair as a cost center, but Patagonia runs it as a second targeting channel reaching the same values-based buyer through a different price-and-age door. STP does not cap a brand at one target forever.

    14. Same logic at student scale. A junior tutoring at $25 an hour faces high-pay pre-meds, a midnight-cramming roommate's sibling, study-group regulars who pay in coffee, and friends expecting half off. The five-criteria filter picks two and declines the rest. Saying yes to all four is the everybody trap on a twelve-credit week.
    09 // looking ahead

    Looking ahead

    The chapter opened on Avis claiming the second rung the buyer had already handed it, and it closes on the same move made by every brand in the running cast. STP picks the rung the brand asks the buyer to remember it on. How that remembering happens, once the position is chosen, is the work of Chapter 7 ("Brands and Brand Management").

    Your memory is less a filing cabinet of brands in alphabetical order and more a web of dots joined by threads, where a brand is one dot linked to the colors, lines, and occasions its buyers tie to it, and the dot with the strongest thread to the moment of need lights up first. Avis won by building a strong thread and Tylenol won by weakening a rival's, so Chapter 7 is the work of building and defending that web (the associative network) and placing the brand among the firm's others on the Brand Relationship Spectrum.

    15. The Brand Relationship Spectrum (Aaker) names four slots on a line from one master brand stamped on everything (Apple's iPhone, Watch, Vision Pro; Stanley's Quencher, Classic, AeroLight) through sub-brands (Toyota Prius) and endorsed brands (Courtyard by Marriott) to a master brand kept invisible at the shelf (P&G's Tide, Crest, Gillette; Toyota's Lexus). A 2016 Journal of the Academy of Marketing Science event study found the slot a firm announces moves its stock-market value, with the sub-brand slot drawing the largest average gain but the widest swing, the highest reward and the highest risk on the line. Chapter 7 owns this; here it is only a preview.

    What you hold now is the working machinery, enough to cut a market, pick a target, write a position, and read it off a map. The field underneath runs much deeper, into brand architecture, mental availability, and how memory itself decides a purchase, the kind of terrain people spend whole careers inside.

    Apply What You Have Learned

    Do this, about ten minutes

    Run STP on a brand you actually buy

    Pick a brand you bought in the last month, then walk it through the chapter's machinery one step at a time.

    1. Name the segment it serves. Write the target in one plain sentence, the way a strategist would, something like "people who treat hydration as part of who they are." If you cannot say it in a sentence, the brand is chasing nobody in particular, which is the everybody trap.
    2. Score the segment you would chase. Run that target through the five-criteria filter. Is it measurable, accessible, substantial, differentiable, and actionable? A slice that fails any one is research, not a target.
    3. Write the position. Fill the template: for [target] who [need], [brand] is the [category] that [biggest difference] because [reason to believe]. Then test your point of difference against desirable, deliverable, and differentiating, and cut any clause a rival could say word for word.
    4. Place it on a map. Pick the two attributes your buyer actually cares about, not the ones the firm likes. Plot your brand and two rivals. Find the empty quadrant, then ask the harder question: do any buyers want to live there, or is it dead air the incumbents already priced in?

    Run it once on a brand you know and the tools stop being abstract. Stanley did this audit the slow way around 2019. Its dashboard read low Quencher sales as a weak product, while the buyer who wanted it was a woman at her desk all day, answering a question Stanley was not even asking. Stanley re-pointed the same 1913 product at the buyer it had found, and revenue moved from about $73 million to past $750 million in a few years.

    Key Takeaways

    • Buyers differ, and that variation is the whole reason STP exists. A seller who ignores it ends up serving the average buyer of an imagined crowd, and that average buyer is a customer who exists nowhere.
    • A brand that tries to mean something to everyone means something specific to no one. The buyer's head holds room for only three to seven brands per category, so a brand with no clear claim never makes the list.
    • Cut on the benefit, not just the label. The four bases describe a segment after the fact, but the benefit a buyer wants is the cause of the choice, which is why two people with the same age and income can pick four different toothpastes.
    • Targeting hunts for the segment you can win, not the biggest one. A segment can be large, growing, and profitable and still be a fight you lose if a stronger rival already serves it better, so compatibility comes before attractiveness.
    • Positioning is work you do to the buyer's mind, not to the product. Avis changed how buyers ranked it without touching the fleet or the prices, because the position lives on the mental ladder, not on the lot.
    • Points of parity earn the brand a place in the category; points of difference win the choice. A missing point of parity loses the buyer before the comparison even starts, and a point of difference only holds if it is desirable, deliverable, and hard for a rival to copy.
    • An empty quadrant is an opening only when buyer ideal points cluster in it. Without the buyers on the map, dead air and real demand look identical.
    • STP picks the category; the penetration laws govern growth inside it. Once the position is set, brands grow by reaching more category buyers, because the smaller brand is small for the reason double jeopardy names, not because it neglected a loyal core.

    Check Your Understanding

    Answer each in your head, then click to check.

    1. Why does a firm that tries to be everything to every buyer usually end up standing for nothing?

    The buyer's memory holds room for only three to seven brands per category, often closer to three. A brand with no clear claim never makes that short list, because a position that means something to everyone means something specific to no one. The cure is to pick a target, claim one thing, and accept the buyers who wanted something else.

    2. The four standard segmentation bases are demographic, geographic, psychographic, and behavioral. Why does benefit segmentation sit underneath all four?

    The four bases describe a segment after the fact, while the benefit a buyer wants is the cause of the choice. The 1968 toothpaste study cut the market into Worriers, Sociables, Sensories, and Independents, four buyers who can share the same age and income yet pick different brands. A demographic cut collapses them and misses the structure entirely.

    3. A segment is large, growing, and full of profitable buyers. Why might it still be the wrong target?

    Because targeting hunts for the segment you can win, not the biggest one. If a stronger rival already serves those buyers better than you could, the size of the prize is exactly what makes losing the fight expensive. Compatibility, whether you can outdo rivals at serving the segment, comes before attractiveness.

    4. Two media profiles target the same segment yet score very differently. What two things are you trading off?

    Effectiveness is whether the profile reaches the valuable buyers you chose. Cost efficiency is whether it reaches only those buyers, or whether you also pay to show ads to a much larger crowd who happen to share the descriptors. A profile that catches every buyer you want plus three times as many you do not is effective and wasteful at once.

    5. Avis claimed the second rung out loud and turned a profit. What did it actually change?

    It changed the position, not the product. The fleet, the prices, and the agents stayed the same. Avis stopped pretending to be Hertz and asked the buyer to value the effort behind the number-two rung, which is why positioning is described as work done to the buyer's mind while the product holds still.

    6. What separates a point of parity from a point of difference, and why does a manager who only audits PODs get burned?

    A point of parity is what every brand must offer to count as a real option, and its absence loses the buyer before any comparison starts. A point of difference is the reason a buyer picks you. A manager who audits only PODs learns about a missing point of parity after the campaign is already live, because the buyer quietly disqualified the brand at the door.

    7. A perceptual map shows an empty quadrant. Why is that not automatically an opening?

    An empty quadrant is an opening only when buyer ideal points cluster in it. A quadrant inside the dominant frame is often empty because the incumbents tested it and judged the buyer pool too thin, so it is dead air they priced in. A quadrant in a different frame can be open because no brand has named that frame yet, the way Liquid Death found a punk frame in a category read as fully covered.

    8. The positioning tradition says target narrow; the penetration evidence says reach broad. How do the two reconcile?

    They answer different questions. STP picks which category a brand competes in and what it wants to mean inside it. The double-jeopardy and duplication-of-purchase laws then govern how the brand grows once that choice is locked, and growth comes from reaching more category buyers, not from digging deeper into a loyal core. Toyota's launch of Lexus was the STP move; adding dealers and occasions afterward was the penetration move.

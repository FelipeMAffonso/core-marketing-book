    01 // why sales and marketing fight

    Why sales and marketing fight at all

    2. Cengage frames the two metric sets plainly: sales objectives might be order size, calls, or the ratio of orders to calls, while marketing-side objectives are lead quality and brand equity. The two sets do not naturally coincide.

    Before the bonus formula, one fact about who is on the other side of a business sale explains the rest of the chapter. Selling to a company is not selling to a person at scale. Business buyers are fewer, larger, and more concentrated than consumers, so a supplier may serve a few dozen accounts rather than a few million shoppers, and losing one can cut a quarter. Their demand is derived, meaning it comes from their own customers further down the chain, so a tire maker’s orders move with how many cars consumers buy, not with anything the tire salesperson says. Buying is professional and multi-person, run by a trained committee against written criteria rather than by one shopper’s preference, and the cycle is long, often months across several departments. Those four facts are why this chapter is about committees, written definitions, and a handful of high-value deals, not about a checkout cart.

The practical rule for a B2B marketer is to watch the customer’s customers, because that is where the real demand is decided, and a forecast built on the account alone misses the swing coming from one level down.

    02 // the modular pipeline

    The modular sales pipeline

    4. The 1898 AIDA funnel is too coarse for modern B2B. Cengage’s six stages with explicit handoff definitions and role ownership add named seams to the same funnel shape.

    The modern sales pipeline is six stages, each owned by a different role. Marketing runs demand generation through content, search, ads, and events, and interested buyers arrive as leads. The BDR screens those leads and decides which are worth a senior salesperson’s time, turning them into qualified leads. Each qualified lead goes to the AE, who runs the demo, negotiates the contract, and develops the qualified lead into a prospect and then a closed deal. Once the contract is signed, the CSM onboards the customer, drives adoption, and grows the account.

    Modular sales pipeline A pipeline where each stage is decoupled from the next, with explicit conversion criteria, a single named owner, and a time cap on how long a deal may sit before review or close. Decoupling lets the firm diagnose which seam is broken when the conversion math fails, because one bad handoff drags every downstream stage. The Predictable Revenue split (inbound MRR, SDR, AE, CSM) is the canonical instance.
    5. The split is empirically anchored. A 2003 split test at Salesforce returned a 0 percent reply rate for a long pitch email and 10 percent for a short referral-style note asking only for an internal forward; the outbound team built on that mechanic added about $100 million in ARR and codified the inbound-MRR/SDR/AE/CSM split modern SaaS teams run. The roles also staff up on their own economics: inbound and outbound SDRs are distinct jobs, paid roughly 60 to 70 percent base plus variable and ramped over three to four months against a 12-to-18-month tenure, leaving 8 to 14 productive months per hire. A 2026 shift moved work off junior reps: about 36 percent of B2B firms cut SDR and BDR headcount in 2025 while about 28 percent expanded AE headcount, with the displaced work going to AI tooling and an emerging revenue-operations role.

    Not every purchase travels the whole pipeline, and the buyclass the deal falls into tells the rep how much apparatus it needs. A straight rebuy is a routine reorder of a no-risk item, like printer paper, that one purchasing agent settles alone with no real pipeline. A modified rebuy changes the spec or terms of something the firm already buys, like upgrading a fabric grade, so the affected departments weigh in at moderate risk. A new task is a first-time purchase of something the firm has never bought, like its first supply-chain system, which carries the most risk and pulls in the largest committee. This is why a new-task deal needs the full discovery-and-stakeholder map the chapter builds toward, while a reorder needs almost none of it, and reading the buyclass first tells the rep which deals are worth the heavy machinery.

    How the conversation actually runs

    The pipeline names who owns each stage of the deal, but it never says what the salesperson actually does once the buyer picks up the phone. The modern business buyer is time-starved and pulled in forty directions at once, what one practitioner named the frazzled customer. Outreach written for a calm, curious reader gets cut in the first four seconds, so the message has to earn its place fast or it dies unread. The rep who survives that window does it by reading the specific buyer in front of her and changing the script in real time, a habit the research calls adaptive selling. A student selling custom-printed tees pitches the honors society on quality and discretion, the Greek-life chapter on the social occasion and the photos, and the intramural team on cost and bulk pricing. The student who runs one script for all three closes about a third as often.

    The discipline underneath adaptive selling has a name and a structure. Tactical empathy is the practice of recognizing what the buyer feels, saying that recognition back to her in her own words, and using the trust it builds to influence the decision. It is not sympathy, which is agreement, and it is not manipulation, which is concealment. The technique was codified inside FBI crisis negotiation over the 1990s and 2000s, and the business sales literature adopted it through a 2016 trade book.     The first move is mirroring, which means repeating the buyer’s last one to three words back as a question, then staying silent. When the buyer says her procurement team is dragging its feet, the rep answers “dragging their feet?” and waits. She almost always keeps going, and now she volunteers the real obstacle, that her CFO got burned twice last year and routes every line item over fifty thousand dollars through her own desk twice.     6. The empirical anchor comes from outside hostage work. In a 2002 study, waiters who repeated the customer’s order back verbatim earned tips averaging 70 percent more than waiters who used positive reinforcement. The customer recognized herself and rewarded the recognition, which is the salesperson’s whole job: show the buyer you heard what she already cares about.

    The second move is labeling, where the rep names the emotion the buyer is sitting in. The openings are tentative and third-person, “it seems like” or “it sounds like” or “it looks like,” so the buyer can correct the label rather than defend against it. Saying “it seems like the last vendor burned you on the timeline” opens her up, while “I’m hearing that you’re cautious about vendors” closes her down, because the word I puts the rep back at the center. The move has a measured neural basis, since naming an emotion lowers activity in the brain’s threat-detection center and raises it in the regions that deliberate. The buyer told “it seems like you’re worried about the cost” is, three seconds later, biologically a little less worried, even though nothing about the cost has changed.

    Where these moves come from: FBI crisis negotiation, adapted to B2B

    The whole toolkit grew out of the FBI Crisis Negotiation Unit’s work in the 1990s and 2000s, where the stakes made listening the only lever that worked. The that’s right move is canonical there. Jeffrey Schilling, held by a kidnapping group for four months against a $10 million ransom framed as “war damages,” was freed after a negotiator summarized the kidnapper’s framing back to him until, two days later, the kidnapper paused and said that’s right; the war-damages demand dissolved. The Ackerman schedule has the same origin, since in the Haitian kidnapping case the negotiator closed at $4,751 rather than $5,000, and the precise number was the part the kidnappers accepted. The religion move is the consulting CEO and stewardship. None of these are about hostages for our purposes. Buyers in high-stakes deals run on the same emotional substrate, so the toolkit transfers, and a rep who summarizes a high-prescribing physician’s philosophy in his own vocabulary, with no pitch attached, earns the same recognition and the sample request that follows it.

    9. The call sorts into three voice tones the rep switches between on purpose. The default, used in roughly 80 percent of conversations, is the warm positive/playful voice with a smile audible in it. The selective tone, saved for the two or three moments that carry the most weight, is the calm, slow, downward-inflected late-night-FM-DJ voice.     16. The five failure modes are not independent. The MQL/SQL drift on the marketing side reliably triggers a parallel-track silo on the sales side within two quarters, because reps stop trusting the lead feed and start prospecting from their own lists.     Parallel-track silos. These are the corporate version of two negotiators working the same counterpart with different offers. A rep emails one price while the marketing automation queue triggers a second offer on a different deadline, and the buyer plays the tracks against each other. The firm loses pricing power inside a single account because it is bidding against itself. This worksheet is the chapter’s operating sequence pulled out of the spine so you can run it on any firm, your own or one you are studying, plus the one-call prep the conversation moves feed. Work top to bottom. Part A diagnoses the alignment, and Part B preps the call.

    Part A. Diagnose your sales-marketing alignment.

    1. Check whether both functions are paid for one number.

    Find the marketing bonus formula and the sales bonus formula and read what each one rewards. If marketing is paid per lead and sales per close, the two are optimizing against each other by design, and no amount of goodwill fixes a comp plan that pays for divergence.

    2. Check whether the qualified-buyer definition is written down.

    Ask for the one document both teams accept as the definition of a qualified lead, usually BANT: budget, authority, need, and timeline. If marketing’s “qualified” and sales’s “qualified” live in two separate heads, the handoff seam is already drifting.

    3. Check whether the SLA is audited on the bonus cadence.

    A real SLA states what marketing owes (qualified-lead volume above a quality bar) and what sales owes (a first touch inside a set window, a capped rejection rate), and both numbers get reviewed on the same schedule the bonus runs on. An SLA nobody audits is a memo.

    4. Name which alignment rung your documents describe.

    Place the firm on the four-rung ladder: Undefined (no shared metric), Defined (a written SLA), Aligned (at least one number merged across both scorecards), or Integrated. Most firms plateau at Defined, because the climb to Aligned means touching the comp plan, which is the lever firms least want to move.

    Part B. Prep one discovery call.

    5. Draft the call before you dial.

    Read the target account’s public artifacts, its mission page and its last earnings call, and write down the buyer’s worldview words to speak back to her, three Black Swan candidates (facts no script would surface), four calibrated how-and-what questions, and a non-round Ackerman anchor for any number you will name.     Key Takeaways

    • Sales and marketing fight because they are paid for different numbers. A marketer paid per lead lowers the bar while a rep paid per close rejects soft leads, and both are acting rationally, so a shared metric is a design choice the firm makes, not a personality clash to manage away.
    • A written SLA on a shared metric is the working fix. Marketing owes qualified-lead volume above a quality bar and sales owes a fast first touch and a capped rejection rate, and both numbers are audited on the same cadence the bonus runs on.
    • The conversation runs on listening, not on pushing. The rep wins by mirroring, labeling, asking calibrated how-and-what questions, and earning a “that’s right,” then surfacing the Black Swan and bargaining on a non-round number.
    • Closed-won is not retention-ready. The handoff from the closer to customer success is one of the two seams where revenue leaks, so a sale only becomes a kept customer after onboarding and expansion do their work.
    • A CRM database without its philosophy tracks the wrong number perfectly. Green dashboards that measure products-per-household while the relationship quietly erodes are the Wells Fargo failure at every scale, so the metric has to be designed jointly across sales, marketing, and customer success.
    • In 2026 the buyer’s AI ranks the shortlist before any rep makes contact. Marketing’s job shifts toward being the citable source the buyer’s agent retrieves, because the rep now enters a conversation the buyer’s assistant has already half-decided.

    2. The chapter says revenue leaks at exactly two seams in the pipeline. Name them and say why each is disputed.

    The first seam is marketing-to-sales, where marketing’s “qualified” meets sales’s “qualified,” and it is disputed because the two teams rarely share a written definition of a qualified lead.     3. A lead clears marketing’s 75-point scoring threshold but a rep has not yet spoken with the buyer. Is it an MQL or an SQL, and what has to happen for it to become the other?

    It is a marketing-qualified lead, an MQL, because it cleared marketing’s lead-scoring threshold but has not been vetted in a live conversation. The MQL-to-SQL conversion is the marketing-to-sales seam in action, and it works only when both teams accept the same definition of qualified.

    4. A buyer says “your price is thirty percent over a competing offer.” Walk the four listening moves a rep uses instead of defending the price.

    First mirror by repeating her last few words as a question (“thirty percent over?”) and going silent, which makes her elaborate. Then label the pressure (“it seems like budget is the real constraint here”), phrased so she corrects you rather than defends herself. Then ask a calibrated how-or-what question (“how am I supposed to make that work?”) that hands the problem back to her. Finally, listen for “that’s right,” the moment she recognizes herself in your summary, which is the reward that signals she feels understood, not a brush-off “you’re right.”

    5. An SLA is a two-way promise. State concretely what marketing commits to and what sales commits to, and say what makes the document real rather than a memo.

    Marketing commits to deliver a stated volume of qualified leads above an agreed quality bar, and sales commits to a first touch inside a set window (often twenty-four hours) and to keeping its lead-rejection rate under a capped ceiling. What makes it real is that both numbers are audited on the same cadence the bonus runs on, so the promise has teeth. An SLA that no one reviews on the comp schedule is just a document, because nothing changes when either side misses.

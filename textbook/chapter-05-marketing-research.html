     01 // why research exists

    Why research exists at all

    Every marketing move is a forecast about how some group of people will behave later. The product team bets buyers will want the thing once it ships, and the pricing team bets the chosen price holds against rivals next quarter. Each bet is wrong with some chance, and research is the work of shrinking that chance before the budget commits. To size any study, write down two numbers, the way Hubbard's measurement framework suggests. The first is how unsure you are right now, and the second is how much of that doubt the study would clear. When the second number is small next to the first, you are paying for theater.

    Research earns its keep because the cost of being wrong is lopsided. A soft headline can be re-shot in two weeks for the price of one creative round, while a formula your most loyal buyers grew up with usually cannot be put back so cheaply. New Coke is the textbook case. In April 1985 Coca-Cola swapped its formula on the strength of blind taste tests, where the new recipe beat the old one. Those tests were run correctly, but they measured the wrong thing for the decision the company was making. Within seventy-nine days the company had taken roughly 400,000 calls and letters demanding the original back, and Coca-Cola Classic returned to the shelf.

    Research is not free. A national survey of 1,200 people runs roughly 80,000 to 120,000 dollars in 2026 and takes nine to twelve weeks to first results, about a quarter of a mid-career analyst's pay and one product-launch cycle. So commissioning research is always a question of whether the answer is worth the bill, and there is a clean ceiling on that bill. The most you should ever pay to learn something is what being wrong about it would cost you. If shipping the wrong product would lose 200,000 dollars, then perfect certainty about which to ship is worth at most 200,000 dollars, and any study priced above the mistake overpays. That ceiling is called EVPI. A working rule of thumb spends 2 to 10 percent of it on any one study, so a 200,000-dollar mistake justifies around 4,000 to 20,000 dollars of research, not a six-figure deck.

    02 // three modes

    The three modes of research

    Picture the Stanley brand manager from the opening in 2018, with the slow Quencher line on her dashboard and one question: keep it or kill it. The dashboard showed which buyers she already had, but it was silent on everything that mattered next. Why might a colorway matter to a buyer? How many women 25 to 50 would buy if Stanley changed how it sold the cup? Those are different questions, and each wants a different shape of answer.

    Picture the Stanley manager in 2018 with one question: keep the slow Quencher line or kill it. Why might a colorway matter to a buyer, and how many women 25 to 50 would buy if Stanley sold the cup differently? Each question wants a different shape of answer.

    Questions answered with words about reasoning are qualitative research. Questions answered with numbers about how many and how often are quantitative research. Questions answered with records of what buyers actually did are observational research. Each mode is a real toolkit with real costs, and the next three sections walk them in turn, so here we only need the shape of each. Some textbooks instead sort research by its purpose, into exploratory, descriptive, and causal, but the method is what actually picks the tool, so this book sorts by how you collect.

    Qualitative research Talking to a small number of buyers in depth, usually eight to thirty, then reading the transcripts for patterns. The output is words, and the point is to figure out what the right question even is. You use it when you do not yet know enough to write a good survey, because a buyer in a long conversation tells you the reason behind a choice that no checkbox would have caught.
    Quantitative research Measuring a clear question on a larger sample, from a couple hundred people to several thousand. The output is numbers with an honest margin around them, and the goal is to estimate how much, how often, who, and when. You reach for it once you know the question well enough to ask it the same way of everyone, because that sameness is what lets you count the answers and trust the count. Surveys, panel data, and behavioral logs all live here.
    Observational research Watching what buyers actually do, usually without asking, and comparing one group to another. The clearest version is an A/B test, where some visitors see one design and the rest see another, and you read the difference in what they click or buy. This mode is trusted because it records behavior instead of opinion, and behavior is harder to fake than an answer on a form.

    The modes are not a menu you pick one from. Most studies of any real size combine two of the three, and qualitative usually goes first, because a few good interviews tell you what to measure before you spend the money to measure it. The same brand-tracker can sit in two modes at once depending on which question the team is asking that week.

    Settle the measurement first

    Premium quality lives inside the buyer’s head, and you cannot reach it with one question. A single item mixes the thing you care about with whatever the wording stirred up that day, like judging a song from one second of audio. So the team asks four or five questions that circle the same idea from different angles (materials, build, design, overall feel) and averages the answers.     The number that tells you whether the scale is signal or noise is a single score between zero and one, called Cronbach’s alpha. Zero means the questions have nothing to do with each other, and one means they are perfectly redundant. The working floor is 0.70 for a brand-new scale and 0.80 for an established one. An alpha of 0.84 says about 84 percent of the movement in the score is the thing you meant to measure, while an alpha of 0.59 says you are mostly reading static. So the brand manager’s two-point drop is worth acting on only if the scale cleared 0.70 to begin with. If the alpha drifts below the floor for two quarters running, she is reading noise, and the right move is to pause the recommendation and rebuild the scale.     Size the sample honestly

    The bigger your sample, the more sure you can be the result is real and not a fluke of who answered. The catch is that the payoff shrinks fast, so going from 50 to 200 respondents tightens your read sharply while going from 1,000 to 1,200 barely moves it. It works like cramming for an exam, where the first hour teaches you most of the material and the tenth adds almost nothing. For a normal attitude question on a national survey, 384 respondents buys you a 5-point margin of error at 95 percent confidence. That means the true number in the population sits within 5 percentage points of your survey number, 95 times out of 100. So if 42 percent of your sample says they would buy, the real rate is almost certainly between 37 and 47 percent. The 384 figure is not a guess. The Cochran sample-size formula returns it for a worst-case 50-50 split, and the square-root law behind it is why the payoff flattens out.

    You also need the difference between two ideas that sound alike.

    Statistical significance Statistical significance is a yes-or-no answer to one question: could this result be luck? If a survey shows women are 7 points likelier to buy than men, significance asks how often you would see a gap that big purely by chance when the truth is they buy at the same rate. If that chance is under 5 percent, it counts as significant. The number that quantifies “could this be luck” is the p-value.
    Effect size Effect size is a separate question: how big is the difference, in plain units? A 2-point lift is tiny, and a 12-point lift is large. You can have a significant difference too small to bother with, or a big difference in a sample too small to clear the bar. The size of the improvement one version caused over another is its lift.

    Draw it at random, not by convenience

    The Stanley misread in 2018 was a sampling problem. The dashboard only held the buyers Stanley already had, while the women who actually wanted the Quencher were nowhere in it. There are three ways to draw a sample, ordered by how seriously to take the resulting numbers.

    Random sampling Every member of the population has a known, equal chance of being drawn into the sample. The gold standard, and rare in practice because you usually cannot list everyone. The list you draw from is the sampling frame.
    Stratified sampling Split the population into groups (men and women, light and heavy buyers), draw a random sample inside each, and weight the groups back together. Most professional consumer surveys work this way, because it guarantees enough of each subgroup to say something about it.
    Convenience sampling You survey whoever is easy to reach, so the numbers describe the people in the sample, not the population they were supposed to stand for. Stanley’s 2018 dashboard was a convenience sample of its own buyers.

    Force the trade-off with conjoint

    Conjoint analysis Conjoint analysis works out how much each feature (graphic, color, price) drives a buyer’s choice, by making her pick between complete bundled concepts instead of rating features one at a time. It recovers each feature’s pull, the dollars a buyer would pay for it, and a simulator that predicts the share for any combination the survey never showed. It has been the standard tool for pricing and product design for more than fifty years.

    Conjoint exists because the obvious alternative breaks in a predictable way. Ask the roster “how important is the graphic, on a scale of 1 to 7,” and almost everyone rates it a 6, then rates price and color a 6 too, so you get three numbers near the top, no rank order, and no idea what anyone would give up. Rating a feature costs the buyer nothing, so the answers carry no weight. Conjoint fixes this by making every question a trade, where the buyer sees a few complete shirts side by side and picks the one she would really buy. When she takes the $25 charcoal over the $20 orange, she just gave up $5 to get it, and that choice means something a rating never could. Repeat across a dozen sets, add up 92 brothers, and you learn how many dollars the average brother trades for each feature.

    The model hands each brother his own scores rather than one chapter average, the same move a Spotify Wrapped genre score makes when it starts from a default and pulls toward what you actually played. The partworth and the hierarchical-Bayes estimator are the machinery, and dividing every non-price score by the price score turns conjoint into a willingness-to-pay tool.     Measurement Churchill’s eight-step paradigm and Cronbach’s alpha

    The eight steps that build and validate a survey scale, codified by Churchill in the Journal of Marketing Research in 1979. Steps 1 to 5 build the instrument: specify the construct domain; generate 15 to 30 candidate items from prior scales (Aaker, SERVQUAL) and, in 2026, AI prompts; pilot on 100 to 300 respondents; purify by dropping items whose item-to-total correlation falls below 0.30 and running exploratory factor analysis; refield on a fresh sample. Step 6 assesses reliability with Cronbach’s alpha (published in Psychometrika, 1951; the 0.70/0.80 thresholds entered practice in 1978). Steps 7 and 8 assess validity and develop norms.

    Computing alpha needs nothing but a spreadsheet: one respondent per row, one item per column, then standardized alpha equals k divided by (k minus 1), all times (1 minus the sum of the item variances divided by the variance of the row totals), where k is the item count.     Sample size The Cochran formula and the square-root law

    The Cochran sample-size formula is n = (Z2 · p · (1-p)) / E2. The n is the sample size you solve for. The Z is the critical value of the standard normal distribution at the chosen confidence level, about 1.96 for 95 percent confidence and larger for higher confidence. The p is the proportion you expect to give the answer you care about, set to 0.5 when you do not know it because that produces the most conservative (largest) estimate. The E is the margin of error as an absolute proportion (0.05 for a 5-point margin). Plugging in 0.5 for p and 0.05 for E at 95 percent confidence returns 384. Cochran, Sampling Techniques, 1977.

    The payoff flattens because of the Central Limit Theorem. Rerun the same survey with fresh respondents and the average answer clusters in a bell centered on the truth, with a width (the standard error) that shrinks as the square root of the sample size. For a proportion the standard error equals the square root of p times (1 minus p), divided by n. The square root is why doubling the sample only narrows the bell by a factor of 1.41, and why cutting the margin from 5 to 2.5 points costs four times the respondents, not twice.

    Conjoint Partworths and the hierarchical-Bayes estimator

    A partworth is the preference score the model assigns to one level of one feature. A hierarchical-Bayes estimator recovers a separate set for each respondent: hierarchical because each respondent’s score starts at the sample average and gets pulled toward her own answers, Bayesian because that starting average is a prior the data then updates. Allenby and Rossi published the canonical version in Marketing Science in 1996, and Sawtooth Software’s module has been the production implementation since the late 1990s.

    The dollar conversion is what turns conjoint into a willingness-to-pay tool: divide every non-price partworth by the slope of the price partworths.     2026 question Can the language model be the sample?

    By 2026 a vendor pitches you a synthetic-respondent panel twice a quarter: a thousand simulated survey answers for the price of a Claude subscription. The optimist research is real, with early papers showing language-model “respondents” tracking human answers closely. The catch shows up in the spread. A model gets the average answer roughly right but squeezes the range of opinion to about half its true width, so any confidence interval you build comes out too narrow and any power calculation is wrong by a factor of two. The defensible move is not to swap the model in for people. You pair a small human sample with a much larger model sample, measure how the model drifts from the humans on the overlap, and correct the model sample with that drift.     04 // qualitative mechanics

    The mechanics of a qualitative study

    Quantitative work tells you how many. Qualitative work tells you why, by listening to a small number of buyers in their own words. You reach for it first, before you write a single survey question, because you cannot ask a buyer to rate a reason you have not yet discovered. Two formats do most of the work. A focus group puts a handful of similar buyers together and lets the disagreement between them bring out what a survey would miss. An in-depth interview sits one researcher with one buyer, the right setting when the honest answer is something a person will not say in front of peers. Two more formats round out the kit: ethnography and online listening.

    Open coding Read each transcript and tag short phrases with a plain label (worried about leak, likes the handle, gives as a gift). Twenty transcripts throw off two-to-four hundred messy tags.

    05 // observational mechanics

    The mechanics of an observational study

    Surveys ask buyers what they would do. Observational research watches what they actually did, and its workhorse is the A/B test.     Apply What You Have Learned

    Do this, about ten minutes

    Scope a study before you spend a dollar on it

    Pick a real decision a brand you know is facing right now: a price change, a new flavor, a packaging refresh, anything where the team is guessing about how buyers will react. Then run the chapter's machinery on it on paper, before any money moves.

    1. Write the two problems separately. State the management decision problem in one line (ship A or B, raise the price or hold it), then state the marketing research problem underneath it (what share of buyers would pick each, and which feature drives the gap). Keeping them apart is what New Coke got wrong.
    2. Put a number on being wrong. Guess what shipping the wrong call would cost the brand, then guess how likely the team's current lean is to be wrong. Multiply the two. That product is the EVPI, the ceiling on what any study is worth, and 2 to 10 percent of it is what a single study should cost.
    3. Pick the mode. Decide whether the question wants words about reasoning (qualitative), numbers about how many (quantitative), or a record of what buyers did (observational). If you do not yet know enough to write a good survey, the answer is qualitative first.
    4. Name the population, then the trap. Write down who the decision is actually about, and name the convenience sample you would reach for if you were lazy. The gap between those two is exactly the selection bias that cost Stanley its women buyers in 2018.

    Run it once and the chapter stops being abstract. The Sigma Chi treasurer who ran the conjoint on all 92 brothers learned that vintage-block orange at $25 took 64 percent of the roster, while the mascot cream the social chair wanted would have sold about 14 against a break-even of 110. The study cost a survey link and an afternoon, and it saved the run.

    Key Takeaways

    • Research is the price of shrinking a guess. Every marketing move is a bet on how people will behave later, and a study earns its keep only when it removes enough doubt to change the decision.
    • The ceiling on research spend is the cost of being wrong. EVPI is that cost times the chance the current lean is mistaken, and a single study should fall at 2 to 10 percent of it, never at last year's budget.
    • The question picks the mode. Words about reasoning go to qualitative, numbers about how many to quantitative, and records of behavior to observational, and most real studies need two of the three with qualitative going first.
    • A tight number around the wrong thing is still wrong. Settle the measurement before you measure, because a scale below 0.70 on Cronbach's alpha is mostly reading static, and a sample drawn by convenience describes only the people in it.
    • Random assignment is what makes a difference causal. An A/B test earns its trust from the coin flip, an A/A test shows that about 5 percent of comparisons cross the line on noise alone, and pre-registration is the defense against fishing for that fluke.
    • AI moves up a ladder of four tiers. A one-off ask, a saved Skill, a connected data source, and a scheduled run each need less of your attention than the one below, and every tier still owes a disclosure of the model, the prompt, and the human check.
    • The failures share one shape. Selection, expectancy, observer, leading-question, non-response, and stopping-early bias all return a number that looks solid while quietly lying, and each has a plain fix a marketer can name.

    Check Your Understanding

    Answer each in your head, then click to check.

    1. What separates the management decision problem from the marketing research problem, and why did collapsing the two sink New Coke?

    The management decision problem is the choice the manager has to make, such as ship the new formula or keep the old one. The marketing research problem is the information question whose answer lets her make that call, such as what loyal buyers would do when the familiar formula disappears. New Coke ran a blind taste test correctly and answered the narrow question, but the decision needed the broad one about loyalty, so the study measured the wrong thing accurately.

    2. A vendor pitches a $90,000 study on a decision where shipping the wrong product would cost about $200,000 and the team thinks it is 30 percent likely to be wrong. Is the price fair?

    No. The EVPI ceiling is the cost of being wrong times the chance of being wrong, $200,000 times 0.30, which is $60,000. The $90,000 study sits above the ceiling, so it costs more than the answer is worth. A single study should fall at 2 to 10 percent of EVPI, somewhere around $1,200 to $6,000 here, which means the question deserves a quick read, not a six-figure deck.

    3. A brand manager wants to know why a colorway matters, what share of women would buy at $45, and whether a new lid photo lifts add-to-cart. Which mode answers each?

    Why a colorway matters is a reason inside a buyer's head, so it is qualitative, answered through a conversation, not a count. The share of women who would buy at $45 is a number estimated on a sample, so it is quantitative. Whether the lid photo lifts add-to-cart is a behavior you watch and compare, so it is observational, an A/B test on the page.

    4. Why does asking each brother to rate the graphic, color, and price on a 1-to-7 scale fail, and what does conjoint do instead?

    Rating a feature costs the buyer nothing, so almost everyone rates all three near a 6, leaving no rank order and no idea what anyone would give up. Conjoint makes every question a trade by showing complete bundled shirts side by side and asking which one she would really buy. When she takes the $25 charcoal over the $20 orange, she gave up $5 to get it, and that choice carries weight a rating never could.

    5. What does an A/A test reveal that an A/B test cannot, and what is the number it puts a face on?

    An A/A test gives both groups the identical page from one population, so any result it flags as significant is a false positive by construction. Run many and about 5 percent still cross the 0.05 line on noise alone, which is the Type I error rate, alpha, made visible. It shows why peeking across twenty metrics harvests a fake winner, and why pre-registering the hypothesis and reporting every comparison is the only honest defense.

    6. A scale's Cronbach's alpha drifts to 0.59 for two quarters running. What does that number mean, and what is the right move?

    Alpha is a zero-to-one score for how much of the movement in the scale total is the thing you meant to measure rather than noise. At 0.59 the scale is mostly reading static, well below the 0.70 floor for a new scale and the 0.80 floor for an established one. A two-point drop on a scale that shaky is not worth acting on, so the move is to pause the recommendation and rebuild the scale before reading anything into it.

    7. A team grabs an A/B winner on day three because the early lift looks strong. Why is that risky, and what is the fix?

    Random noise is loudest in small samples, so an early winner is often a fluke, and a barely-significant winner from a tiny test averages several times larger than the real effect through the magnitude-error distortion. It can even point the wrong way entirely. The fix is to decide the sample size, the metric, and the smallest difference worth detecting before the test starts, then wait for the planned end-date.

    8. An AI codes 25 interview transcripts in five minutes for a few cents. Which two parts of the job does it not do, and what is the check?

    The model cannot write the rubric that says what counts as a theme, and it cannot vouch that it actually followed that rubric, so both stay with you. The check is Cohen's kappa between the model's codes and a human-coded sample, which has to clear 0.70 before you trust the run. Lean on the model for the first pass, where it helps most, but read the rare long-tail answers yourself, because a model reaching for the most common phrasing quietly flattens the distinctive themes.

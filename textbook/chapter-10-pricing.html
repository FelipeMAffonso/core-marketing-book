    Price is the only mix element that creates revenue.

    This chapter arrives from services, where a seat that goes empty tonight is gone and pricing is what decides whether it sells before it expires. Pricing a product works on the same nerve, because the number a firm posts is the one marketing decision that brings in revenue while every other decision spends it.

    In 2007 an EpiPen two-pack cost about $100 at the pharmacy counter. By 2016 the same two-pack cost $608. The drug had not changed and the injector had not changed, yet Mylan held about 90 percent of the U.S. market and parents had no substitute. The price climbed in roughly fifteen separate steps across nine years, and each step was small enough that no single move drew a public reaction, until the cumulative line broke past what parents of allergic children would tolerate quietly. Weeks later a separate hearing covered Turing Pharmaceuticals, where Daraprim went from $13.50 to $750 per tablet overnight, and within a year Mylan committed to a half-price generic while Turing went bankrupt.

    01 // pricing fundamentals

    Pricing Fundamentals

    Before any of the four approaches make sense, the chapter rests on four pieces of cost arithmetic that decide how much room a price move has to lift profit. The first is variable cost, the cost a firm pays only when it sells one more unit, so the wholesale cost of the next can of soda or the next bag of coffee beans counts as variable, and the cost goes to zero if the firm sells nothing this period. The second is fixed cost, the cost the firm carries whether it sells one unit or a million, including a long-term lease, a brand-manager salary, or a year-end software subscription, and a firm's exposure to a slow quarter rises with the share of its costs that sit in this bucket.

    Four pieces of cost arithmetic decide how much room a price move has. Variable cost is paid only when one more unit is sold, while fixed cost is paid whether the firm sells one unit or a million, and the share of costs sitting in the fixed bucket is the firm's exposure to a slow quarter.

    The third piece is contribution margin, the dollars each unit contributes toward fixed cost and profit after the unit's own variable cost has been paid, calculated as price minus variable cost per unit. A $4 latte that costs the shop $0.80 in beans, milk, and a cup carries a contribution margin of $3.20 per latte, and the percentage version, contribution margin divided by price, runs about 80 percent for that latte and closer to 5 percent for a can of soda at a convenience store. The fourth piece is the breakeven volume, the number of units the firm has to sell at a given price to cover its fixed cost, calculated as fixed cost divided by contribution margin per unit. A cafe with $4,000 a month in fixed cost breaks even at 1,250 lattes a month at that $3.20 contribution margin, and a price cut to $3.50 drops the contribution margin to $2.70, which lifts the breakeven volume to roughly 1,481 lattes a month.

    These four numbers carry the rest of the chapter, because every pricing approach reads them differently. Cost-based pricing starts from variable cost plus a target markup and lets contribution margin and breakeven fall out of the calculation. Competitor-based pricing pins price against a rival and lets the contribution margin take whatever shape the rival's number allows. Value-based pricing reads the buyer's willingness to pay first and then walks back to confirm the contribution margin is positive enough to cover fixed cost. The arithmetic also explains why the same one-cent move can be trivial for a grocery store and existential for a software firm, because a grocery store's contribution margin per unit is so thin that a small variable-cost shock erases it, while a software firm's variable cost per copy rounds to zero and almost any price clears the breakeven.

    Asymmetric leverage, the payoff of the arithmetic

    Picture a firm with $100 of revenue and $97 of cost. The $3 left over is profit, a 3 percent net margin, the thin sliver grocery chains and warehouse clubs live with every quarter. Now suppose the firm raises its price by just 1 percent and holds cost constant. Revenue rises to $101, cost stays at $97, and profit jumps from $3 to $4. That is a 33 percent increase in profit on a 1 percent change in price, and it is what people mean when they call pricing the highest-leverage decision in marketing. Matching that same dollar of profit through volume would take 33 percent more units, because every extra unit drags its proportional share of marketing, distribution, and operations cost. A 1 percent price move beats a 1 percent volume move by an order of magnitude, yet most pricing teams are paid to manage volume.

    Marginal-cost asymmetry Price is the only mix lever where extra revenue does not require extra cost. Selling the same unit higher costs zero more, while selling one more unit costs whatever it takes to make and ship it.

    Cost-based pricing

    Cost-based pricing adds a markup to unit cost, so a unit bought for $60 with a 50 percent markup reads $90 on the sticker. It survives because cost is easier to estimate than demand and the number reads fair to a buyer and to a regulator alike. The failure mode is that buyers pay for what a product is worth to them rather than for what it cost the firm, so the approach leaves the ceiling unread. Regulated utilities are its honest home, where a regulator allows a set return and the price is worked backward from that return at the forecast volume through target-rate-of-return pricing.

    Competitor-based pricing

    Competitor-based pricing anchors to what rivals charge, so the firm can match the leader, beat it by 5 percent, or hold a 5-to-10 percent premium for a stronger reputation. It lives in commodity and grocery categories, where in 2024 Publix cut prices below Walmart's on roughly 500 essential items. The danger is the reflexive match, because when a rival drops, an automatic cut either starts a price war the firm did not want or cedes share it cannot win back.     Value-based pricing

    Value-based pricing asks a different question than the first two. Cost-plus asks what the product cost the firm to make, competitor-match asks what the next product on the shelf charges, and value-based asks what the product is worth to a buyer who would otherwise buy that next-best alternative, which is the only question tied to the buyer's wallet. The tool that answers it is economic value to customer (EVC), and it builds in two steps. First the firm finds the alternative the buyer would otherwise have and takes its price as the anchor. Then it walks the buyer through each attribute where this product is better, attaching a dollar premium to each one and showing where that premium came from in the buyer's own operating data.

    Caterpillar's dealer pitch in a competitive bid is the worked example. The dealer opens at the $90,000 competitor-equivalent base the buyer's procurement team has already anchored on, then attaches a premium to each attribute that justifies paying more. Longer component life is worth $7,000 because it stretches the replacement cycle by years, fewer breakdowns are worth $6,000 because less idle rental time shows up on the foreman's downtime log, and same-day parts add $5,000 because three days of idle equipment on a paving contract is itself a number with a dollar sign. The warranty adds $2,000 because it offloads risk the buyer would otherwise self-insure. Since each premium is a number the buyer can verify against their own logs, the four premiums on the $90,000 base sum to a $110,000 EVC. In practice the dealer does not post the full $110,000, because a value-based pitch almost always closes at a discount that lets both sides feel they captured something. The pitch closes at a $10,000 discount, the buyer signs at $100,000, and the dealer captures $10,000 of premium a pure competitor-match at $90,000 would have given away while the buyer captures $10,000 of value. The discount is the closing move, and the EVC walk is what made the discount possible.

    Customer-determined pricing

    Customer-determined pricing inverts the locus, so the buyer names a candidate price and the firm accepts or rejects it. Priceline matches name-your-own-price flight bids against airline databases overnight, Humble Bundle lets the buyer split a slider between the developer and a charity, and B2B reverse auctions have suppliers bid each other down to win the contract. It works under three conditions. The firm has to be willing to accept some prices below its preferred number, which fits zero-marginal-cost inventory like an unsold airline seat. Willingness-to-pay has to vary widely enough that the mechanism does not collapse into one posted price. And the realized prices have to stay private after the sale, because a buyer who pays $200 and then sees the next person pay $120 on the same browser tab reads the gap as unfair, which Section 10.7 returns to as the binding constraint on every pricing approach.

    A 1969 diffusion model put numbers on which posture fits. Buyers split into innovators, who adopt after an ad, and imitators, who adopt after seeing friends adopt. Cumulative adoption traces an S-curve, and the shape depends on the q-to-p ratio. The 1999 regime rule sets the threshold: when q/p is above about 7, the imitation cascade dominates and a cheap launch puts more sparks in early, so penetration wins, and when q/p is below about 4, the cascade is too weak to recover the sacrificed margin, so skimming wins. The campus drink lives in the high-q/p regime because every party photo and group chat is an imitation channel, so a $4 launch beats an $8 launch on two-semester share. The choice also depends on how much the new product asks the buyer to change, because the deeper the workflow change, the more the launch needs penetration, since loss aversion against the old way roughly multiplies the gap between projected and realized adoption.

    Willingness To Pay And Price Elasticity

    Value-based pricing is only as good as the firm's read of willingness to pay (WTP), the most a buyer would pay before walking away. A marketer who personally pays $35 for a Stanley tumbler still has to price for a buyer who pays $20 and a buyer who pays $60, because WTP varies from one customer to the next. That spread across buyers is what gives the demand curve its downward slope.

The fix is the absurdity walkdown, run before the survey fields. The team opens with bounds it thinks ridiculous on both ends, a 40-ounce tumbler at 10 cents and at $400, then tightens each bound one click at a time, defending every move, until it can describe a real buyer paying that number. The output is a price range the team is roughly 90 percent confident contains the buyer's true WTP, which keeps the survey grid off the team's own anchor.

    Price elasticity of demand

    Price elasticity of demand measures how much volume moves when price moves, so an elasticity of negative 2 means a 1 percent price increase costs 2 percent of volume. A meta-analysis of 1,851 brand-level elasticities across 81 studies puts the cross-category average at negative 2.62, so the average brand loses about 2.6 percent of volume for every 1 percent it raises price. The negative sign is just the convention that price and volume move in opposite directions, so the magnitude is what the firm reads against 1.0. Below negative 1 the category is elastic and a price increase loses more revenue than it adds, while close to zero it is inelastic and the increase passes through.

    2. Buyers do not notice the price. Spotify Premium auto-charges on the first of every month, so most subscribers cannot recall the exact number without checking a statement.
    3. Habits are slow to change. A 10-year Costco member keeps the Saturday run after a fee bump, because switching baskets costs more than the bump.
    4. A cost or scarcity story justifies the price. The Stanley color drops that sell out in 90 seconds read as fair, because the limited supply makes the premium make sense.
    5. The spend is small relative to income. A $2 increase on a $4.99 rotisserie hurts, while the same $2 on a $279 jacket is invisible.
    6. A third party bears part of the cost. A company-paid Salesforce seat is charged to the employer, so the user never feels the price the way an out-of-pocket buyer would.

    The six are a distinctive product, a price the buyer does not notice (Spotify's auto-charge), sticky habits (a long-tenured Costco member), a cost-or-scarcity story (the Stanley color drops), small spend relative to income ($2 on a $279 jacket is invisible), and a third party paying (a company-billed Salesforce seat).     Recovering willingness to pay across many buyers

    The matched-market test gives one elasticity number for one SKU at one moment. The bigger question a brand manager faces at a launch or a portfolio review is how to estimate reservation prices across many buyers without an eight-week field test on each one. Four procedures recover the WTP distribution, ordered cheapest-and-least-reliable to most-expensive-and-most-valid, and the gap between their numbers is itself a category-specific diagnostic.

    The four reservation-price procedures Direct asking ("what is the most you would pay?") runs 20 to 40 percent below the other methods, because the buyer has every reason to under-report.
    Van Westendorp's price-sensitivity meter asks four indirect questions and reads an acceptable band off where their curves cross, the launch-stage default when the firm has no analog data.
    Conjoint analysis shows bundled product profiles and infers a dollar value per feature from the trade-offs, the default when an SKU sits inside a product line.
    Scanner-data revealed preference fits a model to what actually sold at each posted price, the most externally valid procedure and the one the elasticity numbers above rest on.

    The four procedures form a sequence, so the team runs Van Westendorp first, then conjoint inside its range, then a scanner-data check once the launch is live. Most undergraduates reach for Van Westendorp first, because 200 respondents and twenty minutes of analyst time is what a campus project supports, while conjoint costs three to five times more in field dollars and two to three times longer in analyst time.

    Conjoint analysis for willingness to pay

    Van Westendorp tells the firm what buyers pay for the whole product, not which feature is doing the work. A 30-ounce Stanley with a steel body, a leak-proof lid, and a lifetime warranty sells at one WTP number, although the brand manager wants to know whether the buyer is paying for capacity, steel, the lid, or the warranty. Conjoint analysis recovers a dollar value for each feature by forcing buyers to choose between complete bundles, where the trade-offs they make reveal what each attribute is worth.

    N. The textbook worked case is EINC, a roofing-materials firm that ran a 900-respondent conjoint to find which attribute drove its premium. Buyers would pay about $387 more for the longer-life roof and $414 more for the better-installation roof, but only $71 more for the better-looking one, per square. The surprise was that aesthetics was where the firm had concentrated nearly all of its R&D for years.     Measuring willingness to pay gives the firm a number, but what the buyer does with that number on the shelf is a separate problem.     Prospect theory is the first foundation, because Kahneman and Tversky's 1979 paper showed that buyers do not read each dollar as an absolute amount but as a gain or loss measured against an internal reference point, and a loss of a given size feels roughly twice as painful as an equivalent gain. The practical consequence for the firm is that an increase from $9.99 to $10.99 carries more pain than the dollar arithmetic suggests, while a cut from $10.99 to $9.99 carries less pleasure than the same arithmetic would predict. A firm raising price therefore frames the move as preserving quality or absorbing inflation, while a firm cutting price rarely produces the loyalty bump a managerial intuition expects, because the cut registers as merely returning to fair.

    The endowment effect is the second foundation, and the cleanest demonstration is the 1990 mug experiment that Kahneman, Knetsch, and Thaler ran at Cornell. Half the students received a $6 university mug and half received nothing, after which the two groups traded the mug in an open market. The result generalizes directly to pricing decisions, because a free trial converts at a higher rate than the same product offered for a small fee since cancelling now reads as giving up a possession, and a trade-in offer outperforms an equal cash rebate because the trade-in lets the buyer realize the endowment value of what they own.

    Reference prices and anchoring

    Buyers read every price through a comparison lens. The reference price is the internal benchmark each new price gets measured against, and it is a weighted moving average of recent exposures rather than a single fixed number. A buyer's reference for a latte moves with the last several lattes they bought plus competitor menus, so a $600 dress reads moderate next to a $1,200 sibling and expensive next to a $200 one. Buyers also miss small changes, because the just-noticeable-difference runs 5 to 15 percent across consumer categories, so a change below that fraction is invisible and the firm keeps the full increase as margin. Around that reference sits a latitude of acceptance, the band inside which a buyer accepts the price without bothering to compare. The lower edge matters as much as the upper one, because a boutique cutting from $60 to $35 to drive volume can watch volume fall when the lower price recodes the product as not the real thing.

    A. The economic baseline is the 1883 Bertrand paradox, where two firms selling identical products at identical cost should compete prices down to marginal cost and earn nothing, because either can undercut by a penny. Real industries escape it through four mechanisms a 1988 Theory of Industrial Organization catalogued: capacity constraints, product differentiation, repeated interaction, and buyer search costs.

    Search costs do most of the modern pricing work.     Three degrees of price discrimination

    The economic foundation is price discrimination, charging different buyers different prices for the same product. It comes in three degrees.

    First-degree price discrimination Each buyer pays their personal maximum willingness to pay, so the firm captures all the consumer surplus. Rare in pure form, though Priceline and bilateral B2B negotiation approximate it.
    Second-degree price discrimination Per-unit price varies by quantity, not by who the buyer is. Quantity discounts, two-part pricing, and tiered subscriptions are second-degree mechanisms.
    Third-degree price discrimination Different segments pay different prices for the same product. Museums charge students, seniors, and adults separate rates, and airlines split business and leisure fares.

    Five conditions have to hold for any of the three degrees to work. The firm needs a market it can split into groups with different demand intensities, since the mechanism rests on charging the high-willingness group more than the low one. It needs to block resale between the segments, because arbitrage flattens the gap back to a single price. It needs no competitor undercutting the higher segment, since that buyer defects once they spot the cheaper offer. The cost of running the segmentation has to stay below the revenue it captures. The fifth condition is the binding one in practice, because the rollout must not produce resentment severe enough to damage the brand, since the dual-entitlement record in Section 10.7 shows that buyers reject economically efficient transactions they read as unfair, even at a cost to themselves.

    C. The clearest welfare evidence is a 2023 JPE randomized experiment at ZipRecruiter, which compared three regimes. Moving from the status-quo price to an optimal uniform price lifted firm profit 55 percent, and layering personalization on top added another 19 percent. Two implications follow for a 2026 rollout. Most firms overestimate personalization and underestimate the uniform-optimization step, so the right sequence is optimal-uniform first and personalization second. And more than 60 percent of buyers under personalization paid less than under the optimal uniform price, because personalization moves prices both ways and reaches low-willingness buyers a single price would have excluded. Aggregate consumer surplus fell about 23 percent, yet an inequity-averse welfare measure can rise, because personalization works as a subsidy for buyers who could not afford the uniform price, financed by a premium on those who could.
    D. The substrate under personalized pricing is the tracking cookie. A 2.5-year European panel of 54,127 users measured an average cookie lifetime of 279 days and an average data-asset value of €2.52 per cookie, with the top 13 percent worth roughly four times the average, so a one-year cap cuts aggregate cookie value about 9 percent but top-decile value about 25 percent.     Freemium, Subscription, And Pricing Innovations

    Yield management rations fixed perishable capacity, so the seat or the room that goes empty tonight is gone. Consumer software faces the opposite condition, because one more user costs the firm close to nothing and capacity is effectively unlimited. That inversion changes the whole pricing problem from rationing access to converting it. The 2010s produced two structures built on that condition, freemium and subscription, and both are variants of two-part pricing.

    Freemium A two-tier price structure: a free tier with limited features acquires users at near-zero marginal cost, and a paid tier with full features generates revenue. The free tier is acquisition; the paid tier is monetization. The conversion rate from free to paid drives the model's economics, with most consumer-software products converting 2 to 8 percent.

    When the marginal cost of one more user falls to near zero, the firm's pricing problem stops being per-unit cost recovery and becomes acquiring users who would not pay anything to try the product. The model works only when the converted cohort covers fixed development and acquisition spend across the whole free-plus-paid population, which makes freemium a conversion problem before it is a pricing one. Conversion across leading platforms shows the spread. Spotify ended 2024 with roughly 40 percent of its near-600-million monthly users on a paid tier, an exceptionally high rate that reflects an integrated free-and-paid app. Duolingo Super converts around 8 percent and Notion around 4 percent. Slack and Dropbox report team conversion of 25 to 30 percent once a workspace crosses an active-member threshold, the network-effect lift that separates team-dynamic freemium from purely individual use.

Three conditions make freemium work: near-zero marginal cost per user, a network or data effect (Slack, Notion, Figma), and a free-to-paid gap large enough to motivate the upgrade yet small enough to keep the free tier genuinely useful. A 680,588-user 2025 field experiment tested 7-day against 3-day trials: the 7-day window raised conversion 20.9 percent overall, driven by a 42.4 percent lift in delayed conversion with near-zero immediate effect, and high-individualism markets responded more, which argues against a uniform multinational trial length. A 2024 corollary holds that a nominal fee produces a sense of ownership a $0 tier does not, which Duolingo Super Family ($1.67 per user) tests inside for-profit freemium.

    Subscription is freemium without the free tier. Adobe replaced boxed Creative Suite at $2,500 one-time with Creative Cloud at $60 a month in 2013, and revenue per user crossed the old boxed number within 42 months. The levers are the same three the calculator exposes: price, churn, and CAC. Netflix's ad tier, launched in 2023, carried 60 percent of new sign-ups by early 2026, and the Disney+/Hulu/HBO Max bundle launched in July 2024 retained 80 percent of subscribers at three months against 55 percent for HBO Max standalone. A 2025 study of online-news publisher panel data found a pattern that inverts the standard demand-curve prediction, because promotional subscribers consumed substantially more than regular-price subscribers and stayed active nearly twice as long. The mechanism is subscription type-selection: the promotional rate sorts buyers on a willingness-to-engage type the subscription decision alone cannot identify.

    The means-and-ends frame: pricing what the buyer actually buys

    Freemium, subscription, and dynamic pricing all sit on top of one upstream question. Is the firm selling the buyer a product, the means, or charging for the outcome the buyer is trying to reach, the end? The buyer walking into the hardware store does not want a drill, she wants a hole in the wall. The price structures that answer this question sit on a risk-transfer spectrum across four families.

    1. Pay-per-use pricing: a per-unit charge on consumption (a flight hour, a printed page, a ride). Rolls-Royce TotalCare prices jet-engine maintenance per flight hour, not per overhaul, which moves consumption risk to the firm.
    2. Tiered subscription: a fixed monthly fee for a defined service level. The buyer absorbs consumption risk in exchange for a predictable bill, and a three-tier ladder (Free, Premium, Family) captures most of the willingness-to-pay variance.
    3. Outcome-based pricing: a charge per measurable outcome. HubSpot Breeze charges $0.50 per resolved support ticket, so the buyer pays nothing when the AI fails. This is the 2026 default for AI-agent products, because an invoice tied to completed work fits a tool whose value is the work done, not the cycles consumed.
    4. Gainsharing: a fraction of the measured benefit, whether cost savings against a baseline or revenue lift against a control. In the 2019 JPMorgan-Persado deal, Persado supplied AI-generated marketing copy and was paid a percentage of the documented lift over JPMorgan's control, which moves almost all the risk onto the supplying firm.

    Feasibility turns on three gates. Measurement asks whether both sides agree on how the unit, outcome, or gain is counted. Alignment asks whether the firm's internal incentives track the external contract. Control asks whether the buyer can actually influence the outcome the contract pays on. Michelin's 2000s Fleet Solutions per-kilometer program failed the alignment gate, because the sales force was still paid on tire units, and the program collapsed back to per-tire pricing within three years.

    07 // pricing fairness

    When The Algorithm Is Right And The Customer Is Angry

    Buyers reject economically efficient transactions they read as unfair, and they will pay a personal cost to do it. That single finding is the fairness ceiling, and it binds tighter on a 2026 pricing rollout than anything the algorithm can compute.

    The literature names the mechanism the dual-entitlement principle: buyers grant the firm a reference profit and the right to defend it against rising costs, but not extraordinary profit captured from a demand shift. A 2025 JCR paper builds on this with a moral-harm model that decomposes the ceiling into three dimensions: consumer vulnerability, the product's welfare impact, and how the firm's strategy is framed. Paying more than a vulnerable group can read as fairer than equal pricing when the differential mitigates an inferred harm.

    A 2024 JACR study changes how a firm should roll out personalized prices. Four experiments (n = 276 to 469 each) showed the same demographic price gap under two framings, a human pricing manager and an algorithm, holding the realized prices, the demographic split, and the product constant. Buyers rated the algorithm-set version as more fair than the human-set version at identical dollar gaps. The path runs through agency and intent attribution in series: a human manager had the freedom to choose otherwise, which invites the buyer to infer an unfavorable intent (the manager wanted to charge that segment more), while an algorithm reads as low-agency and blocks the inference.     Designing for the fairness ceiling

    Buyers tolerate an increase they can attribute to the firm's costs, which is why Netflix price-hike emails cite content investment. A discount during low demand reads better than a surge during high demand even when the arithmetic is identical. Buyers accept price differences across segments more readily than differences across time for the same buyer, and they reject any price that exploits an emergency. When the cost-inflation story is genuine but the posted increase is itself the brand risk, the firm reaches for less-visible routes that hold the shelf price intact.

    The covert routes work because price-recall is bound to the category. The GAO's September 2025 baseline (GAO-25-107451) examined 2021-to-2023 purchase data and found that fewer than 5 percent of items downsized in any category, yet the downsized items captured a disproportionate share of dollar sales: 38.6 percent of paper-towel revenue concentrated in shrunk SKUs, with per-unit price increases of 12 percent on paper towels and 32 percent on coffee. High-recall categories such as gasoline, milk, eggs, and bread detect a shrinkflation move the same week, while low-recall categories such as paper towels, candy, and coffee carry the covert benefit far longer. Frito-Lay's 2021 Doritos rollout is the standard medium-recall case: the team cut the share-size bag from 9.75 to 9.25 ounces, a 5.1 percent per-unit increase with the shelf tag unchanged. Early scanner data showed a small share loss, mostly to private label, that recovered over 6 to 9 months as the attentive cohort re-anchored or churned out while the inattentive cohort never registered the change, so Frito-Lay captured most of the per-unit margin a posted increase would have produced with a fraction of the share loss.

    Brand Applications: Costco, Patagonia, Liquid Death

    The four approaches, the willingness-to-pay methods, the psychological levers, the fairness ceiling, and the covert routes are the chapter's machinery. Three brands now show what happens when a firm picks one approach as a structural commitment and runs the rest of the business off it.

    Costco: low margin, high volume, the membership carries the profit

    Costco is the standard case of low-margin, high-volume pricing, capping its markup at 14 percent per SKU against a typical 11 percent. The membership fee ($65 Gold Star, $130 Executive) is where the profit sits, with roughly 70 percent of net income coming from membership, so every shelf decision (rotisserie chicken at $4.99 since 2009, the hot-dog combo at $1.50 since 1985) is an investment in renewal rather than a margin line. The structural commitment is everyday low pricing at the SKU level, made viable by the membership fee that absorbs the 18 percent margin penalty no unbacked everyday-low-price retailer survives. The consumer-psychology fit follows: the Costco member is taste-focused and prefers the low-variance realized prices the markup cap delivers. Trader Joe's runs the parallel play with a roughly 4,000-SKU curated assortment in place of the membership fee, and Walmart's offset is scale plus its Walmart Connect retail-media revenue.

    Key Takeaways

    • A 1 percent price move beats a 1 percent volume move by an order of magnitude. Extra price needs no extra cost, while every extra unit drags its share of cost, so at a 3 percent net margin a 1 percent price move lifts profit about 33 percent and matching it through volume takes about 33 percent more units.
    • Cost is the floor, the competitor is the frame, and value is the ceiling. Value-based pricing, built through the Caterpillar economic-value walk, is the only one of the four approaches tied to the buyer's wallet, not to the firm's costs or a rival's sticker.
    • Willingness to pay varies across buyers, and four procedures recover it. Direct asking, the Van Westendorp price-sensitivity meter, conjoint analysis, and scanner-data each trade cost against realism, and elasticity says how much pricing room a category has.
    • Buyers read prices in System-1 mode against a reference point. Anchoring, the decoy effect, and charm endings move demand more than the math alone suggests, which is why a dominated third option can shift choice toward the option beside it.

The dealer opens with the $90,000 competitor-equivalent base, then attaches a verifiable dollar premium to each superior attribute (durability, reliability, service, warranty) that sums to a $110,000 economic value to the customer, then closes at a $100,000 discount both sides read as a win.

    4. A brand reads weak quarterly volume and cuts price, but margin erodes without a volume lift. What did it get wrong, and what is the matched-market test?

    It assumed it was on the elastic side of its demand curve when its buyers actually care more about reliability or social proof than sticker price. The test lifts price 5 percent on one SKU in two look-alike markets, holds for eight weeks, and watches volume. A flat response means the firm was on the inelastic side all along and another cut was never the cure.

    5. The Economist subscription page added a $125 print-only option nobody picked. Why is that not a mistake, and how does it differ from the compromise effect?

    It is a decoy. The print-only option is dominated by the identical-priced print-plus-web bundle, so it shifts choice toward the dominator, with about 84 percent choosing print-plus-web when the decoy is present versus about 68 percent choosing web-only without it. The compromise effect needs no dominated option, because a middle option gains share simply by being the defensible middle of a tradeoff, which is how Spotify Free, Premium, and Family works.

    6. Yield management and surge pricing use the same logic, but Wendy's reversed in twelve days while the Indianapolis Zoo's rollout succeeded. What separates a tolerated dynamic price from a rejected one?

    The better-than-static and transparency principles. The Zoo's off-peak Tuesday floor of $8 was genuinely lower than the old single price and the whole calendar was published in advance, so budget-constrained families paid less. Wendy's announced only a peak-hour add with no off-peak discount and framed it as a margin tool on a captive lunch crowd. Same mechanism, opposite fairness ceiling.

    7. A 2024 study showed buyers rated an algorithm-set demographic price gap as more fair than the identical human-set gap. Why, and what is the operational rule?

    The unfairness judgment follows from inferred intent, not the size of the dollar gap. A human manager could have chosen otherwise, which invites the inference that they wanted to charge that segment more, while an algorithm has low agency and blocks that inference. The rule is to put profit-driven personalized pricing behind a published-rule algorithm, but keep a human face on prosocial pricing such as senior, hardship, and student rates, where the contrast reverses.

    Job Aid. The one-page pricing-decision worksheet. This worksheet is the chapter's pricing sequence pulled out of the spine so you can run it on any product. Work top to bottom. Each block is a move the chapter taught.

    1. Build the value with the EVC walk.

    Find the next-best alternative the buyer would otherwise choose and write its price as the base. Then list each attribute where your product is better and attach a dollar premium the buyer can verify against their own data, the way Caterpillar adds durability, reliability, service, and warranty onto a $90,000 base to reach a $110,000 value. Close below the full value, because the discount lets both sides feel they captured something.

    2. Run the four-question diagnostic before matching a rival's move.

    When a competitor changes price, answer four questions in the first day or two: why did they move (a real cost change holds, a quarter-end push reverses), is it permanent or temporary, what does not responding cost in lost share, and what reaction does your response trigger. Think two moves ahead, not one, so a reflexive match does not start a price war you did not want.

    3. Recover the acceptable band with the four PSM questions.

    Ask buyers at what price the product is too expensive to consider, too cheap to trust, getting expensive but still worth it, and a bargain. Where the answers cross gives you the acceptable price band and a sensible opening price, which is the launch-stage default when you have no sales history yet.

    4. Run the six-principle audit before any dynamic-pricing rollout.

    Before you let a price move with demand, check the six principles a 2024 failure tested: is the dynamic price better than the static one for some buyers, is the rule transparent, is the off-peak discount real and not just a peak surcharge, is the rule published in advance, does it avoid singling out a vulnerable group, and does it keep a human face where the pricing is prosocial. A dynamic price that fails these reverses in days.

    5. Check the price against the fairness ceiling.

    Before you post an increase, run three checks: is there a cost story the buyer will accept, is there an off-peak or lower option so the budget-constrained buyer pays less, and was the change published before it took effect. If the increase fails these and the buyer is a vulnerable group buying a necessity, the fairness ceiling will catch up with the climb the way it caught up with Mylan.

    01 // why social media is a different channel

    Why Social Media Is A Different Channel

    Social media behaves differently from every paid or owned channel you have studied, and three properties change the math. The first is speed. The second is that the audience a post reaches is not the audience the account already had. The third is that everything you post lives in public for years. Together they explain why a campaign that would have been routine on television becomes a company emergency on TikTok.

    Start with speed. On a legacy channel a creative ramps over weeks, because a television spot buys views on a media schedule and a mailer sits in a print queue. On social media the timescale shrinks to hours. A new post that clears its first batch of viewers can travel from zero to a million views before the team that posted it has finished lunch. The campus version is the dorm-room clip with 800,000 plays the afternoon it was filmed. The brand version is a corporate post the legal team has not yet read reaching national press before the next morning meeting.

    2. TikTok's For You algorithm distributes a new post in waves of roughly 200 viewers, then 5,000, 50,000, and a million, with completion rate (the share of first-batch viewers who watch past the median dwell point) as the dominant gating signal, followed by like rate and rewatch count. The full ramp can happen in under two hours. Documentation leaked in 2022 and platform updates through 2024 point to that weighting order. The Cybertruck post cleared its first wave at about 78 percent completion; most brand posts stall near 35 percent.

    That speed only matters because of the second property. Call it asymmetric reach. On paid and owned channels the audience is bounded by what the brand paid for or who opted into its list. On social media the strongest predictor of how many people see a post is whether the algorithm decides to push it past the follower base, not how many people follow the account. A creator with 200 followers can outdraw a brand account with 10 million followers when the content is sharper, because the recommender promotes the post to viewers who never followed either account. Here is how that works in plain terms. The system pushes a new upload to a probe group of about 200 viewers chosen to match what the post looks like it is about, then watches what those viewers do. If enough of them watch to the end, it promotes the post to a larger batch, then a larger one, and so on up the ladder. If the first 200 swipe away, the post is forgotten within the hour. The decisive number is not how many people follow you, it is the share of that first batch who watch your post all the way through. A post that collects thumbs-ups but loses viewers at the three-second mark is optimizing the wrong signal, so the algorithm routes it nowhere.

    02 // what makes content travel: the six stepps levers

    What Makes Content Travel: The Six STEPPS Levers

    A decade of word-of-mouth research boils down to six properties that predict whether a piece of content travels, and they spell STEPPS: Social Currency, Triggers, Emotion, Public, Practical Value, Stories. The framework is descriptive, not a recipe. Across 6,500 brands and 800,000 word-of-mouth participants, content that hit two or three of the six letters traveled reliably, while content that hit zero or one stayed put. Think of the six as toppings you can combine, not steps you run in order. Hitting two or three at medium strength beats hitting one at full strength with the rest at zero.

    The umbrella term for all of this is word-of-mouth marketing, the work of getting customers to talk to each other about a brand, and a social platform is simply its fastest channel; placing a post with the people most likely to pass it on is called seeding. These six message-level levers are the canonical framework for engineering that word of mouth, which is why they anchor here in the social chapter; the content-marketing chapter (Chapter 17) applies the same six to a long-form piece like a pillar post rather than teaching them again.

    4. STEPPS is salad toppings, not a recipe: each letter is an independent lever, and a post can travel on any two or three. The framework comes from Jonah Berger's 2013 book Contagious, drawing on a study of 6,500 brands and roughly 800,000 word-of-mouth participants. A 2012 fMRI study adds a neural floor to the first lever: talking about yourself lights up the same reward circuitry as money, so people will take a smaller payout for the chance to share an opinion, which is why "people share what makes them look good" is a structural fact, not a slogan.

    Social Currency is the first lever, and it is the engine behind the owl. People share what makes them look good, so the brand whose post lets the sharer earn status travels. Snapple did this in 2002 with no advertising budget at all, printing a surprising fact under each bottle cap, "a ball of glass bounces higher than a ball of rubber," and turning dead packaging into a reason to talk. The lever runs three ways a brand can pull. A surprising fact worth repeating is one, a score worth bragging about like the Duolingo streak or the Strava record is the second, and an insider feel worth claiming like a secret bar or an early-access drop is the third. Duolingo's whole voice is engineered Social Currency, because posting as the brand makes a 22-year-old look funny and in-the-know to her group chat.

    Triggers are the second lever. A trigger is a cue in the buyer's day that brings a related brand to mind, and the rule is that top of mind leads to tip of tongue. Across hundreds of campaigns, products tied to a frequent everyday cue earned about 15 percent more word of mouth and held the lift for the full campaign, while merely interesting products got an early bump that faded. The canonical case is Kit Kat and coffee. With sales falling and no television budget, the brand paired the bar with coffee in radio spots timed to coffee-drinking hours.     Public is the fourth lever. Products and behaviors people can see get copied more than private ones, so the design question is whether a stranger watching over a friend's shoulder can identify the brand in three seconds. There are two ways to pull it. The first is to make a product that advertises itself, the way Apple's white earbuds did in 2003 when every rival's were black, so a glance across a subway car read as iPod ownership. The second is behavioral residue, the trace a behavior leaves once it is done. The Livestrong wristband sold 85 million units on one mechanism: it stayed visible long after the donation, advertising the affiliation for months. KFC ran the cleanest recent version.     6. Awe and anger travel about the same distance, but they cost the brand differently. An awe-coded campaign builds brand-asset value across a long arc, so the reach it earns compounds. An anger-coded one spends brand reserves to buy a short, sharp spike, so it converts trust the brand already had into reach it can use once. The choice between the two registers is a planning-stage decision about whether the team wants to build value or spend it.

    Knowing arousal beats valence still leaves the hardest question open: which specific post will travel? The honest answer is that nobody can call it in advance. The clearest recent example is Cluely, an AI startup whose two founders launched in April 2025 under a deliberate rage-bait framing built to buy attention by buying outrage. Week one drew 70,000 signups and 4 million views, both about ten times what the same spend would earn without the outrage hook. The investors moved on the same clock, with a 5.3-million-dollar seed round one day after launch, then a 15-million-dollar round from Andreessen Horowitz that June at a roughly 120-million-dollar valuation.

    What makes Cluely a teaching case is the protocol the founder later described, which the team calls the 100-to-1 content factory. The first move makes 100 short videos on one theme in a compressed window, on the working assumption that 99 will sit and 1 will travel. The second move takes the one winner and reposts it across 100 fresh, low-traction accounts, because the platform routes those reposts to audiences the original never reached, which typically squeezes 20 to 30 more viral clips out of the same creative. Virality is a volume bet, not a forecast. The team cannot predict which clip wins, so it manufactures enough attempts that the win becomes likely, then re-runs the winner to find new rooms.

    Engineered anger needs a guardrail, because the same framing that buys reach can sink the brand carrying it. A 2026 team running an anger-coded campaign checks each draft against four questions before it posts. Is the controversial claim defensible if a reporter calls? Does the provocation target a protected group like race, gender, disability, or age? If the campaign goes wrong, can the framing reverse without taking the brand down with it? And can the company's factory, service desk, and legal team absorb a ten-times spike in attention if the post takes off? A dorm-room creator scales the same checklist down to one authentic grievance and a rotation of personal accounts, running the same four checks on their own framing first.

    7. Cluely is also a warning about how fragile a rage-bait frame is. The original “cheat on everything” positioning was retracted in late 2025 once proctoring services showed they could detect the product, forcing a pivot to a meeting-assistant pitch. In March 2026 the founder admitted that the widely quoted 7-million-dollar revenue figure had been inflated, and the real number was about 5.2 million, a 35 percent gap (web-verified 2026-05-21, TechCrunch and Inc.). The 100-to-1 factory is a durable production method. The framing layered on top has to survive fact-checking by the same feed that distributes it, and Cluely's did not.

    The Stories lever from Section 19.2 raises a question every team faces when a sound, format, or hashtag is already moving this week. Should the brand copy the trend faithfully so the audience recognizes it, or change it so the brand stands out? The share-or-sit instinct says copy it, but the largest study of TikTok trend participation says the opposite. Researchers scored 57,000 trend videos for how closely each followed the trend's typical format. Videos that kept the trend's shape but added one visible twist earned more views, likes, comments, and shares than videos that copied it closely. The reward gap is small early in a trend, before viewers have seen many copies, and it grows decisively later, once the feed is full of identical entries and the typical version starts to feel stale. The study went further on which twist pays, because changing what the person on camera does with their body, hands, and timing beat changing the background, and a follow-up experiment confirmed the effect was causal. The working rule is to keep the trend's backbone (the audio, the hashtag, the format) so the algorithm files the post as a participant, then add one visible deviation, preferably in the action rather than the backdrop, and make the deviation bigger the later the brand joins.

    The most reliable travel mechanism turns the audience into the publisher. A share-card is a calendar-locked artifact a brand builds for people to post about themselves, and the cleanest case is Spotify Wrapped. Wrapped 2025 launched on December 3 and pulled more than 200 million engaged users in the first 24 hours, a 19 percent jump over the year before, when the same milestone took 62 hours, and it was shared more than 500 million times on the first day, up 41 percent. The cue is the calendar slot. Every December the audience expects the artifact, the artifact carries the brand identity, and the audience does the publishing. A campus org can run the same play with a year-in-review card pinned to the same December cue, with the org's logo and one number built into the image so a friend reposting the screenshot still carries the org's name.

    Underneath the idea that travels sits the sentence that carries it, which is a separate program a team can run on every caption and reply. The Magic Words research found that customer-service emails written in concrete language earned about 30 percent more customer spending over the next 90 days than the same message written abstractly. The tell in practice is plain: “your package is in our Memphis hub and ships at 6 a.m. tomorrow” travels and converts better than “we are looking into your shipping concern.” A companion Skill can audit a caption for concrete, present-tense language before it ships, which Section 19.6 takes up as one of the AI workflows a 2026 team builds.

    Earned, Paid, And Owned

    Every dollar a brand spends to reach people on social media buys one of three kinds of distribution, and the three behave so differently that mixing them up wrecks a budget. The cleanest way to see them is to ask who gives you the reach. The brand buys paid distribution, controls owned distribution, and gets earned distribution for free when other people pass the content along.

    Every dollar a brand spends on social media buys one of three kinds of distribution, and mixing them up wrecks a budget. The cleanest way to see them is to ask who gives you the reach: the brand buys paid, controls owned, and gets earned for free when other people pass the content along.

    Paid media Distribution the brand buys directly: boosted posts, sponsored Reels, search ads, or a disclosed fee paid to a creator. Reach scales with dollars, so twice the spend buys roughly twice the impressions, and the price is quoted per thousand views.
    Owned media Distribution the brand controls itself: its own TikTok account, its Instagram feed, its email list. Reach is capped by the audience the brand has already gathered, plus whatever the algorithm chooses to push, and the cost is roughly fixed.
    Earned media Distribution other people give the brand for nothing: customer posts, reshares, parodies, press, memes. One more share costs the brand effectively nothing, though only the small slice of content that clears the share-or-sit filter ever wins it.

    The unit economics are the whole reason this matters. A boosted Instagram post in 2026 costs roughly four to eight dollars per thousand views, what advertisers call CPM. A Super Bowl spot costs about seven million dollars to reach roughly 130 million viewers, a CPM near fifty-four dollars. The Duolingo Cybertruck stunt reached 1.7 billion people on a production budget under 100,000 dollars, a CPM below six cents. Earned reach is the cheapest channel by a factor of hundreds, and yet most marketing budgets still put about eighty percent on paid, fifteen on owned, and five on the work that actually wins the cheap channel. The leverage runs in the opposite direction from the money. Knowing the leverage is wrong does not yet tell you how big the paid number should be, and the cleanest way to size it is the objective-and-task method: set the goal, walk it back through the funnel to the reach the goal needs, then cost that reach, rather than spending a fixed percentage of last year's sales out of habit.

    Inside the paid budget sits the influencer line, and it runs on one old question, which is whether a brand should put its money behind one big creator or many small ones.     That unpredictability is not an accident, and a famous music experiment shows why. Researchers loaded the same forty-eight unknown songs into eight identical online worlds and let each world's listeners rate and download on their own. Each world crowned a different winner, because early listeners in each one happened to pile onto different songs and everyone after them followed the early crowd. Virality is path-dependent, which means a brand's job is the portfolio of seeded clips rather than the forecast of which clip catches.

    Paid without earned Spending heavily to push content that nobody wants to share. The brand buys the reach, and the audience does not pass it on, so the spending stops the moment the budget does.     Calm content in a high-energy channel Making something pleasant and warm when the channel rewards excitement. Calm, contented feelings sit while high-energy feelings travel, so a sweet video can earn views without earning a single share that moves a buyer. Evian's Roller Babies, a clip of computer-animated babies roller-skating, pulled about 50 million views and a 25 percent market-share decline in the same year. Cute is contentment, and contentment is low-arousal. The fix is to aim for a high-energy feeling on purpose, the awe, the amusement, or the well-aimed anger from section 19.3, not the warm glow that feels safe in the meeting.
    Pretending to be organic Running a paid post or a paid creator without saying it is paid. The FTC requires a clear #ad label on any post a brand pays for, and an undisclosed paid post counts as deceptive advertising, which the FTC can fine at more than 50,000 dollars per violation. The cost of getting caught is not only the fine. When an audience discovers that a post they trusted was paid, the trust they lose is worth more than the reach the deception bought. The fix is to disclose every paid placement, every time, and to turn on the platform's paid-partnership label so the disclosure is automatic.
    Volume without a voice Posting constantly and hoping that sheer cadence makes up for content nobody wants to share. When Bud Light faced a consumer-trust crisis in 2023, its account posted roughly four times a day for two months, and almost none of those posts addressed what people were actually upset about or gave either side something worth sharing. US sales fell quarter after quarter for more than a year, and the brand lost its long-held spot as America's best-selling beer. Cadence is downstream of quality. One post a week that hits two STEPPS letters beats fifty posts that hit none, so the fix is to slow down and make the post worth passing on.

    Job Aid. The social-media pre-flight card. This is the chapter’s checklist pulled out of the spine so you can run it on any brand, your own or a competitor’s, before you spend a dollar amplifying a post. Work top to bottom.

    1. Score the draft on STEPPS before you pay to amplify it.

    Rate the post 0 to 3 on each of the six letters: Social Currency (does sharing it make the sharer look good), Triggers (is it tied to a frequent daily cue), Emotion (is the feeling high-energy, not calm), Public (does it advertise itself or leave a visible trace), Practical Value (is it useful enough to pass along), and Stories (is the brand un-removable from the retelling). Clear two letters at 2 or higher before you put paid money behind it. A draft that fails the pre-flight does not get fixed by ad spend.

    2. Audit a creator on the floor before you sign.

    Effective reach is not follower count. It is followers times engagement rate times how well the audience matches your brand. Keep sponsored saturation under about a third of the creator’s recent posts, because past that point the audience tunes the ads out and the creator is a depreciating asset. Run a free check on a tool like HypeAuditor or Talkwalker to confirm the audience is real before money changes hands.

    3. Spread across platform tiers so no single host can sink you.

    Build a presence on at least two tiers with different audiences. Tier 1 is mass discovery (TikTok, Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts), where the algorithm pushes you past your own followers. Tier 2 is text-and-journalist (X, Threads, Bluesky, LinkedIn), for quotable lines and press pickup. Tier 3 is community (Discord, Reddit, Substack), the most engaged cohort but a bounded ceiling. Crosspost your distinctive assets across all three within hours of a moment, because every platform is rented and the host can revoke the rental.

    Run the chapter’s machinery on a real brand

    01 // what marketing actually is

    What marketing is, and why it might be your major

    1. Quick orientation. Finance is about money, accounting is about keeping the books straight, and information systems is about the software a company runs on. Marketing is about people: what they want, how they choose, and how a product earns a place in their lives. If you like figuring out people more than spreadsheets, you are already thinking like a marketer.

    You have not picked a major yet, so let me make the case for this one. Marketing is the part of business that is about people instead of paperwork. You get to study why someone buys one water over another, then build the thing that makes them choose yours. It pays well, it travels into every industry from sports to streaming, and it is the rare business job where being curious and a little creative is the actual skill. Finance and accounting reward you for being careful. Marketing rewards you for understanding what people want before they can say it out loud.

    So what is the job, really? Picture a buyer with fifteen dollars and a problem she wants gone. Now picture a seller with the exact thing that would fix it. The catch is that she has never heard of it, is not sure she can trust it, and would not know where to find it. Between the two of them there is silence. Marketing is the work of closing that gap so a trade actually happens, and then doing it again for the next buyer.

    Marketing Marketing is the work of getting the right product to the right people, in a way that makes both sides glad the trade happened. A good trade is voluntary. She hands over her money, the seller hands over the product, and each one walks away thinking they got the better end of the deal.

    Notice the word voluntary. Nobody is forced. That is why marketing is harder than it looks, because the buyer can always walk away. You cannot trick someone into being a happy customer twice. The whole job is making the offer good enough, and clear enough, that she chooses it on her own and comes back.

    It helps to be precise about what the offering is for, because value comes in four kinds and most offers lead with one. Economic value is money saved or made against the next-best option.     Who to serve

    Why one weak decision sinks the other four

    If the five decisions just added up, marketing would be easy. Four good ones and one weak one would average out to a decent plan, a B grade, good enough. That is not how it works. The five decisions multiply, they do not add, and the diagram below shows why: cross out one node and the whole system collapses, not just one fifth of it.

    Think about what a broken decision actually does. Say a company nails the product, the buyer, the price, and the store, but the ad points at the wrong people. Those four good decisions still cost real money, and all that money now works to march the wrong buyer up to a product that was never for her. The four good choices do not soften the bad one. They pay to deliver the buyer straight to the place the fifth decision broke. That is what "multiplicative" means in plain terms: a zero anywhere drags the whole thing toward zero.

    There is a second surprise hiding in the data, and it overturns something most people assume. We tend to think a brand wins because buyers see it as special and different. The numbers say otherwise. When researchers ask buyers across many product types, only about one in ten think their favorite brand is genuinely different from the rest. The other nine buy it because it was familiar, in stock, and the first thing they thought of. So a brand is not really competing to be seen as unique. It is competing to be the one that pops into your head when the need shows up, and to be easy to find once it does. The Stanley cup is the recent proof: the same wide steel cup that a construction worker carried for a century became a hit the moment the company let it reach a buyer it had ignored, without changing the cup at all.

    What happens when one decision goes wrong

    Those three numbers only work if the five decisions agree with each other.     Here are the facts, recent enough to check. In April 2023, Bud Light ran a one-off promotion with a transgender social-media personality named Dylan Mulvaney.     Key Takeaways

    • An exchange needs five conditions at once. Two parties, each holding something the other wants, able to reach each other, free to walk, and each believing the deal is worth it, so marketing is the work of arranging the world until all five hold for as many buyers as possible.
    • A firm decides exactly five things. What to make, who to serve, what to charge, where to sell, and how to talk about it, and the targeting decision lines the other four up behind it.
    • The five decisions multiply, not add. Four good choices and one broken one do not average into a passing plan, because the four good ones pay to deliver the buyer straight to the friction the fifth one created.
    • The frameworks are one system seen from different seats. The four Ps grew into seven because Product hid three decisions and Promotion hid two, while the 4Cs read the same five from the buyer's side of the counter.
    • How a firm thinks shapes what it decides. The century moved through production, product, selling, marketing, and holistic orientations, and marketing myopia is the trap of naming yourself by the product when the buyer hired it for a job.
    • Marketing has to pay on repeat.

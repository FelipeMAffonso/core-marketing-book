    A marketing plan is a closed loop. Budgets and calendars don’t close it.

    A marketing director at Vodafone Italia sat down in the early 2010s to size next year’s retention budget. The number at the top of her spreadsheet was the firm’s aggregate annual churn rate, 17.7 percent, which says roughly eighteen of every hundred customers leave by year-end. Invert that rate the way every analytics class does on day one, and the average customer lasts 5.6 years. That single number set the ceiling on how much marketing could spend to win or keep one customer.

A middle fifth leaves at thirty-five percent, an average of about three years. A high-risk tenth leaves at sixty-five percent, a lifetime near eighteen months. The high-risk tenth churns out fast, and once they are gone the surviving base is dominated by the long-stay seventy percent. The aggregate rate flattens that survival dynamic.     01 // why a plan is a closed loop

    Why a plan is a closed loop

    In any working plan all five elements are present, and each one carries a different question. The goal names the number the plan is sized against. The strategy names which customers the firm will serve and how it wants to be remembered. The tactics name the activities and channels the budget buys. The instrumentation names the measurements that show whether the tactics moved the strategy. The control names the cadence at which the plan checks itself and revises while there is still time. The absence of any one element breaks the rest, and the breakage shows up as one of two recognizable failure modes.

    The first is the budget-plus-calendar collapse. The plan exists as a spreadsheet of dollars and a chart of dates, but the goal layer is unstated, the instrumentation belongs to some other team, and the control layer is “we will see in November.” A plan in that state is a calendar of campaigns, not a plan. The second is the heuristic-bound budget. The firm sizes its spend not by what each channel returns on the next dollar but by a rule of thumb, such as spending five percent of last year’s sales (the percentage-of-sales rule) or matching its largest competitor (the competitive-parity rule). Both rules are easy to defend in a meeting because they sound rigorous, yet neither asks where the firm’s actual elasticities live, so both are causally wrong. A 2014 survey of 1,000 advertising-and-promotion managers found 72 percent allocate by heuristics rather than by attribution, and that share has barely moved since. The persistence of that number is the empirical reason this chapter exists.

    GSTIC (the five plan elements) Five elements every working plan must address: Goal (measurable units), Strategy (customers and positioning), Tactics (activities and channels), Instrumentation (measurements), Control (revising inside the horizon). The Kotler/Chernev textbook labels the fifth Implementation and folds measurement into Control; this chapter splits them so the dashboard layer gets its own treatment in Section 24.5.

    The five questions are a diagnostic, and they are also a writing template. A plan a student can hand in is one page with seven rows, and the rows run in the order the loop runs. The first row is the brand identity, one or two sentences on who the brand is and what it stands for. The second is the target market and situation, naming the customers the plan serves and the one or two facts about the market and the competition that the plan must answer to. The third is the product-market objective, and this is the row the rest of the page is sized against, so it carries one measurable number and one date (“reach 145 million active cardholders by the end of Q4,” not “grow the membership base”). The fourth is the positioning statement, the single sentence that says, for this customer, the brand is the one that delivers this benefit because of this reason, and it is the hinge that ties the strategy rows above it to the tactic rows below it. The fifth row is the marketing mix, the four to seven Ps the budget actually buys. The sixth is the customer-response assessment plan, naming the goal-layer metric the plan will read and the cadence it reads it on. The seventh is the budget, the dollar figure each tactic row gets.

    The closed loop only runs on a substrate the firm has built, and that substrate has measurable maturity. Some firms run on patchy data that lives in spreadsheets on a manager’s laptop.     02 // goal: customer asset value

    Goal: customer asset value, not revenue

    The closed loop opens at the goal layer, and the question for a 2026 plan is what number the goal should be denominated in. Revenue and unit volume are the obvious candidates, and most plans default to one of them, yet both can rise while the customer base quietly loses value. A brand that sells more units this year because it discounted hard to acquire low-retention buyers is growing revenue and shrinking the value of its base at the same time. A plan whose goal is the revenue line cannot see that trade-off, while a plan whose goal is a dollar number at the customer level can. That is why the modern goal-layer number is customer lifetime value summed across the base, not revenue.

    Customer lifetime value (CLV) CLV is the present value of future net cash flows from a customer: forward-looking, predictive not precise, counting only directly applicable acquisition costs. Method differs by setting: retention/churn arithmetic in contractual relationships, latent-attrition models on RFM patterns in noncontractual ones.

    The plain reading is the one to hold: CLV is one customer’s yearly profit, projected forward over the relationship, minus what the firm paid to win them. The simplified contractual formula puts that in one line, and the line is best read as a way to rank deciles of the base against each other, not as a forecast good to the second decimal. The cable firm in the worked example below does not know next year’s retention rate; it knows last year’s and a forecast. Small changes in retention swing the result by large amounts, because retention sits in a denominator that is the difference between two close numbers. So a $235 CLV figure reads best as the upper bound on what the firm should spend on a similar customer in a given decile. A plan that uses CLV to compare top-decile against bottom-decile customers is using it correctly; a plan that uses it to forecast next quarter to two decimals is misreading what it can do.

    3. How to read a CLV number. The simplified contractual formula is CLV = (M − C) / (1 + d − r) − A. M is the annual margin one customer produces (revenue minus the cost of serving them); C is the annual cost of keeping them (retention emails, loyalty offers, service contact); the numerator (M − C) is one year’s worth after the marketing to hold them. The denominator (1 + d − r) turns that into a perpetuity-style total, where r is the annual retention rate and d is the discount rate. The final − A subtracts the acquisition cost paid once. A cable customer with M = $100, C = $10, r = 0.65, d = 0.05, and A = $65 returns about $235 in present-value terms. The full formula lives in the Job Aid; the spine keeps the plain “one customer’s yearly profit, projected forward, minus what you paid to win them.”

    The dollar number at the top of the plan is the sum of CLVs across the base, net of fixed costs. The literature spent two decades calling this customer equity, then retracted the label in 2020, because summing CLVs without netting fixed costs is neither equity in any finance sense nor a useful planning input. The replacement is customer asset value: the same sum, net of fixed costs.

    03 // strategy: AER

    Strategy: Win Them, Grow Them, Keep Them

    Once the goal layer is named in customer-asset-value terms, the next layer of the closed loop is strategy, which decides who the firm serves, with what position, and how the budget splits across winning, growing, and keeping customers. That split resolves into three named stages, and every customer of the firm sits in one of them at any moment. Acquisition runs from first contact through first purchase, where the firm pays once to turn a stranger into a customer. Expansion grows an existing customer through upsell, cross-sell, and deeper engagement. Retention defends the relationship against attrition. The three together shorten to AER, and the strategy line names the budget posture across all three at once.

    AER (acquisition, expansion, retention) Three customer stages every plan’s strategy line allocates across. Acquisition: first contact through first purchase, paid once per customer. Expansion: existing customers via upsell, cross-sell, deeper engagement, where most untapped revenue lives in mature businesses. Retention: customers at risk or lost, recurring every period a customer remains. Chasing acquisition alone burns spend on customers the firm cannot keep; chasing retention alone shrinks the base.

    Three operating constraints shape how the AER budget actually gets allocated, and each one separates a defensible plan from an after-the-fact rationalization. The first is diminishing marginal returns, because the easiest customers in each stage get picked off first, so a plan that sizes spend without modelling that decay assumes the hundredth retention dollar returns what the first one did. The second constraint is the uneven cost of underspending against overspending, because skipping a dollar that would have returned $1.40 hurts more than spending a dollar that returns only $0.80, so the practical rule is to err high when the elasticity estimates are uncertain. The third constraint is the long-run gap between the two ends of the ladder, where poor retention compounds because retention recurs every period a customer stays, so one weak retention year shrinks the base every year that follows, whereas poor acquisition is paid once and stays paid.

    The AER weights only become coherent once the firm has named which structural position the allocation is defending. Three positions have been measured as durable across industries, and a firm has to pick one rather than occupy all three. Cost leadership builds the lowest cost structure at parity quality and passes some of that advantage through to price. Differentiation earns a price premium for something the industry sees as unique. Focus narrows the firm to one segment or product line and pursues cost leadership or differentiation inside that narrow scope. A strategy line that reads “grow share” or “improve customer experience” is not yet a strategy, because it commits to a goal without naming the position the firm’s activity-system defends, which is the difference between strategic positioning and merely doing what rivals do a little better.

    Each position implies a different AER curve, so the same acronym carries three budget postures. A cost leader runs retention-heavy, because a high-volume base is expensive to replace at a thin per-customer margin, so the next dollar usually returns more in retention than in acquisition. A differentiator runs acquisition-heavier in the early years, when the premium funds a higher acquisition cost, then retention-heavy as the brand matures. A focused-differentiation brand runs community-driven retention with values-aligned acquisition, turning down customers whose presence would weaken the values that justify the premium. A plan whose strategy line is anchored in none of the three positions sits in the mid-tier squeeze by default, with Aldi and Kirkland pressing from below and Liquid Death and Patagonia pressing from above.

    The position sets the AER posture, but a second question still has to be answered before the strategy is operational, and that is where the firm should expect its growth to come from. The marketing-science literature gives an answer that looks contradictory until the two halves are read separately. One body of evidence says growth comes from acquiring new buyers, almost regardless of category. A second body of evidence says profit inside the existing base comes from concentrating on the high-value customers rather than spreading attention thin. Both findings are real, and they appear to clash only because they answer different questions.

    The first half is the regularity that brands grow primarily by acquiring more buyers, not by getting current buyers to buy more often.

    04 // tactics: media mix

    Tactics: Where The Next Dollar Goes

    Tactics are where the plan turns into spend, and the question is no longer which channels exist but how large each one should be. The 4Ps name those decisions, and the channel and promotional choices from earlier chapters get sized against the firm’s own data rather than a rule of thumb. Two anchor numbers govern most of the tactical math, and both come from meta-analyses that pooled hundreds of studies. An advertising elasticity is the percent change in sales from a one-percent change in ad spend, and the pooled short-term average is about 0.12, so doubling ad spend buys roughly 12 percent more sales that quarter. A sales-force elasticity is the same idea for sales-force investment, and the pooled average is about 0.31, roughly two and a half times the advertising number.

    The reason the two elasticities differ by that much is structural, and the structure tells the planner which channel to reach for in which situation. Picture the buyer’s path to a purchase as a ladder, climbing from no idea the brand exists up through awareness, knowledge, liking, preference, and conviction, and finally onto the rung where the buyer actually buys. The hierarchy of effects is the planning vocabulary for that ladder, and its rungs group into a cognitive stage, an affective stage, and a conative stage.

    Linear television and sponsorship work mostly on the cognitive rungs, putting the brand into the buyer’s head, so their short-term elasticity is small because an awareness impression does not have to convert this quarter to have done its job. Search advertising and sales-force activity work mostly on the conative rungs, arriving when the buyer is already preference-formed and ready to decide, so their elasticity is high on a single-quarter horizon because the spend reaches the buyer near the moment of action. That is why 0.31 dwarfs 0.12 on a quarterly window, and it is also why a mix sized only by short-term lift would starve the cognitive rungs that feed every later conversion. The planner’s question is no longer whether to size tactics against elasticity but how to do it on the firm’s own data, and a three-step preference order answers it. First preference is response-model attribution, a regression on the firm’s own history that returns one elasticity per channel. Second preference, when the data are too thin for a regression to separate the channels, is an experimental attribution design, a deliberate holdout in some markets or weeks. Heuristic methods like the percentage-of-sales rule come last, used only when both regression and experiment fail.

    Key Takeaways

    • A marketing plan is a closed loop of goal, strategy, tactics, instrumentation, and control. Most plans fail because they collapse the loop into a budget plus a calendar, funding tactics before the goal number and the measurement plan are named, so nothing revises the plan inside the planning horizon.
    • Denominate the goal in customer-asset value, not revenue. Customer-asset value is the sum of customer lifetime value across the base net of fixed costs, and it sees a trade-off the revenue line cannot, because a brand can grow revenue while its base loses value by discounting hard to acquire low-retention buyers.
    • Split the budget across acquisition, expansion, and retention. Growth comes mostly from acquiring more buyers, as the double-jeopardy law shows, while profit in the existing base concentrates in the high-value deciles, so a plan grows by penetration and defends profit by concentration at the same time.
    • Size tactics by what each channel actually returns, not by a rule of thumb. Advertising elasticity pools around 0.12 and sales-force elasticity around 0.31, and marketing-mix modeling reads those returns from weekly data, so a budget set by percentage-of-sales leaves the return-on-each-dollar question unanswered.
    • The customer-base audit is not the dashboard. The audit reads the customer and the cohort quarterly for the CEO and CFO and finds where the profit lives, while the dashboard reads channels weekly for the marketing manager, and a firm that runs only the dashboard counts a one-time shopper identically to a sixth-year repeat buyer.
    • Pick one attribution method as the budget spine and cross-check it. Marketing-mix modeling is the 2026 spine and incrementality tests are the causal cross-check, because the same customer journey credits a different channel under each attribution rule, so the budget cut swings with the rule you pick.
    • An AI can run the analyst’s weekly recipe once it is written down. The audit, the attribution check, and the retention fit become a Skill an agent runs on demand, with the human in the loop on the decisions, and a sharp question with the real data beats a vague one at the same cost.

Acquisition is sized by penetration logic. Separately, profit in the existing base concentrates in the high-value deciles, so retention and expansion are sized by concentration logic. The two postures run at once.

    5. Advertising elasticity pools around 0.12 and sales-force elasticity around 0.31. What does the gap mean for a planner cutting sales-force to fund an advertising push?

    The planner is trading a 0.31-elasticity dollar for a 0.12-elasticity one, so each shifted dollar returns less than half of what it returned in its previous home. The gap is structural, because a salesperson reaches the buyer near the moment of action while an awareness impression does not have to convert this quarter. A mix sized only by short-term lift starves the cognitive work that feeds every later conversion.

    6. A board is shown “$583M revenue, $280M profit, 3.2M active customers” and infers a healthy base. What does a customer-base audit add, and why does the dashboard miss it?

    The audit reports that 63 percent of customers made exactly one transaction at a $113 median, that the top 10 percent of customers produce 40 percent of profit while the bottom 10 percent produce 1 percent, and that the C3 chart shows how much of next year’s revenue depends on customers not yet acquired. The dashboard counts a one-time shopper identically to a sixth-year repeat buyer, because the customer is not the unit of analysis the dashboard reports on.

    7. A team sizes its budget by last-click attribution. What is the bias, and what should the plan run instead?

    Last-click credits whichever paid touch happened to be last (usually search or retargeting), so it systematically over-credits the bottom-funnel channels that close customers the top-funnel channels (television, sponsorship, organic social) actually produced. A plan that reallocates by last-click starves the demand-generation work that fed the funnel. The fix runs marketing-mix modeling as the budget spine, incrementality tests as the causal cross-check, and last-click only as a flag for when the methods disagree.

    8. A marketer asks an AI “how is our marketing doing?” and gets generic advice. What should they ask instead, and how does an AI fit the analyst’s weekly work?

    A sharp, situational question (“read this customer file as a customer-base audit: which deciles make the profit, what does the retention curve’s shape imply for CLV, and what is the one budget move?”) gets an anchored, buildable read, especially when the model searches the live web for the named firm’s current profile. The output quality is downstream of the question quality. In practice the senior analyst’s weekly recipe is written down once as a Skill the agent runs against the warehouse, with the human in the loop on the decisions and any irreversible action.

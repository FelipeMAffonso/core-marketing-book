The Canon Behind Each Chapter

Felipe M. Affonso · Oklahoma State University, Spears School of Business

Every chapter inherits a literature. This appendix names it.

A textbook chapter is the visible tip of a deeper reading list. Each chapter in this book draws on two to four canonical authors who first stated the principle in print, plus a handful of contemporary frontier sources that updated it for the 2026 marketing environment. The chapter prose cannot afford to walk through every original text. This appendix can.

Each entry below names an author and a primary text, gives a one-paragraph TL;DR, and links to the deeper reader file in the wiki where the full evidence chain, definitions, and AI-workflow notes live. The link target is the markdown deep reader (60-200KB each); the chapter's inline citations point you here, and this appendix points you to the full source synthesis. Read in this order: chapter prose, this appendix entry for the relevant reader, the deep reader file when you need to teach or extend the principle yourself.

How to use this appendix

The chapters cite canonical authors with numbered superscripts. When a chapter says Sharp's Ehrenberg-Bass team audited 15 categories2, the superscript points to the chapter's references list. To go further, the references list points here, and this appendix points to the full markdown reader at core-course/wiki/external/canonical/{slug}.md. The depth lives one tab away.

A.1 Chapter 1 — What Marketing Is

Chapter 1 builds the working definition of marketing as a coordinated set of decisions about what to make, who to serve, what to charge, where to make it available, and how to communicate. Its canonical foundation is the Levitt-Drucker doctrine that the firm exists to create a customer.

  • CH 1 ANCHOR Theodore Levitt & Peter F. Drucker "Marketing Myopia" (HBR 1960) & The Practice of Management (1954) Levitt's 1960 essay reframed industry definition as a customer-need question rather than a product question; railroads failed because they thought they were in railroads when they were in transportation. Drucker's 1954 doctrine that the purpose of the firm is to create a customer is the structural complement: marketing is not a department, it is the orientation of the entire enterprise. Together they anchor the chapter's claim that the marketing question precedes the product question. read the deep reader →
02 // chapter 02 — marketing environment

A.2 Chapter 2 — The Marketing Environment

Chapter 2 builds the structural read on the forces a marketer reacts to. Porter's Five Forces is the load-bearing framework; the chapter teaches industry profitability as the structural ceiling that no campaign can lift.

  • CH 2 ANCHOR Michael E. Porter "How Competitive Forces Shape Strategy" (HBR 1979) & "The Five Competitive Forces That Shape Strategy" (HBR 2008) Porter's 1979 framework decomposes industry attractiveness into five compressive pressures on margin: rivalry among existing competitors, threat of new entrants, threat of substitutes, supplier power, and buyer power. The 2008 retrospective added complements as a quasi-sixth force and explicitly distinguished industry profitability (the structural ceiling) from positioning within an industry (the operational lever). The framework anchors the chapter's claim that marketing campaigns cannot fix structural problems; the five forces define the ceiling. read the deep reader →
03 // chapter 03 — how buyers buy

A.3 Chapter 3 — How Buyers Actually Buy

Chapter 3 builds the picture of buyers as pattern-matchers who deliberate rarely and remember loosely. Two canonical readers: Kahneman-Tversky on the dual-process architecture of decision, and Keinan on the consumption-experience research that grounds memory's role.

  • CH 3 ANCHOR Daniel Kahneman & Amos Tversky "Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision under Risk" (Econometrica 1979); Thinking, Fast and Slow (FSG 2011) Kahneman and Tversky's 1979 prospect theory replaced the rational-utility model of consumer choice with an empirically calibrated picture of buyers as loss-averse, reference-dependent, and probability-distorting. The 2011 synthesis named the dual-process architecture (System 1 fast and intuitive, System 2 slow and deliberate) that explains why most marketing decisions are made by buyers operating in System 1. The chapter's claim that memory wins, attention is leased, and deliberation is rare, is built directly on this canon. read the deep reader →
  • CH 3 EXTENSION Anat Keinan Selected work on consumption experiences and memory (HBS faculty research) Keinan's research program operationalizes how buyers store and retrieve consumption experiences in memory, with implications for how brands build memory structures. The reader covers experiential consumption, productivity orientation, and the role of memorable peaks in buyer recall. Pairs with Kahneman-Tversky to give the chapter a complete dual-track picture: the cognitive architecture (Kahneman) and the memory-formation mechanics (Keinan). read the deep reader →

A.4 Chapter 4 — Marketing Research

Chapter 4 is the model chapter. Its canonical foundation is Hubbard's measurement framework (the EVPI calculation that anchors the 10 percent rule) plus Cochran's sampling foundations and Fisher's experimental design, with three v3 source contribution files that synthesize the books.

  • CH 4 ANCHOR Naresh K. Malhotra; Burns, Veeck, Bush; Cochran; Fisher; Hubbard Methods canon for marketing research (multiple texts, 1925-2020) The chapter draws on Malhotra (definition + sampling), Burns-Veeck-Bush (three-mode taxonomy of qualitative, quantitative, observational research), Cochran 1977 (sample-size foundations), R.A. Fisher 1925 (randomized controlled trial as the foundation of A/B testing), and Hubbard (measurement, calibration, EVPI). Together they define the methods canon a marketing researcher must speak fluently. The chapter's references section enumerates every individual citation; this entry is the synthesis. LLM coding deep reader →
  • CH 4 FRONTIER Argyle, Boelaert, Brand-Israeli-Ngwe, Toubia, Gui Synthetic respondents and machine bias literature (2023-2025) The 2023-2025 literature on whether language models can substitute for survey respondents. Argyle's 2023 silicon-sample paper showed algorithmic fidelity for demographically conditioned LLM responses. Boelaert et al. 2025 named the load-bearing risk: machine bias, where each model has a strong preference per item that varies unpredictably with topic and is not explained by the demographic prompt. The chapter's verdict is narrow and stable: synthetic respondents are useful for instrument pre-testing, hypothesis generation, and exploration; not safe substitutes on decision-grade studies. deep reader →
05 // chapter 06 — brands

A.5 Chapter 6 — Brands and Brand Management

Chapter 6 has the deepest canonical stack in the book. Six readers anchor it: Aaker (brand equity), Keller (CBBE pyramid), Sharp/Ehrenberg-Bass (empirical laws), Kapferer (brand identity prism), Neumeier (brand gap), Fournier-Holt (relationships and meaning).

  • CH 6 ANCHOR David A. Aaker Managing Brand Equity (1991), Building Strong Brands (1996), Brand Leadership (2000), Brand Portfolio Strategy (2004), Brand Relevance (2010) Aaker's contribution is to treat the brand as a balance-sheet-grade asset with named components a CFO can argue about. Where Sharp's contribution is empirical, Aaker's is structural: brand equity decomposes into awareness, perceived quality, brand associations, and loyalty. The Brand Identity Model (1996) and the Brand Relationship Spectrum (Branded House to House of Brands, 2000) are the practitioner-strategy half of contemporary branding theory. deep reader (Brands) →
  • CH 6 ANCHOR Kevin Lane Keller Customer-Based Brand Equity (CBBE) Pyramid; Strategic Brand Management (5th ed., 2019) Keller's CBBE pyramid is the academic-research-tradition counterpart to Aaker's practitioner framework. Brand resonance is built bottom-up through four layers: salience (am I aware?), performance and imagery (what is it like?), judgments and feelings (what do I think and feel?), and resonance (what about you and me?). The pyramid is the load-bearing diagnostic for whether a brand is at the salience step or the resonance step, which dictates which marketing investments will move the needle next. deep reader →
  • CH 6 ANCHOR Byron Sharp & the Ehrenberg-Bass Institute How Brands Grow (Vol. 1, 2010); How Brands Grow Part 2 (with Romaniuk, 2015) Sharp's empirical-laws tradition: brands grow primarily by increasing penetration (number of buyers), not loyalty among existing buyers; the double-jeopardy law says small brands have fewer buyers AND lower loyalty as a structural consequence; duplication of purchase law says brands share customers with rivals in proportion to rivals' market share, not by segment. The chapter teaches these laws as the empirical floor that any branding strategy must respect or contradict deliberately. Sharp / Aaker overview reader →
  • CH 6 SUPPORTING Jean-Noël Kapferer The New Strategic Brand Management (5th ed., 2012); Brand Identity Prism Kapferer's Brand Identity Prism (six facets: physique, personality, culture, relationship, reflection, self-image) is the European-tradition counterpart to Aaker's brand identity model. The prism is more useful than Aaker's diamond when the brand has heavy cultural meaning (luxury brands, heritage brands, brands tied to national identity). Liquid Death's brand identity is more legible through the prism's culture and relationship facets than through Aaker's awareness-quality-associations stack. deep reader →
  • CH 6 SUPPORTING Marty Neumeier The Brand Gap (2003); Zag (2006) Neumeier defines the brand as a gut feeling, not a logo or a product, and argues the brand gap is the chasm between business strategy and customer experience. The five disciplines (differentiate, collaborate, innovate, validate, cultivate) are the operating manual for closing the gap. Trader Joe's narrow assortment as a positioning move is a Neumeier-style brand decision: the curated 4,000-SKU catalog is the brand strategy, not a procurement constraint. deep reader →
  • CH 6 EXTENSION Susan Fournier & Douglas B. Holt Selected work on brand relationships and cultural meaning (HBR / JCR) Fournier's 1998 paper "Consumers and Their Brands" reframed brand-buyer relationships as actual relationships with named types (best friends, casual acquaintances, abusive partnerships). Holt's cultural-branding framework (How Brands Become Icons, 2004) shows that iconic brands carry cultural mythology, not just functional benefit. Together they explain why Liquid Death and Patagonia resonate beyond their utility: they carry cultural meaning the buyer wants to be associated with. deep reader →
  • CH 6 FRONTIER Jenni Romaniuk Better Brand Health (2023); building on How Brands Grow Romaniuk's 2023 book updates the Ehrenberg-Bass empirical-laws tradition with a measurement framework for brand health. Mental availability is operationalized as Category Entry Points (CEPs): the situations, motives, and contexts that prompt category buying. A brand is mentally available to the extent it is linked to a wide range of CEPs in buyers' memory. The CEP audit is the chapter's working tool for diagnosing where a brand's mental availability is thin. deep reader →
06 // chapter 07 — product management

A.6 Chapter 7 — Product Management

Chapter 7 has three canonical readers: Cooper on the stage-gate process discipline, Christensen on the jobs-to-be-done research framing that fuels the front of the funnel, and Ulrich-Eppinger on the engineering and design methods that fill the inside of each Cooper stage. The principle: the offer is the proof of the positioning, and the three frameworks compose into the canonical product-development methodology.

  • CH 7 ANCHOR Robert G. Cooper Winning at New Products (5th ed., 2017); Stage-Gate process (1986 onward) Cooper's Stage-Gate process is the dominant new-product-development discipline in consumer goods, B2B, and increasingly software: idea screening, business case, development, testing, validation, launch. Each stage produces deliverables; each gate is a go/kill/recycle decision made by a senior cross-functional team. The chapter teaches Stage-Gate as the practical answer to product portfolio management, with the BREXI scoring rubric (Believable, Relevant, Engaging, X-factor, Inspiring) as the gate-3 concept evaluation. deep reader →
  • CH 7 ANCHOR Clayton M. Christensen, Taddy Hall, Karen Dillon, David S. Duncan; Anthony W. Ulwick Competing Against Luck (Harper Business, 2016); What Customers Want (Ulwick, McGraw-Hill, 2005); “Know Your Customers’ Jobs to Be Done” (HBR September 2016) Christensen's Jobs-to-Be-Done framework reframes the front-end of NPD from “what kind of customer is this?” to “what progress is the customer trying to make in a particular circumstance, and what existing solutions are they hiring to make that progress?” The Four Forces of Progress (push, pull, anxiety, habit) model explains switching behavior and predicts which products will win the customer. Anthony Ulwick's Outcome-Driven Innovation extension converts the qualitative framework into a quantitative input the firm's stage-gate process can act on at Stage 2 and Gate 3: the job is decomposed into 50-150 desired outcomes, customers rate each on importance and current satisfaction, and the opportunity score (Importance + max(Importance − Satisfaction, 0)) identifies the underserved outcomes where new products create the most value. The chapter pairs JTBD/ODI with Cooper's stage-gate as the front-end input that makes Cooper's Stage 1-2 productive. deep reader →
  • CH 7 ANCHOR Karl T. Ulrich & Steven D. Eppinger Product Design and Development (7th ed., McGraw-Hill, 2020) Ulrich (Wharton) and Eppinger (MIT Sloan) supply the engineering-management methods that fill the inside of Cooper's stage-gate funnel. Five load-bearing contributions: (1) the Pugh concept-selection matrix, a 6-step weighted-criteria comparison against a reference benchmark that produces 40-60 percent reductions in late-stage concept-change decisions; (2) product architecture as a marketing decision (modular vs. integral), which determines variety, manufacturing cost, and platform strategy across a product family; (3) the prototyping taxonomy (analytical vs. physical, comprehensive vs. focused) that converts “build a prototype” into a milestone-driven plan matched to specific decisions; (4) Design for Manufacturing (Boothroyd-Dewhurst-style part-count reduction and assembly-time reduction) producing 25-50 percent unit-cost reductions in disciplined applications; (5) product-development economics, a four-lever DCF (development cost, development time, manufacturing cost, sales volume) for gate decisions. The chapter pairs Ulrich-Eppinger with Cooper for inside-the-stages discipline and with Christensen-Ulwick for outside-the-stages customer-need input. deep reader →
07 // chapter 08 — pricing

A.7 Chapter 8 — Pricing

Chapter 8 has three canonical readers: Nagle on strategy and tactics, Simon on monetization, Ramanujam-Tacke on pricing as a product-development discipline. The frontier reader on dynamic pricing 2024-2026 (Wendy's, Uber, FTC) is in Appendix B.

  • CH 8 ANCHOR Thomas T. Nagle, John Hogan, Joseph Zale The Strategy and Tactics of Pricing (6th ed., 2016) Nagle is the dominant text on pricing strategy in MBA programs and corporate practice. The framework moves from cost-plus pricing (deficient) to value-based pricing (preferred): start from the buyer's perceived value of the differentiated benefits, work backward to a price that captures a defensible share of that value. The economic value to the customer (EVC) calculation is the load-bearing tool. The chapter pairs Nagle's framework with Hubbard's EVPI math from Ch 4 to give the student a value-then-research-then-set price discipline. deep reader →
  • CH 8 ANCHOR Hermann Simon Confessions of the Pricing Man (2015) Simon, founder of Simon-Kucher, distills four decades of consulting into a single thesis: pricing is the strongest single profit lever any company has, and most firms underinvest in pricing science by an order of magnitude. The book is rich with worked cases (Apple's price ladders, BMW's hidden-discount discipline, the deception of unit pricing) and the diagnostic question Simon asks every CFO: when did you last hold a half-day pricing review at the executive committee level? The answer for most firms is "never." The chapter teaches pricing as a continuous management discipline, not a once-a-year SKU exercise. deep reader →
  • CH 8 SUPPORTING Madhavan Ramanujam & Georg Tacke Monetizing Innovation (Wiley, 2016) Ramanujam and Tacke argue that monetization (price + monetization model + packaging) is a product-development discipline, not a launch decision. Most innovation flops are not bad products; they are good products with the wrong monetization. The book introduces nine product flops (Bezos's Amazon Fire phone, Tata Nano, Webvan) and shows that each had a monetization fault, not a product fault. The chapter pairs this with Cooper's Stage-Gate to teach pricing as a question that must be answered at gate 2, not gate 5. deep reader →
  • CH 8 EXTENSION Richard Thaler & Daniel Kahneman Mental accounting (Thaler 1985, 1999); fairness in pricing (Kahneman, Knetsch, Thaler 1986) Thaler's mental-accounting framework explains why buyers separate spending into psychological accounts (vacation budget, treat budget, household budget) that are not fungible in their heads even when they are fungible in their wallets. Kahneman-Knetsch-Thaler 1986 on fairness explains why price increases are accepted in some contexts (rising costs) and rejected in others (windfall opportunism), even when the absolute increase is identical. Wendy's 2024 surge-pricing reversal is the textbook case for the second principle. deep reader →
08 // chapter 09 — distribution

A.8 Chapter 9 — Distribution and Retail

Chapter 9 anchors on Coughlan's marketing channels framework. The principle: a buyer who cannot find you cannot buy you. Distribution is mental availability's physical twin.

  • CH 9 ANCHOR Anne T. Coughlan, Erin Anderson, Louis W. Stern, Adel I. El-Ansary Marketing Channels (7th ed., 2006) Coughlan-Anderson-Stern-El-Ansary is the dominant academic text on distribution channel design. The framework decomposes channel decisions into channel length (number of intermediaries), channel intensity (intensive, selective, exclusive), channel breadth (single-channel vs multi-channel), and the service-output demands buyers actually have (lot size, waiting time, spatial convenience, product variety, service backup). Costco's no-advertising strategy is a Coughlan-style channel choice: Costco invests in distribution intensity (warehouse density and member-only access) instead of advertising-driven mental availability. deep reader →
09 // chapter 10 — services

A.9 Chapter 10 — Services Marketing

Chapter 10 has two canonical readers: Lovelock-SERVQUAL on the gaps model and service quality dimensions, and Vargo-Lusch on the service-dominant logic that reframes all marketing as services.

  • CH 10 ANCHOR Christopher Lovelock; Parasuraman, Zeithaml, Berry Services Marketing (8th ed., 2017); SERVQUAL (1988) Lovelock's services-marketing framework names the four characteristics that distinguish services from goods (intangibility, inseparability, variability, perishability) and walks through the operational implications of each. The Parasuraman-Zeithaml-Berry SERVQUAL instrument operationalizes service quality across five dimensions (reliability, assurance, tangibles, empathy, responsiveness) measured as the gap between buyer expectation and buyer perception. Singapore Airlines' service-quality leadership and Carnival Cruise's recovery from COVID disruption are the chapter's worked SERVQUAL cases. deep reader →
  • CH 10 EXTENSION Stephen L. Vargo & Robert F. Lusch "Evolving to a New Dominant Logic for Marketing" (JM 2004); service-dominant logic Vargo-Lusch reframe all marketing as services: even a physical good is a vehicle for delivering a service. A car is a service of mobility; a coffee is a service of caffeination plus social ritual. The reframe is conceptually elegant and operationally useful when designing the value proposition: the offer is the bundle of services the buyer is buying, with the physical good as the artifact through which the services are delivered. The chapter uses S-D logic to bridge to Ch 7 (product as service-bearing artifact) and to Ch 11 (advertising as a service of memory refresh). deep reader →
10 // chapter 11 — promotions

A.10 Chapter 11 — Promotions and Advertising

Chapter 11 has the second-deepest canonical stack: Cialdini on persuasion, Reeves-Caples on the direct-response tradition, Sutherland on behavioral-economic advertising, Binet-Field on long-and-short, Petty-Cacioppo (ELM) on the dual-route to persuasion.

  • CH 11 ANCHOR Robert B. Cialdini Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion (rev. 2021); Pre-Suasion (2016) Cialdini's seven principles of influence (reciprocity, commitment-consistency, social proof, liking, authority, scarcity, unity) are the dominant framework for understanding persuasion at the message and channel level. Pre-Suasion (2016) extends the framework to attentional priming: the moment before a message lands often matters more than the message itself. The chapter teaches Cialdini as the load-bearing diagnostic for whether a creative concept is leveraging the right principle for the buyer's decision context. The dark-pattern audit in Ch 17 builds on Cialdini's distinction between persuasion and manipulation. deep reader →
  • CH 11 ANCHOR Rosser Reeves & John Caples Reality in Advertising (Reeves 1961); Tested Advertising Methods (Caples, 5th ed., 1997) The American direct-response tradition. Reeves named the Unique Selling Proposition (USP) as the load-bearing claim in any ad: a single benefit, expressed in a way the competition cannot or will not match, large enough to move buyers. Caples' tested-advertising-methods framework codified the discipline that ads should be tested by response (coupons, mail orders, eventually clicks) rather than by intuition, opinion, or focus group. Together they anchor the chapter's claim that advertising must be measured against business outcomes, not creative awards. deep reader →
  • CH 11 ANCHOR Rory Sutherland Alchemy (2019); Ogilvy & Mather behavioral-economics practice Sutherland applies Kahneman-Tversky behavioral-economics to advertising practice with sustained focus on what he calls non-obvious, asymmetric, branded ideas: small framing changes that shift behavior at scale (Eurostar's faster-train problem solved by attractive escorts on the platform; Costa Coffee's repositioning around the daily ritual rather than the price). The chapter uses Sutherland to teach that the advertising idea is rarely the rational benefit; it is the framing that bypasses rational calculation. Liquid Death's water-as-rebellion is a Sutherland-style positioning. deep reader →
  • CH 11 ANCHOR Les Binet & Peter Field The Long and the Short of It (IPA, 2013); Marketing Effectiveness in the Digital Era (IPA, 2019) Binet and Field analyzed 30 years of IPA Effectiveness Awards data and produced the load-bearing finding for marketing budget allocation: the optimal split between brand-building (long-term, broad-reach, emotional) and sales-activation (short-term, targeted, rational) is approximately 60/40 across categories. Brands that over-weight short-term activation grow faster on quarterly metrics and lose share over multi-year horizons. The 60/40 rule is the chapter's load-bearing budget heuristic. deep reader →
  • CH 11 EXTENSION Richard E. Petty & John T. Cacioppo Elaboration Likelihood Model (ELM, 1986); pairs with Kahneman The ELM is the academic-psychology counterpart to Kahneman's System 1 / System 2 dichotomy, applied to persuasion. Buyers process persuasion messages through either the central route (high motivation, high ability, careful argument-by-argument evaluation) or the peripheral route (low motivation, heuristic-driven, source credibility and surface features dominate). Most marketing communication lands in the peripheral route. The chapter uses ELM as the structural read on which persuasion principle (Cialdini) the brand should leverage given the buyer's processing state. deep reader →

A.11 Chapter 12 — Digital Marketing and SEO/GEO

Chapter 12 anchors on Kaushik's web analytics framework, then extends to the 2026 GEO (generative engine optimization) literature. The frontier on the search-channel bifurcation is in Appendix B.

12 // chapter 14 — sales alignment

A.12 Chapter 14 — Sales and Marketing Alignment

Chapter 14 anchors on Dixon's Challenger sales research, the dominant empirical framework for B2B selling in the 2010s and 2020s.

  • CH 14 ANCHOR Matthew Dixon & Brent Adamson The Challenger Sale (2011); The Challenger Customer (2015) CEB (now Gartner) research across 6,000+ B2B sellers identified five seller archetypes (relationship builder, hard worker, lone wolf, reactive problem solver, challenger). The challenger archetype outperformed all four others in complex B2B sales by a wide margin. The challenger teaches the buyer something new about their own business, tailors the message to specific economic drivers, and takes control of the conversation. The chapter uses Dixon's framework to teach sales-marketing alignment as alignment on the teaching content (marketing creates the insight, sales delivers it) rather than alignment on lead handoff mechanics. deep reader →
13 // chapter 15 — international

A.13 Chapter 15 — International Marketing

Chapter 15 has four canonical readers: Levitt 1983 on globalization, Bartlett-Ghoshal on transnational management, Ghemawat on CAGE distance, Hall on context. The 2024-2026 case file (Apple India, Walmart Germany, Costco China, BYD Europe, Liquid Death UK exit) is in Appendix B.

  • CH 15 ANCHOR Theodore Levitt "The Globalization of Markets" (HBR 1983) Levitt's 1983 argument that technology is converging consumer tastes worldwide and that the multinational firm should standardize its offer globally rather than localize aggressively for each market. The argument is partially vindicated (Shein, BYD) and partially refuted (Liquid Death's UK failure, Walmart Germany's exit). The chapter teaches Levitt's globalization-as-convergence as the structural backdrop, then layers Hall, Hofstede, Ghemawat, and Bartlett-Ghoshal on top to show what doesn't converge. deep reader →
  • CH 15 ANCHOR Christopher Bartlett & Sumantra Ghoshal Managing Across Borders (1989, 2nd ed. 1998) Bartlett-Ghoshal's transnational typology: international (HQ as the source, exports the model), multidomestic (each country a near-independent operation), global (worldwide standardization with central coordination), transnational (integrated network with local responsiveness AND global integration). BYD in 2024-2026 is the canonical contemporary transnational case: local production in Hungary and Brazil, multidomestic responsiveness on European trim and pricing, global integration on the supply chain. deep reader →
  • CH 15 ANCHOR Pankaj Ghemawat Redefining Global Strategy (2007); CAGE Distance Framework Ghemawat's CAGE framework decomposes the distance between any two markets into Cultural (language, social norms, religion), Administrative (regulatory, currency, political), Geographic (physical distance, time zones, climate), and Economic (income levels, infrastructure quality, costs). The framework operationalizes Levitt's "globalization is real but unevenly so": the four distances expand and contract independently, and a brand's market-entry decision should weight each distance against the brand's specific source of competitive advantage. deep reader →
  • CH 15 ANCHOR Edward T. Hall Beyond Culture (1976); high-context vs low-context cultures Hall's high-context vs low-context distinction is the foundational read on cross-cultural communication. Low-context cultures (US, Germany, Scandinavia) embed meaning in explicit verbal content; high-context cultures (Japan, China, Saudi Arabia) embed meaning in shared context, relationship history, and non-verbal cues. The same advertising message lands very differently across the dimension. The chapter uses Hall to teach that brand-voice translation across markets is not a translation problem; it is a context-density adaptation problem. deep reader →
  • CH 15 EXTENSION Susan P. Douglas, C. Samuel Craig, Yoram (Jerry) Wind "The Myth of Globalization" (Columbia Journal of World Business 1987) Douglas, Craig, and Wind wrote the 1987 critique of Levitt's globalization argument: empirical evidence does not support universal taste convergence, and the multinational firm that standardizes prematurely loses to local competitors who read context better. The paper introduced the concept of glocalization (think globally, act locally) that became the dominant 1990s-2000s posture before Bartlett-Ghoshal's transnational typology refined it. The Trader Joe's no-internationalization decision and Walmart Germany's exit are both Douglas-Wind validations. deep reader →
14 // chapter 16 — plan + analytics

A.14 Chapter 16 — Marketing Plan and Analytics

Chapter 16 anchors on Palmatier-Fader's customer-centricity framework. The principle: customer-base economics (CLV, retention, cohort behavior) is the closed-loop discipline that turns a marketing plan from a budget-plus-calendar into a measurement-driven asset-management program.

  • CH 16 ANCHOR Robert W. Palmatier & Peter S. Fader Marketing Strategy: Based on First Principles and Data Analytics (Palmatier 2017); Customer Centricity (Fader 2012, 2nd ed. 2020) Palmatier's first-principles framework distills marketing strategy to four root problems (customer heterogeneity, dynamics, competition, scarcity of resources) and the analytics discipline for managing each. Fader's customer-centricity argument is operational: the firm's most valuable asset is its customer base, customer lifetime value (CLV) is the load-bearing metric, and retention curves rather than acquisition counts are the leading indicators of the asset's health. Together they anchor the chapter's claim that a marketing plan is a closed loop with measurement as the validation gate. deep reader →
15 // chapter 17 — ethics + AI governance

A.15 Chapter 17 — Marketing Ethics, Privacy, AI Governance

Chapter 17 has Zuboff on surveillance capitalism as its anchor; the frontier reader on AI emergent misalignment is in Appendix B.

  • CH 17 ANCHOR Shoshana Zuboff The Age of Surveillance Capitalism (PublicAffairs, 2019) Zuboff's framework reframes the data-driven marketing economy as a new economic mode: surveillance capitalism, in which the asset is buyer behavior captured at scale, the product sold is prediction of buyer future behavior, and the market that buys those predictions is advertisers and political campaigns. The Cambridge Analytica case is the canonical 2016-2018 deployment of the framework. The chapter uses Zuboff to set the ethical floor: every AI deployment that uses behavioral data is operating in the regime Zuboff names, and the human-fallback path is the operating discipline that prevents the regime from collapsing into manipulation. deep reader →
16 // chapter 19 — retail

A.16 Chapter 19 — Retail Marketing

Chapter 19 has two canonical readers: Underhill on shopper observation, Sorensen on the physics of in-store buyer movement. The frontier reader on retail tech 2024-2026 (Amazon Just Walk Out reversal, BOPIS flow, TikTok Shop) is in Appendix B.

  • CH 19 ANCHOR Paco Underhill Why We Buy (Simon & Schuster, 1999, rev. 2009) Underhill is the dominant text on retail observation as a research method: trained observers track buyers through stores, count interactions, and identify the operational moments where the path from interest to purchase breaks down. The butt-brush effect (women avoid aisles where they will be brushed by other shoppers from behind) and the decompression zone (the first 10-15 feet inside a store entrance, where buyers don't yet absorb merchandising) are two of the canonical findings the chapter teaches. Underhill is the methods foundation for the chapter's working tool: the in-store observation audit. deep reader →
  • CH 19 EXTENSION Herb Sorensen Inside the Mind of the Shopper (FT Press, 2009) Sorensen extends Underhill's observational tradition with the quantitative end: hours of in-store path tracking across hundreds of stores, behavioral segmentation by buyer mission (quick trip, fill-in trip, stock-up trip), and the load-bearing finding that buyers spend 80 percent of their store time on 20 percent of the merchandise. The retail layout's job is to put the right merchandise in the right 20 percent. The chapter teaches Sorensen's quick-trip vs stock-up segmentation as the diagnostic for store-format decisions (Trader Joe's narrow assortment is a quick-trip optimization). deep reader →
17 // how the readers compose

A.17 How the readers compose

Each chapter's anchor is the canonical text that names the load-bearing principle. Each chapter's extensions are the readers that complicate, refine, or refute the anchor for specific situations. The chapter prose pulls a working synthesis through; this appendix lets a student or instructor walk back to the source when they want to teach the principle themselves, extend it to a new domain, or argue against it.

For situations not covered by a single canonical text, the deep readers also link to frontier sources at core-course/wiki/external/frontier/{slug}.md that synthesize 2024-2026 case material. The frontier readers are summarized as case-by-case entries in Appendix B; the practical paste-able prompts that operationalize each chapter's tools are catalogued in Appendix C.

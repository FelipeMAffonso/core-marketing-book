    The offer is the proof of the positioning.

    1. The Tropicana redesign is one of the most expensive packaging mistakes on record: roughly 35 million dollars spent before the orange-with-the-straw image was restored.

    In January 2009 PepsiCo redesigned the Tropicana Pure Premium carton. The decades-old orange-with-a-straw image gave way to a juice-glass close-up in a cleaner typographic system. Consumer tests run before launch said the new design read as fresher. Six weeks after the rollout, sales had dropped roughly 20 percent, and cartons were coming back to retailers in pallets.

    PepsiCo reverted within two months, at a reported 35-million-dollar cost. Only the package had moved, and buyers scanning a crowded shelf for a carton they already knew could no longer find the brand. The brand promises something, and the product is what proves the promise. When the product changes in a way that breaks the promise, the brand pays the bill.

    01 // what a product actually is

    What a product actually is

    2. Older texts taught a five-levels-of-product framework (core, generic, expected, augmented, potential). The current edition uses the three-element decomposition. Both say the same thing: the buyer is buying more than the box on the shelf.

    Walk into a Costco. The rotisserie chicken is the product. The hot bag, the aroma ducted toward the entrance, and the 4.99-dollar price held since 2009 are the service. The promise that any of 870 warehouses serves the same chicken at the same price is the brand. Take any one element away and you have a different offer.

    Walk into a Costco. The rotisserie chicken is the product, the hot bag and the 4.99-dollar price held since 2009 are the service, and the promise that any warehouse serves the same chicken at the same price is the brand. Take one element away and the offer changes.

    So the audit on any offer asks three plain questions. What is the buyer touching? What does the firm do around it? What does the buyer believe before opening the box? When one of the three is out of step with the other two, the offer is paying to lose customers.

    Product, service, brand: the three elements Product: the good the buyer takes ownership of. Service: the work around it (ordering, delivery, installation, training, maintenance, repair). Brand: the meaning attached before purchase. Strong offers fuse the three. Weak offers leak value on whichever leg the firm forgot to design.

    The nine attributes a product can win on

    3. Read the nine attributes as an audit menu the firm chooses from, never as a checklist it must complete. Strong products win on two or three and hold the floor on the rest. The trap is the product sitting in the middle on all nine.

    Once the offer is decomposed, the next question is which attributes the firm will win on. The methods literature names nine, and a strong product leads on two or three while holding the floor on the rest.

    Nine attributes of product differentiation Core functionality. Features. Performance quality. Conformance quality. Durability. Reliability. Form. Style. Customization. Each is an axis on which a product can differ from its competitors. A product that wins on none of them has nothing to say.

    Most of the nine are quick to read. Core functionality is whether the product does the basic job, because a phone that drops calls fails no matter how good the camera is. Features are the extras that help right up until the product crosses feature fatigue, after which each new feature lowers value. Durability is how long the product lasts under stress, and reliability is how rarely it breaks while it is still in use. The undergrad cue: durability is how long it lasts before it wears out, reliability is how often it fails while still under warranty. Form is size and shape, the 16.9-ounce tallboy set against the 12-ounce stubby, and it decides what shelf the product lives on and what hand position it asks for. Style is look and feel, the thing that makes a product recognizable from across a room before a word of copy is read. Customization is the firm tailoring the product to one buyer, and it carries a premium even when the underlying function is unchanged.

    Mercedes in the early 2000s is the worked case for what a leaky floor costs. It was the brand most buyers would have named first for engineering rigor, winning on roughly seven of the nine attributes. The one that had drifted was conformance: two cars with the same model number, built the same week on the same line, reached dealers with different door fit, different rattles, and a different feel through the steering. By 2003 BMW had passed Mercedes on global luxury-sedan volume for the first time in the segment's modern history. The board reorganized the quality function and ran roughly 10,000 diagnostic tests per model before release. Reported flaws per vehicle fell about 72 percent, and warranty costs dropped about 25 percent, a quarter of the repair dollars going straight back into operating profit.

    Customization is the ninth attribute, and it earns one extra line because the word hides four different strategies. Pine and Gilmore's four faces of mass customization sort those faces by two yes-or-no questions: does the underlying product change from one buyer to the next, and does the buyer perceive the change. The face a brand picks decides whether it pays for variety on the factory floor, at the surface-finishing step, or in the data pipeline. The cosmetic face scales all the way down: a dorm-room Etsy shop selling name stickers runs the same face Stanley runs at scale, one base template plus a buyer-specified name, with no second engineering line. Customization is an architecture choice, not a Fortune-500 luxury, and the lesson is to pick the face before the assembly line or the recommendation engine is built.

    A firm rarely sells one product, though. It sells a whole portfolio, and the next section gives you the four dials that describe it.

    02 // the product mix

    The product mix: width, length, depth, consistency

    4. The four dimensions are independent in principle and entangled in practice. Adding a line usually pulls length up, and deepening one line borrows attention from another's depth. The product manager picks which of the four to expand each quarter knowing the other three will move.

    Tropicana was one carton on a shelf of dozens, which is the normal case. A firm sells a portfolio with four dimensions: how many lines, how many items per line, how many variants per item, and how related the lines are. Each one is a lever, and pulling any one costs something elsewhere.

    Tropicana was one carton on a shelf of dozens, the normal case.     Width, length, depth, consistency Width: the number of product lines. Length: total items across all lines. Depth: variants per item (sizes, scents, formulations). Consistency: how related the lines are in end use, production, and distribution.

    Procter and Gamble's fabric-care portfolio is the textbook illustration. Width is three lines (oral care, skin and personal care, fabric care). Length is roughly twelve items, with Tide as the anchor. Depth is six commercially active Tide variants (two scents, two formulations, two additives) plus dozens of smaller permutations. Consistency is high inside fabric care and lower across the lines.

    Stanley's 2018-to-2024 run shows the four dials moving at different speeds. In 2018 Stanley sold a small set of vacuum-insulated drinkware built around the rugged-hunter image its catalog had carried for a century: the green camp mug, the work-site thermos, the lunch-box flask. The portfolio was narrow on width (one category), short on length (a handful of shapes), shallow on depth (one or two colors each), and tightly consistent. By 2024 only one dial had moved. Depth is what changed: the Quencher alone stretched into roughly thirty colorways, four capacities, and seasonal Target and Starbucks drops the secondary market resold for multiples of retail. Consistency held, because every Quencher still read as the same vessel in a new finish. Stanley grew by pushing one dimension hard while the other three sat still, and the four-dimension vocabulary is what lets a product manager name that move precisely as a depth play, not a vague case of getting bigger.

    The menu reads as if more is always better, but variety is not free for the firm and is not always welcome for the buyer. Three arguments push toward variety: preference matching (the buyer who wants unsweetened almond milk is lost if you only stock sweetened), flexibility (today's preference differs from next month's), and exploration (some buyers arrive in a discovery mood). Three push toward restraint: information overload, choice overload, and raised expectations (the buyer who settles for an almost-right variant feels worse than one who picked the only option). Kotler's verdict is direct: fewer options can often make purchase more likely, lower returns, and produce more satisfied customers.

If yes, the firm pays production, warehouse, and shelf costs to move a buyer from a higher-margin item to a lower-margin one under its own roof.     03 // line and brand extensions

    Line stretching and brand extensions

    6. A new-brand launch in 2026 U.S. consumer-packaged goods carries a one-to-three-year runway and media commitments above 50 million dollars before national retail listings stabilize. The extension reuses every dollar of that prior spend, which is why finance approves them even when brand teams flag dilution risk.

    The brands doing that stretching are almost always ones the buyer already knows, reaching into a new flavor, size, or category. Reported figures put 80 to 90 percent of annual new products as extensions of an existing brand. The reason is money. Launching a major new United States consumer brand can cost more than 100 million dollars, while an extension reuses the awareness, retailer relationships, and trust the parent already paid to build. A brand team that flags dilution risk on an extension still watches finance approve it, because the extension spends the prior investment a second time.

    Stretching a line up, down, or both ways

    When a firm stretches its line in price, it has three directions, each with its own risk. A down-market stretch adds a cheaper tier below the existing line, under the parent name (Tide Basic), under a sub-brand that signals the step down (Charmin Basic), or under a separate brand on the same factory line (Old Navy under Gap). The deeper the brand is tied to its current tier, the higher the chance the cheaper version damages existing-tier sales without adding the new buyers underneath it that the move promised. Tide Basic was killed in test market in 2009 because the damage to mainstream Tide buyers outran the revenue from new lower-tier ones. An up-market stretch adds a pricier tier, and the risk inverts. Buyers may not grant the parent permission to play above its tier, so a mainstream brand reaching for luxury usually launches a new name on the same production base. Toyota launched Lexus, Nissan launched Infiniti, and Honda launched Acura. A two-way stretch covers the full price range and demands crisp boundaries among all three tiers, because a buyer who feels the firm is selling a cheaper version next door will defect. Robert Mondavi runs three named tiers from everyday to special-occasion wine, and Purina runs the same play in pet food through different retail channels.

    When the brand does not stretch: the Bic perfume failure

    8. Bic's razors and pens had built cheap-functional-throwaway meaning; perfume buyers in 1989 wanted lasting, premium, considered. The fit was wrong before the first bottle hit the shelf, and the 20-million-dollar campaign accelerated the failure.

    Not every extension works, and the costly failures are the ones where the parent carries a meaning the new category's buyers were trying to escape. Bic in 1989 is the textbook case. The firm led the world in disposable pens, lighters, and razors, all sold on one argument repeated across three categories: cheap, functional, throwaway. Reading that consistency as proof the brand could go anywhere convenient, Bic launched a perfume priced at five dollars a bottle, sold in flat plastic containers reviewers compared to its own lighters, and backed by a twenty-million-dollar campaign. On paper the case looked clean, with distribution in place and an unserved price tier. What buyers experienced was a different product. Perfume was a self-presentation purchase, and buyers paid for the opposite of what Bic offered. They wanted a fragrance that lasted, signaled premium taste, and read as a considered choice, while the brand carried disposable, cheap, and impulsive meanings into the bottle. Within about two years the line was discontinued. The large campaign accelerated the failure, because it pushed the conflicting meaning into more buyers' awareness, not fewer.

    04 // product life cycle

    The product life cycle and the strategy at each stage

    9. The PLC is a teaching tool, not a forecasting model. Patterns vary too widely in shape and duration to generalize, and skillful marketing extends a product's life past what a textbook curve predicts. Treating the PLC as a deterministic schedule harvests products that could still be growing.

    Every product has a life: launch, growth, maturity, decline. The classic picture draws this as a bell-shaped curve of sales over time in four stages. The product life cycle works as a diagnostic, not a prediction. It does not tell you how many months a product has left. It tells you which job the product is asking you to do right now.

The buyers are the small group of innovators willing to try something unproven, so profit is negative while launch costs are paid up front. The job is not revenue yet but building awareness in the right channel.     PLC variants: scalloped, cycle-recycle, growth-slump-maturity

    The bell is the textbook shape, but it is not the only path a product traces, and each variant calls for a different move. A scalloped curve goes through more than one growth phase, with each wave triggered by a new use. Nylon ran four waves across four decades, from wartime parachutes to hosiery to carpeting to tire cord, and the lesson is that a mature product is not automatically a declining one. It is a product whose first use has saturated, and the question is whether a second use exists. The cycle-recycle variant shows a first decline interrupted by a deliberate relaunch, common in pharmaceuticals when a drug finds a second indication. The growth-slump-maturity variant shows a strong early-adopter peak that drops when the enthusiast pool churns out, then settles at a durable plateau below the peak, the way a new air fryer or sous-vide circulator runs hot through food media and then steadies into a replacement-and-gifting market. A manager who reads that slump as terminal decline pulls investment too early.

    The stage-gate New product development runs on one method above all others, the stage-gate. The work breaks into five stages, with a hard review at each boundary, and the design intent is to fail bad ideas earlier and cheaper than a single end-of-pipeline launch decision would.

    Cooper built the method by comparing hundreds of successful launches against hundreds of failed ones. The successful launches shared one feature the failures lacked. The work was broken into stages, with a real review at the boundary between each one, where a senior committee either advanced the project, killed it, or sent it back.     Where ideas come from

    Ideas come from two directions, and the direction sets the failure rate. Top-down ideation starts from a buyer need the market does not yet meet, and works backward to a spec. The Nest thermostat is the clean case. Tony Fadell's team did not start with a sensor and hunt for a use. It started from the observation that people pay to heat empty houses, then built a thermostat that learned the household's pattern and adjusted itself. Bottom-up ideation runs the other way, starting from a technology the firm already has and asking what market it might serve. The Slinky is the textbook case. A naval engineer knocked a tension spring off a shelf in 1943, watched it walk down a stack of books, and the toy market was the application found for the behavior. Top-down launches more products per dollar and fails less, because the buyer need is validated before engineering commits. Bottom-up fails more often, but the rare hit creates a category no buyer could have asked for. A balanced pipeline runs both, weighted toward where the firm's growth is supposed to come from.

    The single highest-leverage piece of front-end homework is figuring out what the buyer is actually trying to get done. That homework has a name.

    Jobs-to-Be-Done Jobs-to-Be-Done asks not who the buyer is but what progress the buyer is trying to make in the moment they reach for the product. The job exists independently of any product, and existing solutions get hired and fired against it.

    A quick-serve chain spent years asking milkshake buyers who they were and got nowhere. When the team asked instead what the milkshake was for, the answer surprised them. Commuters were buying it at 7am as a thirty-minute one-handed boredom-killer for the drive to work, so the real competition was a banana or a bagel, not another milkshake. Switching from one solution to another is governed by four forces. The push of frustration with the current option (the bagel falls apart on the wheel), the pull of the new option's promise (the milkshake lasts half an hour in one hand), the anxiety about the new option failing (this is a lot of calories before lunch), and the habit of the present (every Tuesday I grab a banana on the way out).     Moore's chasm The discontinuity between early adopters (visionaries who tolerate rough edges) and the early majority (pragmatists who require references, completeness, and reassurance). Many technology products cannot cross. The required shift is a different brand argument and often a different product spec, not more marketing spend.

    Crossing the chasm takes three moves in sequence. The first move is to pick one head-pin segment, one buyer group with an acute, expensive problem the product solves better than any alternative they can find. The second move is to deliver the whole product for that one segment, every adjacent piece the buyer needs to actually use it. The third move is to pivot into adjacent niches one at a time, using references from the first segment as the proof the next one demands.

    08 // failure modes

    What goes wrong

    Five failure modes recur across categories, and each one is a chapter idea read backward.

    Line-extension dilution A new variant drags the parent brand's meaning somewhere the firm did not want it to go. Bic perfume from Section 8.3 is the worked case, where a disposable-lighter maker asked buyers to wear its name. The fix is the brand-fit audit you already know, because listing the parent's meanings against what the new category requires flags the conflicts before the launch.
    Brand-stretch failure A brand stretches into a tier where buyers do not grant it permission. Volkswagen carries this risk across VW, Audi, Seat, and Škoda, since a shopper can talk herself into a Seat as the same chassis for less money. The fix is a separate brand for the tiers outside the parent's franchise, the way Toyota built Lexus, or a sub-brand boundary clear enough that the two never blur.
    PLC denial A firm keeps investing in a declining product as though it were still climbing. Polaroid is the case people remember, because instant-photography demand fell through the early 2000s while digital cameras and phones took the use, and the company spent into bankruptcy in 2001. The diagnostic from Section 8.4 is the fix, since falling unit sales mean the job has become harvest or divest.
    Poor concept testing The firm runs the wrong audience, asks the wrong question, or skips the test. Crystal Pepsi tested weakly in 1992 and launched anyway, while New Coke from Chapter 5 tested taste in a blind sip and missed the brand relationship buyers had with the can. BREXI scoring with the real target buyer is the fix, paired with a kill criterion that a score below three on any factor does not ship.
    Feature creep The product keeps gaining features past the point where each new one raises buyer value. Buyers grow overwhelmed, support cost passes the revenue the feature brought in, and the value proposition stretches across too many capabilities to market in one line. The iPhone-versus-Nokia contrast below names the move.

    Two 2024 launches stacked these modes on top of each other. Apple's Vision Pro shipped at 3,499 dollars and sold roughly 370,000 units in six months against an internal forecast of one million. The Humane Ai Pin shipped at 699 dollars plus a monthly fee, and returns passed sales by mid-2024. Both are chasm failures, where visionaries bought and pragmatists did not, layered on a concept-test failure, with the Vision Pro stretched across work, entertainment, and communication at once. Both also broke the design surface a buyer reads in the first session, because every offer presents affordances, signifiers, mappings, and a conceptual model. The Vision Pro's eye-and-pinch vocabulary had no familiar analog, and the Humane Pin gave no signal when a palm gesture misfired. Each launch also waited on partners, since the Vision Pro depended on content apps from Adobe, Microsoft, and Netflix whose adoption-chain risk the business case under-counted.

    The same eighteen-month window holds the counter-case. Apple's AirPods Pro 2 hearing-aid feature was announced in September 2024, cleared by the FDA days later as the first over-the-counter hearing-aid software for any device, and shipped by firmware to roughly 66 million pairs already sitting in customers' drawers, against an incumbent industry whose dedicated devices ran 4,000 to 6,000 dollars a pair. The Vision Pro was a radical bet on new technology, while the hearing-aid feature was architectural, taking technology Apple already owned into a newly regulated category. The outcome difference is consistent with the pattern that architectural innovation outperforms radical innovation when the firm already controls the underlying technology.

    At student scale the same four design-surface primitives govern a 29-dollar-a-month Shopify Basic dorm-room T-shirt drop. The PRE-ORDER button has to look like a button, the price has to sit next to the size dropdown, the sizes have to read small-to-large, and the checkout has to skip a forced account-creation step. Against a 2026 cart-abandonment baseline near 70 percent, forced account creation pushes an estimated 14 to 18 more points of buyers off the cart. A sophomore who audits her own checkout on a Sunday catches the failure mode that drove the Vision Pro returns curve, on a flow she can fix before the next post goes live.
    The platform itself is a positive 2023–2026 launch case that reads like a stage-gate run in public. Shopify Magic shipped its AI writing tools in July 2023, Shopify Sidekick added a conversational layer the same day, and the Winter '26 release in December 2025 shipped Agentic Storefronts, which exposes a merchant's catalog to ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Microsoft Copilot shopping conversations with one admin toggle. The whole-product layer the chapter describes as a Sunday checklist now runs on infrastructure that did not exist when the chapter's running brands crossed their own chasms a decade earlier.

    The Gate 3 that broke the Vision Pro from above also broke the GLP-1 weight-loss launches from below. Eli Lilly's Zepbound, approved in November 2023 against an internal year-one target widely reported at 2 to 3 billion dollars, booked into combined Mounjaro-plus-Zepbound revenue of 16.4 billion dollars in 2024, and Lilly committed more than 17 billion dollars in manufacturing capacity to catch a demand the Gate 3 case had under-forecast by roughly five times. Gate 3 demand risk is therefore two-tailed, and both tails share one root cause, a single point estimate where the real uncertainty needed a range. The fix is to forecast demand as a range with explicit upside and downside, then read the gap between the demand forecast and the supply system's metabolizable growth rate as the number Gate 3 should be conditioned on.

    You can ask an AI to run this five-mode audit on a real roadmap, scoring each item on value and focus, tagging the failure mode, and naming the one item to cut. Widget 8.5 carries the demonstrable version of that audit. When the marketed feature has not actually cleared its gate, the brand pays for the gap. Apple Intelligence is the 2025 reminder, since it was announced as the iPhone 16 differentiator, partly shipped, then walked back in March 2025 when the personal-context Apple Intelligence Siri features were delayed.

    A vague prompt ("is this roadmap good?") hedges every item to a middling score, while a sharp prompt ("score each item only with named evidence, default to high-risk where evidence is absent, and name the one cut that lifts focus") returns a verdict you can act on.

    Apply What You Have Learned

    Do this, about twenty minutes

    Run the chapter's machinery on a real product with AI

    Pick a real product you know well, or a product idea you would actually launch, then put it through the chapter's machinery inside Claude or ChatGPT. The assistant will reason about the brand and search the web for current category facts on its own, so treat it as up to date and check every number it gives you.

    1. Decompose the offer on its three elements. Ask the AI to name the product, the service that wraps it, and the brand meaning the buyer carries before opening the box, and to flag the weakest leg. A weak leg drags the other two.
    2. Place it on its life-cycle stage. Describe its sales trend, its category's saturation, and its competitors, then ask which of the four stages it is in and which job that stage hands the marketer. The curve is a diagnostic, not a forecast schedule.
    3. Write a one-page concept brief and run a four-risks audit. Have the AI score value, usability, feasibility, and viability, and ask it to name the cheapest experiment that would kill the biggest risk before any real spend.
    4. Audit one proposed extension for brand-fit. Name a variant or new category the product could enter, then ask the AI whether the parent's meaning grants permission, or whether the move dilutes the brand the way Bic perfume did.

    The lesson is in the question, not the tool. Run the vague version first ("is this a good product?") and read the generic answer it returns. Then run the sharp version ("score each risk only with named evidence, default to high-risk where evidence is missing, and name the one experiment that kills the biggest risk first"). The same model at the same cost gives you a falsifiable verdict you can build a plan around. Stanley ran this kind of audit in practice when it read low cup sales as product weakness, found the daily-hydration job its outdoor catalog had never named, and grew the line from 73 million to 750 million dollars in five years without changing the cup.

    Key Takeaways

    • An offer is product, service, and brand at once. A weak leg drags the other two, which is why the Tropicana package change cost sales when only the carton had moved.
    • The product mix has four dials. Width, length, depth, and consistency move together, so pulling one hard reshapes the other three, and variety is never free.
    • Most new products are extensions, and brand-fit decides. The test is whether the parent's meaning grants permission for the category, since a good fit reinforces the brand while a poor fit dilutes it.
    • The product life cycle is a diagnostic, not a forecast. Each stage asks a different job, and treating the curve as a schedule harvests products that could still be growing.
    • The stage-gate fails bad ideas earlier and cheaper than the launch would. Jobs-to-Be-Done is the highest-leverage front-end homework, because naming the real job is what most concepts get wrong.
    • The chasm is a product-and-argument problem. The early majority will not buy what the early adopters bought, so crossing it takes a new system around the product, not more advertising spend.
    • Five failure modes recur across categories. Line-extension dilution, brand-stretch failure, PLC denial, poor concept testing, and feature creep all trace back to a chapter idea ignored, and the Gate 3 demand forecast can miss on either tail.

    Answer each in your head, then click to check.

    1. The Tropicana 2009 redesign tested as "fresher" before launch but dropped sales about 20 percent in six weeks. Which element of the three-element offering had the design team changed without realizing it?

    The brand element. The orange-with-the-straw image was carrying brand meaning that the package team read as decoration. Only the package moved, and buyers scanning a familiar carton on a crowded shelf could no longer find the brand. The product inside and the service around it were both unchanged, which is why the loss traces entirely to the brand leg of the offer.

    2. A firm wants to grow a product line. What is the difference between a line extension and a brand extension, and which one carries higher risk?

    A line extension is a new variant inside a category the brand already serves, like a new Stanley Quencher colorway. A brand extension enters a different category or tier, like Honda moving from cars to lawn mowers. The brand extension carries higher risk, because the buyer may not grant the brand permission to operate in the new category. Bic perfume is the failure that names the risk.

    3. Stanley's revenue went from 73 million dollars in 2018 to 750 million in 2023 without changing the cup. Which of the four portfolio dials moved, and which three held?

    Depth moved, through roughly thirty colorways and four capacities, while width, length, and consistency held. The growth came from pushing one dimension hard, plus naming the daily-hydration job the outdoor catalog had never claimed, plus a drop cadence that rewarded buyers on an unpredictable schedule. Pull any one of those three decisions out and the case does not hold.

    4. Why is the product life cycle described as a diagnostic and not a forecast, and what is the cost of treating it as a forecast schedule?

    Stage length and curve shape vary too widely across categories to generalize, and skillful marketing can extend a product's life well past any textbook curve. Treating the life cycle as a fixed schedule harvests products that are still growing. The useful question is which stage's job the product is asking the marketer to do, not how many months the curve says are left.

    5. The stage-gate breaks new-product work into stages with a hard review at each boundary. What is the design intent, and why does a project that clears two of the three gate questions still fail?

    The intent is to fail bad ideas earlier and cheaper than a single launch decision at the end of the pipeline. A project killed at the first gate has burned only the concept-brief budget, while one killed at the last gate has burned the whole development budget. Clearing two of desirability, feasibility, and viability is not ready. The gate exists to catch the missing third before the spend turns irreversible.

    6. Casper sold well in year one, then growth stalled. What was the chasm, and why was the move across it not the launch product?

    The chasm was the gap between the visionary early adopters who would buy a mattress unseen and the pragmatist early majority who would not. The move across was the system around the mattress, not the mattress itself: a 100-night return guarantee, a bedroom-system extension, and selective showrooms. Each one answered a specific pragmatist objection, and none of them was the original launch product.

    7. The Apple Vision Pro and the Humane Ai Pin both shipped in 2024 and both underperformed. What two failure modes did they stack, and how is the AirPods Pro 2 hearing-aid feature the counter-case?

    Both stacked a chasm failure, where visionaries bought and pragmatists did not, on a concept-test failure, with the Vision Pro stretched across work, entertainment, and communication at once. The AirPods Pro 2 hearing-aid feature was architectural, taking technology Apple already owned into a newly regulated category, and it shipped by firmware to roughly 66 million pairs already in customers' drawers. Architectural innovation tends to outperform radical innovation when the firm already controls the underlying technology.

    8. Eli Lilly's Zepbound under-forecast its own demand by roughly five times. Why does the chapter call Gate 3 demand risk "two-tailed," and what is the fix?

    A new-product business case can miss on either side of its forecast. Over-forecast launches like the Vision Pro and the Humane Pin arrive at empty showrooms, and under-forecast launches like Zepbound and Wegovy arrive into FDA shortage listings and patient leakage to substitutes. Both tails share one root cause, a single point estimate where the demand uncertainty needed a probability distribution. The fix is to forecast a range with explicit upside and downside scenarios.

Chapter 10 Pricing — Test Bank

Chapter: 10 — Pricing Source note: Adapted from Kotler MM16 Test Bank Chapter 11 (Kotler/Keller/Chernev, Marketing Management 16e) + original AI-era items authored against OUR chapter. Kotler seed items have been rewritten into this course's voice, updated to 2026 context, and re-tagged to our learning objectives. No item is a verbatim lift. Export format: Mirrors Kotler markdown schema (stem, A...E options, Answer, Diff, Objective, Tags) for QTI / Canvas Common Cartridge conversion.

Learning Objectives (Ch10, Our Chapter)

  • LO 10.1 Explain why price is the only mix element that creates revenue and how a small price change produces an asymmetric profit effect.
  • LO 10.2 Describe the four pricing approaches (cost-based, competitor-based, value-based, customer-determined) and choose the right approach for a given context.
  • LO 10.3 Distinguish markup from margin and apply breakeven and marginal-cost arithmetic to a pricing decision.
  • LO 10.4 Explain how willingness to pay is measured using Van Westendorp's PSM, conjoint analysis, price experiments, and scanner data.
  • LO 10.5 Identify the major psychological pricing mechanisms (reference pricing, decoy effect, price-quality inference) and predict how each shifts buyer choice.
  • LO 10.6 Describe how dynamic pricing and price discrimination work, and apply the six-principle fairness audit to a real rollout.
  • LO 10.7 Explain freemium, outcome-based, and gainsharing pricing models and connect them to AI-agent product economics.
  • LO 10.8 Analyze pricing fairness using the dual-entitlement principle and explain why shrinkflation and skimpflation carry detection risk.

Section I — Multiple-Choice Items (25 items)

Item 1

Price is unique among the four marketing mix elements because it is the only one that _______.

A) requires the least capital investment B) creates revenue rather than spending it C) can be changed without affecting the brand D) is set by the market rather than the firm E) operates independently of cost

Answer: B

Diff: 1

Objective: LO 10.1 — Price is the only mix element that creates revenue; all others spend it.

Tags: pricing fundamentals; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 2

A mid-size beverage firm earns a 3 percent net margin. According to the asymmetric-leverage principle in our chapter, a 1 percent increase in price at constant volume would lift profit by approximately _______.

A) 1 percent B) 5 percent C) 10 percent D) 33 percent E) 100 percent

Answer: D

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 10.1 — At a 3 percent margin, a 1 percent price increase produces roughly a 33 percent profit lift because the extra revenue needs no extra cost.

Tags: asymmetric leverage; margin arithmetic; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 3

A brand manager is setting price for a new reusable water bottle. She knows her unit cost is $18. She adds a standard 25 percent markup. The resulting price is _______.

A) $18.25 B) $20.50 C) $22.50 D) $24.00 E) $27.00

Answer: C

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 10.3 — Markup uses cost as the base: $18 x 1.25 = $22.50.

Tags: markup arithmetic; cost-based pricing; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 4

A manager says her gross margin on a product is 40 percent. A colleague says the markup is 40 percent. Which statement correctly distinguishes the two?

A) Margin and markup are two words for the same calculation, so both colleagues are correct. B) Margin uses price as the base; markup uses cost. The two will produce different dollar amounts even if the percentage stated is the same. C) Markup uses price as the base; margin uses cost. They produce the same result only when price equals cost. D) Margin is used for services; markup is used for physical goods. E) The difference only matters when the product is sold through an intermediary.

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 10.3 — Margin = (price minus cost) / price; markup = (price minus cost) / cost. At a 40 percent margin, markup is 67 percent. They are not interchangeable.

Tags: markup vs margin; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 5

Liquid Death sells canned Alpine spring water at $2.49 per 16-ounce can, five to eight times the per-ounce price of Smartwater on the same shelf. The input cost is under $0.30. Which pricing approach best describes Liquid Death's strategy?

A) Cost-based pricing with a high markup B) Competitor-based pricing anchored to the shelf average C) Value-based pricing anchored to identity, not hydration D) Customer-determined pricing that emerged from focus groups E) Penetration pricing intended to grow market share rapidly

Answer: C

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 10.2 — Liquid Death's price reflects what the buyer is purchasing: identity and social signaling, not water. That is value-based pricing anchored to perceived value, not cost.

Tags: value-based pricing; Liquid Death; Veblen effect; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 6

Costco caps its per-SKU markup at 14 percent, well below a typical grocery margin, and prices the rotisserie chicken at $4.99 and the hot-dog combo at $1.50. The firm earns roughly 70 percent of its net income from _______.

A) private-label margins on Kirkland Signature items B) membership fees paid annually by cardholders C) supplier slotting allowances collected at the warehouse door D) volume rebates from the top 10 national brands E) licensing its logistics model to international partners

Answer: B

Diff: 1

Objective: LO 10.2 — Costco's model is two-part pricing: the markup cap keeps the shelf visibly low; the membership fee carries the profit. This is a structural commitment, not a promotional tactic.

Tags: Costco; competitor-based pricing; two-part pricing; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 7

A specialty outdoor apparel startup has fixed costs of $200,000 per year. Each jacket costs $80 to produce and retails for $160. What is the annual breakeven unit volume?

A) 1,250 units B) 2,000 units C) 2,500 units D) 3,125 units E) 5,000 units

Answer: C

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 10.3 — Contribution margin per unit = $160 - $80 = $80. Breakeven = $200,000 / $80 = 2,500 units.

Tags: breakeven analysis; contribution margin; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 8

The Van Westendorp price-sensitivity meter (PSM) is best suited to _______.

A) tracking price changes weekly once a product is already in distribution B) estimating a launch-stage acceptable price band when the firm has no sales history C) inferring the value of individual product features inside a product line D) setting dynamic prices in real time using demand signals E) measuring price elasticity across multiple geographic markets

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 10.4 — The PSM asks four indirect questions and reads an acceptable band from where the cumulative curves cross. It is the launch-stage default when analog data is absent. Conjoint is the choice for feature-level value estimation once a product line exists.

Tags: Van Westendorp PSM; willingness to pay; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 9

A market researcher runs a Van Westendorp study on a new premium oat-milk creamer. The "too cheap" and "too expensive" curves intersect at $7.99. The "bargain" curves cross at $5.49 and $9.99. What is the optimal price point in this study?

A) $5.49 — the lower edge of the acceptable range B) $7.99 — the center where "too cheap" and "too expensive" cross C) $8.75 — the midpoint between $5.49 and $9.99 D) $9.99 — the upper edge of the acceptable range E) $6.24 — the average of the two "bargain" crossings

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 10.4 — In Van Westendorp, the optimal price point is where "too cheap" meets "too expensive." The "bargain" crossings mark the band edges.

Tags: Van Westendorp PSM; optimal price point; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 10

A soft-drink company raises the price of its 12-ounce can from $1.50 to $1.55 and observes no measurable change in weekly unit sales. This result is consistent with the concept of _______.

A) a Giffen good, where demand rises as price rises B) the Veblen effect, where higher prices signal status C) a price indifference band, within which price changes produce no detectable demand shift D) an experience curve, where per-unit costs fall as volume grows E) price elasticity above 1, meaning demand is elastic

Answer: C

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 10.4 — The price indifference band is the zone where a small price change produces no detectable demand response. A $0.05 move on a $1.50 drink is likely within that band for a habitual buyer.

Tags: price indifference band; elasticity; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 11

When a buyer uses a mental estimate of what they paid last time for a product to evaluate today's shelf price, they are using a _______.

A) Van Westendorp optimal price point B) lower-bound reservation price C) internal reference price based on last price paid D) psychological anchor set by the competitor E) decoy price inserted by the retailer

Answer: C

Diff: 1

Objective: LO 10.5 — An internal reference price is the buyer's remembered or expected price. Last price paid is one common form. The shelf price is evaluated against it.

Tags: reference pricing; consumer psychology; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 12

The Economist once offered three subscription options: web-only for $59, print-only for $125, and print-plus-web for $125. With the print-only option present, 84 percent of buyers chose print-plus-web. Without it, 68 percent chose web-only at $59. The print-only option is an example of _______.

A) second-degree price discrimination targeting volume buyers B) a loss leader intended to move readers toward digital subscriptions C) a decoy that makes the dominated option irrelevant while shifting choice toward the target D) a two-part pricing structure with a fixed and a variable component E) market-skimming pricing used at launch to maximize early revenue

Answer: C

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 10.5 — The print-only option is dominated by print-plus-web on every attribute at the same price. It shifts 52 percentage points from web-only to the higher-revenue bundle. That is asymmetric dominance, the decoy effect.

Tags: decoy effect; asymmetric dominance; psychological pricing; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 13

A buyer inspects two noise-canceling headphones on the same shelf: a recognizable brand at $349 and a no-name brand at $89. She picks the recognizable brand partly because the price itself signals that the components must be higher quality, even though she has read no review. This illustrates _______.

A) the decoy effect, where the cheap option makes the expensive one look better B) price-quality inference, where buyers use price as a proxy for quality when direct quality information is absent C) third-degree price discrimination, where the retailer prices by customer segment D) a reference price violation, where the posted price exceeds the buyer's fair-price expectation E) shrinkflation, where the cheaper option has reduced content

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 10.5 — Price-quality inference occurs when buyers use price as a signal of quality because they lack direct quality information. It is especially strong for ego-sensitive or unfamiliar product categories.

Tags: price-quality inference; psychological pricing; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 14

Patagonia sells its Down Sweater at $279, more than twice the price of a comparable mass-market jacket. The firm defends that premium through a lifetime repair guarantee, a 1% for the Planet pledge, and a Worn Wear resale program. Which pricing approach best describes Patagonia's strategy?

A) Cost-based pricing, because the premium materials justify the higher cost B) Competitor-based pricing, because the firm monitors mass-market prices closely C) Value-based pricing defended through purpose and durability, not advertising alone D) Penetration pricing intended to build a loyal base quickly E) Skimming pricing designed to capture early adopters before the price falls

Answer: C

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 10.2 — Patagonia positions a values premium through structural moves: the repair guarantee turns the high price into a competitive per-year cost; the pledge converts part of the price into identity; resale cannibalizes new sales on purpose. That is value-based pricing.

Tags: Patagonia; value-based pricing; premium positioning; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 15

A hotel in Indianapolis uses yield management to offer rooms at $89 in January and $239 during the Indianapolis 500 in May, with the same room available via advance-purchase discount at $189 in March. This is an example of _______.

A) first-degree price discrimination, where each buyer pays their maximum willingness to pay B) product-form pricing, where the room is a different product in each period C) time-based price discrimination using yield management to match price to demand D) predatory pricing, where the hotel undercuts competitors to drive them out E) loss-leader pricing, where the January rate generates traffic for more profitable periods

Answer: C

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 10.6 — Yield management varies prices by season, day, and advance-purchase window to match supply to demand. This is time-based (third-degree) price discrimination applied to a perishable inventory asset.

Tags: yield management; dynamic pricing; price discrimination; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 16

The fairness ceiling a firm faces when rolling out dynamic prices is grounded in the _______, which holds that buyers grant the firm the right to defend its reference profit against rising costs but not to capture windfall profit from a demand spike.

A) Veblen effect B) price indifference band C) dual-entitlement principle D) decoy asymmetry rule E) moral-hazard model

Answer: C

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 10.8 — The dual-entitlement principle (Kahneman, Knetsch, and Thaler) distinguishes cost-driven increases from demand-driven ones. A cost story makes the increase read as fair; a demand story reads as gouging.

Tags: dual-entitlement principle; pricing fairness; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 17

Wendy's announced in a 2024 earnings call that it planned to use dynamic menu pricing during peak hours. Public backlash followed within days, and the firm reversed the plan within twelve days. According to our chapter's six-principle fairness audit, the primary failure was _______.

A) explicability, because the price had no cost-based justification B) cooperative framing, because the story read as a margin extraction tool rather than a supply-demand alignment benefit C) brand-promise compatibility, because Wendy's premium positioning cannot absorb surge pricing D) pre-launch communication, because no discounts were offered to draw attention away from the increases E) category tolerance, because fast food is a luxury good with a tight reference price

Answer: B

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 10.6 — Wendy's framed peak-hour pricing as a margin tool aimed at lunch traffic, not as a mutual benefit (e.g., saving money by going at off-peak times). The cooperative-framing principle says the price story must read as the firm and buyer sharing an interest in matching supply to demand.

Tags: Wendy's; dynamic pricing; fairness audit; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 18

Stonegate pubs in the UK faced customer anger when they announced a 20-pence surcharge on pints during peak hours. Our chapter argues the failure was primarily one of _______.

A) explicability, because no cost story supported the surcharge B) temporal discrimination, where legal restrictions prohibit time-based pricing in hospitality C) category tolerance, because a familiar pint has a tight reference price and high purchase frequency D) brand-promise compatibility, because Stonegate had explicitly advertised a fixed national price E) pre-launch communication, because the surcharge launched before regulatory approval

Answer: C

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 10.6 — Category tolerance requires that the product not carry a tight reference price and high purchase frequency. Buyers screenshot familiar-product prices and recognize a 20p variation immediately, which makes the surcharge read as a betrayal rather than ordinary price variation.

Tags: Stonegate; fairness audit; category tolerance; dynamic pricing; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 19

A chip brand holds its shelf price steady while quietly reducing the share-size bag from 9.75 to 9.25 ounces. This pricing tactic is called _______.

A) skimpflation B) shrinkflation C) unbundling D) delayed quotation pricing E) experience-curve pricing

Answer: B

Diff: 1

Objective: LO 10.8 — Shrinkflation holds the posted shelf price constant while reducing the package quantity, so the per-unit price rises but the shelf tag shows the same number. Frito-Lay's 2021 Doritos rollout is the chapter's case.

Tags: shrinkflation; covert price increase; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 20

A consumer-software company offers a free tier with basic features and a $12-per-month tier with the full product. Its conversion rate from free to paid is 4 percent. If a new marketing campaign raises that conversion rate to 6 percent while keeping churn constant, the primary economic effect is _______.

A) higher lifetime value per paid user, because the same users stay longer B) a larger paid-user base generating more monthly revenue from the same free-user cohort C) lower total marketing cost, because fewer free users are needed to reach revenue targets D) a reduced server cost per paid user, because the fixed infrastructure is spread more widely E) a higher gross margin, because the free tier's marginal cost falls to zero

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 10.7 — In a freemium model the conversion rate drives paid-user volume. A higher conversion rate from the same free cohort expands the paid base and the recurring revenue it generates. The key variable is conversion, not the free tier's cost.

Tags: freemium; conversion rate; subscription economics; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 21

HubSpot Breeze charges $0.50 per resolved support ticket rather than a monthly subscription rate. This pricing model is best described as _______.

A) value-based pricing anchored to the firm's cost of providing the service B) outcome-based pricing, where the buyer pays per measurable unit of work completed C) gainsharing, where the firm receives a fraction of the buyer's documented revenue lift D) captive-product pricing, where the core software is free and add-ons generate revenue E) penetration pricing designed to undercut competitors' monthly subscription rates

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 10.7 — Outcome-based pricing charges per measurable outcome, so the buyer pays nothing when the tool fails. The chapter identifies this as the 2026 default for AI-agent products, because an invoice tied to completed work fits a tool whose value is the work done.

Tags: outcome-based pricing; AI-agent economics; freemium alternatives; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 22

A 2024 study found that buyers rated demographically differentiated prices as more fair when those prices were set by an algorithm than when set by a human manager, even when the dollar gaps were identical. The authors traced this result to _______.

A) the buyer's perception that algorithms are more accurate than humans B) agency and intent attribution: humans read as able to choose otherwise, inviting inferences of unfavorable intent; algorithms read as low-agency rule-followers C) the dual-entitlement principle: buyers grant algorithms the right to capture windfall profit that they deny to humans D) the decoy effect: the presence of a third pricing option shifted fairness perceptions E) category tolerance: algorithmic prices are more transparent and therefore accepted more readily

Answer: B

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 10.8 / LO 10.6 — The chapter reports a 2024 JACR study showing that buyers rate algorithmic demographic pricing as more fair because they read algorithms as having low agency and therefore infer no unfavorable intent, whereas a human manager could have chosen otherwise.

Tags: algorithmic pricing; fairness; intent attribution; agency; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 23

A brand manager wants to hold the shelf price constant but recover margin lost to ingredient inflation. Which route does our chapter describe as most easily detected by buyers?

A) Posted price increase B) Absorbing the cost C) Skimpflation D) Shrinkflation in a low-recall category like paper towels E) Shrinkflation in a high-recall category like gasoline

Answer: A

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 10.8 — A posted increase is detected immediately; shrinkflation in a low-recall category can go unnoticed for months or indefinitely; skimpflation requires taste or lab analysis to catch. The GAO data in the chapter shows 38.6 percent of paper-towel revenue concentrated in shrunk SKUs with very slow detection.

Tags: shrinkflation; skimpflation; covert price increase; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 24

A market researcher using conjoint analysis for a new line of wireless earbuds collects responses from 300 participants. The exercise reveals that buyers value a 30-hour battery life far more than a premium carrying case. The primary managerial implication is _______.

A) Set the price by adding a standard markup to the cost of the carrying case. B) Focus next R&D investment on battery life because that is what buyers are paying for. C) Introduce a decoy SKU at a higher price to make the mid-range option read as a deal. D) Reduce the price because the carrying case inflates cost without corresponding willingness to pay. E) Switch to a freemium model and make the battery upgrade a paid tier.

Answer: B

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 10.4 — Conjoint analysis produces part-worths for each attribute. The smallest part-worth (the carrying case) reveals where the firm is overspending; the largest (battery life) reveals where it can defensibly charge more. The chapter states this explicitly.

Tags: conjoint analysis; willingness to pay; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 25

A new subscription streaming service enters a market with weak digital-music infrastructure and almost no existing paying subscribers. According to the chapter's launch-timing logic (the Bass diffusion S-curve), which pricing approach is most appropriate?

A) Market skimming, because early adopters will pay a premium and the firm should harvest that surplus first. B) Market penetration, because low prices accelerate imitation-driven adoption and build the installed base before competitors arrive. C) Everyday-low-price with a membership fee, following the Costco model. D) Outcome-based pricing, because the buyer should pay only for streamed hours completed. E) Van Westendorp PSM to discover the upper-bound price the market will bear.

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 10.2 / LO 10.4 — When the imitation parameter (q) dominates the innovation parameter (p) in Bass diffusion, an installed base is the asset and penetration pricing accelerates it. The chapter uses Spotify entering a new-market context as the worked preset. Skimming is appropriate when q/p is low.

Tags: market penetration pricing; Bass diffusion; launch pricing; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Section II — Scenario / Short-Answer Items (4 items)

SA-1

A startup is launching a premium canned oat-milk latte priced at $3.49, positioned above the $2.49 shelf average. The founder argues: "We just add our production cost plus a 40 percent markup and that's the price." A second co-founder says, "We should charge what someone with a $6 Starbucks habit thinks is a fair trade-down." Identify which pricing approach each co-founder represents and explain which better fits the brand's premium positioning strategy. Use terms from our chapter in your answer.

Sample answer: The first co-founder describes cost-based pricing (specifically markup pricing), which adds a standard percentage to the unit cost and ignores what buyers actually value or what competitors charge. The second describes value-based pricing, which anchors the price to the buyer's willingness to pay relative to their current reference transaction (a $6 Starbucks habit). For a premium brand competing on the basis of quality and identity, value-based pricing is the stronger foundation because it starts from the ceiling (WTP) rather than the floor (cost). Cost-based pricing may set the price too low if WTP exceeds cost-plus, or position the brand wrong relative to the buyer's reference if the calculation overshoots WTP without qualitative support.

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 10.2, LO 10.3

Tags: cost-based vs value-based pricing; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

SA-2

A regional grocery chain wants to run a dynamic pricing pilot on prepared foods, varying the hot-bar price by 20 percent from 11 a.m. to 1 p.m. versus 2 p.m. to 4 p.m. Using the chapter's six-principle fairness audit (explicability, transparency, cooperative framing, category tolerance, brand-promise compatibility, pre-launch communication), identify the two principles this pilot is most likely to violate and explain why. Then propose one adjustment for each that would reduce the fairness risk.

Sample answer: The two most likely failure points are cooperative framing and category tolerance. Cooperative framing: A 20 percent lunch-hour premium reads as a margin extraction tool rather than a consumer benefit (e.g., a discount for visiting during the slow afternoon window). The adjustment is to reframe the pricing as an off-peak discount rather than a peak-hour surcharge — mathematically identical, but the reference point becomes the higher price and the buyer is rewarded for flexibility. Category tolerance: Hot-bar entrees in a familiar grocery are purchased several times per month at a price the customer carries in memory. A $0.80 change on a $4.00 portion will be noticed immediately and screenshot-shared. The adjustment is to limit the variation to a smaller range (10 percent or less) or to restrict the pilot to a lower-recall product category before expanding.

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 10.6, LO 10.8

Tags: dynamic pricing; fairness audit; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Reflective thinking

SA-3

A cosmetics brand is trying to set a launch price for a new overnight moisturizer. It has no historical scanner data and no product line to run conjoint analysis against. The VP of Marketing asks what method the brand should use and what four questions it asks. Answer her question, then state the two landmark prices the firm should pay most attention to from the resulting curves and explain what each represents.

Sample answer: The brand should use Van Westendorp's price-sensitivity meter (PSM), the launch-stage default when no analog data exists. The four questions are: (1) At what price is the moisturizer so cheap you would doubt its quality? (too cheap) (2) At what price is it inexpensive — a good deal? (bargain) (3) At what price is it expensive but you might still consider it? (expensive) (4) At what price is it so expensive you would not consider it? (too expensive). Aggregate responses into four cumulative curves and read the crossings. The two most important landmark prices are: (a) the optimal price point, where the "too cheap" and "too expensive" curves cross — this is the center of the acceptable range; and (b) the range margins, where the "bargain" curves cross on either side — these are the lower and upper edges of the acceptable band, below which the product reads as cheap and above which most buyers walk away.

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 10.4

Tags: Van Westendorp PSM; willingness to pay; launch pricing; AACSB: Application of knowledge

SA-4

The chapter describes two covert pricing routes a firm can take when a posted price increase would damage the brand: shrinkflation and skimpflation. Define each. Explain why shrinkflation in a paper-towel category carries lower detection risk than shrinkflation in a gasoline category. Then identify the 2026 regulatory trend the chapter cites that may shift that risk calculus.

Sample answer: Shrinkflation holds the posted shelf price constant while reducing the package quantity (ounces, count, volume), so the per-unit price rises but the shelf tag does not change. Skimpflation holds both the posted price and the package size constant while substituting lower-cost ingredients or reducing the active potency of the product; detection requires taste, performance, or lab analysis, none of which average shoppers run at purchase. Paper towels carry low detection risk because purchase frequency is low (several weeks between purchases) and the buyer rarely memorizes the sheet count or square footage. By contrast, gasoline is purchased every few days and the price-per-gallon is visible on a large roadside sign, creating very high recall and near-immediate detection of any per-unit change. The 2026 regulatory trend cited in the chapter is the Casey-Warren bill (S.3819, 2024) directing the FTC to write a rule prohibiting shrinkflation absent a commensurate price reduction, mirroring the Junk Fees Rule architecture. If enacted, prominent per-unit disclosure would become mandatory, eliminating much of the low-detection advantage in any category.

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 10.8

Tags: shrinkflation; skimpflation; regulatory risk; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

Section III — AI-Integration Items (4 items)

These items are original to this course and do not appear in any Kotler bank. They test students' ability to evaluate AI-generated pricing analysis and apply the chapter's frameworks to AI-era pricing models.

AI-1 — Identify the Pricing Approach in AI Output

A student asks an AI assistant: "Help me price my new productivity app." The AI responds:

"Based on your $4.20 unit cost (server, support amortization, billing overhead), I recommend a $9.99/month price. That gives you a 138 percent markup and aligns with the $8 to $12 band where most comparable apps on Product Hunt cluster."

Which pricing approaches does the AI's output combine, and what critical input does it fail to incorporate?

A) It combines value-based and outcome-based pricing; it fails to incorporate the cost of customer acquisition. B) It combines cost-based (138 percent markup on unit cost) and competitor-based (comparable-app band); it fails to incorporate any willingness-to-pay measurement from the actual target buyer. C) It uses purely cost-based pricing and ignores competitors entirely. D) It applies Van Westendorp PSM implicitly, using the Product Hunt band as a proxy for the optimal price point. E) It uses freemium logic by recommending a price near the trial-to-paid conversion threshold.

Answer: B

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 10.2, LO 10.4 — The AI mixes cost-based (markup on unit cost) and competitor-based (Product Hunt band) approaches. Both can inform the floor and frame, but neither is the ceiling. The ceiling requires willingness-to-pay data from the actual buyer. The AI output has no survey, no conjoint result, no PSM, and no experiment — it is anchoring off competitor prices with no evidence that those prices reflect this product's value to its specific target.

Tags: AI pricing output; pricing approaches; willingness to pay; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

AI-2 — Critique an AI-Drafted Dynamic Pricing Statement

A marketing manager asks an AI to draft a customer communication explaining a new dynamic pricing system for a chain of family-friendly indoor playgrounds. The AI produces:

"Our new smart pricing system uses real-time demand data to set admission prices. On busy Saturdays the price may be 30 percent higher than on slow Tuesday afternoons, maximizing revenue efficiency across our network. We appreciate your understanding."

Using the chapter's six-principle fairness audit, identify the two strongest violations in this communication and rewrite the opening two sentences in a way that reduces at least one violation.

Sample answer and rubric:

Violation 1 — Cooperative framing: The phrase "maximizing revenue efficiency across our network" frames the pricing change as a benefit to the firm, not to the customer. There is no story about how the buyer benefits (e.g., a lower price on slow days, reduced wait times by smoothing demand). A fairness-compliant communication must frame the price mechanism as something the buyer and firm share an interest in.

Violation 2 — Brand-promise compatibility: A family-friendly indoor playground has an implicit promise of accessible, predictable family pricing. A 30 percent Saturday surcharge contradicts the "any weekend the family can go" expectation that is core to the family venue category. This creates a breach between the positioning and the pricing mechanism.

Revised opening: "To help spread out the crowds and make your visit more enjoyable, we're introducing lower prices on Tuesday through Thursday afternoons, starting at $X per child. Weekend pricing remains $Y. We want every family to find a great time that works for their schedule."

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 10.6, LO 10.8 — The item requires students to apply the fairness audit criteria to a specific AI-generated communication, identify concrete failures, and rewrite toward a compliant framing.

Tags: AI-drafted pricing communication; fairness audit; cooperative framing; brand-promise compatibility; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Written communication

AI-3 — AI Pricing Fairness Scenario

A national pharmacy benefit manager (PBM) uses an AI system to set copay amounts for chronic-disease medications. The algorithm charges lower copays to patients in ZIP codes with median household income below $40,000 and higher copays to patients in ZIP codes above $80,000. The same drug, different price by geography used as an income proxy.

According to the 2024 JACR study discussed in our chapter, buyers are likely to rate this algorithmic pricing as _______ than the same differential set by a human pharmacist or benefits administrator.

A) less fair, because algorithms cannot be held accountable for discriminatory outcomes B) more fair, because the algorithm is seen as a low-agency rule-follower rather than a decision-maker who chose to charge more C) equally fair, because the dollar gap is what buyers evaluate, not the mechanism D) more fair only when the buyer is in the lower-income ZIP code; those in the higher-income ZIP code will rate it less fair E) less fair, because medical pricing is a category where human oversight is expected and algorithms are seen as cold

Answer: B

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 10.8 — The 2024 JACR study showed buyers rate algorithm-set demographic price gaps as more fair than human-set gaps because algorithms read as low-agency and do not invite inferences of unfavorable intent. The chapter notes, however, that prosocial pricing (senior, hardship, student rates) reverses this result because a human face is the expected fairness signal in those contexts. This item tests whether students can extend the chapter's finding to a health-care context.

Tags: algorithmic pricing fairness; intent attribution; agency; demographic pricing; AACSB: Ethical understanding; Reflective thinking

AI-4 — AI-Agent Pricing Model

A B2B software firm is debating which pricing model to use for its new AI-powered contract-review agent. Option A is a $500 per-user per-month subscription. Option B is $3.00 per contract reviewed. Option C is 1 percent of the documented cost savings per contract versus the firm's prior manual process. A procurement manager says Option A is simplest; the product team says Option B matches how AI-agent value is delivered; the CFO says Option C aligns incentives perfectly.

Which option does our chapter identify as the 2026 default for AI-agent products, and what is the key structural reason?

A) Option A (subscription), because predictable recurring revenue is the most stable model for an early-stage B2B firm. B) Option B (per-outcome), because an invoice tied to completed work fits a tool whose value is the work done, and the buyer pays nothing when the agent fails. C) Option C (gainsharing), because aligning incentives perfectly produces the highest long-run customer satisfaction. D) Option A (subscription), because it is the dominant model for enterprise software and buyers expect it. E) Option B or C equally, because both are accepted as valid AI-agent pricing models in 2026 and the choice depends only on the firm's risk tolerance.

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 10.7 — The chapter states: "This is the 2026 default for AI-agent products, because an invoice tied to completed work fits a tool whose value is the work done, not the cycles consumed." HubSpot Breeze at $0.50 per resolved ticket is the chapter's worked case. Gainsharing (Option C) is a valid model (JPMorgan-Persado), but it requires a measurable baseline and a trusted attribution method, which makes it harder to implement at launch.

Tags: outcome-based pricing; AI-agent economics; freemium alternatives; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

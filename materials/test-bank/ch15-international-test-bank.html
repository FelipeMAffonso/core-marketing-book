Chapter 15 International Marketing — Test Bank

Chapter: 15 — International Marketing

Learning Objectives (Ch15, Our Chapter)

  • LO 15.1 Explain the localization principle and describe why the cost of localizing too little is asymmetric relative to the cost of over-adapting.
  • LO 15.2 Describe the five-mode entry ladder (indirect export, direct export, licensing, franchising, joint venture, wholly-owned subsidiary) and explain how commitment, risk, control, and profit potential rise together as a firm climbs the rungs.
  • LO 15.3 Apply Hofstede's six cultural dimensions and the CAGE distance framework to identify which distance is binding for a specific product entering a specific market, and explain the fifth regulatory-and-geopolitical distance that has become load-bearing in 2026.
  • LO 15.4 Distinguish standardization from local adaptation from glocalization, and apply the three product strategies (straight extension, product adaptation, product invention) and the international pricing triad (extension, adaptation, geocentric) to a market-entry decision.
  • LO 15.5 Identify the four EPRG postures (Ethnocentric, Polycentric, Regiocentric, Geocentric) and explain how each posture shapes who inside the firm makes the foreign-market call.
  • LO 15.6 Analyze worked cases (Walmart Germany, Apple India, Euro Disney, Shanghai Disney, Liquid Death UK) using the chapter's diagnostic frameworks, and explain what each case reveals about the framework it violated or honored.
  • LO 15.7 Explain inclusive marketing as the localization principle applied to the home market's plural buyer, and apply the four-stage inclusive review to a campaign concept.

Section I — Multiple-Choice Items (22 items)

Item 1

A Tata engineer designed the Nano for a Mumbai buyer who wanted to upgrade from a motorcycle to a car. The car's engineering hit every cost and safety target. Sales fell under 10,000 units by its fifth year against a plan of 250,000 a year. The chapter argues the primary failure was _______.

A) pricing the car too high relative to a comparable Maruti-Suzuki Alto B) failing to achieve the engineering target on fuel efficiency C) building the product around the word "cheap" while the buyer was shopping for the word "arrived" D) choosing the wrong distribution channel for rural buyers E) inadequate advertising reach in the cities where the car launched

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 15.1 — The Nano failed because it solved the wrong problem. The buyer who needed to leave the rain still wanted a car that announced she had made it, not a car that announced she could not afford a real one.

Tags: Tata Nano; localization principle; social meaning; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 2

Walmart entered Germany in 1997 by purchasing two German chains and running its U.S. supercenter playbook unchanged, including everyday-low-price discounting and centralized Bentonville buying. It exited in 2006 at roughly $1 billion in charges plus an estimated 3 billion euros in cumulative losses. According to our chapter, the primary failure was _______.

A) setting retail prices too high relative to German competitors Aldi and Lidl B) over-localizing the assortment, which confused German buyers who expected a standardized U.S. format C) under-localizing the retail format, channel model, and labor practices into a market where competition law prohibited its core price weapons D) selecting the joint-venture entry mode instead of building stores from scratch E) relying on indirect exporting rather than committing capital to a direct retail presence

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 15.1 — Walmart's failure was the ethnocentric assumption that its U.S. playbook traveled. The German Cartel Office banned loss-leader pricing, the Ladenschlussgesetz capped trading hours, and labor law gave works councils authority over scheduling, stripping away every tool the format relied on.

Tags: Walmart Germany; localization principle; ethnocentric; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 3

A U.S. specialty outdoor brand wants to enter a new country. It currently sells exclusively in the United States through its own retail stores and direct-to-consumer website. It has no experience with foreign buyers, no foreign distribution relationships, and limited capital for overseas investment. Which entry mode does our chapter identify as the appropriate starting rung for a firm in this position?

A) Wholly-owned subsidiary, because it delivers the most control over brand and customer experience B) Joint venture, because shared equity gives the brand access to local knowledge immediately C) Franchising, because it puts the whole operating system in the market on local capital D) Indirect exporting, because it requires the least capital and produces the least country-specific learning risk E) Licensing, because it places the brand in foreign hands at a fixed royalty with no ongoing management commitment

Answer: D

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 15.2 — Indirect exporting is the lowest rung: the brand sells to a domestic middleman who handles the foreign sale, so capital at risk is minimal and the brand does not have to learn foreign logistics or regulation on its first move.

Tags: entry-mode ladder; indirect exporting; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 4

Moving up the five-mode entry ladder from indirect export toward a wholly-owned subsidiary, four things rise together. Which of the following correctly names all four?

A) Commitment, risk, control, and profit potential B) Speed, capital efficiency, operating simplicity, and local knowledge C) Tariff exposure, licensing revenue, joint-venture equity, and brand equity D) Advertising spend, sales-force size, inventory depth, and customer service levels E) Cultural distance, administrative friction, geographic cost, and economic gap

Answer: A

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 15.2 — The chapter's entry-ladder rule is exact: commitment, risk, control, and profit potential all climb together as the firm moves up the five rungs. Speed, capital efficiency, and operating ease fall against them.

Tags: entry-mode ladder; commitment; risk; control; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 5

Coca-Cola enters most countries by shipping syrup concentrate to local bottlers who manufacture and distribute the finished beverage under a formal arrangement in which Coca-Cola retains the trademark and formula. This entry mode is best described as _______.

A) indirect exporting through domestic export merchants B) licensing, where a foreign firm uses the trademark and recipe for a fee or royalty C) franchising, where the whole brand concept and operating system are transferred D) a joint venture producing the product in a jointly-owned local facility E) a wholly-owned subsidiary with a greenfield bottling plant

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 15.2 — Coca-Cola grants local bottlers the right to produce and sell under the Coca-Cola trademark for a royalty. The bottlers do not own the brand. This is licensing. Franchising would hand over the full operating system, which Coca-Cola does not do.

Tags: licensing; entry mode; Coca-Cola; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 6

McDonald's operates more than 40,000 stores in over 100 countries. The brand, service architecture, and supply-chain playbook stay unchanged globally, while the menu shifts country by country. This model is best described as _______.

A) full standardization, because the McDonald's brand is identical everywhere B) full local adaptation, because each country's menu is independently designed C) glocalization, where brand and operating standards are global but language, menu, and channel are rebuilt locally D) product invention, because the McAloo Tikki is a new product built for the Indian market from scratch E) an ethnocentric posture, because the U.S. headquarters controls all global decisions

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 15.4 — Glocalization keeps the brand promise and operating standards global while letting the executional layer, particularly menu and daypart, vary by market. McDonald's is the chapter's canonical glocalization case.

Tags: glocalization; standardization; adaptation; McDonald's; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 7

A U.S. consumer-goods firm is evaluating two nearby markets: the United Kingdom and Mexico. The United Kingdom shares a language and legal tradition with the United States. Mexico is geographically adjacent and bound by the USMCA trade deal. Using the CAGE framework, which distance is most likely to favor Mexico for a firm selling physical consumer goods?

A) Cultural distance, because shared language with the U.K. is the strongest market signal B) Geographic and Administrative distance, because physical proximity and the USMCA reduce the friction on moving products and clearing customs C) Economic distance, because Mexico's per-capita income is lower and the firm can charge more D) Geographic distance, because the U.K.'s location makes it closer to European markets the firm might want to enter next E) Administrative distance, because the U.K.'s common law tradition is easier for a U.S. firm to navigate

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 15.3 — CAGE asks which distance is binding. For physical consumer goods, the axes that move trucks and lower customs friction often outweigh the cultural gap. Mexico is adjacent, on the same trade bloc, and reachable by same-day truck shipment; the U.K. requires transatlantic freight and support hours that barely overlap.

Tags: CAGE distance; Administrative distance; Geographic distance; USMCA; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 8

Geert Hofstede's six cultural dimensions include power distance, individualism versus collectivism, masculinity versus femininity, uncertainty avoidance, long-term versus short-term orientation, and indulgence versus restraint. A product manager for a new retirement-savings platform notes that her target market scores very high on long-term orientation. The Hofstede score tells her _______.

A) buyers in this market prefer high-context communications and will not respond to direct advertising B) buyers defer gratification, accept a long payoff horizon, and are a relatively receptive audience for a slow-return financial product C) buyers are averse to uncertainty and will require extensive regulatory certifications before purchasing D) buyers in this market treat the individual as the primary unit of identity, so campaigns should focus on personal achievement E) buyers in a high-long-term market are already saving enough and do not need a new platform

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 15.3 — Long-term orientation captures whether a society defers gratification and accepts a long payoff horizon versus weighting present obligations and wanting results now. A high LTO score makes a retirement platform a more natural fit for the buyer's mental model.

Tags: Hofstede; long-term orientation; cultural dimensions; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 9

Shein built a $32 billion business on a U.S. customs rule that let any parcel under $800 enter duty-free. The U.S. ended the de minimis exemption for China-origin parcels in 2025, and by September Shein's U.S. sales had fallen about 8 percent year over year. This case illustrates the _______ that the chapter argues has become load-bearing in 2026.

A) cultural distance between the United States and China, which re-priced consumer sentiment B) economic distance, because Chinese manufacturing costs rose relative to U.S. alternatives C) geographic distance, because longer shipping routes increased Shein's freight costs D) regulatory-and-geopolitical fifth distance, which moved on a government timeline faster than any adaptation strategy could match E) administrative distance, which is measured only by formal trade agreements and not by single customs rules

Answer: D

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 15.3 — The regulatory-and-geopolitical fifth distance is the friction a host government can create through tariff action, platform regulation, or forced divestiture. The U.S. de minimis change was a single regulatory line that rewrote Shein's entire cost model in 2025, illustrating why a 2026 entry plan that runs CAGE without a fifth-distance scan is incomplete.

Tags: fifth distance; regulatory-and-geopolitical distance; Shein; de minimis; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 10

Apple's response to a roughly 45-percentage-point U.S. tariff gap between India-made and China-made iPhones (about 10 percent versus 55 percent) was to manufacture the iPhone 17 inside India for the September 2025 global launch, reaching about 25 percent of global iPhone output two years ahead of plan. This case illustrates _______.

A) an ethnocentric posture, because Apple imposed its U.S. supply-chain playbook on an Indian partner B) product invention, because the iPhone 17 was a new product built specifically for the Indian market C) reading the fifth regulatory-and-geopolitical distance correctly and acting on the policy timeline rather than being caught by it D) a polycentric posture, because Apple gave Indian factories autonomous control over design and features E) glocalization failure, because Apple kept the same iPhone design instead of adapting it for Indian buyers

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 15.3 — Apple read the same kind of regulatory distance that punished Shein and moved supply-chain production ahead of the tariff timeline rather than being forced into it. The product, brand, and iOS stayed standardized; the manufacturing location shifted because the tariff gap made it the right location decision.

Tags: Apple India; fifth distance; tariff; regulatory-and-geopolitical; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 11

Hindustan Unilever could not convert rural Indian buyers to a 150-rupee shampoo bottle when the daily rural wage was about 80 rupees. It invented a one-rupee single-use sachet sized to one day's wash and distributed it through Project Shakti, a village-level direct-selling network. Which of the three product strategies does the sachet represent?

A) Straight extension, because Unilever kept the same shampoo formula in a smaller package B) Product adaptation, because the brand identity stayed the same while the package size changed C) Product invention, because the sachet is a different product with a different cost structure, distribution model, and buyer relationship that the home product could not become by downsizing D) Glocalization, because the global brand promise traveled while the executional layer was rebuilt locally E) Licensing, because Hindustan Unilever transferred the formula to a local village partner for a fee

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 15.4 — Product invention is the third and most expensive strategy, used when the home product cannot serve the local buyer's job at any acceptable cost. The sachet is not a smaller bottle but a new product built from the buyer's daily cash outward, with a new distribution model the existing retail infrastructure could not supply.

Tags: product invention; sachet; bottom of pyramid; Hindustan Unilever; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 12

A product manager at a global consumer-electronics firm sets one retail price worldwide, letting the foreign shelf price fall wherever cost stacking and currency push it. According to the chapter, the primary risk of this approach is _______.

A) gray markets, where authorized distributors buy in low-price countries and resell in high-price ones, because uniform pricing eliminates the arbitrage incentive B) the foreign shelf price may be too high to serve the local buyer at all, because freight, tariffs, and distributor margins stack on top of the home factory price C) dumping liability, because a single global price is typically set below the cost of production in most markets D) transfer-pricing penalties, because the home factory is charging its foreign subsidiary below cost E) EPRG misalignment, because only a geocentric posture can legally sustain a uniform global price

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 15.4 — The price-escalation effect means that a $10 home-market product can reach $18 to $25 on a foreign shelf as freight, import tariffs, importer margin, and retail margin stack on in sequence, pricing out the buyer the entry was built for. Setting a uniform global price means allowing escalation to decide whether any buyer in each market can afford the product.

Tags: price escalation; international pricing; extension price; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 13

A U.S. apparel brand priced its standard line at $80 in the United States. A retail partner in Japan quotes the brand $140 per unit on the shelf, after adding Japanese import duty, ocean freight, and its own margin. No adaptation was made to the product. This phenomenon is called _______.

A) gray-market arbitrage, where the brand's own distribution has undercut the standard price B) dumping, where the brand is exporting at below its home-market price C) transfer pricing, where the brand is under-billing its Japanese subsidiary D) price escalation, where transportation, tariff, and distribution-layer costs stack onto the factory price before anyone marks it up for profit E) glocalization failure, where a standardized price could not compete locally

Answer: D

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 15.4 — Price escalation is the cumulative rise from the factory price to the foreign shelf caused by freight, tariffs, and extra distribution layers. The brand did nothing; the local cost structure did all the stacking.

Tags: price escalation; international pricing; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 14

Howard Perlmutter's EPRG framework identifies four organizational postures a firm takes toward foreign markets. A firm that treats each foreign country as its own world, run by locally hired managers with real autonomy who write their own operating playbooks, is best described as _______.

A) Ethnocentric, because local managers are hired to implement the home-office plan B) Geocentric, because the firm treats all markets as equal without a home preference C) Regiocentric, because the firm groups countries into regional clusters for efficiency D) Polycentric, because each country is treated as its own world with local managers running the local strategy E) Uppsala, because the firm is following the stage-by-stage commitment model

Answer: D

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 15.5 — A polycentric firm treats each country as its own world. Local managers have real autonomy and write local playbooks. Classic Nestlé and Unilever operated this way, trading scale for local fit.

Tags: EPRG; polycentric; Perlmutter; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 15

Patagonia's mission, "We're in business to save our home planet," is decided once at the global level and never adapted. The shops, repair centers, wholesale partners, and regulatory filings are all built locally in each of the 36 countries it operates in. According to the EPRG framework, Patagonia is best described as _______.

A) Ethnocentric on values, because the mission originates in the U.S. home market B) Geocentric on values and polycentric on operations, because the mission crosses borders untouched while the commerce is rebuilt locally C) Regiocentric, because Patagonia groups its 36 countries into three major trading regions D) Polycentric on values and geocentric on operations, because local teams write their own sustainability commitments while the commerce is standardized E) Fully geocentric, because no single country is the default for any element of the business

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 15.5 — Patagonia shows that one firm can carry two postures simultaneously: the mission is geocentric (no single country is the default; the value is universal), while the commerce is polycentric (local managers build the shops and partnerships market by market).

Tags: EPRG; geocentric; polycentric; Patagonia; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 16

Euro Disney opened outside Paris in 1992 with an alcohol-free policy and a food-court layout, over-standardizing the U.S. playbook into a country where wine accompanies the mid-day meal. A $1.1 billion restructuring averted collapse, and the park recovered over twenty years as Disneyland Paris after adding wine and redesigning the food. When Shanghai Disney opened in 2016, Disney applied the brief "Authentically Disney, Distinctly Chinese," adapting the castle, the food, and the ride content while keeping the IP standardized. The teaching point the chapter draws from these two parks is _______.

A) international theme parks always require joint ventures to succeed B) standardizing the brand promise and adapting the executional layer produces better outcomes than standardizing the full playbook C) cultural distance between the United States and France is larger than cultural distance between the United States and China D) the only reliable solution to a localization failure is a wholly-owned subsidiary entry mode E) the Euro Disney failure was caused by an ethnocentric EPRG posture at the parent level, not by any element of the park itself

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 15.6 — The Disney matched pair shows that the load-bearing question in a high-distance market is which elements stay standardized and which get adapted, not whether to adapt at all. Keeping the IP (brand promise) while adapting food, alcohol, and cultural references produced a durable recovery in Paris and a strong launch in Shanghai.

Tags: Euro Disney; Shanghai Disney; glocalization; per-element adaptation; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 17

Liquid Death launched in the United States in 2017 as canned still water with a heavy-metal brand voice and the tagline "Murder Your Thirst." It expanded into the United Kingdom through Tesco, Iceland, Co-op, Ocado, and Nisa. U.K. sales grew 761 percent year over year, reaching roughly £2.0 million in the year to September 2024. The firm announced its U.K. exit in early 2025. The chapter's explanation for the exit is _______.

A) the U.K. distribution partners were not the right channel for a challenger water brand B) brand voice is the layer of an offer least likely to travel, and Liquid Death's entire proposition was the brand voice, which read as imported irony in a market where tap water is the trusted free default C) Liquid Death priced its cans too low relative to premium U.K. bottled-water brands like Highland Spring D) the U.K.'s high uncertainty-avoidance score made buyers resistant to a brand with no track record E) Liquid Death failed to adapt its product formula for the U.K. buyer's taste preferences

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 15.6 — Brand voice is the layer of an offer least likely to cross a border, and for Liquid Death the voice was the whole offer. In the United States the heavy-metal posture carved a fresh slot in a crowded, bland market. In the U.K., where tap water is free, trusted, and everywhere, the same voice read as imported irony rather than fresh provocation.

Tags: Liquid Death; brand voice; localization; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 18

Dolce and Gabbana's 2018 Shanghai promotional videos showed a Chinese model eating Italian food with chopsticks while a voiceover mispronounced each dish in a tone many viewers heard as condescending. Before Chinese social media had settled its verdict, the founders' private messages calling China "dirty smelly mafia" leaked. Within 48 hours Tmall, Lane Crawford, and Net-a-Porter delisted the brand. Our chapter frames this as an instance of _______.

A) gray-market diversion, where unauthorized sellers undercut the brand on Tmall B) brand-promise incompatibility, where Dolce and Gabbana had promised lower prices than it could deliver in China C) cultural appropriation and its consequence, where the brand borrowed the surface of a culture without its authorship, and private communications contradicted the campaign's premise D) EPRG confusion, where the brand switched from a polycentric to an ethnocentric posture mid-launch E) country-of-origin liability, where Italian luxury goods carry a negative association in mainland China

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 15.7 — The chapter uses the D&G case to show cultural appropriation failure at scale: the creative borrowed Chinese visual culture without Chinese authorship, and the leaked messages showed the team's actual stance contradicted the campaign. A market-entry creative cannot survive a private message that contradicts it, because the buyer reads both at once.

Tags: inclusive marketing; cultural appropriation; Dolce and Gabbana; AACSB: Ethical understanding

Item 19

The chapter defines inclusive marketing as _______.

A) translating all marketing materials into the languages spoken by the largest demographic groups in a home market B) applying a localization audit to ensure that imagery, price, and channel are rebuilt for each underrepresented buyer segment C) building campaigns, products, and brand voice so that buyers from underrepresented groups are treated as intended buyers rather than afterthoughts, using the same logic the localization principle applies to foreign buyers D) requiring all marketing agencies to employ staff from a minimum number of demographic backgrounds E) using AI-generated synthetic models to represent a wider range of buyers in promotional materials

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 15.7 — Inclusive marketing is the localization principle applied to the plural home-market buyer. The mechanism that fails a foreign launch and the mechanism that fails a home-market campaign are the same: the program was built on assumptions the dominant audience makes invisible, and a buyer from outside that audience reads the program as excluding her.

Tags: inclusive marketing; localization principle; plural buyer; AACSB: Diverse and multicultural work environments

Item 20

A 2024 survey found that roughly 78 percent of enterprise localization workflows used AI as a first-pass translation layer. The chapter notes the quality of AI translation is asymmetric: close language pairs like English to Spanish or French match human-only output on standard quality scores, while distant pairs like English to Japanese, Korean, or Mandarin still trail human output by a wide margin. The managerial implication is _______.

A) firms should use only human translators for all markets to avoid the inconsistency risk B) AI should be used for marketing copy in every language pair because error rates are low enough to accept C) distance is no longer a tax on localization, because AI has equalized the cost across all language pairs D) AI turns language distance from a fixed tax into a per-language decision about how much human review to layer on top of the machine output E) the quality asymmetry means firms should restrict AI translation to close pairs and never use it for distant ones

Answer: D

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 15.4 — The chapter's specific finding is that AI has compressed adaptation costs but asymmetrically. The chapter frames this as converting a flat tax into a per-language review decision: close pairs get a light human pass, distant pairs get a heavier rewrite.

Tags: AI translation; localization; language distance; asymmetry; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 21

A global brand learns that a competitor in China has diverted stock purchased at a low-price market into a high-price one, undercutting the brand's authorized distributors with its own genuine product. This situation is called a _______.

A) counterfeit goods problem, where fake versions of the brand's product are being manufactured B) gray market, where branded goods are diverted from authorized distribution channels through channels the brand did not authorize C) dumping case, where the competitor is pricing below cost to win market share D) transfer-pricing violation, where the brand's own subsidiary is selling below the internal price E) straight-extension failure, where the product was sold in a market that required adaptation

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 15.4 — A gray market consists of genuine branded goods diverted from authorized channels, usually moving stock from a low-price country to a high-price one. The brand's own price gaps, created by adaptation pricing, generate the arbitrage the gray-market operator exploits.

Tags: gray market; parallel importing; international pricing; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 22

The chapter describes the transcreation gap between close and distant language pairs. Clairol launched the "Mist Stick" curling iron in Germany, where "Mist" is slang for manure. Coors put "Turn It Loose" into Spanish, where the phrase read as a phrase for diarrhea. Mercedes entered China as "Bensi," which sounded like "rush to die," before the name was changed to the auspicious "Benchi." Each of these failures shares a root cause, which is _______.

A) inadequate advertising spend to establish brand meaning before the product launched B) the brand's failure to conduct a Van Westendorp price study before entering each market C) reading the words rather than the local ear, which is exactly the miss the back-translation move is built to catch D) choosing the indirect-exporting entry mode, which left the naming decision to a middleman E) using an ethnocentric EPRG posture, which placed naming decisions in the home-country office

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 15.4 — The chapter cites these three blunders to show that literal-translation failure predates AI and recurs because internal reviewers read the words, not how a native speaker hears them. The back-translation technique, translating the output back into the source language word for word, surfaces the gap before the name or tagline reaches the market.

Tags: transcreation; back-translation; brand naming; localization; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Section II — Scenario / Short-Answer Items (4 items)

SA-1

A U.S.-based premium canned-coffee brand (annual U.S. revenue $120 million, no foreign operations) is evaluating two international markets: Japan and Mexico. The brand's managing director argues Japan is the obvious choice because the Japanese coffee culture is well-developed and aspirational brands command premium pricing. A market analyst argues Mexico is the better first move because it is geographically adjacent, covered by USMCA, and the brand has already identified a distributor network there.

Using the CAGE distance framework and the entry-mode ladder from our chapter, evaluate both arguments and recommend the better first move. Name the binding distance, identify which rung on the entry ladder the brand should occupy, and explain the primary risk on your recommended path.

Sample answer: The managing director is using cultural proximity as a proxy for market readiness, which the chapter warns is a common bias. Japan is culturally receptive to premium coffee but geographically far (ocean freight, time-zone mismatch, support hours barely overlapping), administratively complex (Japanese retail regulation, labeling requirements, cold-chain norms), and economically demanding on inventory terms. The binding distance for a $120M brand with no foreign logistics infrastructure is Geographic and Administrative together: Japan requires a commitment level the brand has not yet built.

Mexico is adjacent (same-day truck via USMCA), administrative friction is structurally lower, and the brand already has a distribution lead, which is a partial solution to the Learning gap the chapter names as the primary cost of early-rung entry. The binding distance to manage in Mexico is Cultural (Spanish copy, distinct retail channel structure, different shopper cadence), but that is addressable with a limited localization investment before any physical product ships.

Recommendation: Mexico via direct exporting through the identified distributor (rung 2 on the ladder), which commits the brand to a real market relationship while limiting capital exposure to the distributor margin and local marketing. Primary risk: the distributor holds the market relationship, so the brand learns less than it would if it ran its own outposts, and a poor distributor performance is hard to reverse quickly. The chapter's guidance is to choose the lowest rung that still produces enough learning to climb to the next one when ready. Japan is a later rung, not a first move.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 15.2, LO 15.3

Tags: CAGE distance; entry-mode ladder; direct exporting; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

SA-2

A U.S. fast-casual burger chain (250 locations, $300 million revenue) is preparing to open its first 10 international locations in South Korea over 18 months. The CEO wants to run the same menu that works in Ohio: beef burgers, chicken sandwiches, milkshakes, and thick-cut fries. The head of marketing notes that South Korea has a high uncertainty-avoidance score, strong collectivist orientation, and a premium culture around branded Western food that rewards visible quality signals. The head of supply chain notes that South Korean regulatory approval for imported beef takes about 12 months.

(a) Using the three product strategies from our chapter, identify which strategy the CEO is proposing and which strategy the data supports, and explain the difference. (b) Apply two Hofstede dimensions from the marketing head's data to describe how the brand should adapt its campaign approach. (c) Identify the regulatory issue as an example of which CAGE axis and the chapter's fifth distance.

Sample answer: (a) The CEO is proposing straight extension: the same product, the same format, no rebuild. The data supports product adaptation, because high uncertainty avoidance means South Korean buyers rely on certifications, provenance, and tangible quality signals, and an unmodified Ohio menu may not carry the documentation and premium cues the market expects. At minimum, the beef provenance needs a clean labeling story. The supply-chain issue makes straight extension structurally unavailable anyway: if imported beef requires 12 months of regulatory approval, the brand has to source locally or skip beef for the first year.

(b) High uncertainty avoidance tells the brand to lead with credentials (origin labeling, freshness marks, food-safety certifications) before any creative runs. Collectivism tells the brand to frame the dining experience around the group rather than the individual, which means meal-sharing formats, group-pricing, and imagery of friends rather than a solo diner.

(c) The 12-month beef approval is an Administrative distance issue (regulatory norms that govern which products may enter the market and on what timeline). It also illustrates the regulatory-and-geopolitical fifth distance, because it is a government rule that can move independently of any cultural or geographic factor, and it has a concrete supply-chain consequence that the brand must plan around before a single store opens.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 15.3, LO 15.4

Tags: product adaptation; Hofstede; uncertainty avoidance; collectivism; CAGE; fifth distance; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

SA-3

The Disney India streaming case offers a textbook example of the wealth-driven consumption divergence our chapter names. Disney+ Hotstar India generated about $0.59 average revenue per user in the period before the Reliance joint venture, against roughly $4.40 for Disney+ globally. Disney's exclusive digital rights to the Indian Premier League were its load-bearing asset. When Reliance outbid it for the 2023 to 2027 cricket cycle and streamed the season free, Disney lost the one differentiator that justified a paid subscription in a market where the average subscriber was worth about thirteen cents on the dollar relative to a U.S. subscriber.

Using the EPRG framework and the standardization-versus-adaptation spectrum from our chapter, explain (a) what posture mistake Disney made in India and (b) what the terms of the November 2024 joint venture reveal about the outcome when that mistake is not corrected before a local competitor forces the issue.

Sample answer: (a) Disney applied a near-ethnocentric posture to its Indian streaming strategy by assuming that a subscription model pricing the service at a single global-equivalent rate would convert Indian buyers at a meaningful rate, as it had converted buyers in the United States and Western Europe. The chapter identifies this as the Self-Reference Criterion error: judging a foreign market's willingness to pay by the home market's own consumption norms. India's per-capita income structure means that a streaming price point representing an easy discretionary spend in the United States represents a meaningful monthly budget decision for the same age cohort in India. Disney's CEO later acknowledged the company had misjudged how readily Indian viewers would convert from free to paid, which is the on-the-record admission of the bias.

(b) The November 2024 joint venture in which Reliance took 63.16 percent control of the merged JioStar entity and Disney became a 36.84 percent minority owner reveals that when an ethnocentric misread is not corrected organically, the correction is imposed by the local competitor who does understand the local buyer's economics. Disney did not exit; it ceded operating control and absorbed the renewal risk on Reliance's balance sheet, exchanging the possibility of a large profit for the certainty of a smaller share. The chapter frames this as a structured retreat: the brand earns from its IP through the venture, but the local operating decisions now belong to the local partner who should have been brought in earlier.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 15.5, LO 15.6

Tags: Disney India; EPRG; ethnocentric; polycentric; Self-Reference Criterion; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

SA-4

A mid-size U.S. beauty brand is preparing to launch a new skincare line in Mexico and Brazil simultaneously. The creative director proposes using AI-generated synthetic models to depict Latin American buyers rather than hiring local photographers and talent, arguing it will reduce the localization budget by 40 percent. The marketing director pushes back, calling it an inclusive marketing failure. The CEO asks you to adjudicate the dispute using the chapter's frameworks.

Define inclusive marketing as the chapter uses the term, explain why the creative director's proposal fails the chapter's test, and describe the four-stage review process the brand should run before any creative is approved. Then name the specific risk the chapter associates with AI-generated synthetic models used as a diversity substitute.

Sample answer: Inclusive marketing is the localization principle applied to a plural buyer within a market: it builds campaigns, products, and brand voice so that buyers from underrepresented groups are treated as intended customers rather than afterthoughts. The test is whether a buyer from the targeted community sees herself as an intended buyer (a mirror), sees into another community without distortion (a window), or is invited into the brand as a participant (a door).

The creative director's proposal fails because it spends the budget on a generator rather than on hiring underrepresented humans, while keeping the diversity claim. The chapter states directly that representation is the work itself, not the deliverable. A synthetic model in a campaign whose team and supply chain stay monocultural fails the same test the Pepsi spot did in 2017: the visual surface claims inclusion but the production process excludes the communities it depicts from authorship and pay.

The four-stage review: (1) The brief names the buyers the campaign claims to serve, including those from underrepresented groups, and names any cultural element borrowed from a community outside the team. (2) A diverse panel of four to six people from those named communities reads the creative before any media is bought. (3) A paid cultural consultant from the originating community signs off in writing or vetoes the line. (4) Revise-or-launch is the default, with the revision priced into the schedule rather than treated as a fire.

The specific AI risk the chapter names: when a brand announces synthetic models as a way to increase diversity, the practical effect is to spend the diversity budget on a generator instead of on hiring underrepresented humans, while keeping the diversity claim. The community that is supposed to be represented gets neither the authorship nor the income.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 15.7

Tags: inclusive marketing; AI-generated models; cultural appropriation; four-stage review; AACSB: Ethical understanding; Diverse and multicultural work environments

Section III — AI-Integration Items (4 items)

These items are original to this course and do not appear in any Kotler bank. They test students' ability to evaluate AI-generated international-marketing analysis and apply the chapter's frameworks to AI-era localization and entry decisions.

AI-1 — Evaluate AI Output on Entry-Mode Selection

A student asks an AI assistant to help a hypothetical mid-size U.S. outdoor-gear brand enter Japan. The AI responds:

"Japan is a great choice for your brand because it has a strong outdoor culture and buyers there pay premium prices for quality gear. I recommend starting with a joint venture with a Japanese trading company. This gives you local knowledge and shared risk. You should also localize your website into Japanese and consider adding some Japan-exclusive colorways."

Using the entry-mode ladder and the OLI paradigm from our chapter, identify the two strongest weaknesses in the AI's advice and explain what a better-structured recommendation would include.

Sample answer and rubric:

Weakness 1 — Missing OLI read: The AI jumps to a specific mode (joint venture) without asking the three OLI questions. Ownership: does this brand carry an advantage (technology, brand recognition, process) that Japanese rivals cannot match? If the brand is not meaningfully differentiated from established Japanese outdoor brands, a joint venture will not fix the competitive problem. Location: Japan offers a large premium market, but the OLI read also asks about institutional stability, cost structure, and regulatory access. Internalization: if the brand's key asset is a product formula or brand identity that a partner could learn and replicate, keeping it in-house may matter more than the capital savings from sharing. A better recommendation names all three scores before naming a mode.

Weakness 2 — No rung-sequence logic: Joint ventures are the fourth rung on the five-mode ladder. Recommending them directly assumes the brand has already accumulated the Japan-specific learning (buyer behavior, distributor relationships, regulatory norms) that usually comes from the earlier rungs. For a brand with no Asian experience, the chapter's guidance is to pick the lowest rung that still produces enough learning and margin to climb to the next one. Direct exporting through a selected Japanese distributor would be a more appropriate first rung, with a joint venture as a later move once the brand has earned the local-knowledge base.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 15.2 — The item requires students to apply the OLI paradigm and the rung-sequence logic, then identify what the AI output skipped. A better response names the OLI answers before naming the mode and explains why a joint venture is a later rung, not a first move.

Tags: OLI paradigm; entry-mode ladder; joint venture; AI output evaluation; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Analytical thinking

AI-2 — Transcreation Back-Translation Demo

A marketing student runs the following prompt on an AI assistant:

"Translate the tagline 'Unlock Your Best' into Spanish and into Japanese. Then back-translate each translation into English literally, word for word. Show me all four lines and tell me which language lost more of the original's feeling and why."

The AI returns:

  • Original: "Unlock Your Best"
  • Spanish: "Desbloquea Tu Mejor" / Back-translation: "Unblock Your Better"
  • Japanese: "Saikono Anata wo Hiraite" / Back-translation: "Open Your Best Self's Door"

Using the chapter's discussion of translation versus transcreation, and the close-pair versus distant-pair quality asymmetry in AI translation, explain (a) what the back-translation exercise reveals about Japanese, (b) why "Open Your Best Self's Door" is a problem even though it sounds poetic in English, and (c) what the student should do next with this finding.

Sample answer: (a) The back-translation of the Japanese version returned a structurally different sentence: "Open Your Best Self's Door" adds a philosophical framing ("Self's Door") that is not in the original. Japanese tends to require fuller grammatical context and often re-frames short English imperatives into longer constructions with additional implied meaning. The back-translation reveals that the AI's Japanese output carried meaning the brand never put in.

(b) The problem is not that the Japanese version sounds bad in back-translation. It sounds richer. The problem is that the brand did not choose that richness: a door, a self, an implied spiritual opening. Any of those additions could read beautifully to one Japanese buyer and overdramatic or presumptuous to another, and the brand has no idea which because it was not in the room when the translation was made. A tagline should carry the brand's intended meaning, not whatever richness a translation system adds by necessity.

(c) The student should treat the Japanese output as a first-pass draft requiring human review by a native Japanese speaker with brand context, not as a final tagline. The chapter's rule is that distant language pairs still trail human output on brand-voice copy specifically, and the correct management decision is to price a human-review layer into the Japanese localization budget. The Spanish output ("Unblock Your Better") is also not a finished tagline (grammatically correct but flat), but it is closer to the original's meaning and requires a lighter rewrite.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 15.4 — The item requires students to apply the close-pair/distant-pair quality asymmetry, interpret a real back-translation output, and draw the managerial conclusion about human review. The exercise makes visible the distance the chapter describes abstractly.

Tags: transcreation; back-translation; AI translation; close pair; distant pair; language distance; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

AI-3 — Localization Audit AI Loop

A brand manager at a U.S. energy drink company is preparing a landing page for a launch in Saudi Arabia. She pastes her existing U.S. landing page copy and a description of three product images into an AI assistant and asks:

"Audit this landing page against the seven localization checkpoints for Saudi Arabia: copy strings, currency and number formats, payment methods buyers actually use, image talent and lifestyle cues, color symbolism, the holiday calendar, and required regulatory disclosures."

The AI flags the following: (1) no Saudi Riyal pricing; (2) three images show mixed-gender social scenes; (3) the background music reference in the copy assumes Spotify availability; (4) the primary call-to-action says "Buy Now" with no alternative payment option listed.

Using the chapter's seven-checkpoint localization audit and the four CAGE axes, identify which checkpoint each AI flag corresponds to, which CAGE axis each flag maps to, and whether each requires a simple copy update or a deeper production change.

Sample answer:

Flag 1 (SAR pricing): Corresponds to the "currency and number formats" checkpoint. Maps to the Economic CAGE axis, because the buyer's local transaction medium differs from the U.S. default. Requires a simple copy update: add Riyal pricing and conform to local decimal/digit grouping norms.

Flag 2 (mixed-gender social scenes): Corresponds to the "image talent and lifestyle cues" checkpoint. Maps to the Cultural CAGE axis, because Saudi social norms govern public depiction of mixed-gender settings. Requires a deeper production change: the images must be re-shot or replaced with gender-separated scenes or individual-use imagery appropriate for the market.

Flag 3 (Spotify reference): Corresponds to the "payment methods and platform access" checkpoint. Maps to the Administrative CAGE axis, because Spotify's availability and licensing terms vary by country. The fix is a copy update replacing the platform reference with a locally available alternative (e.g., Anghami), and verification that the referenced service is licensed in Saudi Arabia.

Flag 4 (no alternative payment option): Corresponds to the "payment methods buyers actually use" checkpoint. Maps to the Economic and Administrative CAGE axes, because Saudi buyers rely heavily on Mada (local debit) and Buy Now Pay Later services like Tamara or Tabby rather than a generic "Buy Now" button pointing to a card-only checkout. Requires a deeper production change: the payment integration must add local payment rails.

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 15.3, LO 15.4 — The item requires students to map each AI audit flag to a named checkpoint and a CAGE axis, and to distinguish a surface copy fix from a production rebuild. The skill is knowing the seven checkpoints exist so the student can recognize when the AI has missed one.

Tags: localization audit; seven checkpoints; CAGE distance; AI first-pass scan; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

AI-4 — Inclusive Review AI Move

A brand manager at a U.S. sportswear company is approving a campaign that uses imagery of the Dia de los Muertos holiday to launch a new line of bright-pattern activewear targeting Hispanic buyers in the United States. The creative director says the campaign tested well internally and has already been approved by legal for trademark use of the imagery. The manager wants to run a quick AI-based inclusive review before final sign-off, using the chapter's five-question pressure test.

She pastes the following into an AI assistant:

"Here is a campaign concept: a U.S. sportswear brand will use Dia de los Muertos imagery (sugar skulls, marigolds, colored patterns) in activewear designed for Hispanic buyers in the United States. Pressure-test it on five questions and answer each honestly: (1) Does it mirror the buyers it claims to serve, or are they set dressing? (2) Who from the originating community holds authorship and is getting paid? (3) Is the cultural reference specific and accurate, or a flattened stereotype? (4) What would a panel from that community most likely flag? (5) Does that community get value back, or only the brand?"

The AI returns a confident assessment that the campaign is well-positioned for Hispanic buyers and raises no significant risks.

Using the chapter's inclusive-marketing framework and the four-stage review process, explain (a) why the AI's reassurance does not satisfy the chapter's review standard, and (b) what the brand manager should do before final approval.

Sample answer: (a) The AI's reassurance fails the chapter's standard for two reasons. First, the AI can analyze text but cannot substitute for a paid cultural consultant from the Mexican and Mexican-American community who has the standing to judge whether the use of Dia de los Muertos imagery honors authorship and adds value to the originating community. The chapter states that inclusive marketing requires a paid consultant from the originating community to sign off in writing or veto the line; an AI running the five questions is the prescan, not the review itself. Second, the fact that the creative director says the campaign "tested well internally" is exactly the condition the chapter warns against: internal approval by a room without members of the originating community in it. The D&G Shanghai case, the Pepsi Kendall Jenner spot, and the H&M and Burberry cases in the chapter all cleared internal review before their failures became public.

(b) Before final approval the brand manager should run steps 2 through 4 of the four-stage review. Step 2: Assemble a diverse panel of four to six Mexican and Mexican-American consumers who observe Dia de los Muertos as a living tradition and ask them to review the specific creative before any media is bought. Step 3: Engage a paid cultural consultant from the Mexican and Mexican-American community (not a general diversity consultant) to evaluate whether the specific imagery is used accurately, whether it reads as flattened stereotype or specific reference, and whether the community receives any value back (authorship credit, partnership, charitable give-back) rather than only the brand. Step 4: Build "revise-or-launch" into the schedule, with the revision priced in, so the brand is not in a position where rejecting the creative means losing the launch date. The five-question AI output can sharpen the brief the manager walks into those human conversations, but it cannot replace them.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 15.7 — The item requires students to apply the four-stage inclusive review, identify why an AI prescan does not satisfy the chapter's standard, and name the specific next steps. The chapter distinguishes AI as a surface-the-weakness tool from human review as the actual gate.

Tags: inclusive marketing; four-stage review; AI inclusive prescan; cultural appropriation; Dia de los Muertos; AACSB: Ethical understanding; Diverse and multicultural work environments

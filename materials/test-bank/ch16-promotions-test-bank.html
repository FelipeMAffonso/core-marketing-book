Chapter 16 Promotions and Advertising — Test Bank

Chapter: 16 — Promotions and Advertising

Learning Objectives (Ch16, Our Chapter)

  • LO 16.1 Explain why advertising's primary job is to refresh memory the brand already owns, and connect the 84% wastage finding to the build-vs-refresh distinction.
  • LO 16.2 Name the six tools in the promotional mix, sort them into paid, owned, and earned, and apply the 6M planning sequence to a communications brief.
  • LO 16.3 Apply Schwartz's five awareness levels and five sophistication stages to match the right headline to the right buyer in the right category state.
  • LO 16.4 Use the STEPPS and SUCCES frameworks to predict whether a piece of content will travel and whether its message will stick, and identify the role attention measurement plays in media planning.
  • LO 16.5 Audit a brand's distinctive assets on the Fame-by-Uniqueness grid, prescribe the correct move for each cell, and explain why the Tropicana case is the cost of destroying a top-right asset.
  • LO 16.6 Apply the elaboration likelihood model and the autopilot/pilot distinction to explain why most purchase decisions bypass a careful product argument.
  • LO 16.7 Apply the brand-to-activation budget split near 60/40 and explain the IPA data that grounds it.
  • LO 16.8 Evaluate generative AI tools in the creative process, including text-to-image, text-to-video, LoRA fine-tuning, brand-consistency limits, and the consent and watermarking obligations the FTC rule requires.

Section I — Multiple-Choice Items (20 items)

Item 1

An Ehrenberg-Bass study of 143 Australian television ads found that 40 percent of viewers recognized a recent ad they had seen, and 40 percent of those correctly named the brand. What share of the television ad spend in the study produced a branded memory?

A) 40 percent B) 32 percent C) 20 percent D) 16 percent E) 8 percent

Answer: D

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 16.1 — 40% of 40% = 16% of impressions left a correctly branded memory; the remaining 84% refreshed nothing.

Tags: advertising wastage; Ehrenberg-Bass; branded memory; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 2

The chapter argues that advertising's primary job is to _______ the memory the brand already owns, not to argue that the brand is better.

A) build B) refresh C) create D) compare E) defend

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 16.1 — The word that separates the modern view from the older USP-and-persuasion view is refresh. Advertising rehearses memory links the brand already has rather than constructing new ones from scratch.

Tags: advertising purpose; mental availability; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 3

A brand manager sorts her communication tools into three buckets: the television spot her firm paid for, the brand's own Instagram account, and the press coverage the product earned. This tripartite framework is called _______.

A) reach, frequency, and impact B) advertising, sales promotion, and public relations C) push, owned, and pull D) paid, owned, and earned E) mass, targeted, and organic

Answer: D

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 16.2 — Paid is media the firm rents, owned is what the firm runs itself, earned is what other people give the brand for free. Liquid Death's launch is the chapter's worked example: a $1,500 paid spark, the can and its TikTok as owned, and millions of earned shares.

Tags: paid owned earned; promotional mix; IMC; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 4

A communications team is building a plan. They begin by writing a single measurable objective, then name one buyer they can picture in their head, then write the one line the campaign will say. In the 6M planning sequence, they have completed the first three Ms. Which step comes immediately next?

A) Money — set the budget B) Measurement — decide which metrics to watch C) Medium — rename the broadcast channel D) Media — choose the channel mix and set the weight across paid, owned, and earned E) Market — revisit the audience definition

Answer: D

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 16.2 — The 6M sequence runs in order: Mission, Market, Message, Media, Money, Measurement. After the message is set, the next decision is which channels carry it and at what weight.

Tags: 6M planning; communications planning; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 5

Liquid Death launched in 2017 with a single $1,500 Facebook video. The video did not pitch a hydration benefit. It pitched an attitude: a tall black can, heavy-metal type, and a closing line, Murder Your Thirst. According to the Schwartz awareness ladder from the chapter, that audience was at which rung?

A) Most Aware B) Product-Aware C) Solution-Aware D) Problem-Aware E) Unaware

Answer: E

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 16.3 — In 2017 nobody believed canned water needed to exist, so the audience could not weigh a feature claim they did not know they needed. The Unaware rung is where identity and attitude win; a product argument fails there.

Tags: awareness ladder; Schwartz; Liquid Death; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 6

A buyer who has never heard of cold-brew coffee needs a different headline than a buyer who drinks it daily and is debating which brand to reorder. The Schwartz awareness ladder calls the daily drinker who already knows the brand and the price _______.

A) Unaware B) Problem-Aware C) Solution-Aware D) Product-Aware E) Most Aware

Answer: E

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 16.3 — The Most Aware buyer knows the brand and the price and needs only an offer. The daily drinker reconsidering her regular order is the chapter's clearest Most-Aware case.

Tags: awareness ladder; Schwartz; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 7

A skincare brand is entering a market where buyers have now seen dozens of competing moisturizer campaigns, all claiming "hydrates skin deeply." Buyers have stopped believing the claims. According to the Schwartz sophistication stages, the brand has entered a _____ category, and the move that cuts through is _____.

A) Stage 1; a direct claim about deep hydration B) Stage 2; a bigger claim about even deeper hydration C) Stage 3; naming a specific mechanism that explains why the product hydrates D) Stage 4; showing the brand in the buyer's life rather than listing its benefits E) Stage 5; pure identification with the buyer's identity

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 16.3 — At Stage 3, direct claims are disbelieved, so naming a mechanism, a specific reason the product works, is what restores credibility. Stage 5 is pure identification; Stage 3 is still a product-centric claim, just a mechanistic one.

Tags: sophistication stages; Schwartz; category maturity; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 8

Jonah Berger and Katherine Milkman studied approximately seven thousand New York Times articles and found that awe lifted an article's chances of reaching the Most-E-Mailed list by 30 percent, anger by 34 percent, but sadness reduced it by 16 percent. The variable that explains the pattern is _______.

A) moral valence: positive emotions help and negative emotions hurt B) physiological arousal: high-arousal emotions activate sharing and low-arousal emotions do not C) cognitive elaboration: articles that required more thinking were shared more D) narrative length: shorter articles always outperformed longer ones E) credibility: emotions tied to a named source were shared more than anonymous ones

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 16.4 — Both awe and anger are high-arousal, and both lift sharing. Sadness is low-arousal and cuts it. Moral valence does not explain the pattern, since anger is negative but helps. The operant variable is arousal.

Tags: STEPPS; emotion; sharing; virality; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 9

The STEPPS framework names six levers that predict whether content will travel. A content team checks their draft and finds it scores near zero on one lever. Which lever, if at zero, would most directly indicate that the audience has no reason to pass the content along to anyone else?

A) Social currency — the content does not make the sharer look good B) Triggers — the content has no environmental cue linking it to daily life C) Emotion — the content produces no high-arousal feeling D) Public visibility — the behavior the content promotes cannot be seen E) Practical value — the content offers no useful information the sharer can gift

Answer: A

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 16.4 — Social currency is the STEPPS lever tied to self-presentation. If sharing the content makes no one look interesting or well-informed, the social incentive to forward it disappears. That is the most proximate lever for whether someone has a reason to share at all, distinct from whether they feel something or learn something from it.

Tags: STEPPS; social currency; content sharing; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 10

The SUCCES framework from Made to Stick identifies six traits that predict whether a message will be remembered. Which single trait runs through all the others because a message that lacks it will die at every step of the audience's mind?

A) Unexpected B) Concrete C) Credible D) Simple E) Emotional

Answer: D

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 16.4 — Simple is the load-bearing trait that runs through all the others: a complicated core idea undermines Unexpected (because the surprise cannot land without a clear subject), Concrete (because abstraction hides the idea), Credible (because excess complexity reads as hedging), Emotional (because no one cares about a blur), and Story (because a tangled plot loses the audience).

Tags: SUCCES; Heath and Heath; message stickiness; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 11

The chapter reports that by late 2025, the IAB and MRC had published formal Attention Measurement Guidelines. A media planner using Lumen Research's webcam panel data learns that only 30 percent of technically viewable digital display impressions were actually viewed. If her plan called for 200,000 viewable impressions, how many impressions she actually paid for are likely to have been truly viewed?

A) 200,000 B) 140,000 C) 100,000 D) 60,000 E) 30,000

Answer: D

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 16.4 — 200,000 x 0.30 = 60,000. Viewable does not equal viewed. A brand buying 200,000 viewable impressions buys roughly 60,000 truly viewed ones on the Lumen panel baseline.

Tags: attention measurement; viewability; IAB/MRC; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 12

Pepsi paid approximately $35 million to redesign Tropicana's packaging in 2009, replacing the orange-with-a-straw image with a generic glass of orange juice. Within two months, Tropicana's sales fell about 20 percent, and the company reverted. The chapter identifies this as a failure to protect which type of brand asset?

A) A bottom-left asset that carried low fame and low uniqueness B) A bottom-right category code that was generic across the juice category C) A top-left asset that needed more investment before buyers recognized it D) A top-right asset that carried high fame and high uniqueness E) A co-branded asset shared with Florida's Natural

Answer: D

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 16.5 — The orange-with-a-straw image was a 13-year distinctive asset that buyers matched against at the shelf in half a second. It held high Fame (most buyers tied it to Tropicana) and high Uniqueness (no competitor shared it). The move for top-right assets is to invest and never retire them.

Tags: distinctive asset; Tropicana; Fame-Uniqueness grid; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 13

Romaniuk's Distinctive Asset Grid plots assets on Fame and Uniqueness. A brand discovers that its green color is recognized by many buyers but is shared with several other brands in the natural-foods category. In the grid, this asset sits in the _____ cell, and the prescribed move is _____.

A) top-right; invest in it and never retire it B) top-left; amplify it through paid reach until fame catches up C) bottom-left; kill it, because nobody recognizes or owns it D) bottom-right; decide whether to break from the category code or spend elsewhere E) center; combine it with a second asset to raise its uniqueness score

Answer: D

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 16.5 — High Fame and low Uniqueness defines a category code. Green in natural foods is recognized by many but owned by none. The grid's bottom-right cell says: decide whether to differentiate from the code (as Trader Joe's does by refusing the standard supermarket palette) or redirect that spend to an asset the brand can own.

Tags: distinctive asset; Fame-Uniqueness grid; category codes; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 14

A brand audits a candidate distinctive asset and finds that only a small share of category buyers name the right brand when shown the asset, but those who do name no other brand. The asset sits in the _____ cell of the grid, and the recommended move is _____.

A) bottom-right; decide whether to break from the category code B) top-right; invest and never retire it C) top-left; amplify through paid reach until Fame catches up D) bottom-left; kill it because the spend returns nothing E) top-right; retire it and replace it with a fresher execution

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 16.5 — Low Fame and high Uniqueness is top-left: the few who see it get it right, so the asset is distinctive but insufficiently known. The move is to amplify it, because three to four years of consistent paid reach typically migrates a top-left asset into the top-right.

Tags: distinctive asset; Fame-Uniqueness grid; Romaniuk; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 15

The elaboration likelihood model holds that persuasion runs through two routes. When a buyer is not motivated or able to think carefully about an ad — which describes most routine purchase situations — persuasion relies on _______.

A) the central route, where the argument's logical quality determines acceptance B) the peripheral route, where surface cues like repetition, credibility, and feeling carry the message C) the dual route, where both argument quality and surface cues are evaluated together D) the cognitive route, where the buyer processes explicit and implicit value simultaneously E) the rational route, where the buyer compares the product to its closest competitor

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 16.6 — The peripheral route operates when the buyer has low motivation or ability to process the central argument. Surface cues — how many times she has seen the brand, whether the spokesperson seems credible, whether the feeling the ad creates is pleasant — carry the persuasion. Most routine purchases run on this route.

Tags: elaboration likelihood model; peripheral route; Petty and Cacioppo; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 16

A brand-briefing framework from 2013 renames Kahneman's System 1 as the "autopilot" and System 2 as the "pilot." According to this framework, for a $4 shampoo purchase, a buyer's autopilot reads the brand's creative in roughly _______ and makes the purchase choice without engaging the pilot.

A) one to two seconds B) five to ten seconds C) fifteen to twenty seconds D) thirty seconds E) two to three minutes

Answer: A

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 16.6 — The autopilot reads creative through peripheral perception in about one to two seconds — the attention window a shelf glance or a social-feed scroll actually provides. For a small routine purchase, the pilot is too expensive to engage, so the autopilot makes the match.

Tags: autopilot pilot; System 1 System 2; Kahneman; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 17

IPA Effectiveness data analyzed by Binet and Davis finds that the size of the absolute media budget explains approximately _____ of the variation in long-term profit, while ROI explains the remaining share. The practical implication is that chasing ROI alone tends to _____ brand investment.

A) 50 percent; eliminate B) 89 percent; under-invest in C) 11 percent; over-invest in D) 75 percent; overestimate E) 33 percent; correctly calibrate

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 16.7 — The IPA data shows the absolute budget predicts ~89% of long-term profit variation and ROI explains ~11%, yet most CMOs polled ranked ROI as the more important driver. Teams that optimize the ratio tend to shrink the absolute return by cutting enough reach to fall below the brand-building floor.

Tags: IPA effectiveness; ROI vs budget; brand-to-activation split; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 18

The chapter recommends a brand-to-activation spending split near _____ for most packaged goods, adjusted for category. "Brand" in this context refers to spending aimed at _____, while "activation" refers to spending aimed at converting buyers ready to buy now.

A) 40/60; building new product features B) 50/50; building mental and physical availability over time C) 60/40; building mental availability and memory links over time D) 70/30; managing the distribution channel and retail partners E) 80/20; defending existing buyers from competitor switching

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 16.7 — Binet and Field's IPA analysis anchors the split near 60/40 for FMCG (74/26 for some categories, 46/54 for B2B). Brand spend builds the long-term memory-link network; activation converts buyers already ready. A plan that drifts from this toward pure activation loses the compounding return the brand side produces over two or more years.

Tags: brand-to-activation; IPA; Binet and Field; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 19

A junior brand manager uses a text-to-image model and types the unbranded prompt "artisan coffee bag in a cozy kitchen." The model returns a generic dark-green bag with serif lettering that her brand does not use. According to the chapter, why did this happen, and what is the fix?

A) The model misunderstood the prompt; the fix is to use a different model. B) The model leaned toward the training-data average; the fix is to attach the brand's reference images or train a LoRA on its own visuals. C) The model lacked sufficient compute; the fix is to use a higher-resolution tier. D) The prompt was too short; the fix is to add five to ten additional descriptive words. E) The model is biased toward green; the fix is to specify the brand's exact hex color in the prompt.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 16.8 — Every generative model defaults to the statistical center of its training data, so a raw prompt returns something that looks like the most common visual in the category. The Heinz case illustrates this: "ketchup bottle in a Renaissance painting" returned Heinz-shaped bottles because Heinz dominates the training data. The fix is a visual anchor: reference images attached to every prompt, or a LoRA fine-tuned on the brand's own image set.

Tags: text-to-image; LoRA; brand consistency; generative AI; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 20

The FTC's October 2024 rule on AI-generated voice requires which of the following before a marketer may clone a real person's voice and use it in advertising?

A) A disclosure only, displayed in 12-point type at the end of the ad B) Written consent from the person whose voice is being cloned, plus an audible disclosure on the output C) Registration with the FTC's AI Voice Registry and a six-week waiting period D) A license from the relevant performing-rights organization E) No specific consent, because a synthetic voice is legally distinct from the original

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 16.8 — The chapter cites the FTC's October 2024 rule: written consent is required before cloning a real person's voice, and the finished ad must carry an audible disclosure. This is the legal gate on top of the brand-consistency challenge, distinct from the copyright questions that are still unsettled for image generation.

Tags: synthetic voice; FTC rule; AI consent; AACSB: Ethical understanding

Section II — Scenario / Short-Answer Items (4 items)

SA-1

A regional energy-drink brand has been on the market for four years and carries a yellow lightning-bolt logo. At launch, the brand ran energetic ads showing the bolt. Over time, in-house brand-tracking surveys show the team rating the bolt's Fame at 80 and its Uniqueness at 85. An outside category-buyer panel comes back with Fame at 22 and Uniqueness at 66. The marketing director wants to retire the bolt and launch a new visual identity.

Using the chapter's distinctive-asset framework, (a) identify which cell the bolt actually sits in on the outside-panel data, (b) explain the gap between the in-house and panel estimates, and (c) advise the marketing director on the correct move.

Sample answer: (a) On the outside panel, Fame is 22 and Uniqueness is 66, which places the bolt in the top-left cell of the Fame-by-Uniqueness grid: low Fame, high Uniqueness. Buyers who see it tend to name only this brand, so the asset is distinctive, but most category buyers do not yet recognize it. (b) The chapter reports that in-house teams overestimate their own Fame and Uniqueness by 60 to 80 percent against what category buyers actually return, and the error runs largest on designs the team feels closest to. The bolt has been prominent in every internal meeting for four years, so the team's panel of one is maximally misleading. (c) The correct move for a top-left asset is to amplify it through paid reach until Fame catches up, not to retire it. Retiring a top-left asset resets the Uniqueness advantage the brand already earned. Three to four years of consistent media typically migrates a top-left asset into the top-right. The marketing director should hold the bolt, increase reach behind it, and re-audit in 12 months.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 16.5

Tags: distinctive asset; Fame-Uniqueness grid; Romaniuk; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Reflective thinking

SA-2

A challenger sparkling-water brand is launching nationally in 2026. Its research team runs the Schwartz awareness and sophistication maps on the category. They find the target buyer is at the Solution-Aware rung (she wants lighter hydration without soda's sugar but has not connected the brand to that solution) and the sparkling-water category is at Stage 3 sophistication (direct claims about bubbles and purity are disbelieved; every brand says the same things).

Using both the awareness rung and the sophistication stage, write the headline rule the brand should follow for its launch campaign. Then explain why a price-off offer would fail at this moment even if the brand has a genuine price advantage over competitors.

Sample answer: The headline rule for Solution-Aware buyers in a Stage-3 category is to bridge the buyer's desired outcome to a specific, credible mechanism the brand alone names. The buyer knows she wants lighter hydration without sugar; she does not yet know this brand delivers it. And because every direct claim is disbelieved at Stage 3, the headline cannot lead with "naturally sparkling" or "zero sugar" (those are already said by five competitors). The winning move is a mechanism line: a specific, verifiable reason why this brand delivers the outcome. For example, naming a distinctive water source, a mineral profile, or a unique carbonation process turns the claim from assertion into explanation. A price-off offer would fail because the buyer is not yet at the rung where price is the deciding variable. She is Solution-Aware, meaning she has not yet accepted any brand as the answer to her outcome goal. A coupon or a buy-one-get-one does not answer the question she is holding, which is whether this brand solves the solution she is already seeking. Price-based activation belongs at the Most-Aware rung, where the buyer has already decided the brand works and price is the final nudge toward purchase.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 16.3

Tags: Schwartz; awareness ladder; sophistication stages; sales promotion timing; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

SA-3

Duolingo's TikTok grew past 16.8 million followers by 2026, with individual videos clearing 20 million views. Its ad spend stayed well below category peers. The chapter attributes this performance to one distinctive asset, the green owl Duo, and to content that travels via the STEPPS framework.

Identify the two STEPPS levers the green-owl content most consistently pulls, and explain the mechanism behind each. Then identify the one STEPPS lever the owl's content is least likely to pull by design, and explain why leaving that lever unengaged is actually correct strategy for Duolingo's goals.

Sample answer: The two strongest STEPPS levers in the Duolingo owl's TikTok strategy are Social Currency and Emotion. Social Currency: the owl appears in costume, dancing or menacing users who have skipped a lesson, in a register of mock-aggression that is recognizable to Duolingo users as an inside joke. Sharing a video of the owl menacing you signals that you are in on the joke, which is a classic identity-based social-currency mechanism. Emotion: the content is high-arousal, landing between amusement and low-level anxiety ("what if the owl actually gets me"), both of which are sharing-positive emotions in Berger and Milkman's data. The STEPPS lever the owl's content least pulls by design is Practical Value, the "news you can use" lever where content travels because the sharer is giving a gift of useful information. A video of a cartoon owl in a Halloween costume contains no actionable tip. Leaving this lever unpulled is correct strategy because Duolingo's goal is brand-memory rehearsal at scale, not tutorial distribution. Practical-value content (e.g., a grammar tip) would make the brand look like a textbook, not a character to follow. The decision to maximize Social Currency and Emotion while leaving Practical Value low is a deliberate bet on entertainment over instruction, and the follower count validates the bet.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 16.4

Tags: STEPPS; Duolingo; distinctive asset; earned media; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

SA-4

Integrated marketing communications (IMC) starts from the buyer's day, not from the channels the firm is used to buying. The chapter names this the "outside-in" move.

A team is planning a launch for a new premium oat-milk creamer. The team proposes buying 60-second radio spots and weekly newspaper inserts because the brand's legacy dairy line has used those channels for twenty years. Apply the IMC outside-in principle to challenge this media plan. Name the two pieces of information the team needs before the media decision is made, and explain where in the 6M sequence those decisions belong.

Sample answer: The outside-in principle says the media plan should start with the buyer's actual day, tracing where she physically and digitally meets the category across a typical week, and only then map the channels to those contact points. The team's legacy plan (radio + newspaper) starts inside-out: it begins with channels the firm is comfortable buying and trusts that the buyer will be there. Two pieces of information the team needs before any media decision: (1) a mapped week for the target buyer — what she opens, scrolls, passes, and listens to at each point in a typical day, specifically in relation to the morning coffee ritual where the creamer category is activated; and (2) the paid/owned/earned split that can reach her at each of those points, including the brand's owned channels (its own social accounts, its packaging) and the earned layer (influencer coffee content, recipe communities). In the 6M sequence, both pieces belong at the Media step, the fourth M, which immediately follows the Message step. The budget (Money, fifth M) cannot be set correctly until the channels are chosen for the buyer's actual day rather than the brand's familiar history. The proposed radio-and-newspaper plan may turn out to be partially correct if the buyer actually listens to morning radio while making coffee, but that is an empirical question the outside-in audit answers, not an assumption the plan should carry.

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 16.2

Tags: IMC; 6M planning; outside-in; media planning; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Reflective thinking

Section III — AI-Integration Items (4 items)

These items are original to this course and do not appear in any Kotler bank. They test students' ability to evaluate AI-generated promotional work, apply the chapter's frameworks to AI-era creative decisions, and identify where human judgment is still required.

AI-1 — Identify the Awareness Rung in AI-Generated Copy

A student uses an AI assistant to write three headlines for a new reusable water bottle brand launching on Instagram. The AI returns:

Option A: "The last water bottle you will ever need — BPA-free, insulated, and built for life." Option B: "Upgrade your hydration. 40% off this week only." Option C: "Most people replace their bottle every six months and never think about where it ends up."

Using the Schwartz awareness ladder, identify which awareness rung each headline is aimed at, and explain which one would work best for a brand-new Instagram audience who has never seen the brand before.

A) A is Most Aware, B is Product-Aware, C is Unaware; A works best for a new audience. B) A is Product-Aware, B is Most Aware, C is Problem-Aware; C works best for a new audience. C) A is Most Aware, B is Most Aware, C is Solution-Aware; B works best because it drives immediate purchase. D) A is Solution-Aware, B is Product-Aware, C is Problem-Aware; B works best because it combines an offer with the solution. E) A is Problem-Aware, B is Most-Aware, C is Unaware; A works best because it defines the solution clearly.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 16.3, LO 16.8 — Option A names what the product does and argues it is the best version, which assumes the buyer already knows the category and is comparing products, a Product-Aware rung. Option B is a price-and-availability hook that works only when the buyer already trusts the brand and is deciding whether to buy now, a Most-Aware rung. Option C opens by naming a problem the buyer may not have consciously articulated, which is the Problem-Aware rung. For a new Instagram audience unfamiliar with the brand, Option C is correct: it meets the buyer where she likely is and gives her a reason to care before it asks her to consider a product.

Tags: AI-generated copy; awareness ladder; Schwartz; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

AI-2 — Audit an AI-Generated 6M Communications Plan

A student asks an AI to draft a 6M communications plan for a campus food-delivery startup. The AI produces:

"Mission: raise brand awareness. Market: college students ages 18-24. Message: fast, affordable food delivery, right to your door. Media: Instagram, TikTok, and email list. Money: allocate 60% to paid social, 30% to influencers, 10% to email. Measurement: track follower count and email open rate."

Identify two specific weaknesses in this AI-generated plan using the chapter's 6M framework, and for each weakness explain what a stronger version would look like.

Sample answer and rubric:

Weakness 1 — Mission: "Raise brand awareness" is not an actionable objective because it has neither a quantitative benchmark nor a temporal benchmark. A stronger Mission would read: "Reach 40 percent unaided brand awareness among students within one mile of campus who order food online at least once per week, within the first eight weeks of launch." The objective is now measurable and time-bound, which lets the team evaluate whether the campaign worked.

Weakness 2 — Measurement: Follower count and email open rate are activity metrics, not business-outcome metrics. They measure whether content was seen, not whether it moved the objective the Mission named. A stronger Measurement would watch two numbers tied to the Mission: (1) unaided awareness in the target geography, measured by a brief post-campaign survey against the pre-launch baseline, and (2) first-order conversion from new accounts, measured by the share of new app installs in the campaign window that placed at least one order. Both metrics are readable against the Mission's quantitative and temporal benchmarks.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 16.2, LO 16.8 — The item requires students to apply the 6M framework to evaluate AI output, not just accept it. Mission weakness is the most common AI-planning failure: models produce goal statements without measurable benchmarks because the prompt did not specify them. Measurement weakness follows directly: without a real objective, the model defaults to vanity metrics.

AI-3 — Generative Creative and Brand Consistency

A marketing intern uses a text-to-image model to produce ten product-shot variants for a hot-sauce brand. The brand's existing identity uses a distinctive red-and-black label with a hand-drawn chili illustration. The intern types the prompt: "Hot sauce bottle on a kitchen counter, dramatic lighting." All ten variants return bottles in different colors with clean, modern label designs.

According to the chapter, what caused this output, and what are the two specific actions the intern should take before running the next batch to get brand-consistent results?

A) The model chose random colors; the intern should specify colors in the prompt and remove the word "dramatic." B) The model defaulted to its training-data average, which is populated by the most common hot-sauce visual styles; the intern should (1) attach the brand's own reference photos to every prompt and (2) consider training a LoRA on the brand's image set for consistent brand-style outputs. C) The lighting specification overrode the model's color logic; the intern should remove the lighting instruction and rerun with a plain background. D) The model lacks a hot-sauce training set; the intern should switch to a different model with more food-packaging data. E) The prompt needed more words; the intern should expand it to 50 words and include every attribute of the brand's label design in the text.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 16.8 — The chapter states that every generative model leans toward the statistical center of its training data, and a plain prompt gives generic output regardless of what was typed. The Heinz case makes the same point: the most common visual in the category fills the frame. Reference images condition the model on the specific brand's look; a LoRA fine-tuned on thirty to a hundred brand images holds that consistency across a full production batch.

Tags: text-to-image; LoRA; brand consistency; generative AI; AACSB: Application of knowledge

AI-4 — AI Creative Tools and the Human Judgment Gate

A direct-to-consumer candle brand is preparing a social-media launch. A team member uses an AI model to generate fifty image variants showing candles in lifestyle settings, runs them through a STEPPS scorer, and proposes posting the fifteen that score highest on social currency and emotion. A second team member objects: "The model never looked at whether our distinctive asset is visible in any of these images."

According to the chapter's one-page promo audit (the Job Aid), which step does the second team member's objection name, and why does this step have to happen before any image ships, regardless of the STEPPS score?

A) Step 1 of the audit (awareness rung) — because the model cannot assess the buyer's knowledge state. B) Step 3 of the audit (STEPPS score) — because a human must verify the STEPPS output rather than trusting AI scoring. C) Step 5 of the audit (distinctive asset) — because a STEPPS score measures whether content will travel, not whether the brand will be remembered when it arrives. D) Step 6 of the audit (build or refresh) — because the model generates new assets by default, which defaults to build when refresh is the correct strategy. E) Step 8 of the audit (generative work) — because the model did not watermark or sign its output.

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 16.1, LO 16.5, LO 16.8 — The chapter's Job Aid makes this distinction explicit. Step 5 asks whether the brand's one distinctive asset is on screen before the product appears and stays on screen until the brand registers. A STEPPS score measures the content's sharing potential, not its brand-attribution outcome. The Ehrenberg-Bass 84% wastage finding is precisely this failure: content that traveled but refreshed no correctly branded memory. The AI can optimize for virality; only the human who knows which asset the brand owns can verify that the brand shows up.

Tags: promo audit; distinctive asset; AI creative; STEPPS; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

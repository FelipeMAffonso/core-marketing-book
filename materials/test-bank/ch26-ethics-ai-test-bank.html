Learning Objectives (Ch26, Our Chapter)

  • LO 26.1 Explain why marketing ethics is structural rather than aspirational and describe the four-axis accountability test a 2026 marketing decision must pass.
  • LO 26.2 Identify the three operative privacy regimes (GDPR, CCPA/CPRA, EU AI Act) and state the key obligation each places on a marketer processing personal data.
  • LO 26.3 Apply Brignull's 12-pattern catalog and the three-question brightline to distinguish a legitimate nudge from a dark pattern.
  • LO 26.4 Audit an AI vendor using the model-card, constitution, and responsible-scaling-policy procurement gate; apply the three fairness criteria (demographic parity, equalized odds, calibration) to a marketing decision; and explain the SAFE framework as the chapter's organizational spine.
  • LO 26.5 Describe the seven-step human-fallback architecture for an AI customer-facing deployment and explain why the Klarna case makes each step mandatory.
  • LO 26.6 Apply the SAFE spine to a brand's one-page AI-use policy; evaluate greenwashing claims against the FTC Green Guides and EU ECGT Directive; and identify the right-of-publicity exposure created by an AI-generated spokesperson or voice clone.
  • LO 26.7 Recognize the IP questions an AI-generated asset carries (authorship, training-data infringement) and identify the exposure the Bartz v. Anthropic settlement and the NYT v. OpenAI litigation create.
  • LO 26.8 Name the six structural ethical failure modes from the 2018-2026 enforcement record and pair each with its pre-launch fix.

Item 13

Section II — Scenario / Short-Answer Items (4 items)

SA-1

A mid-size fitness-apparel brand is launching an AI chatbot that handles returns, product recommendations, and sizing questions. Before launch the legal team asks whether it needs a "human in the loop." The product team argues it just needs to log every decision and review logs monthly. Using the chapter's seven-step parallel-track architecture, explain what "human in the loop" actually requires — and specifically what the Klarna case says about monthly log review as a substitute.

Sample answer: The seven steps are: (1) calibrated confidence scoring so the bot knows when it does not know; (2) risk-tier routing that sends low-confidence or high-stakes cases to a human queue automatically; (3) tamper-evident decision logging; (4) continuous drift monitoring; (5) a staffed reviewer queue with a defined SLA; (6) per-path CSAT/NPS instrumentation that discriminates AI-handled from human-escalated cases; and (7) a 60-minute kill switch. Monthly log review fails steps 4, 5, and 6. The Klarna case shows why: Klarna's dashboards tracked metrics the automated path moved cleanly, such as resolution rate and handle time, while the satisfaction on the hardest cases deteriorated invisibly because no one was measuring per-path CSAT/NPS in near real time. By the time a monthly review surfaced the problem, the cost had compounded. GDPR Article 22 also makes the human-fallback architecture legally non-optional for any EU data subject subjected to a consequential automated decision.

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 26.5

Tags: human-fallback; Klarna; seven-step architecture; GDPR Article 22; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

SA-2

A sustainable clothing startup uses three marketing claims in its fall campaign: (a) "Our hoodies are made from 100% recycled ocean plastic, certified by Ocean Protocol." (b) "We are carbon neutral." (c) "The most eco-conscious brand in activewear." Using the FTC Green Guides and the EU ECGT Directive — both cited in our chapter — classify each claim as substantiated-defensible, unsubstantiated, or banned-generic, and explain your classification.

Sample answer: (a) Substantiated-defensible. The claim is specific (100% recycled ocean plastic), quantified, and backed by a named certification scheme. Both the FTC Green Guides and the ECGT Directive permit specific, substantiated, and certified claims. (b) Unsubstantiated or banned depending on how it is earned. If "carbon neutral" rests on purchasing carbon offsets rather than actual emissions reductions, the ECGT Directive specifically bans offset-based carbon-neutral claims from September 27, 2026 onward. In the US, the FTC Green Guides require that carbon-offset claims be backed by competent, reliable evidence and that the offsets not have already been claimed by another entity. Without disclosing the offset methodology and its verification, this claim is unsubstantiated in the US and banned in the EU after September 2026. (c) Banned-generic. "Most eco-conscious" is a comparative superlative without a specified dimension, methodology, or verification. The FTC Green Guides prohibit unqualified general claims, and the ECGT bans claims like "eco-friendly" and "eco-conscious" that lack verifiable evidence. This claim violates both regimes.

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 26.6

Tags: greenwashing; FTC Green Guides; ECGT; substantiation; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Ethical understanding and reasoning

SA-3

Cambridge Analytica obtained data on up to 87 million Facebook users through a personality-quiz app that harvested each installer's network via the Open Graph API. The chapter analyzes this case through Nissenbaum's contextual-integrity framework. Using contextual integrity's five parameters, identify which ones shifted from what the original quiz consent authorized, and state one structural fix that would have prevented the breach.

Sample answer: Contextual integrity names five parameters of every information flow: subject, sender, recipient, attribute, and transmission principle. The quiz-app consent authorized: subject = installer; sender = installer via quiz app; recipient = quiz-app developer; attribute = quiz responses; transmission principle = voluntary disclosure for quiz personalization. Four of the five parameters shifted in the Cambridge Analytica flow: (1) Recipient shifted from the quiz-app developer to a political-campaign data broker. (2) Attribute shifted from quiz responses to a 5,000-trait psychographic profile built by combining quiz responses with scraped network data. (3) Transmission principle shifted from voluntary disclosure for quiz personalization to indirect re-sale for partisan electoral targeting. (4) Effectively the subject expanded from the consenting installer to 87 million friends who had consented to nothing. The structural fix is an Article 4(11)-compliant consent architecture that requires affirmative, specific, informed consent for each downstream data use. The Open Graph API that let third-party apps harvest friends' data without those friends' consent was the structural gap; Facebook's 2018-2019 API restrictions closed it after the fact. Running a contextual-integrity check across the five parameters before launch would have flagged the friend-harvest path before it operated for five years.

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 26.2, LO 26.8

Tags: Cambridge Analytica; contextual integrity; GDPR consent; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Reflective thinking

SA-4

The chapter describes how Trader Joe's refusal to collect customer data eliminates its algorithmic-fairness exposure. Explain the logic of this argument — what specific fairness problem does the absence of the model solve? Then name one business consequence of this design choice, positive and negative, using numbers from the chapter.

Sample answer: The logic is the chapter's load-bearing statement: "a model that does not exist cannot exhibit disparate impact." Every fairness criterion the chapter covers — demographic parity, equalized odds, and calibration — presupposes that a predictive model is running and making differential decisions across groups. Trader Joe's runs no loyalty program, no customer file, no behavioral segmentation, no recommendation engine, no algorithmic pricing. Without a model, there is no disparate-impact claim a regulator or plaintiff can bring, because the structural precondition for the claim is absent. The positive business consequence: roughly $2,100 in revenue per square foot per year, three to four times the supermarket average, and approximately $16.5 billion in total revenue across 631 US stores as of January 2026, without the compliance and audit cost the Section 26.4 gates impose. The negative consequence: without algorithmic assortment optimization and targeted promotions, the chain can only run about 4,000 SKUs versus the industry average of roughly 50,000 SKUs, which limits category breadth and forces all assortment decisions through a centralized buying team running Coulombe's Four Tests rather than a data-driven optimization system.

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 26.4, LO 26.8

Tags: Trader Joe's; algorithmic fairness; disparate impact; data minimalism; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

AI-1 — Apply the SAFE Spine to a Draft AI-Use Policy

A student drafts the following one-page AI-use policy for a DTC supplement brand:

"We use AI to help write copy, answer customer questions, and personalize emails. We are committed to using AI responsibly. Our team reviews AI outputs before they go live. We respect our customers' privacy."

Using the SAFE spine — Safeguards, Accountability, Fairness, Empowerment — identify one specific gap in each letter and state the chapter's fix for each gap.

A) The policy covers all four SAFE letters adequately because it mentions privacy, output review, and responsible use. B) The policy's only gap is Accountability: it does not name a specific AI vendor or model card. C) All four letters have gaps: Safeguards has no consent standard named, Accountability has no vendor-procurement gate or bias-audit commitment, Fairness has no criterion identified for algorithmic decisions, and Empowerment has no human-fallback or kill-switch provision. D) The policy satisfies Safeguards (mentions privacy) and Empowerment (team reviews) but is weak only on Accountability and Fairness. E) The policy's primary gap is Fairness: it does not specify which fairness criterion the brand uses for email personalization.

Answer: C

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 26.4, LO 26.6 — The chapter's standard for each SAFE letter is specific: Safeguards means GDPR/CCPA-compliant consent and data-minimization commitments, not a privacy sentence. Accountability means the three-document procurement gate and a named bias-audit schedule. Fairness means the chosen fairness criterion and a documented trade-off. Empowerment means the seven-step fallback architecture and a kill-switch provision. The sample policy hits none of these with specificity and is therefore a "values poster" in every letter.

Tags: SAFE framework; AI-use policy; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Reflective thinking

AI-2 — Audit a Campaign for a Dark Pattern

A streaming service uses the following subscription-cancellation flow: (1) a retention offer page that loads automatically when "Cancel" is clicked; (2) a single "Continue cancelling" link in small gray text at the bottom; (3) a second pop-up asking "Are you sure?" with the "Stay subscribed" button highlighted in color and the "Cancel anyway" button in plain text. Using the chapter's three-question brightline and Brignull's dark-pattern catalog, identify the specific dark pattern type this flow uses and explain the FTC enforcement precedent that prices this design risk.

Sample answer: This flow uses a Roach Motel (also cataloged as obstruction and confirmshaming). The Roach Motel pattern makes it easy to get into a subscription and hard to exit. Applying the three-question brightline: (1) Would the user object if they understood the design? Yes, because the interface is engineered to create friction on exit and ease on retention. (2) Is the friction asymmetric? Yes, signing up requires one click but cancelling requires navigating three screens with the exit option visually subordinated. (3) Does the design exploit a cognitive bias against the user? Yes, the highlighted "Stay subscribed" button exploits the visual-salience bias and the confirmshaming "Cancel anyway" phrase triggers loss aversion. The enforcement precedent is FTC v. Amazon (September 2025), in which the Amazon Iliad Flow — a cancellation path that buried the exit behind multiple confirmation screens and marketing pitches — settled for $2.5 billion in civil penalties and redress, the largest single-firm dark-pattern remedy in US history. That settlement priced this exact pattern type at the 9-figure level, establishing that a UX decision that engineers asymmetric exit friction carries UDAP exposure under FTC Section 5(a).

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 26.3, LO 26.8

Tags: dark patterns; Roach Motel; FTC; Amazon; brightline test; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Ethical understanding and reasoning

AI-3 — Evaluate an AI-Vendor Claim Under the Procurement Gate

A vendor salesperson tells a brand manager: "Our AI recommendation engine is completely bias-free. We ran a fairness audit that showed 94 percent overall accuracy across the product catalog." Using the chapter's fairness-is-multiple-choice principle, the three procurement documents, and the Rite Aid case, write three specific follow-up questions the brand manager should ask before signing a contract.

Sample answer: The vendor's claim has three testable gaps the chapter identifies.

Question 1 (Accountability — model card): "Can you share your published model card that documents the system's known limitations and the demographic groups on which it was evaluated?" A 94 percent overall accuracy figure without demographic disaggregation is the same error the Rite Aid facial-recognition system made: the system scored acceptable aggregate accuracy while producing error rates between 20.8 and 34.7 percent on darker-skinned women. The Pilot Parliaments Benchmark and the Gender Shades audit are the methodological precedent; the procurement standard is intersectional disaggregation, not aggregate accuracy.

Question 2 (Fairness criterion): "Which of the three fairness criteria did your audit test against — demographic parity, equalized odds, or calibration — and why was that criterion chosen for a product recommendation decision?" Chouldechova's 2017 impossibility result means the three criteria are incompatible when group base rates differ. A vendor claiming a single accuracy figure is "bias-free" is implicitly avoiding this choice and the trade-off it requires.

Question 3 (Responsible-scaling policy): "Do you publish a responsible-scaling policy that specifies how your firm responds when the model's performance on protected-group slices degrades in production?" The Rite Aid consent order shows that deployment without ongoing intersectional monitoring counts as negligence in the FTC's frame. The brand carries deployer accountability even when the vendor built the model.

AI-4 — Draft an AI-Use Policy on the SAFE Spine

Your running brand from the course is a DTC single-origin coffee subscription at $22 per bag. The brand uses an AI chatbot for customer service, an AI personalization engine for email recommendations, and an AI copywriting tool for ad headlines. Using the chapter's Apply-box prompt as a model, identify the single highest-risk exposure the brand faces on each of the four SAFE letters and state the Monday-morning fix for each.

Sample answer rubric (four required exposures and fixes):

Safeguards — Exposure: The email personalization engine requires processing behavioral data (purchase history, open rates, click signals). The brand may not have GDPR Article 4(11)-compliant consent infrastructure if any subscribers are EU residents, and "no PII" from its email vendor does not immunize it if the engine processes quasi-identifiers (ZIP, inferred demographics, behavioral fingerprints). Fix: Audit the email personalization data flow against CCPA/CPRA for US subscribers and GDPR for any EU subscriber, confirm Article 4(11)-compliant consent is in place before personalization runs, and confirm the vendor's "no PII" claim against the Sweeney 87.1-percent quasi-identifier standard.

Accountability — Exposure: The AI chatbot is customer-facing and handling returns and complaints, which can become consequential for individual customers (missed refunds, policy misinterpretation). The brand has not named a bias-audit schedule and has not confirmed the vendor publishes a model card. Fix: Request the three-document procurement gate (model card, constitution or character spec, responsible-scaling policy) before the next contract renewal. Add a bias-audit clause that requires the vendor to run intersectional accuracy audits on a quarterly basis.

Fairness — Exposure: The personalization engine's email recommendations could exhibit differential performance across demographic groups in ways the brand has never measured. Fix: Require the vendor to specify which fairness criterion its audit ran (demographic parity, equalized odds, or calibration) and provide a disaggregated report by at least two demographic axes. Document the trade-off accepted on the criteria not tested.

Empowerment — Exposure: The chatbot has no described human-fallback mechanism, and GDPR Article 22 requires an available human-intervention path for any EU data subject subjected to a consequential automated decision. Fix: Build the seven-step fallback architecture before the next holiday-season peak, prioritizing steps 2 (risk-tier routing), 5 (staffed reviewer queue with SLA), and 6 (per-path CSAT/NPS) as the three Klarna-lesson steps.

Chapter 27 Capstone — Build Your Marketing AI Stack — Test Bank

Chapter: 27 — Capstone: Build Your Marketing AI Stack Source note: Original authoring. No Kotler parent chapter exists. All items are authored from scratch against our Chapter 27 ground truth. Frameworks, examples, and vocabulary are those of our chapter: the five agent primitives (prompt, tools, memory, planning, reflection), the three-layer eval harness (golden set, LLM-as-judge, human spot-check), the 10-waypoint staged build, the "when NOT to build an agent" maturity section, the connect-the-Skills synthesis, the four-tier deployment ladder (ask/use/connect/build/publish), and the running example brands (Liquid Death, Patagonia, Costco). No item is adapted from or dependent on any Kotler test-bank chapter. Export format: Mirrors the course test-bank schema (stem, A...E options, Answer, Diff, Objective, Tags) for QTI / Canvas Common Cartridge conversion.

Learning Objectives (Ch27, Our Chapter)

  • LO 27.1 Decompose an AI agent into its five components (prompt, tools, memory, planning, reflection) and explain what each one is responsible for.
  • LO 27.2 Distinguish an agent from a workflow, and apply the rule that most marketing AI work is workflows.
  • LO 27.3 Describe the Model Context Protocol (MCP), explain the M-by-N argument that drove its adoption, and identify the three flavors of tool available to a marketing agent.
  • LO 27.4 Describe the three-layer eval harness (golden set, LLM-as-judge, human spot-check), explain why pass^k matters more than pass@1, and apply the Reflexion architecture to a failing run.
  • LO 27.5 Name and apply the five canonical agentic patterns from Anthropic's December 2024 post: prompt chaining, routing, parallelization, orchestrator-workers, and evaluator-optimizer.
  • LO 27.6 Apply the four-tier deployment ladder (Tier 1 chat, Tier 2 Skill, Tier 3 MCP, Tier 4 scheduled agent) to classify a marketing task and select the correct rung.
  • LO 27.7 Follow the 10-waypoint staged build to plan, execute, and document a marketing AI agent, naming the deliverable at each waypoint.
  • LO 27.8 Apply the six "when NOT to build an agent" cases to evaluate whether a proposed agent is the right solution, and identify the five failure modes in the production failure taxonomy.
  • LO 27.9 Explain the connect-the-Skills synthesis: how each prior chapter packages into one installable Skill, and how the capstone assembles those Skills into a single agent stack.

SA-1

A senior marketing manager at an outdoor apparel company is planning a capstone agent. She describes her plan: "I want the agent to monitor social media mentions, classify them by sentiment, draft weekly reports, send those reports to the CMO's Slack, update HubSpot contact records for anyone who posted a negative review, and delete the contact if they mention a competitor twice." Her colleague says the plan needs a Waypoint 0 step and a Waypoint 9 review before a single line of code is written.

Using our chapter's frameworks, (a) apply Waypoint 0 to identify the most likely gap in her plan as described, and (b) apply the "when NOT to build an agent" section to identify the two clearest cases that make her agent the wrong design and propose a safer alternative architecture.

Sample answer: (a) Waypoint 0 requires four sentences before building: target segment, positioning statement, mix lever, and single metric the agent moves. As described, the manager has named several tasks but has not named the marketing decision any of them serve. A weekly social-monitoring report is only worth automating if it advances a specific decision — for example, a positioning defense against competitor attacks, measurable by citation share in negative-review posts. Without naming the decision, the agent is automating activity rather than serving strategy. This is the gap her colleague is flagging.

(b) Two "when NOT to build an agent" cases apply clearly. Case 2 (low-trust domain, write-enabled): updating HubSpot contact records and deleting contacts based on mention behavior is write-enabled work on a live CRM. A prompt injection in one fetched social post could tell the agent to delete contact records it should not touch. The chapter states that once human approval is required on every write, the agent is no longer autonomous and a workflow is the better answer. Case 3 (blast radius too large): deleting a contact who mentions a competitor twice has no obvious recovery path if the classifier is wrong. The chapter requires a named human-in-the-loop checkpoint for every destructive action; as planned, there is none. A safer architecture: read-only monitoring agent that drafts the weekly report (Tier 3 or 4, parallelization pattern, all tools read-only); human-gated workflow for any CRM write; contact deletion removed entirely pending a policy decision above the agent's scope.

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 27.7, LO 27.8

Tags: Waypoint 0; when not to build; guardrails; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Ethical understanding

SA-2

A capstone team has finished Waypoints 1 through 6 and now needs to instrument the eval harness (Waypoint 8). They have an agent that lints marketing copy for banned words and rewrites violating sentences. They plan to write five golden-set cases, a run_evals.py script, and an LLM-as-judge step.

(a) Describe what belongs in one golden-set JSON case file for this agent, including the three required fields the chapter names. (b) Explain what the LLM-as-judge step is and why a second model call is needed rather than asking the first model to grade its own output. (c) Name the metric the chapter says the eval harness should report, and explain why pass@1 is insufficient for a weekly scheduled agent.

Sample answer: (a) Each golden-set case is a JSON object with three fields: input (a sample piece of copy the agent will lint), expected_contains (the specific violations the ideal lint should flag — for example, banned words present in the input), and pass_criteria (a plain-language statement of what the output must do to count as passing — for example, "Output must flag all three banned words and rewrite without them"). The team writes five real cases the agent will face on Mondays, not invented edge cases, and pairs each with the ideal output the team would produce without the agent.

(b) LLM-as-judge is a second model call with a different system prompt that reads the agent's output and scores it against the pass criteria. It is a separate call from the generating model because the generating model reads its own output as a writer, not a grader; it is prone to over-rating its own work and misses the confident-but-wrong failures that a grader with a strict rubric catches. The chapter says cost for a judge call runs a few cents per run and catches high-confidence-but-wrong failures the first model cannot catch itself.

(c) The chapter says the eval harness should report pass^k, not pass@1. Pass@1 measures whether the agent succeeds on a single attempt; pass^k measures whether it succeeds on k consecutive attempts — the actual reliability a weekly scheduled agent needs. The tau-bench finding cited in the chapter shows that agents clearing 86 percent on single attempts fall below 25 percent on pass^8. For a weekly digest agent that runs 52 Mondays a year, failing once every eight runs is materially worse than pass@1 suggests.

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 27.4, LO 27.7

Tags: eval harness; golden set; LLM-as-judge; pass^k; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

SA-3

A student is designing a capstone agent for her P2 client, a local coffee chain. She wants the agent to post one Instagram caption per day, varying tone by day of week. She plans to connect it to a Tier 4 schedule. Her instructor says Tier 4 might not be the right starting rung.

Sample answer: (a) The chapter's rung-selection rule asks four mechanical questions in order: Is it a one-time lookup? (Tier 1.) Does the same task repeat three or more times? (Tier 2 Skill.) Does it need live data the model cannot copy-paste? (Tier 3 MCP server.) Does it run while the marketer is asleep, without a human in the loop? (Tier 4.) AI-1 — Design the Eval Harness

A teammate has just shipped the first version of a weekly GEO citation tracker for the Stanley brand. The agent probes four AI engines (ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity, Claude) in parallel, each with the same query. After three Monday runs, the team is celebrating a high citation rate for Stanley. You notice, however, that the team has (a) no written golden-set cases, (b) no LLM-as-judge rubric, and (c) no human spot-check process.

Which of the following best describes the risk the chapter names for this situation, and what is the minimum the team must add before trusting the weekly results?

A) There is no significant risk; if the citation rate is rising, the agent is working. B) Without the three-layer eval harness, the agent may produce confident-but-wrong outputs the team has no systematic way to catch; the minimum is five golden cases, an LLM-as-judge call scoring each run on correctness and completeness, and a human review of 100 percent of outputs in the first month. C) The risk is cost overrun; the missing evals folder means the agent reruns unnecessarily. D) The risk is prompt injection from public web pages; the eval harness is the defense against adversarial inputs. E) The risk is rung mismatch; without evals the agent cannot be classified as Tier 4.

Answer: B

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 27.4 — An agent without reflection produces confident garbage when something subtle is wrong. The chapter requires three layers: a golden set the team writes by hand (five cases to start, naming what the ideal output looks like for each query), an LLM-as-judge rubric pass scoring each run against dimensions like citation accuracy and query completeness, and a human spot-check (100 percent for the first month, dropping to 10 percent once pass rate is steady). None of the three layers alone is sufficient. A rising citation rate without a rubric can mean the agent is inflating its own citation counts rather than accurately reporting them.

Tags: eval harness; GEO citation tracker; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Reflective thinking

AI-2 — Pick the Right Waypoint

A team has an agent that drafts Liquid Death Instagram captions. Waypoints 1 through 5 are complete. The system prompt is tight (Waypoint 2), one tool is wired (Waypoint 3), memory is in CLAUDE.md (Waypoint 4), and planning is implicit because the task is two steps (Waypoint 5). The team now wants to skip Waypoint 6 (reflection) and go straight to Waypoint 7 (schedule it). The team argues: "The captions are already good; a second model call adds cost with no clear return."

Using the chapter's argument for the reflection component, critique the team's decision and explain what specific failure the missing reflection step leaves undetected.

Sample answer and rubric: The chapter's argument against skipping reflection is that a generator cannot grade its own output with a grader's mindset. The system prompt that makes the agent produce captions is the same one that makes it evaluate them; without a separate judge call with a different system prompt, the agent ships whatever it produces, including drafts that violate the brand voice, contain banned words, or make factual claims that need a human catch. The specific failure left undetected is a high-confidence-but-wrong caption: one that reads fluent and on-topic but uses a word on the banned list (leverage, synergy), or references a product detail incorrectly, or uses a tone the brand has explicitly rejected. Without the judge call, the only way to catch it is a human reading every output before posting, which defeats the purpose of the schedule. The minimum reflection step costs a few cents per run on Haiku 4.5 and produces a 1-5 score with a one-sentence justification. At a score below 4, the agent re-prompts and tries again up to three times. The team's cost argument is correct in absolute terms but wrong in relative terms: the cost of one publicly posted wrong caption to a live brand account exceeds the cost of every judge call the team will make in a semester.

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 27.1, LO 27.7 — The reflection component's purpose is to catch the confident-but-wrong outputs the generating model cannot see. Skipping it and scheduling the agent unattended creates a pipeline with no internal quality gate.

Tags: reflection; Waypoint 6; eval harness; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Written communication

AI-3 — When NOT to Build an Agent: Maturity Judgment

A student has found an article describing a 2024 AI startup that built an AI Sales Development Representative (AI-SDR) platform for outbound email. The article reports 500 percent month-over-month growth in 2024. The student wants to replicate the model for her P2 client's B2B lead generation.

Using the chapter's "when NOT to build an agent" section, identify two cases that most clearly apply to this proposal, explain why each applies, and propose what a lower-rung alternative would look like.

Sample answer: Two cases from the chapter apply clearly.

Case 5 (the AI-SDR cautionary tale): The chapter names AI-SDR platforms directly as the 2024-2025 cautionary tale. The boom produced a deliverability collapse as Gmail and Outlook tightened sender reputation rules and volume-without-quality machines burned the sending domains. The chapter states that the class of agent that automates outreach to people who did not ask hits the inbox-reputation wall first and should not be a P2 capstone. The 2024 growth figure in the article reflects the early phase before the deliverability wall collapsed the model.

Case 3 (blast radius too large): An agent that sends personalized emails to real prospects at scale has a blast radius that includes the client's domain reputation, deliverability rate, and relationship with every prospect on the list. A single prompt-injection in a scraped data source, or a model that writes an off-brand or factually wrong outreach email at scale, damages relationships that the client cannot easily recover.

Lower-rung alternative: A Tier 2 Skill that drafts one personalized email at a time, triggered manually for each prospect, with the human reading and approving before sending. The Skill lints the draft for brand voice, suggests personalization points, and flags any factual claims the sender should verify. The human makes the send decision. This approach captures the drafting-efficiency benefit of AI without the inbox-reputation and blast-radius risks of a fully automated outbound agent.

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 27.8 — The AI-SDR cautionary tale and the blast-radius case from the chapter's six "when NOT to build" cases both apply to mass automated outbound email, and the chapter names the category explicitly.

Tags: when not to build; AI-SDR; blast radius; AACSB: Ethical understanding; Application of knowledge

Chapter 4 How Buyers Buy — Test Bank

Chapter: 4 — How Buyers Actually Buy (Consumer Behavior)

Learning Objectives (Ch4, Our Chapter)

  • LO 4.1 Explain how memory retrieval, mental availability, and implicit memory drive brand choice before any deliberation begins.
  • LO 4.2 Describe the cue-context-response loop, the habit discontinuity window, and how Lewin's B = f(P, E) equation accounts for environmental change as a driver of purchase behavior.
  • LO 4.3 Apply the Howard-Sheth three-mode typology (EPS, LPS, RRB) and the MAO triad to predict when buyers deliberate and when they revert to shortcuts.
  • LO 4.4 Identify the four families of shortcuts (social, authority, loss-and-scarcity, reference-point) and explain the mechanism behind each major lever within a family.
  • LO 4.5 Explain how consideration sets are built before the search begins, and describe the role of category entry points, first-recall premium, and choice-context effects.
  • LO 4.6 Analyze how AI shopping agents alter the buyer's decision process, apply the distinctive-asset audit to both human and agent storefronts, and evaluate algorithm aversion and its reversals.

Section I — Multiple-Choice Items (22 items)

Item 1

A buyer walks into a gas-station convenience store and reaches for a Gatorade before she has scanned the cooler. She would describe the choice as automatic. Which concept best explains why the brand arrived before she looked?

A) Selective distortion, where she reinterprets ambiguous stimuli in line with her existing brand beliefs B) Mental availability, where prior exposure built strong memory links that the situational cue triggered automatically C) Attitude formation through the central route, where prior argument-processing stored a strong preference D) Operant conditioning, where a previous reward reinforced the behavior until the response became fixed E) Selective retention, where she forgot the competing brands and remembered only Gatorade

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 4.1 — Mental availability is the ease with which a brand comes to mind when a situational cue fires. The buyer is not deliberating; the cue (cooler, thirst) retrieves the brand that has the strongest existing link to that moment.

Tags: mental availability; implicit memory; retrieval; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 2

A construction-equipment manager cannot recall seeing any Caterpillar advertising this month. However, when her foreman asks her to name a heavy-machinery brand for a rental, "Caterpillar" arrives in her mouth before she finishes hearing the question. This result is consistent with _______.

A) selective attention, because she filtered the ads out before encoding B) implicit memory, where repeated exposure created a retrievable trace without conscious awareness C) the elaboration likelihood model's central route, where she processed the ads carefully but forgot them D) low involvement, meaning she has no real preference and simply names the most common brand E) social proof, because someone at her company must have mentioned Caterpillar recently

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 4.1 — The chapter's opening case: implicit memory stores traces the buyer cannot consciously report, but those traces still surface at the moment of category need. The billboard encoded itself roughly fifty times without conscious awareness.

Tags: implicit memory; Caterpillar; brand retrieval; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 3

According to the chapter, the primary job of marketing is to _______.

A) persuade buyers to switch from a competitor's brand they already prefer B) explain the functional differences between the brand and its rivals C) ensure the brand is the one most easily retrieved when a buyer needs the category D) build a loyal heavy-buyer base that repurchases more often than average E) reduce post-purchase dissonance by confirming the buyer made the right choice

Answer: C

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 4.1 — The chapter states the marketer's central job is ensuring the brand is the one that comes to mind fastest. Retrieval at the moment of need beats stated preference measured at the desk.

Tags: mental availability; memory-first framing; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 4

A brand-asset team runs a Fame-and-Uniqueness audit. "Fame" measures the share of buyers who recognize the asset and connect it to some brand. "Uniqueness" measures the share of recognizers who attach the asset to the correct brand. A high-Fame but low-Uniqueness result indicates _______.

A) the asset is so novel that most buyers have not seen it yet, so more media investment is needed B) the brand has built strong mental availability and no further distinctive-asset investment is warranted C) the asset has leaked into the category and is now building mental availability for every brand in the set, not just the brand that owns it D) the audit methodology is unreliable and should be rerun with a larger sample E) the brand's social shortcuts are working, but its reference-point shortcuts are not

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 4.1 — High Fame means buyers see the asset; low Uniqueness means they cannot tie it back to the right brand. The asset has become generic, so it funds the category rather than the specific brand.

Tags: distinctive assets; Fame and Uniqueness; brand audit; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 5

A 2026 benchmark of 1,162 distinctive assets across twenty-one categories found that shape-based assets substantially outperformed color-based assets on Uniqueness. The chapter's explanation is that _______.

A) color is more expensive to own legally and most brands cannot protect it B) shape survives the low-resolution conditions where most exposures occur, such as thumbnail icons and glanced billboards C) buyers perceive color as manipulative and discount it when forming brand associations D) color assets are regulated under the Interactive Advertising Bureau's 2026 AI-content framework E) shape activates the peripheral route more reliably than color, which is processed only by the central route

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 4.1 — The chapter cites the 2026 IJA study directly: shape-based assets (roughly 40% Fame, 71% Uniqueness) beat color-based assets (12%, 39%) because shape is legible even in degraded viewing conditions.

Tags: distinctive assets; shape versus color; 2026 benchmark; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 6

Experience-sampling diaries find that roughly _______ of daily actions repeat in the same context while the person is thinking about something else entirely, suggesting that most buying is not deliberate.

A) 12 percent B) 27 percent C) 43 percent D) 62 percent E) 78 percent

Answer: C

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 4.2 — The chapter cites the 43% figure as the replicated finding across cohorts, age groups, and categories. The 27-62% range applies to impulse purchases specifically, while 43% is the daily-habit share.

Tags: habitual behavior; cue-context-response loop; autopilot; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 7

A grocery chain wants to acquire new households who just moved to a neighborhood. According to the chapter's habit discontinuity window, the optimal time to reach those households with a high-value promotion is _______.

A) immediately before they move, because planning behavior is elevated in the anticipation period B) within four to twelve weeks after the move, before a new routine locks in C) six months after the move, after the household has established its grocery preferences D) only at the annual renewal of their loyalty card, when deliberation naturally rises E) during major holidays, when category shocks push all buyers back into deliberate mode

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 4.2 — The habit discontinuity window is the four-to-twelve-week period after a life event dissolves the old cue layer. A promotion delivered inside this window produces structural lift; outside it collapses to near zero.

Tags: habit discontinuity window; life events; cue-context-response; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 8

Kurt Lewin's equation B = f(P, E) says behavior is a function of the person and the environment together. Applied to the Stanley Quencher, the chapter argues that the cup's sales increase from $73 million to $750 million between 2019 and 2024 was driven primarily by _______.

A) improvements to the product's functional performance (better insulation, new lid design) B) a change in the person-side variable, as buyers shifted their stated preference for hydration C) changes to the environment side, including TikTok-built social meaning and color-drop gift positioning D) a reduction in the competing brands' advertising budgets, freeing shelf space for Stanley E) a demographic shift as the target buyer aged into a higher income bracket

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 4.2 — The chapter uses Stanley as the B = f(P,E) case: the product and the buyer's underlying motives barely changed, but TikTok and color-drops transformed the environment side and with it the behavior.

Tags: Lewin equation; Stanley Quencher; environmental change; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 9

A college junior signed an apartment lease after seven weeks of research, then bought a Coke from the lobby vending machine two hours later without noticing she chose it. The Howard-Sheth typology labels those two decisions _____ and _____, respectively.

A) limited problem solving; extensive problem solving B) routinized response behavior; limited problem solving C) extensive problem solving; routinized response behavior D) routinized response behavior; extensive problem solving E) limited problem solving; routinized response behavior

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 4.3 — EPS is the heavy mode for the lease (no prior knowledge, high stakes); RRB is the autopilot mode for a familiar vending-machine purchase (choice retrieved in under a second). The same buyer runs both modes within hours.

Tags: Howard-Sheth typology; EPS vs RRB; decision modes; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 10

The MAO triad specifies three conditions that must all be open for a buyer to actually deliberate. Which of the following scenarios closes the "opportunity" gate and forces the buyer onto a shortcut?

A) She does not care which brand of sunscreen she buys because she considers them interchangeable. B) She cannot afford any of the premium options on the shelf and must choose from the budget tier. C) She would like to compare sunscreen reviews on her phone, but her toddler is trying to escape the cart. D) She has never heard of any of the brands on the shelf and has no prior schema to draw on. E) She already owns a sunscreen she likes and is only buying a backup.

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 4.3 — Opportunity is whether the situation gives the buyer the time and quiet to evaluate alternatives. A distracted parent in an aisle has the motivation and, in principle, the ability, but the situation denies the opportunity, so she falls back on a shortcut.

Tags: MAO triad; opportunity gate; deliberation; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 11

A buyer watching a 30-second television ad for a car she rarely thinks about forms a positive impression because the background music sounds warm and the driver looks likable. She is taking the _______ through the Elaboration Likelihood Model.

A) central route, because she processed the car's actual attributes B) peripheral route, because she reacted to cues unrelated to the argument C) dual route, because both the music and the spokesperson contributed D) cognitive route, because all attitude formation involves some cognitive processing E) affective route, which operates only on emotional cues and is distinct from both ELM routes

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 4.3 — The peripheral route is used when motivation, ability, or opportunity is low. The buyer forms a quick opinion from executional cues (music, spokesperson), not the product's performance claims.

Tags: Elaboration Likelihood Model; peripheral route; low involvement; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 12

The chapter's four families of shortcuts are social, authority, loss-and-scarcity, and reference-point. A store tag reading "Only 2 left at this price" is pulling the _______ family.

A) social B) authority C) loss-and-scarcity D) reference-point E) commitment

Answer: C

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 4.4 — Scarcity activates the asymmetric pain of missing out, which is part of the loss-and-scarcity family. The tag is signaling that the option is about to disappear.

Tags: four families of shortcuts; scarcity; loss aversion; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 13

Costco's free weekend samples are designed to trigger which loss-and-scarcity lever?

A) Scarcity, because the sample is available only while supplies last B) Reciprocity, because an unsolicited gift sets a small obligation in motion before the ask arrives C) Commitment, because trying the product is a small public act that increases purchase intention D) Present bias, because the taste reward occurs now rather than after a future shopping decision E) Endowment, because touching the product creates temporary psychological ownership

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 4.4 — The chapter names reciprocity as the engine behind the Costco sample: an unsolicited gift sets an obligation before any ask, which converts indifferent shoppers at a rate no markdown could match.

Tags: reciprocity; loss-and-scarcity; Costco; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 14

A menu lists wine at $18, $32, and $75. Most guests choose the $32 option. The $75 bottle's primary function is to _______.

A) satisfy the high-demand segment and prevent capacity loss at premium price points B) anchor the middle option as moderate by providing a high reference point neither extreme is typically ordered C) trigger social proof by demonstrating that some guests order expensive wine D) activate authority by associating the restaurant with luxury positioning E) create present bias by making the $32 option feel like an immediate savings win

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 4.4 / LO 4.5 — The chapter uses a Manhattan trattoria wine list to illustrate anchoring: the Barolo's only job is to sit at the top and reset the reference point so the Brunello reads as moderate. This is the reference-point family.

Tags: anchoring; reference-point shortcuts; decoy; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 15

The chapter distinguishes "socially scarce" options from "logistically scarce" ones. Which statement correctly explains why social scarcity outperforms logistic scarcity?

A) Social scarcity is regulated under FTC guidelines, so it signals higher legal credibility to buyers. Item 16

According to the chapter's Ehrenberg-Bass framing, roughly half of buyers at the point of purchase consider _______ brand(s) before choosing.

A) five or more B) three or four C) exactly two D) only one E) zero; most buyers are on autopilot and bypass evaluation entirely

Answer: D

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 4.5 — The chapter cites Ehrenberg-Bass panel data: roughly half of buyers consider only one brand at the actual moment of purchase. A brand outside the consideration set effectively does not exist for that buyer in that moment.

Tags: consideration set; Ehrenberg-Bass; mental availability; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 17

Category Entry Points (CEPs) are situations, occasions, emotions, and cues that prompt a buyer to access the category. The Ehrenberg-Bass framework organizes the CEP inventory along seven W-spokes. Which of the following is NOT one of the seven spokes?

A) Why (the goal) B) When (time of day) C) Who benefits (the end user) D) With whom (social context) E) How feeling (emotional state)

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 4.5 — The seven spokes are: why (goal), when (time of day), where (location), with whom (social context), while doing what (parallel activity), with what (paired product), and how feeling (emotional state). "Who benefits" is not one of them; "with whom" is the social-context spoke.

Tags: category entry points; Ehrenberg-Bass; seven W-spokes; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 18

An attribute drives a buyer's final choice only when it is both salient and diagnostic. "Tells time" is salient for a watch but not diagnostic, because _______.

A) buyers do not actually care whether their watch keeps accurate time anymore B) every watch tells time, so the attribute cannot separate one option from another C) the attribute is not encoded in long-term memory and therefore cannot surface during retrieval D) telling time is an authority-family cue, not a functional one E) the salient attribute activates the peripheral route and blocks diagnostic attributes from being processed

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 4.5 — A diagnostic attribute is one that is useful for discrimination. If every option in the set shares the attribute, it has no discriminatory power regardless of its salience.

Tags: salient vs diagnostic attributes; evaluation of alternatives; consideration set; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 19

The chapter reports that a buyer evaluating a hundred-dollar sunscreen on its own product page behaves differently than the same buyer seeing it on a comparison grid alongside cheaper options. In separate evaluation she leans on brand cachet; in joint evaluation she focuses on dollars-per-ounce. The reason is that _______.

A) she is on the central route on the comparison grid and the peripheral route on the standalone page B) the comparison grid activates authority shortcuts while the standalone page activates social shortcuts C) joint evaluation supplies a reference point that makes price-per-ounce visible, while separate evaluation hides it D) she spends longer on the comparison grid, which allows more attributes to be encoded in long-term memory E) the comparison grid triggers the habit discontinuity window, pushing her back into extensive problem solving

Answer: C

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 4.5 — Joint-versus-separate evaluation is a reference-point family mechanism. The comparison grid provides rivals as reference points that make a comparative attribute (price-per-ounce) visible; the standalone page denies that reference point.

Tags: joint versus separate evaluation; reference-point shortcuts; Drunk Elephant; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 20

A buyer asks ChatGPT for "a forty-ounce insulated tumbler under fifty dollars for weekend hiking." The model returns three ranked options. The chapter argues this shifts brand competition because _______.

A) the buyer now has more information than she could have gathered in a store, which favors higher-quality brands B) the model's consideration set is assembled from training data and content the brand can influence, not from the buyer's own memory, so a brand absent from the model's answer is invisible at the moment of choice C) shopping agents prefer brands with higher SEO domain authority, which advantages large incumbents D) the model's recommendations are randomized across product categories to prevent bias, so mental availability no longer predicts outcomes E) the buyer's algorithm aversion means she will discard the model's top pick and choose the second option anyway

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 4.6 — The model writes the consideration set from its own associative network (training data, reviews, schema markup). A brand absent from that set is in the same position as one absent from a buyer's memory.

Item 21

Algorithm aversion is defined as the preference buyers show for a human recommender over an algorithm even when the algorithm is provably more accurate. The chapter says the aversion is sharpest for _______.

Objective: LO 4.6 — The chapter identifies identity-laden categories as the zone where algorithm aversion concentrates, via the mechanism of uniqueness neglect: the buyer believes her case is special in ways the algorithm cannot see.

Item 22

According to the chapter, algorithm aversion inverts when the human alternative reads as corruptible. A loan applicant often prefers a fixed-rule algorithm over a human loan officer because _______.

A) algorithms process applications faster and the buyer values the time saving B) the algorithm cannot quietly approve a faster decision for a relative who resembles the officer, so it reads as fairer C) regulatory requirements mandate algorithmic approval in most financial contexts D) the buyer's algorithm aversion only applies to product recommendations, not financial decisions E) algorithms can access a larger data set than any individual officer, so the applicant expects a better outcome

Section II — Scenario / Short-Answer Items (4 items)

SA-1

A regional chain of coffee shops is losing repeat traffic among buyers in their mid-twenties. A consultant recommends they "run more promotional ads targeting that demographic." A second consultant says the real problem is that the chain has weak category entry points. Using the chapter's memory-first framework, explain what "weak category entry points" means in practice and how it differs from "not enough advertising." Then propose two specific CEP-building moves the chain could take that do not require buying additional media.

Sample answer: Weak category entry points means the brand has few strong links in the buyer's associative network connecting the brand to the situations where she might reach for coffee: morning commute, post-gym fuel, study break, date-night dessert stop, work meeting before a presentation. Awareness of the brand (she can recognize the logo) is not the same as retrieval; retrieval fires at the moment of need, and if the brand has no link to "I am at the library at 10 p.m. and need something warm," the chain does not come to mind even if the buyer has seen the ad. More advertising at the same target demographic builds Fame at the same set of moments she already associates with the brand; it does not add new moments. Two CEP-building moves without paid media: (1) partner with nearby gyms to place a co-branded hydration sign in the locker room, creating a post-workout retrieval link that the buyer walks past on every gym visit; (2) run a "study hall" late-hours campaign with ambient seating and free power strips, building a link between the chain and the late-study-session CEP, a moment the chain currently does not own.

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 4.1, LO 4.5

Tags: category entry points; mental availability; brand building; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Reflective thinking

SA-2

A CPG brand manager is running a campaign that lists four product benefits in sixty seconds. A colleague argues the campaign is unlikely to move brand choice for existing category buyers, even though awareness is high. Using the ELM and the chapter's discussion of habitual behavior, explain why the colleague is probably right. Then describe what type of campaign approach would more likely move choice in a stable, low-involvement category.

Sample answer: Existing category buyers running routinized response behavior (RRB) are on the peripheral route. Their motivation, ability, or opportunity to process a sixty-second argument is low, not because they dislike the brand but because they are not listening to arguments: they are answering cues. A list of four benefits is central-route content and requires the buyer to devote attention she has rationed away to other tasks. The result is near-zero attitude shift and therefore near-zero behavior change. The ELM's peripheral route holds that attitudes formed through cues (music, familiarity, spokesperson warmth) are faster to assemble and faster to abandon, which is fine in a low-involvement category where the goal is breadth of retrieval, not depth of conviction. What actually moves choice in this context is increasing the brand's distinctive-asset salience in the environments where the cue fires, and widening the number of category entry points it is linked to. Concretely: replace the benefit list with a campaign that associates the brand with one new underserved CEP (for example, "the beer for a Tuesday cookout with colleagues," not just "great beer"), and put that cue in the physical environment where it fires, not just in paid media.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 4.2, LO 4.3

Tags: ELM; peripheral route; habitual behavior; RRB; low involvement; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

SA-3

A retail brand is running three A/B tests on its product page. Version A shows the product alone with no comparison. Version B adds a premium tier at twice the price at the top of the page. Version C lists the product alongside three cheaper alternatives on a comparison grid. Using the chapter's reference-point family and joint-versus-separate evaluation, predict which version will produce the highest average order value, which will produce the lowest, and why.

Sample answer: Version B (premium tier added) will likely produce the highest average order value. Anchoring places the high-price tier first, resetting the reference point so the core product reads as the moderate and sensible choice. The buyer forms her sense of reasonable value against the anchor before reading any other number. Version C (comparison grid against cheaper alternatives) will likely produce the lowest average order value for the core SKU. Joint evaluation activates the reference-point family differently: when rivals appear in adjacent columns, comparative attributes like price-per-unit and feature parity become visible and diagnostic. The core product is now evaluated against cheaper competition with explicit side-by-side numbers, so the buyer who might have accepted the premium in isolation now weighs the delta. Version A (isolated product page) sits between the two: separate evaluation hides price-per-unit comparisons, but without an anchor the buyer's reference point defaults to whatever she paid last time, which may or may not favor the brand.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 4.4, LO 4.5

Tags: anchoring; joint versus separate evaluation; reference-point shortcuts; choice architecture; AACSB: Analytical thinking

SA-4

The chapter describes consumer behavior as covering three stages: acquisition (research, comparison, payment), consumption (use across the days that follow), and disposition (throwing out, reselling, donating, passing on). A startup founder argues the company only needs to focus on acquisition because "we win at the point of sale." Using the chapter's framework, explain two reasons why ignoring the consumption and disposition stages creates strategic exposure, and give a real brand example from the chapter for each.

Sample answer: First, disposition shapes whether the buyer returns for the replacement. If the brand's end-of-life experience is negative, the buyer re-enters the acquisition stage with a negative anchor. The chapter cites Patagonia's repair-and-resale program: end-of-life is treated as a brand experience, not a service-desk problem. A buyer who sends a jacket back for a free repair returns with a stronger schema link than a buyer who threw it in a landfill. The startup that ignores disposition concedes the replacement purchase to whichever brand shows up at that re-entry moment with the strongest CEP. Second, the consumption stage shapes the identity the buyer builds around the brand, and that identity is what makes her recommend or post about it. The chapter notes that the identity a brand confers stays attached long after the receipt is forgotten. Stanley owners post pictures of the cup on TikTok during use, which is a consumption-stage behavior that creates the social-proof CEPs for the next buyer. A brand whose product experience produces no social signal during use cannot generate the organic CEP infrastructure that drove Stanley's curve.

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 4.1, LO 4.2

Tags: three stages of consumer behavior; acquisition consumption disposition; Patagonia; Stanley; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

Section III — AI-Integration Items (4 items)

These items are original to this course. They test students' ability to apply the chapter's frameworks to AI-shopping agents, the distinctive-asset audit, and AI-mediated consideration-set formation.

AI-1 — The Distinctive-Asset Audit on Two Storefronts

A brand manager for a premium water-filtration company asks an AI assistant to audit the brand's mental availability. The AI responds:

"Based on your brand guidelines document, your primary distinctive assets are your navy-blue color palette and your tagline 'Pure by Design.' These have high consumer awareness. I recommend maintaining both."

Using the chapter's 2026 benchmark findings and its discussion of the AI storefront, identify two problems with this AI output and explain what a more complete audit would require.

A) The AI correctly identified the two most important assets; no problems exist with the output. B) The AI failed to include the brand's physical retail distribution, which is the only relevant availability measure. C) The AI did not apply the Fame-and-Uniqueness rubric to separate recognition from correct attribution, and it assessed only the human storefront while ignoring whether those assets surface in AI-model recommendation outputs. D) The AI applied the peripheral route analysis but ignored the central route, so the audit is incomplete for high-involvement buyers. E) The AI diagnosed the asset portfolio correctly but recommended the wrong channels for the refresh campaign.

Answer: C

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 4.1, LO 4.6 — The chapter reports two gaps. First, the 2026 benchmark found color-based assets carry only 12% Fame and 39% Uniqueness on average; "high consumer awareness" does not equal correct brand attribution. A proper audit applies the Fame-and-Uniqueness rubric: the navy palette may be recognized (Fame) but not attached to this brand specifically (Uniqueness). Second, the chapter argues the retrieval problem now has two storefronts: the buyer's own memory and the AI model she may ask for a recommendation. A brand can have high human-side mental availability and zero presence in model outputs. The audit needs to run the brand's CEPs as queries into ChatGPT or Claude and check whether the brand surfaces.

Tags: distinctive-asset audit; AI storefront; Fame and Uniqueness; answer-engine optimization; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Reflective thinking

AI-2 — Algorithm Aversion in Practice

A direct-to-consumer apparel startup wants to deploy an AI shopping agent that selects a complete outfit for the buyer based on her stated occasion, budget, and three style keywords. The marketing director says the rollout will be easy because "buyers already trust algorithms for product recommendations." A product manager pushes back, arguing the category will trigger algorithm aversion.

Who is more likely correct, and what would reduce the aversion risk in this specific context?

Sample answer and rubric:

The product manager is more likely correct. The chapter identifies identity-laden categories such as clothing and gifts as those where algorithm aversion concentrates most strongly, via the mechanism of uniqueness neglect: the buyer believes her personal taste is special in ways the algorithm cannot model. Buying a complete outfit is a higher-identity-stakes task than buying a phone charger or comparing flight times. The marketing director's claim that buyers "already trust algorithms" is accurate for functionally-bounded, low-identity categories (routes, prices, ratings), not for those where the buyer's sense of self is at stake.

Two moves that reduce aversion risk in this context: (1) layer a human stylist's commentary or brief personalization question on top of the algorithmic selection, because the chapter notes Stitch Fix beats both pure versions by giving the buyer a hedge against each one's blind spot; (2) treat the agent as a starting-point builder rather than a final-decision maker, preserving the buyer's sense that the final choice is hers. The chapter also notes that the aversion inverts when inventory is vanishing, so a limited-edition or low-stock high-demand item is a place where an autonomous agent will be welcomed without modification.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 4.6 — The item requires students to identify algorithm aversion, apply the identity-category mechanism correctly, and propose a structural design fix (hybrid model) rather than a messaging fix.

Tags: algorithm aversion; AI shopping agent; identity-laden categories; Stitch Fix; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Written communication

AI-3 — The Four-Type Buyer-AI Taxonomy

The chapter proposes a four-type taxonomy of buyer-AI interactions: data capture, classification, delegation, and social. A student spends an afternoon with several AI systems. Match each of the following behaviors to the correct interaction type, then identify which type creates the most significant brand-trust risk if the buyer discovers it is occurring without her knowledge.

Behavior 1: Her Instagram feed logs which posts she lingered on. Behavior 2: Spotify places her into a "Sunday folk" playlist bucket. Behavior 3: She asks ChatGPT to draft an email to her professor. Behavior 4: She opens a companion app and talks through a hard day.

A) 1-data capture; 2-classification; 3-delegation; 4-social. The social type (4) creates the highest brand-trust risk if undisclosed. B) 1-classification; 2-data capture; 3-social; 4-delegation. The delegation type (3) creates the highest brand-trust risk. C) 1-data capture; 2-classification; 3-delegation; 4-social. The data-capture type (1) creates the highest brand-trust risk. D) 1-classification; 2-data capture; 3-delegation; 4-social. The classification type (2) creates the highest brand-trust risk. E) 1-social; 2-data capture; 3-delegation; 4-classification. The data-capture type (1) creates the highest risk.

Answer: A

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 4.6 — The chapter presents the four-type taxonomy from the 2021 review and assigns these exact examples: Instagram = data capture, Spotify = classification, ChatGPT draft = delegation, companion chatbot = social. The social type carries the highest undisclosed-trust risk because a buyer who discovers she bonded with what she believed was a human-to-human interaction and was actually a brand surface experiences a more severe trust violation than she would learning her feed was logged.

AI-4 — The New Shelf and Paid Placement

A media-buying team is considering allocating budget to "paid inclusion" inside AI model answers, the emerging model where a brand name appears in a conversational response the buyer asked for, without a visual banner or disclosed ad label. Using the chapter's discussion of paid inclusion and the Interactive Advertising Bureau's 2026 disclosure framework, answer the following: What makes paid inclusion more effective per impression than a display banner, and what disclosure risk does the team need to weigh before committing budget?

Sample answer and rubric:

Paid inclusion is more effective per impression than a display banner for two related reasons the chapter identifies. First, the format has no banner shape and no visual ad boundary, so it arrives in the same conversational register as the model's unpaid answers. Buyers trained their persuasion-knowledge defenses against display formats over two decades; a conversational mention in a trusted assistant's voice slips past those defenses because it does not look like an ad. Second, the model delivers the brand name at the exact moment the buyer is in need-recognition or information-search mode, contextually matched in a way a traditional impression cannot achieve.

The disclosure risk the team must weigh: the chapter reports that content merely suspected of being AI-generated lost roughly half its trust and fourteen percent of purchase consideration in a 2025 study, on suspicion alone. The IAB's January 2026 framework names six categories of AI-generated or AI-mediated content that require consumer-facing labels, and a paid native mention inside a conversational AI response may fall inside that framework. If a buyer later discovers she was given a brand recommendation that was a paid placement without a label, the trust damage is likely to be steeper than for a conventional display ad, because it exploits the trust she placed in the model as a neutral recommender. The disclosure question is a legal and ethical gate the team should resolve before the first campaign launches, not after.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 4.6 — The item requires students to explain the mechanism (no banner shape, conversational register, contextual match) and apply the disclosure stakes from the IAB 2026 framework and the 2025 trust-damage study.

Tags: paid inclusion; AI native advertising; IAB 2026 disclosure; brand trust; persuasion knowledge; AACSB: Ethical understanding; Application of knowledge; Written communication

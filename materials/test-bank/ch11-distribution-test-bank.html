Chapter 11 Distribution — Test Bank

Chapter: 11 — Distribution and Marketing Channels

Learning Objectives (Ch11, Our Chapter)

  • LO 11.1 Explain why distribution exists by connecting the four channel utilities (place, time, form, possession) to the concept of physical availability, and distinguish a marketing channel from the broader supply chain.
  • LO 11.2 Describe the three channel-structure decisions (length, intensity, integration) and apply the reach-vs-control trade-off to a real brand, using the Iron Law to explain why going direct does not eliminate channel functions.
  • LO 11.3 Distinguish markup from margin and apply backward price-waterfall arithmetic to calculate what a maker keeps at each tier of the channel.
  • LO 11.4 Explain the three types of vertical marketing systems (corporate, administered, contractual), identify the five French-Raven channel powers, and predict which power source produces the most durable partner performance.
  • LO 11.5 Describe push and pull strategies, explain when each is appropriate, and map the three-tier reach-vs-control trade-off to a brand's intensity choice (exclusive, selective, intensive).
  • LO 11.6 Define omni-channel and multichannel, score the six omni-channel components, and apply conflict-response tactics (dual compensation, co-optation, superordinate goals, employee exchange) to a named channel conflict scenario.
  • LO 11.7 Explain the sell-through-Amazon vs. sell-to-Amazon distinction and its implications for margin, data, and brand control.
  • LO 11.8 Apply the Triple-A supply-chain test (agility, adaptability, alignment) to a JIT-fragility case, and name the bullwhip effect as the mechanism that amplifies upstream disruption.
  • LO 11.9 Evaluate AI-assisted channel-mix analysis, describe what a channel-optimization Skill does, and critique the limits of model-generated channel audits.

Section I — Multiple-Choice Items (22 items)

Item 1

A working marketing channel closes four gaps that separate production from consumption. A Tulsa buyer purchasing a coffee brewed from Buffalo-roasted beans is receiving value from all four utilities at once. Which utility is specifically responsible for making the coffee available in Tulsa rather than only in Buffalo?

A) Time utility — the channel timed the delivery to coincide with the buyer's morning routine B) Form utility — the channel transformed green beans into a brewed cup C) Place utility — the channel moved the product to where the buyer is located D) Possession utility — the channel transferred legal ownership and enabled payment E) Value utility — the channel added margin to reflect the higher demand in Tulsa

Answer: C

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 11.1 — Place utility closes the geographic gap. Time, form, and possession utilities each close a different gap. There is no "value utility" in the chapter's taxonomy.

Tags: channel utilities; place utility; physical availability; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 2

A new premium pet-food brand is outselling its nearest rival on mental availability scores among dog owners, but its quarterly revenue growth is flattening while the rival's is accelerating. Ehrenberg-Bass research most directly suggests the flatline growth is correlated with _______.

A) declining brand equity as the premium segment matures B) inadequate trade promotion investment in the push channel C) loss of physical availability — the brand is no longer on the shelf when buyers arrive D) a weaker pull strategy relative to the rival's social-media spend E) the brand's failure to move from selective to intensive distribution

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 11.1 — Ehrenberg-Bass research shows that declining brands lose physical availability first and mental availability second, because a delisting is immediate while mental availability decays slowly. The scenario's flatline despite strong mental availability scores points to a distribution gap, not a brand-equity gap.

Tags: physical availability; mental availability; Ehrenberg-Bass; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 3

A craft soda sells for $3.00 on the grocery shelf. The retailer takes a 40 percent margin and the wholesaler takes a 20 percent margin. What does the maker receive from the wholesaler per can?

A) $1.44 B) $1.20 C) $1.80 D) $1.08 E) $0.36

Answer: A

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 11.3 — The channel is priced backward. The retailer keeps 40 percent of $3.00 = $1.20, paying the wholesaler $1.80. The wholesaler keeps 20 percent of $1.80 = $0.36, paying the maker $1.44.

Tags: price waterfall; channel arithmetic; backward pricing; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 4

Using the same $3.00 craft soda from Item 3 (40 percent retailer margin, 20 percent wholesaler margin), the maker's own cost to produce a can is $1.08. What is the maker's margin per can when sold through the two-tier channel, and what would it be if the brand sold direct at the same $3.00 shelf price?

A) Two-tier: $0.36; direct: $1.92 B) Two-tier: $1.44; direct: $3.00 C) Two-tier: $0.64; direct: $2.00 D) Two-tier: $0.36; direct: $3.00 E) Two-tier: $1.08; direct: $1.92

Answer: A

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 11.3 — Two-tier: the maker receives $1.44 minus the $1.08 cost = $0.36. Direct: the maker keeps the full $3.00 minus $1.08 cost = $1.92. The $1.56 gap is the cost of two-tier distribution, or equivalently, the value the channel partners provide for the maker.

Tags: price waterfall; margin arithmetic; direct vs indirect; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 5

A manager argues: "If we cut our distributor and sell direct, we eliminate the 30 percent margin he takes and keep it ourselves." According to the Iron Law our chapter names, which statement best identifies the flaw in this argument?

A) Distributors earn more than 30 percent once trade-promotion costs are factored in. B) Cutting a distributor shifts the functions he was performing — inventory, delivery, returns, sales calls — onto the manufacturer's own P&L, replacing a margin cost with a cash cost. C) Going direct lowers the retail price because there are fewer tiers, which reduces total revenue. D) The distributor's 30 percent margin is tax-deductible, so the after-tax saving is smaller than it appears. E) Direct selling reduces brand equity because consumers associate premium brands with independent retail.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 11.2 — The Iron Law states that cutting a channel partner does not eliminate the functions the partner performed; it moves those functions onto the manufacturer's books, where they are paid for in cash rather than margin. The margin saved is often absorbed by the new operating costs.

Tags: Iron Law; direct vs indirect; channel functions; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 6

A national snack-food brand sells its chips in approximately 200,000 U.S. retail outlets, from major grocery chains to convenience stores to airport kiosks. Which intensity strategy does this describe, and why is it appropriate for this category?

A) Exclusive — the brand wants full control over the service experience at each outlet B) Selective — the brand chooses outlets whose format supports premium positioning C) Intensive — the product is purchased frequently in a variety of locations, so maximum outlet penetration is the correct intensity rung D) Administered — the brand's VMS coordinates all outlets through contractual power E) Penetration — the brand is still in the launch phase, so wide placement is the temporary strategy

Answer: C

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 11.5 — Intensive distribution places a product in as many outlets as possible. Snack foods are the chapter's canonical intensive-distribution category because they are purchased frequently, on impulse, in many locations, and any outlet a buyer walks past without seeing the brand is a lost sale.

Tags: intensive distribution; intensity ladder; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 7

Hermès limits its leather-goods and silk distribution to approximately 305 company-owned stores worldwide and declines all wholesale requests. Which intensity strategy does this illustrate, and what is the primary trade-off the brand accepts?

A) Selective — the brand accepts some loss of control in exchange for broader coverage B) Exclusive — the brand accepts narrower coverage in exchange for control over the service experience and brand equity C) Intensive — the brand's high price signals scarcity even at high outlet counts D) Administered VMS — the brand controls coverage through contractual coordination rather than ownership E) Horizontal marketing system — Hermès partners with other luxury firms to jointly cover distribution gaps

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 11.5 — Exclusive distribution severely limits outlets, often to a single dealer per territory or owned stores only. Hermès sacrifices reach for control over service level, brand environment, and equity, which is the core trade-off the chapter maps on the intensity ladder.

Tags: exclusive distribution; intensity ladder; luxury brands; Hermès; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 8

Sherwin-Williams owns more than 4,800 paint retail stores in addition to manufacturing the paint. Disney routes the same characters through theaters, streaming on Disney+, owned stores, Best Buy shelves, and theme parks. Both firms exemplify a _______ vertical marketing system.

A) Contractual VMS — both rely on franchise agreements to coordinate the channel B) Administered VMS — both coordinate the channel through brand power rather than ownership C) Corporate VMS — both combine successive stages of production and distribution under single ownership D) Horizontal marketing system — both partner with unrelated firms at the same channel level E) Reverse-flow channel — both move product back upstream from consumer to manufacturer

Answer: C

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 11.4 — A corporate VMS combines production and distribution under single ownership. Sherwin-Williams makes the paint and owns the stores. Disney controls production, streaming, parks, and retail under one corporate umbrella. Both are textbook corporate VMS cases.

Tags: corporate VMS; vertical marketing system; Disney; Sherwin-Williams; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 9

McDonald's, Ace Hardware, and True Value each integrate independent firms through signed agreements that specify operating standards, licensing terms, and revenue-sharing. Which type of vertical marketing system do all three represent?

A) Corporate VMS B) Administered VMS C) Contractual VMS D) Horizontal marketing system E) Reverse-flow channel

Answer: C

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 11.4 — A contractual VMS consists of independent firms at different channel levels integrating their programs on a contractual basis. Franchise organizations (McDonald's) and retailer cooperatives (Ace, True Value) are the chapter's canonical examples.

Tags: contractual VMS; franchise; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 10

A 2006 meta-analysis pooling 94 studies and roughly 38,000 channel relationships found that which two of the five French-Raven channel powers produced the largest and most durable empirical lift on seller performance?

A) Coercive and reward, because they produce measurable short-term compliance B) Reward and legitimate, because they are grounded in written contracts C) Expert and referent, because they are belief-based and harder for partners to withdraw D) Legitimate and coercive, because both are objectively observable in writing E) Referent and reward, because partners respond to both pride and monetary incentives

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 11.4 — The chapter cites a 2006 meta-analysis showing expert (specialized knowledge the partner depends on) and referent (pride of association) power produce larger and more durable lift than coercive and reward levers. Both depend on belief; the cost of defection is status, not money, which makes them more durable.

Tags: French-Raven power taxonomy; expert power; referent power; channel management; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 11

Walmart holds so much of the average manufacturer's business that it can threaten a SKU with delisting on the next planogram review if the manufacturer does not comply with its demands. According to our chapter, which type of channel power is Walmart exercising, and what is the documented downside of relying on this lever?

A) Reward power; the downside is that rewards become baselines the payer must sustain in perpetuity B) Coercive power; the downside is that it breeds resentment, withheld information, and grudging cooperation that shows up later under stress C) Referent power; the downside is that it erodes once the partner finds an equally prestigious alternative D) Legitimate power; the downside is that it becomes unenforceable once the contract expires E) Expert power; the downside is that the partner eventually acquires the same expertise and the leverage disappears

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 11.4 — Coercive power threatens a worse outcome if the partner does not comply. The chapter notes it can be effective in the short run but breeds the resentment that later appears as withheld information and grudging cooperation. Walmart's implied delisting is the textbook coercive-power example.

Tags: coercive power; French-Raven; channel conflict; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 12

A beverage brand uses heavy trade-promotion spending, slotting fees, and sales-force incentives aimed at grocery chains and convenience stores, so those retailers push the product to shoppers. A competing brand invests instead in national TV advertising and social media that build consumer demand, so shoppers ask for the product at retail. Which strategies are the two brands respectively using?

A) Push and pull B) Pull and push C) Vertical integration and horizontal cooperation D) Intensive and selective distribution E) Corporate VMS and contractual VMS

Answer: A

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 11.5 — Push directs marketing investment at channel members, who then push the product to buyers. Pull directs investment at buyers, who then pull the product through the channel. The beverage brand spending on trade promotion is pushing; the rival building consumer demand is pulling.

Tags: push vs pull; trade promotion; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 13

A footwear brand sells through its own brand.com website, a flagship store in Manhattan, four authorized wholesale partners, and Amazon. Three of those channels show different prices for the same SKU during a weekend sale, the authorized partners' systems do not show stock from the brand's warehouse, and a buyer who bought online cannot return at the flagship store. Which term describes the root cause of these problems?

A) Channel-length fragmentation — too many tiers between the maker and the buyer B) Multichannel operation without omni-channel integration — multiple channels running in parallel without a unified inventory, data, or return policy C) Vertical conflict between the manufacturer and authorized partners D) Administered VMS breakdown — brand power is insufficient to enforce standards E) Intensive over-extension — too many outlets have diluted service quality

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 11.6 — Multichannel means selling through multiple channels; omni-channel means those channels share one inventory, one customer record, one price ladder, and one return policy. The scenario describes a multichannel brand that has not yet integrated. The chapter distinguishes the two explicitly.

Tags: omni-channel vs multichannel; channel integration; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 14

A brand manager wants to resolve a conflict with independent retailers who are angry that the brand's new direct-to-consumer website is undercutting their prices. The manager proposes paying the retailers a commission on every online sale within their geographic zip code, whether the buyer found the product on the brand's site or in the store. Which conflict-response tactic does this represent?

A) Superordinate goals — aligning all parties around a shared goal that transcends the conflict B) Co-optation — including the retailers on an advisory board to win their support C) Dual compensation — paying existing channel partners for sales made through the new channel D) Employee exchange — rotating staff between the manufacturer and the retailer E) Arbitration — referring the dispute to a neutral third party for binding resolution

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 11.6 — Dual compensation pays existing channel partners for sales made through the new channel. The chapter identifies it as one of the mechanisms that smooths the transition to multichannel selling by making the online channel a revenue source for, rather than a competitor of, existing partners.

Tags: dual compensation; channel conflict; omni-channel; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 15

A manufacturer invites its top five distributors to join the firm's national advisory committee, which reviews channel policy and has input on product-launch timing. The chapter identifies this tactic as _____, and describes its goal as _____.

A) Employee exchange; rotating staff to build cross-organizational empathy B) Co-optation; winning the support of partner leaders by including them in decision-making C) Superordinate goals; identifying a shared objective that transcends the conflict D) Dual compensation; aligning partner economics with the manufacturer's new channel investments E) Joint membership; encouraging both parties to join the same industry association

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 11.6 — Co-optation is an effort by one organization to win the support of the leaders of another by including them in advisory councils, boards, or policy committees. The goal is to reduce conflict by making potential opponents into invested insiders.

Item 16

Stanley did not commission the Instagram account The Buy Guide or the #StanleyTumbler TikTok wave that drove the Quencher from near-discontinuation in 2018 to approximately $750M in revenue by 2023. According to our chapter, once a brand discovers a high-performing channel it did not authorize, the correct response is _______.

A) Impose brand controls immediately to prevent equity dilution B) Terminate the channel to avoid setting a precedent for unauthorized distribution C) Feed the channel rather than gatekeep it, because the channel is producing the inflection the commissioned channels were not D) Convert the channel to exclusive distribution to preserve the discovery advantage E) Acquire the channel operator to bring the distribution in-house

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 11.2 — The Stanley case teaches that when an uncommissioned channel produces the inflection, the move is to feed it rather than gatekeep it. Gating it cuts the growth engine; feeding it means authorizing product supply, joining the conversation, and tracking the metrics.

Item 17

A mid-size apparel brand is deciding whether to list on Amazon as a third-party seller or to sell directly to Amazon as a first-party vendor. Under the sell-through-Amazon arrangement (third-party), which of the following does the brand retain that it gives up under the sell-to-Amazon arrangement (first-party)?

A) The retail margin above the wholesale price B) Control over its own retail price, customer data from each transaction, and the ability to respond to buyer reviews C) The ability to advertise within Amazon's sponsored-product network D) The product's eligibility for Amazon Prime shipping E) Preferred shelf placement in Amazon's algorithmic search results

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 11.7 — Selling through Amazon (third-party) means the brand sets its own price, fulfills the order, and owns the buyer interaction data. Selling to Amazon (first-party) means Amazon sets the retail price, holds the inventory, and owns the transaction data. The brand gains simplicity and Prime eligibility at the cost of price control, data, and relationship with the buyer.

Item 18

A brand sells the same product at $49.99 on its own website, $44.99 through Amazon (first-party, with Amazon controlling the price), and $52.99 at a specialty retail partner. The specialty retailer contacts the brand to complain. Which type of channel conflict is this, and what structural condition caused it?

A) Horizontal conflict caused by two retailers at the same tier pricing the same product differently, with the cause being the brand's sell-to-Amazon arrangement surrendering price control B) Vertical conflict between the manufacturer and its retailers, caused by the manufacturer setting an unfair minimum advertised price C) Multichannel conflict arising from having too many channels, which the brand should resolve by cutting one tier D) Corporate VMS breakdown because the brand does not own its retail channels E) Bullwhip conflict because the price variation reflects upstream inventory shocks

Answer: A

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 11.7 / LO 11.6 — Two channels at the same retail level (specialty partner and Amazon) showing different prices for the same SKU is horizontal conflict. The root cause is the brand's first-party Amazon arrangement, which surrendered price-setting to Amazon, creating a price floor the specialty partner cannot match.

Item 19

During COVID-19, a large electronics manufacturer ran its supply chain on a strict just-in-time discipline, holding minimal buffer stock. When a single semiconductor supplier in Taiwan had to shut a line for six weeks, assembly lines in three countries stopped, and shelves stayed empty for four months. According to our chapter, the mechanism that amplifies a small upstream shock into a large downstream stockout is called _______.

A) The last-mile effect B) The bullwhip effect C) Supply-chain myopia D) The Tower fragility problem E) Channel-length amplification

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 11.8 — The bullwhip effect is the amplification of order variance as demand information travels upstream: a small fluctuation in end consumption becomes a larger swing in retailer orders, a larger one still in distributor orders, and the largest of all in manufacturer orders. JIT removes buffer stock, so the bullwhip has nothing to absorb it.

Tags: bullwhip effect; JIT fragility; supply chain; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 20

According to Hau Lee's Triple-A supply chain framework cited in our chapter, a brand that holds safety stock pre-positioned at key choke points and shares point-of-sale data with suppliers in near real time is building _____, while a brand that qualifies second-source suppliers and can redesign its supply-chain structure when demand shifts permanently is building _____.

A) Adaptability; agility B) Alignment; agility C) Agility; adaptability D) Agility; alignment E) Adaptability; alignment

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 11.8 — Agility is the capacity to respond to short-term shocks within hours using shared data and safety stock. Adaptability is the capacity to redesign the chain when shifts are structural, requiring second-source suppliers and contract flexibility. Alignment means contracts share risk and gain across tiers.

Tags: Triple-A supply chain; agility; adaptability; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 21

Costco's channel design features: membership-gated entry at $65 Gold Star; roughly 3,800 SKUs in bulk packaging; a 14 percent ceiling on branded markups; 914 warehouses, of which about 70 percent of households earning over $50,000 live within ten miles; and almost no traditional advertising. These four decisions (membership fee, narrow assortment, markup cap, and warehouse density) are best described as _______.

A) Independent channel decisions that each optimize a single distribution objective B) An interlocking system where each decision reinforces the others, so removing any one breaks the whole model C) Examples of a push-heavy strategy that trades pull investment for trade-promotion efficiency D) An administered VMS structure in which Costco's brand power coordinates suppliers without ownership E) A penetration strategy designed to build household trial through low price and wide availability

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 11.2 / LO 11.5 — The chapter's Costco case teaches that a well-designed channel is an interlocking system, not a list of independent choices. The membership fee enables the markup cap; the narrow assortment enables the fee revenue model; the warehouse density enables the bulk format. Pulling any one piece breaks the logic of the others.

Tags: Costco; channel design; interlocking system; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 22

Trader Joe's produces roughly $2,000 to $2,100 in revenue per square foot annually against an industry average near $600. The chapter attributes this gap to four decisions that lock together: narrow assortment, roughly 80 percent private-label share, no loyalty program or e-commerce, and fast new-SKU rotation. A rival chain wants to copy only one element, the fast new-SKU rotation. According to the chapter's logic, this partial copy will likely _______.

A) Produce the same revenue-per-square-foot advantage, because SKU rotation is the single most important driver B) Fail to close the gap, because the economic advantage comes from the four decisions interlocking, and running one without the others produces a fragmented system C) Succeed only if the rival also abandons its loyalty program, since that is the second most important element D) Accelerate the rival's revenue per square foot, because SKU velocity is the main signal buyers respond to E) Backfire because SKU rotation increases working-capital costs that outweigh any demand benefit

Answer: B

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 11.2 — The Trader Joe's case demonstrates that retail-format economics are not the sum of independent feature choices. The moat comes from the system, not from any single piece. Fast SKU rotation without the narrow assortment, private-label dominance, and absence of digital overhead cannot replicate the economics because the four pieces are interdependent.

Tags: Trader Joe's; channel design; interlocking system; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Section II — Scenario / Short-Answer Items (4 items)

SA-1

A founder is launching a new premium hot sauce brand. Her product costs $1.20 to make per bottle. She wants a 25 percent maker margin on every unit. She is considering two channel routes. Route A sells through a specialty retailer at a standard 40 percent retail margin and a regional distributor at a standard 25 percent margin, with a target retail price of $8.99. Route B sells direct through her own e-commerce site at $8.99, with $1.50 in fulfillment and packaging cost per order on top of the $1.20 product cost.

(a) Use backward price-waterfall arithmetic to calculate the maker's revenue per bottle under Route A. Does she hit her 25 percent maker margin?

(b) What does she keep per bottle under Route B after fulfillment? Does Route B hit her target margin?

(c) Name the Iron Law and explain what additional cost the founder must absorb in Route B that the distributor and retailer were absorbing for her in Route A.

Sample answer:

(a) Route A backward pricing: Shelf price = $8.99. Retailer takes 40 percent: $8.99 x 0.40 = $3.60, leaving $5.39 paid to the distributor. Distributor takes 25 percent of $5.39 = $1.35, leaving $4.04 paid to the maker. Maker's margin: ($4.04 - $1.20) / $4.04 = 70 percent gross margin on the unit revenue, which exceeds the 25 percent target. However, the maker receives only $4.04 per bottle, not the full $8.99, meaning the two tiers took $4.95 per bottle in aggregate.

(b) Route B: Maker receives $8.99 minus $1.50 fulfillment minus $1.20 product cost = $6.29 net. Margin = ($8.99 - $1.50 - $1.20) / $8.99 = about 70 percent of the retail price, a far larger absolute dollar return per unit. Route B exceeds the margin target.

(c) The Iron Law states that going direct does not eliminate the functions a partner would have performed; it moves those functions onto the manufacturer's own books, where they are paid for in cash rather than margin. In Route A, the distributor was performing warehousing, order fulfillment, sales calls to retailers, returns processing, and route delivery. In Route B, the founder must fund all of those functions herself through the $1.50 fulfillment cost (and potentially more for customer acquisition, returns, and demand generation). If the founder scales and her fulfillment, marketing, and returns costs rise, the advantage over Route A may compress or reverse.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 11.3, LO 11.2

Tags: price waterfall; backward pricing; Iron Law; direct vs indirect; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

SA-2

A mid-size cosmetics brand sells through three channels: its own brand.com website, a Sephora wholesale partnership (250 U.S. doors), and as a third-party seller on Amazon. The brand manager is reviewing a report showing three problems: (1) the same mascara SKU is priced at $22 on brand.com, $20.99 at Sephora online, and $19.49 on Amazon because Amazon's algorithm periodically lowers prices to match other sellers; (2) a buyer who purchased on brand.com cannot exchange the product at Sephora without a receipt showing Sephora as the original seller; (3) each platform uses a separate customer identity system, so the brand cannot tell if the buyer who bought once on Amazon has also purchased three times on brand.com.

Name the two types of channel conflict present in this scenario and explain the structural source of each. Then propose one actionable remedy for each.

Sample answer:

Conflict 1 — Multichannel or horizontal conflict: The same SKU is priced differently across three retail channels (brand.com, Sephora, Amazon). The structural source is the brand's sell-through-Amazon arrangement, where it listed as a third-party seller and Amazon's repricing algorithm undercut the MAP (minimum advertised price) without violating any obligation, because Amazon is not party to the brand's MAP agreement with Sephora. This creates horizontal conflict between Sephora and Amazon's price point. Remedy: Convert the Amazon presence from third-party sell-through to a branded direct storefront with strict MAP enforcement tied to the brand's own pricing API, or withdraw from third-party Amazon and use the brand.com and Sephora channels only, eliminating the algorithmic repricing source.

Conflict 2 — Vertical conflict (manufacturer vs retail partner): The return-policy mismatch creates a vertical conflict between the brand and Sephora, because Sephora cannot honor the brand's implied customer promise of easy returns, and the brand's own promise to its buyer is not being fulfilled. The structural source is the absence of a unified return policy in the wholesale agreement. Remedy: Negotiate a returns-parity clause in the Sephora wholesale contract that commits Sephora to accepting brand.com purchases for exchange, funded through a credit mechanism the brand issues to Sephora for each cross-channel return processed.

The identity fragmentation across platforms is a missing omni-channel component (single customer record), not a conflict per se, but it prevents the brand from diagnosing the conflicts above and should be addressed through a customer data platform that stitches the three identifiers into one persistent record.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 11.6, LO 11.7

Tags: multichannel conflict; vertical conflict; sell-through Amazon; omni-channel; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Reflective thinking

SA-3

A new energy-drink brand is deciding between three distribution intensities for its U.S. launch: (1) exclusive distribution through one national health-and-fitness retail chain, (2) selective distribution through specialty health retailers and upscale gyms, or (3) intensive distribution targeting every convenience store, gas station, grocery, and mass merchandiser it can access from day one.

Using the six conditions our chapter lists under "sell direct or through someone" and the reach-vs-control trade-off, explain which intensity rung best fits the brand's launch stage and why. Name the one failure mode the chapter describes that the brand should guard against most carefully as it scales.

Sample answer:

The chapter's six conditions for a direct or narrow channel are: concentrated large buyers, complex or high-touch products, products that are destination purchases, producer size and brand pull sufficient to fund the channel, proximity of production to consumption, and a competitive-experience-driven strategy. The energy drink is a low-complexity, impulse-bought product with no installation or demonstration need, and the brand lacks the brand pull and capital to fund a zero-tier or exclusive-only presence at launch. That points away from exclusive distribution.

Between selective and intensive, the right choice at launch depends on whether the brand is positioning as a premium product (selective fits because it controls the brand's retail neighbors and avoids devaluation from mass placement) or a challenger brand competing on availability (intensive fits for reach). For a new entrant, selective distribution through specialty health retailers and gyms makes sense because: (a) the brand's equity is not yet proven, so controlling the shelf context matters; (b) the health-and-fitness format signals product credibility to the early-adopter segment; and (c) adding intensive later is easier than pulling back from intensive once the brand is over-extended.

The failure mode to guard against is over-extension on intensity: the chapter's Dial-A-Mattress case shows that climbing the intensity ladder faster than brand equity can absorb leads to secondary locations that cannot cover their fixed cost. An energy drink that pushes into every convenience store before building pull faces low sell-through rates, return pressure from retail buyers, and eventual delisting, which is harder to recover from than not having gained the doors in the first place.

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 11.2, LO 11.5

Tags: intensity ladder; exclusive, selective, intensive; channel design; failure modes; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Reflective thinking

SA-4

A specialty outdoor-gear brand has operated a just-in-time supply chain for ten years, holding near-zero buffer stock across its three-tier channel. In early 2026, a factory fire at its sole-source tent-pole supplier in Vietnam shut production for eight weeks. Shelves went empty for three months across its entire retail network while the brand scrambled to qualify a backup supplier.

Define the bullwhip effect and explain how JIT made the disruption worse. Then apply the Triple-A test (agility, adaptability, alignment) from our chapter and recommend one concrete action under each dimension that would reduce the brand's exposure to a similar shock.

Sample answer:

The bullwhip effect is the amplification of order variance as demand information travels upstream through a multi-tier supply chain. A small fluctuation in what end consumers buy becomes a larger swing in retailer orders, a larger swing in distributor orders, and the largest swing of all in what the manufacturer orders from raw-material suppliers. Under JIT, there is no buffer inventory at any tier to absorb that amplification, so a single upstream disruption propagates immediately to the shelf.

In this case, JIT made the eight-week supplier shutdown into a three-month shelf disruption because: (a) no safety stock existed at any tier to cover even the first days of outage; (b) with no pre-qualified backup supplier, sourcing an alternative required qualification time that extended the outage; and (c) retailer orders spiked once supply resumed, creating the classic bullwhip overshoot.

Triple-A remedies:

Agility: Pre-position safety stock of the highest-sell-through tent-pole SKUs at the brand's own 3PL warehouse or in vendor-managed inventory at the top two retail partners, sized to cover the longest realistic supplier disruption (six to eight weeks). Fund the carrying cost as a resilience line item in the unit economics.

Adaptability: Qualify at least one backup supplier in a different geography for the top five volume SKUs before the primary supplier fails, so that a production shift takes weeks rather than months. Budget the qualification process as routine annual capital, not a crisis expense.

Alignment: Renegotiate supplier contracts to include shared risk provisions — force majeure clauses that require the supplier to maintain a minimum days-of-supply buffer, and an incentive for the supplier to pre-build safety stock ahead of peak season in exchange for a price guarantee.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 11.8

Tags: bullwhip effect; JIT fragility; Triple-A supply chain; agility, adaptability, alignment; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

Section III — AI-Integration Items (4 items)

These items are original to this course and do not appear in any Kotler bank. They test students' ability to apply AI tools to channel decisions, evaluate model-generated channel audits, and reason about the data and control trade-offs that AI channel tools introduce.

AI-1 — Evaluate an AI Channel-Mix Output

A student asks an AI assistant to audit her brand's channel mix. The model returns the following:

"Your brand sells through five channels: brand.com, Amazon third-party, Target, Sephora, and TikTok Shop. Based on typical CPG benchmarks, brand.com produces the highest margin per unit, Amazon provides the widest reach, Target and Sephora provide physical-availability credibility, and TikTok Shop builds discovery among under-35 buyers. I recommend continuing to invest in all five, scaling brand.com and TikTok Shop as the growth levers."

Which of the following best identifies the most important limitation of this output?

A) The model used CPG benchmarks rather than the brand's own unit economics, so the margin ranking is based on category averages that may not match this brand's actual costs and channel agreements. B) The model did not recommend cutting Amazon, which is always the lowest-margin channel for consumer brands. C) The model ignored the brand's mental availability score, which is the primary driver of channel performance. D) The model's recommendation to scale all five channels is factually incorrect, because the chapter limits brands to a maximum of three channels. E) The model searched the web for competitor channel strategies rather than analyzing the brand's own data.

Answer: A

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 11.9 — The chapter's channel-audit framework requires each channel to be scored on the brand's own unit economics (price, margin, CAC, conflict exposure), not CPG category averages. A model working from benchmarks rather than the brand's own P&L produces generic advice. The output may be directionally plausible but cannot substitute for the actual backward pricing the chapter teaches.

Tags: AI channel audit; unit economics; model limitations; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

AI-2 — AI-Assisted Seam Detection

A student runs the chapter's omni-channel seam detector on an apparel brand. The model returns the following diagnosis:

"Channel seams detected: (1) The brand's app and website show different stock levels for the same SKU during peak shopping hours, suggesting two separate inventory pools rather than one unified pool. (2) Returns purchased on the app are not accepted in-store, creating a negative buyer experience seam. (3) The brand's TikTok Shop product page shows a price $4 lower than the brand's own website, which may trigger MAP violations with wholesale partners. I recommend fixing inventory pooling first, as it affects the largest share of buyers."

Identify the one omni-channel component from our chapter's six-item scoring rubric that seam (1) most directly violates. Then identify which seam is most likely to generate horizontal channel conflict with the brand's wholesale partners, and explain why.

Sample answer:

Seam (1) violates the single-inventory component. Our chapter's six-component omni-channel scoring rubric includes single inventory as the first and most foundational element, because two channels showing different stock for the same SKU means the brand's supply cannot be routed efficiently to the buyer's chosen channel and buyers who reserve online cannot be sure of pickup availability. All other integration benefits depend on a unified inventory pool underneath them.

Seam (3) generates horizontal channel conflict with wholesale partners. The brand's TikTok Shop price is $4 below the brand's own website, and the chapter notes that any channel running below the brand's MAP creates a price-comparison pressure that punishes the wholesale partners who are holding the full price. The wholesale partner loses buyers to the TikTok Shop link, which undermines the economics that justify stocking the brand. This is exactly the multichannel price-conflict scenario the chapter identifies as the most common form of horizontal conflict in 2026, where DTC or social-commerce channels undercut the retail partners who believed they were selling at parity.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 11.6 — The item requires students to apply the chapter's six-component omni-channel rubric and the conflict taxonomy to a specific AI-generated output, then explain the causal mechanism behind one conflict type.

Tags: omni-channel components; single inventory; horizontal conflict; AI seam detector; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

AI-3 — Channel Skill Design

The chapter describes a channel-audit Skill that can be loaded with a brand's own data and wired to its live SKU and channel-pricing feed, running the audit on a schedule rather than by hand. A marketing manager asks: "Can I just run this with the publicly available version of the AI and skip building the Skill?" Which of the following best describes what the publicly available model can and cannot do in this context?

A) The public model can run the full audit identically because it searches the web in real time and therefore has access to the same data a wired Skill would have. B) The public model can produce a structurally complete channel diagnosis by web-searching the brand's current channels, but it cannot access the brand's own internal unit economics, SKU-level margins, customer data, or channel conflict history, so the output is a plausible first-pass hypothesis rather than a decision-ready audit. C) The public model is incapable of diagnosing channel conflicts because channel data is proprietary and not available on the public web. D) The public model can replace the Skill entirely, because the Skill only adds a branded interface rather than any new analytical capability. E) The public model produces a worse diagnosis than the Skill because the model's training data is from 2023 and does not include 2026 channel developments.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 11.9 — The chapter is explicit that a live-web AI search produces a candidate read of the brand's public channel mix but cannot access the brand's own P&L, margin schedules, customer records, or conflict history. A wired Skill closes that gap by connecting to the brand's own data through tool connectors. The public model's output is a useful first-pass hypothesis, not a substitute for internal data.

Tags: AI channel Skill; public model vs wired Skill; channel audit limits; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

AI-4 — AI Route-and-Channel Optimization

A DTC snack brand is considering using an AI tool that claims to optimize its channel mix automatically, recommending which channels to scale, throttle, or exit based on a real-time feed of sales velocity, margin, and customer acquisition data. The brand's CMO says: "If the AI recommends exiting Amazon, we just exit. The tool has better data than we do." According to the channel-management principles in our chapter, which concern should the CMO address before delegating the exit decision to the model?

A) The AI tool cannot recommend exiting a channel because channel decisions require human approval under FTC platform-neutrality regulations. B) An AI model optimizing for short-run sales velocity and margin may recommend exiting channels that serve strategic functions the model is not scoring, such as physical-availability credibility, retail-media learning, or the data the brand collects by being on the platform. C) Exiting Amazon is always a mistake for DTC brands, because Amazon accounts for more than 50 percent of U.S. e-commerce search and a brand not present there loses discovery. D) The model's recommendation is valid only if the channel data feed is from the prior twelve months; older data underweights seasonal demand signals. E) The AI tool should be trusted on scaling decisions but not on exit decisions because exit decisions require shareholder approval under standard corporate governance rules.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 11.9 / LO 11.7 — An AI optimization tool trained on quantitative channel metrics (sales velocity, margin, CAC) will miss the strategic channel functions the chapter teaches: physical-availability credibility, the discovery layer a platform provides, the first-party data the brand collects through the channel, and the conflict implications of exiting. Amazon may look low-margin in a short-run model while serving as the safety-net channel that captures buyers who searched there. The CMO's instinct to delegate the full decision to the model assumes the model is scoring everything the channel does, which it almost certainly is not.

Tags: AI channel optimization; strategic channel functions; Amazon; decision automation; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Analytical thinking

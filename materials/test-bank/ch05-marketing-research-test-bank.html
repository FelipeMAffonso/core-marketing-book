Chapter 5 Marketing Research — Test Bank

Chapter: 5 — Marketing Research

Learning Objectives (Ch5, Our Chapter)

  • LO 5.1 Define marketing research and explain why it exists: research earns its keep only when it removes enough doubt to change a decision, and its budget ceiling is EVPI.
  • LO 5.2 Distinguish primary from secondary data, identify the four bins of secondary data, and explain the secondary-first discipline.
  • LO 5.3 Identify the three modes of marketing research (qualitative, quantitative, observational) and match each mode to the question shape that calls for it.
  • LO 5.4 Apply the mechanics of a quantitative study: scale construction (Cronbach's alpha), sample size (the Cochran formula and the square-root law), and the four commercial survey types.
  • LO 5.5 Explain qualitative research methods (in-depth interviews, focus groups, ethnography, conjoint analysis) and the saturation rule.
  • LO 5.6 Describe observational research and experimental design, including A/B tests, the A/A sanity check, pre-registration, and the Type S / Type M error problem.
  • LO 5.7 Describe the four-tier AI workflow for research and explain the AAPOR disclosure standard for AI-touched studies.
  • LO 5.8 Identify and name the six major research failure modes (selection bias, expectancy bias, observer effect, leading-question bias, non-response bias, stopping-early) and state the plain fix for each.

Section I — Multiple-Choice Items (22 items)

Item 1

The AMA defines marketing research as systematically gathering, analyzing, and interpreting information to support a decision someone is about to make. The word systematically in that definition primarily rules out _______.

A) studies that use random sampling B) asking a few colleagues in the office for their opinions C) external research vendors with standardized panels D) quantitative studies that involve statistical significance tests E) secondary data collected by government agencies

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 5.1 — "Systematically" rules out informal opinion-gathering. The phrase "to support a decision" separates research from curiosity.

Tags: definition; marketing research; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 2

A brand manager at a regional food company is deciding whether to launch a new line of frozen burritos. The cost of launching the wrong flavor combination is estimated at $300,000, and the team believes it is 25 percent likely that its current flavor preference is wrong. What is the ceiling (EVPI) on what the firm should pay for research on this decision?

A) $7,500 B) $30,000 C) $75,000 D) $300,000 E) $1,200,000

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 5.1 — EVPI = cost of being wrong times the chance of being wrong = $300,000 x 0.25 = $75,000. The 2-to-10 percent rule for a single study would place the study budget at $1,500 to $7,500.

Tags: EVPI; research budget ceiling; Hubbard; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 3

A beverage startup wants to understand how it compares to Liquid Death and Topo Chico in the premium sparkling water category. The team's first move should be to _______.

A) commission a 1,200-person online survey from Prolific at $2.50 per complete B) run a focus group of 8 loyal buyers to hear what they value most C) scan the competitors' 10-Ks, earnings calls, and open job postings before spending anything on primary research D) launch an A/B test comparing two bottle-label designs on the company's website E) build a conjoint study to measure buyer willingness to pay for carbonation level

Answer: C

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 5.2 — Secondary-first discipline: a competitor's 10-K, earnings call, and open job postings are external-and-free secondary data that the team should exhaust before commissioning anything.

Tags: secondary data; secondary-first discipline; competitive intelligence; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 4

A firm's secondary data falls into four bins depending on two questions: is the data already inside the firm, and does it cost anything to reach? Which of the following correctly pairs a data source with its bin?

A) Nielsen category-level scanner data — internal and free B) The firm's own CRM and sales records — external and paid C) U.S. Census demographic tables — external and free D) A competitor's Qualtrics survey results — internal and paid E) Prolific panel responses commissioned last quarter — external and free

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 5.2 — Census data is public, costs nothing to reach, and lives outside the firm: external and free. Nielsen is external and paid. The firm's CRM is internal and free. A competitor's survey results are generally inaccessible, not an owned asset.

Tags: secondary data; four-bin matrix; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 5

The Stanley brand team in 2018 read its own sales dashboard, saw that the Quencher line was slow, and recommended discontinuing it. A quiet internal signal pointed the other way but never reached the strategy conversation. The core research failure here is best described as _______.

A) expectancy bias, because the team's positive attitude toward the brand distorted the analysis B) selection bias, because the dashboard sampled only the buyers the brand already had and missed the women about to make the product a phenomenon C) non-response bias, because women did not answer the brand's customer surveys D) leading-question bias, because the team asked buyers loaded questions about the product E) the Type M error, because the team stopped the analysis before the sample was large enough

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 5.8 — Stanley's dashboard was real data from the wrong sample. That is selection bias: the sample systematically excluded the population the decision was actually about.

Tags: selection bias; Stanley; failure modes; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 6

A researcher needs to find out why a new line of athletic socks is failing with women runners aged 25 to 40. She does not yet know enough to write a useful survey question. The correct research mode is _______.

A) quantitative, because she needs a number to take to management B) observational, because she should watch women choose socks in-store C) qualitative, because she needs words about reasoning before she can write good survey questions D) experimental, because she needs to rule out competing explanations E) descriptive, because she wants to estimate the size of the market

Answer: C

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 5.3 — Questions answered with words about reasoning are qualitative. The researcher does not yet know the right question to ask at scale, so qualitative goes first.

Tags: three modes; qualitative research; research design; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 7

A brand tracker shows that Stanley's premium-quality score dropped 2 points this quarter. A manager wants to order a packaging redesign based on that move. The right question to ask before commissioning any design work is _______.

A) How large is the competitor's share-of-shelf in the key retailers? B) What is the Cronbach's alpha of the quality scale, and has it held above 0.70? C) Has the team run a Van Westendorp price-sensitivity meter on the current lineup? D) Which Prolific demographic filter should the team use to screen the next survey wave? E) What would the Net Promoter Score be if the packaging were changed?

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 5.4 — Before acting on a tracker movement, verify the measurement instrument. A scale below 0.70 on Cronbach's alpha is reading mostly static, so the 2-point drop may not reflect a real shift in buyer perception.

Tags: Cronbach's alpha; scale reliability; measurement; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 8

A marketing researcher builds a four-item scale to measure "brand trust" and runs a pilot on 200 respondents. She finds a Cronbach's alpha of 0.62. The correct interpretation is _______.

A) The scale is acceptable; 0.62 is above the industry floor of 0.50. B) The scale is reading mostly static; 0.62 is below the 0.70 floor for a new scale, and the researcher should purify or rebuild it before fielding. C) The scale is excellent; any alpha above 0.60 confirms high reliability. D) The alpha is too high, suggesting the items are perfectly redundant and one should be dropped. E) The alpha is irrelevant for a four-item scale; minimum item count for alpha to apply is seven.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 5.4 — Cronbach's alpha floor is 0.70 for a new scale and 0.80 for an established one. An alpha of 0.62 is below that floor, meaning roughly 38 percent or more of the scale's movement is noise rather than the construct being measured.

Tags: Cronbach's alpha; scale reliability; measurement; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 9

For a national attitude survey with a worst-case 50-50 split, the Cochran sample-size formula returns 384 respondents for a 5-percentage-point margin of error at 95 percent confidence. A researcher wants to cut the margin in half, to 2.5 points. All else equal, the sample she needs is closest to _______.

A) 192 respondents B) 384 respondents C) 768 respondents D) 1,536 respondents E) 3,072 respondents

Answer: D

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 5.4 — The square-root law: halving the margin of error requires four times the sample, not twice. 384 x 4 = 1,536. This is why the payoff from adding respondents flattens sharply and why over-investment beyond 1,200 rarely changes a decision.

Tags: Cochran formula; sample size; square-root law; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 10

A brand-image survey asks: "How well does 'durable' describe our product, on a scale of 1 to 7?" A brand manager says this question gives her the data for Chapter 6's perceptual map. She is correct because _______.

A) a 1-to-7 Likert item can be used to estimate willingness to pay B) the attribute ratings a brand-image survey collects are exactly the data a perceptual map is plotted from C) brand-image surveys are the same instrument as a customer-satisfaction survey D) a 7-point scale is required by Canvas Common Cartridge before the data can be exported E) the result doubles as a Net Promoter Score calculation

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 5.4 — Our chapter explicitly bridges the brand-image row to Ch6: attribute ratings from a brand-image survey are the input data for a perceptual map.

Tags: brand-image survey; perceptual map; Chapter 6 bridge; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 11

A team conducts focus groups with 8 participants per group to explore why shoppers abandon a gym membership app. The researcher reports: "Three of the seven people in Tuesday's group said the onboarding was too long." The manager immediately tells the development team to shorten the onboarding. What is the primary methodological concern with this response?

A) The focus group should have had at least 20 participants before any conclusion was drawn. B) Focus group results cannot be generalized to the broader population; additional quantitative research is needed before acting on what three people said. C) The finding is invalid because the group was conducted on a Tuesday. D) Three out of seven is a statistical majority and the finding should be actioned immediately. E) The researcher should have conducted individual depth interviews rather than focus groups for this topic.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 5.5 — Qualitative research generates hypotheses; it does not produce generalizable estimates. Acting on three voices out of a small recruited group without quantitative follow-up is the classic misapplication of focus group data.

Tags: focus groups; qualitative limitations; generalizability; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 12

Saturation is the principle that governs when a qualitative study should stop fielding. A researcher reaches saturation when _______.

A) the survey response rate falls below 10 percent B) three consecutive interviews produce only themes already in the codebook, typically between 15 and 25 interviews for consumer products C) the sample size equals 384, the Cochran benchmark D) the vendor's incentive budget is exhausted E) at least one interview from each demographic subgroup has been completed

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 5.5 — Saturation stops qualitative fieldwork when new interviews stop generating new themes. The standard rule is to stop after three interviews in a row add no new codes, usually at 15 to 25 for consumer products.

Tags: saturation; qualitative research; in-depth interviews; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 13

A consumer-products company conducts conjoint analysis with 300 respondents on a new line of reusable water bottles, varying graphic, color, and price. A researcher from one group says all three attributes scored 6 out of 7 in separate rating questions. A conjoint analyst says color and graphic barely matter; price drives most of the choice. The conjoint analyst is more likely correct because _______.

A) rating studies use larger samples than conjoint studies, producing more reliable estimates B) conjoint forces a real trade-off by showing complete product bundles side by side, so a buyer who picks the $25 charcoal over the $20 orange reveals she was willing to give up $5 for that color; a rating costs nothing and inflates every attribute equally C) the researcher's 1-to-7 scale is Likert, which is invalid for physical attributes such as color D) conjoint analysis controls for demographic confounds that rating scales cannot address E) price must always drive choice because it is the only monetary variable in the study

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 5.5 — Conjoint reveals part-worths by forcing real trade-offs between complete bundles. Rating scales let buyers claim they care about everything equally with no cost, so they overstate attribute importance. The Sigma Chi t-shirt example in the chapter makes this explicit.

Tags: conjoint analysis; trade-off; part-worth; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 14

The purpose of an A/A test — running an experiment where both groups see the identical version — is to _______.

A) measure the baseline conversion rate before any new variant is introduced B) confirm that the testing infrastructure produces false positives at roughly the expected 5 percent rate, showing how often noise alone crosses the significance threshold C) estimate the minimum detectable effect for the next real A/B test D) establish a control group that can be compared to multiple treatment variants simultaneously E) comply with pre-registration requirements before the main study

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 5.6 — An A/A test assigns an identical experience to both groups, so any result flagged as significant is a false positive by construction. Running many A/A tests confirms that about 5 percent cross the conventional line on noise alone, which is the Type I error rate made visible.

Tags: A/A test; Type I error; experimentation; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 15

A researcher watches an A/B test and calls the result on day 3 because the early lift looks strong. According to our chapter, the two named distortions she risks are _______.

A) selection bias and leading-question bias B) non-response bias and observer effect C) the Type S error (the winner may point the wrong direction) and the Type M error (the winner's size is likely inflated several times above the true effect) D) Cronbach's alpha decay and saturation error E) the Cochran undercount and the double-barrel problem

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 5.6 — Stopping an A/B test early is the "stopping early" failure mode. The two named distortions from Gelman and Carlin (2014) are Type S (sign: the early winner can point the wrong way) and Type M (magnitude: a barely-significant winner from a small test overstates the effect size several-fold).

Tags: stopping early; Type S error; Type M error; A/B test; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 16

A brand manager wants to measure how loyal buyers are to her product using a single recurring metric. She is describing _______.

A) a brand-awareness survey that captures unaided recall B) the Net Promoter Score, computed as percent promoters minus percent detractors on a 0-to-10 recommendation question C) Cronbach's alpha for the loyalty scale D) the Cochran sample-size benchmark for a weekly tracker E) an A/B test of two versions of the loyalty-program landing page

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 5.4 — Net Promoter Score is the single most-cited loyalty metric in industry: one question (0-to-10 recommendation likelihood), promoters (9-10) minus detractors (0-6), tracked week to week. The chapter uses Stanley's post-purchase NPS as the worked example.

Tags: Net Promoter Score; NPS; brand survey types; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 17

A survey item reads: "How satisfied are you with the quality and price of our product?" A researcher flags this and asks the team to rewrite it. She is applying the _______ rule from the survey-design section of the chapter.

A) saturation rule: stop adding items when they produce no new information B) double-barrel rule: split a question that bundles two distinct constructs into one sentence C) retrieval rule: shorten the recall window to 7 or 30 days D) comprehension rule: anchor vague words to concrete examples E) mapping rule: balance the scale and give the respondent a real middle option

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 5.8 — A double-barreled question bundles two constructs (quality AND price) into one stem, so a respondent who likes the quality but resents the price has no honest answer. Split into two items.

Tags: leading-question bias; double-barrel; survey design; Tourangeau four-stage model; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 18

In 2026, a growing share of responses on cheap online panels come from bots or panelists gaming the incentive. According to the chapter, which of the following is NOT one of the recommended defenses against contaminated panel data?

A) A trap item that a bot is likely to answer incorrectly B) An attention-check question mid-survey C) A speed limit that flags responses faster than a plausible reading rate D) Offering a higher incentive per response to recruit higher-quality panelists E) An open-ended item requiring the panelist to write a free-form answer

Answer: D

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 5.8 — The chapter's layered defense is: trap item, attention check, speed limit, reverse-worded items, and an open-ended write-in. Raising the incentive is not a defense; bot operators charge the same rate and earn more, not less. Panel choice (which vendor) sets the contamination floor more than price does.

Tags: non-response bias; panel fraud; 2026 panel quality; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 19

Patagonia generates continuous qualitative research data through its Worn Wear repair program, where each repair generates a ticket recording the garment, the use case, and what failed. According to our chapter, the primary advantage of routing research through a program the brand already operates is _______.

A) Patagonia avoids legal disclosure requirements by not calling it research B) the research data comes at a lower marginal cost because it rides the repair budget that is already funded as an environmental-mission line C) repair tickets are legally admissible in consumer-protection disputes, unlike survey responses D) the repair data automatically satisfies the AAPOR AI disclosure standard E) the Worn Wear program produces NPS scores that competitors cannot replicate

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 5.1 — The calibration lesson: the EVPI logic applies here. Patagonia's decision cost on product durability is high, so continuous qualitative is justified. Routing it through a program already funded inverts the cost: the research is free as long as repairs run.

Tags: Patagonia; research calibration; qualitative; EVPI; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 20

The four-stage model of how a respondent answers a survey question identifies four cognitive steps in order. A question asks: "How many cups of coffee did you buy at a cafe last year?" According to the chapter, this question breaks at the _______ stage.

A) comprehension, because "cafe" is ambiguous B) retrieval, because buyers cannot accurately count year-long purchase histories and instead estimate a recent rate and multiply, adding noise C) judgment, because the previous question about coffee attitudes biased the answer D) mapping, because the response scale does not offer enough options E) comprehension, because "last year" could mean calendar year or the past 12 months

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 5.8 — Retrieval breaks when the recall window is too long. Buyers estimate a rate (one cup per week) and multiply by the period (52 weeks), compounding estimation noise. The fix is to ask about the last 7 or 30 days.

Tags: four-stage model; Tourangeau; retrieval failure; survey design; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 21

Trader Joe's operates with no loyalty program, no app, no customer account, and no CRM. According to our chapter, this is _______.

A) an accidental gap that has exposed the chain to competitive risk B) a deliberate decision: the brand's cost of being wrong on its brand-identity commitment is low enough to justify near-zero research investment, and its fast-rotating assortment and call-in podcast serve as research instruments in disguise C) evidence that Trader Joe's relies entirely on syndicated panel data from Nielsen instead D) a compliance response to California privacy regulations requiring opt-in before collecting customer data E) the reason Trader Joe's ranked last on the 2025 dunnhumby retailer-preference index

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 5.1, LO 5.2 — The calibration map lesson: Trader Joe's sits in the low-cost/low-research cell. Its brand-identity decision does not depend on customer-level tracking; informal channels substitute for a formal database. The 2025 dunnhumby ranking confirms the strategy has not been punished.

Tags: Trader Joe's; research calibration; secondary-first; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 22

New Coke is the chapter's example of a research failure where the study was run correctly but answered the wrong question. The conceptual error that caused the failure is called _______.

A) selection bias, because the taste-test sample excluded loyal buyers B) the leading-question problem, because testers knew the new formula was on trial C) collapsing the management decision problem with the marketing research problem — the company needed the answer to a brand-loyalty question but ran a blind-taste study D) non-response bias, because only people who liked the new formula agreed to taste it E) stopping early, because Coca-Cola discontinued the test before the full sample was reached

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 5.1 — The management decision problem was: keep the formula or change it? The marketing research problem needed was: what do loyal buyers do when their familiar formula disappears from the shelf? The blind-taste study answered a narrower question correctly, while the real question went unmeasured.

Tags: management decision problem vs marketing research problem; New Coke; research design; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Section II — Scenario / Short-Answer Items (4 items)

SA-1

A product team at a mid-size snack brand is debating which of two new chip flavors to launch: Smoky Habanero or Honey Butter. The VP of Marketing says: "Let's just run a few focus groups and pick the winner based on what people say they love." The research director pushes back and says a conjoint study would give a better answer.

Explain what the research director means by "a better answer." Define conjoint analysis, explain why rating questions inflate attribute importance, and state what type of information conjoint produces that ratings cannot. Use terms from our chapter.

Sample answer: Conjoint analysis reveals part-worths by forcing respondents to make real trade-off choices between complete product bundles shown side by side, rather than rating each attribute in isolation. When a respondent rates "flavor intensity" a 6 out of 7 and "package size" also a 6 out of 7, the rating costs her nothing and tells the team nothing about which attribute she would actually sacrifice when she has to pick between a more intense flavor in a smaller bag versus a milder flavor in a full-size bag. Conjoint makes every question a real trade: the team might show "Smoky Habanero, 8-oz bag, $3.99" versus "Honey Butter, 5-oz bag, $2.99" and force a choice. The response carries weight because the respondent gave up something to get it. The resulting part-worths measure how much each level of each attribute contributed to the buyer's preference in utility units that can be compared across attributes. A focus group or simple rating exercise produces ordinal preference statements ("I love this one") that cannot be used to predict which specific configuration the buyer would actually purchase against a real competitive set, and will almost always overstate the importance of every attribute because rating is low-cost. Conjoint's output is what the launch decision actually needs: a rank-ordered estimate of buyer utility per attribute level and a share simulation across realistic market configurations.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 5.5

Tags: conjoint analysis; part-worth; qualitative vs quantitative; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

SA-2

A startup founders team has $15,000 to research whether to launch a premium reusable coffee mug. Before they spend anything, the chapter's framework requires them to write down two numbers and compute a ceiling.

Name that ceiling and the two numbers it requires. Compute the ceiling for the following scenario: the team estimates that launching the wrong design would cost them $180,000 in wasted production and inventory, and they believe there is a 40 percent chance their current preferred design is the wrong one. Then state what research, if any, the ceiling justifies.

Sample answer: The ceiling is EVPI (Expected Value of Perfect Information), from Hubbard's measurement framework. The two numbers are (1) the cost of being wrong on the decision and (2) the probability that the current leaning is wrong. EVPI = $180,000 x 0.40 = $72,000. The 2-to-10 percent rule for a single study places the budget at $1,440 to $7,200. The team's $15,000 budget exceeds that range but sits below the EVPI ceiling, so they are not paying for theater. However, they should consider whether a smaller study that buys the decision-relevant information could be run for $1,500 to $7,000 rather than spending the full $15,000. A reasonable choice given the target market would be a 20-interview qualitative study (approximately $3,000 to $5,000 through User Interviews) to first understand the reasons behind design preference before committing to a quantitative choice study. The team should not spend the full $15,000 without asking whether that amount removes proportionally more doubt than a $5,000 study would.

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 5.1

Tags: EVPI; research budget; Hubbard; AACSB: Analytical thinking

SA-3

A researcher sends a 30-question brand tracker to an online panel and receives 800 responses back from Dynata. She reports an overall satisfaction score of 74 percent. A senior colleague questions the validity of the result, pointing to two specific threats.

Identify the two most likely validity threats in this scenario and explain each. For each threat, state the number or rule from the chapter that quantifies it, and name the specific defense the researcher should have built in.

Sample answer: The two most likely threats are non-response bias and panel contamination (bot / low-quality-response fraud). Non-response bias: the people who completed the survey may be systematically different from those who were invited but declined. Chapter figures show that Pew's cumulative response rate (the share of randomly invited adults who both join a panel and complete a given survey) is around 3 percent, meaning the 800 responses represent only the self-selected, engaged tail of the invited pool. If the disgruntled buyers who want to vent are more likely to respond, or if satisfied buyers who see no value in spending 12 minutes are more likely to decline, the 74 percent satisfaction score is biased in one direction. The defense is post-survey demographic weighting so the respondent profile matches the known population distribution. Panel contamination: the chapter cites Dynata's own data quality whitepaper showing roughly 23 percent gross fraud and low-quality rate before filtering, falling to 6-9 percent after a four-layer post-filter. Ten to twenty-five percent of a raw cheap-panel pull may be bots or speeders before cleaning. The defense is a layered cleaning protocol: one trap item per fifteen substantive questions, a mid-survey attention check, a speed limit flagging responses below one-third of the median completion time, and an open-ended item requiring a free-form written response. The researcher should report the cleaned n after applying these filters, not the gross 800, and the methods section should name which panel, which vendor quality-tier, and what exclusion rules were applied.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 5.4, LO 5.8

Tags: non-response bias; panel fraud; survey validity; 2026 panel quality; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Reflective thinking

SA-4

Our chapter describes a five-stage research process and argues that the discipline lives almost entirely in the first step. Walk through the New Coke case to illustrate why. Define both the management decision problem and the marketing research problem as they applied to Coca-Cola in 1985. State what problem the company actually studied and what problem it should have studied. Then name the failure mode and its plain fix.

Sample answer: The management decision problem is the choice the decision-maker has to make. For Coca-Cola in 1985 it was: keep the original formula or replace it with the sweeter new formula. The marketing research problem is the information question whose answer lets the decision-maker make that call. The correct formulation would have been: what do loyal, habitual buyers do when the formula they grew up with is permanently removed from the shelf, and how does the emotional/identity loss of the familiar formula compare to any preference advantage the new formula carries in a blind taste test? What Coca-Cola actually studied was a narrower question: in a blind taste test, does the new formula produce higher preference ratings than the old one? The study was executed correctly and answered that question accurately. The problem was that it answered a different question than the decision required. The new formula won on taste in isolation, but the decision was about brand loyalty, habit, identity, and the reaction to loss when a familiar product disappears. The failure mode is collapsing the management decision problem into the marketing research problem: building a study that answers the narrow tactical question while leaving the real strategic question unmeasured. The plain fix is to write the two problems separately before the research plan is designed, keeping them as distinct sentences, and then confirming that the proposed study's output actually answers the marketing research problem rather than a convenient proxy for it.

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 5.1

Tags: management decision problem; marketing research problem; New Coke; research process; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

Section III — AI-Integration Items (4 items)

These items are original to this course and do not appear in any Kotler bank. They test students' ability to evaluate AI-assisted research workflows, identify validity threats in AI-generated research outputs, and apply the chapter's frameworks to the 2026 AI research stack.

AI-1 — Identify the Validity Threat in AI-Generated Survey Instrument

A brand manager asks an AI assistant to draft a five-question satisfaction survey for a new line of insulated bottles. The AI returns:

  1. How much do you love the amazing quality and design of your new bottle?
  2. Don't you agree that the lid is far more convenient than our competitors' lids?
  3. How satisfied are you with the value and durability of your purchase?
  4. How likely are you to recommend us to your friends and family?
  5. Overall, don't you think we deserve 5 stars?

Which of the following best describes the validity threat this instrument carries?

A) Non-response bias, because the questions are too long and will drive low completion rates B) Expectancy bias introduced through leading questions: multiple items signal the desired answer to the respondent before she has formed her own opinion, so the scores will reflect the questions' framing rather than the buyer's actual experience C) Selection bias, because the survey will be routed only to buyers who already completed a purchase D) Retrieval failure, because "quality" and "design" are abstract concepts that a respondent cannot recall accurately E) Type M error, because AI-generated surveys systematically overstate effect sizes

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 5.8, LO 5.7 — Items 1, 2, 3, and 5 all embed the desired answer in the wording. "How much do you love," "don't you agree," and "don't you think we deserve 5 stars" are leading questions that contaminate the response at the judgment stage of Tourangeau's model. This is an AI output that looks authoritative but breaks a fundamental survey-design rule that the chapter names. The AI should be prompted with the four-stage model and a neutrality rule before generating survey items.

AI-2 — Evaluate AI Qualitative Coding Output

A research team uploads 60 consumer interview transcripts to a Claude Project and asks the model to code every response into themes. The model returns a clean summary in 8 minutes and reports that "premium packaging" and "convenience features" are the top two themes, each appearing in roughly 45 percent of transcripts. A team member wants to publish those findings immediately.

Using the chapter's framework for AI-assisted qualitative coding, identify the two steps the team skipped that are required before the findings can be trusted. Explain why each step matters.

Sample answer and rubric:

Step 1 skipped: The team did not write a rubric before running the model. The chapter states that the model cannot write the rubric that defines what counts as a theme; that judgment belongs to the human researcher. Without a rubric, the model decides what "premium packaging" means and which quotes get that label, and there is no documented standard against which the output can be checked. Two researchers reading the same transcript might disagree on whether a comment about the lid counts as "convenience feature" or "premium packaging," and without a rubric the model has no way to handle that ambiguity consistently.

Step 2 skipped: The team did not compute Cohen's kappa between the model's codes and a human-coded sample. The chapter states the kappa check has to clear 0.70 before the run can be trusted. Kappa measures the degree of agreement between two coders above what chance alone would produce. A kappa of 0.75 between the AI and the human coder means the coding is substantially reliable; a kappa of 0.45 means nearly half the codes are effectively arbitrary. Publishing 45 percent frequencies without a kappa check means the team has no evidence the 60 percent are real themes rather than the model's idiosyncratic interpretations. The fix is to have one team member independently code a random sample of 10 to 15 transcripts against the model's codes and compute kappa before accepting the full run.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 5.5, LO 5.7 — The chapter is explicit: the model cannot write the rubric, and the kappa check must clear 0.70. These are two human steps that cannot be outsourced to the AI.

Tags: AI qualitative coding; Cohen's kappa; rubric; intercoder reliability; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

AI-3 — Apply the AAPOR Disclosure Standard

A marketing research firm completes a brand-tracker study in which an AI coded 1,200 open-ended responses into 14 themes, and the firm's report cites those themes as findings. A client asks the firm to provide the methodology section. According to the chapter's description of the AAPOR task force standard on responsible AI integration in survey research, the methodology section must name three things. Which of the following options correctly lists all three?

A) The vendor who supplied the panel, the response rate, and the demographic breakdown of respondents B) The AI model used for coding, the prompt given to the model, and the human-review step that vetted what came back C) The sample size, the confidence interval, and the p-value for each reported theme frequency D) The number of responses coded by AI versus human coders, the cost per coded response, and the turnaround time E) The client's name, the date of fieldwork, and the Cronbach's alpha of the theme-coding scale

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 5.7 — The AAPOR 2025 task force report prescribes a disclosure block for every stage an AI touched: the model, the prompt, and the human-review step that vetted the output. This is the standard a sophisticated buyer holds any AI-touched study to, regardless of which of the four workflow tiers produced it.

Tags: AAPOR disclosure standard; AI-touched research; responsible AI; methodology; AACSB: Ethical understanding; Reflective thinking

AI-4 — Tier Placement and Validity of an AI-Assisted Research Workflow

A brand analyst at a consumer-goods company describes her weekly workflow: "Every Monday at 9 a.m., a script I set up pulls the new Qualtrics responses, drops the speeders and trap-item failures, codes the open-ended items with the rubric I wrote last month, compares this week's themes against last week's, and posts the summary to our team's Slack channel. I read it with my coffee."

According to our chapter's four-tier AI workflow, this workflow sits at _____, and the primary validity safeguard the analyst built in that makes the output trustworthy is _____.

A) Tier 1 (one-off ask); the rubric she wrote B) Tier 2 (saved Skill); the Slack integration C) Tier 3 (connected data source); the Qualtrics connection D) Tier 4 (scheduled, runs without a human in the loop); the rubric she wrote, which is the human judgment step that governs what the model codes and against which its output can be audited E) Tier 4 (scheduled); the trap item and speed-limit cleaning, which substitute for human review and allow the workflow to run fully unsupervised

Answer: D

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 5.7 — The workflow is Tier 4: scheduled, running end to end without a click. The chapter is clear that no tier excuses you from saying how the work was done. The rubric is the human judgment step that anchors the AI's coding behavior and allows it to be audited; without it, a Tier 4 run is ungoverned output. Option E is wrong because the cleaning steps remove bad respondents but do not govern what the model codes or make the output reviewable.

Tags: four-tier AI workflow; Tier 4 scheduled; rubric; responsible AI; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Ethical understanding

Chapter 19 Social Media Marketing — Test Bank

Chapter: 19 — Social Media Marketing Source note: Original authoring. No Kotler MM16 parent chapter exists for this topic. Every item is authored directly from OUR chapter 19 ground truth (book/v7/chapter-19-social-media.html). No item is adapted from or lifted from any Kotler test bank. Export format: Mirrors the course test-bank markdown schema (stem, A...E options, Answer, Diff, Objective, Tags) for QTI / Canvas Common Cartridge conversion.

COORDINATION NOTE: This bank owns the STEPPS definition items and virality-engineering items canonically. Ch17 Content Marketing test bank applies STEPPS to long-form content and does not re-teach the six definitions.

Learning Objectives (Ch19, Our Chapter)

  • LO 19.1 Explain the three structural properties that make social media behave differently from every other paid or owned channel: speed, asymmetric reach, and public by default.
  • LO 19.2 Name and define all six STEPPS levers and predict which combination makes a piece of content travel.
  • LO 19.3 Apply the arousal-beats-valence principle to content design, using the NYT most-emailed study as the evidentiary basis, and distinguish engineered high-arousal content from rage-bait.
  • LO 19.4 Distinguish earned, paid, and owned media and explain the cost economics that make earned the highest-leverage channel (the 6-cent vs. $54 CPM gap).
  • LO 19.5 Build a three-tier platform portfolio and apply the two-account rule, explain the TikTok USDS joint venture outcome (January 22, 2026), and distinguish long-form spillover and search-surface posting.
  • LO 19.6 Apply AI workflows to social media production: the STEPPS-scoring Skill, the cmp-tiktok-script model-routing rule, the will-it-travel auditor, and the social-listening triage loop.
  • LO 19.7 Identify the five failure modes of social media marketing and diagnose which failure a given campaign is committing.
  • LO 19.8 Describe the AI-persuasion frontier — per-user AI content personalization, bot-detection erosion, feed reranking effects — and explain where the EU AI Act manipulation ban applies.

Section I — Multiple-Choice Items (22 items)

Item 1

Social media marketing is defined in our chapter as the use of platform-native posting, paid amplification, and earned sharing to build awareness and convert demand at a cost traditional broadcast cannot match. The cost advantage is rooted in _______.

A) the lower production cost of a short-form video compared with a 30-second television spot B) the fact that each earned share adds reach at close to zero marginal cost, while broadcast charges a fixed rate per view C) the ability to precisely target by age and income, which reduces waste D) the elimination of agency fees when a brand manages its own accounts in-house E) the shorter campaign cycle, which reduces the need for pre-production testing

Answer: B

Diff: 1

Objective: LO 19.1 — The chapter's cost comparison turns on earned media: once a post travels, each additional share costs the brand nothing, while a Super Bowl spot costs roughly $54 CPM regardless of how many people forward it.

Tags: earned media economics; social media definition; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 2

TikTok's For You algorithm seeds a new post to approximately 200 viewers and gates further distribution on _______.

A) the number of followers the account already has B) the ratio of likes to impressions in the first hour C) the share of first-batch viewers who watch past the four-second median, a signal called the first-batch completion rate D) how recently the account was created and whether it has run paid campaigns before E) the number of hashtags attached to the post that match trending topics

Answer: C

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 19.1 — The chapter specifies that the recommender evaluates first-batch completion rate as the dominant gating signal, followed by like rate and rewatch count. Follower count never enters the calculation.

Tags: TikTok algorithm; asymmetric reach; For You Page; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 3

When a passenger was dragged off a United Airlines flight in 2017, the phone video erased approximately $1.4 billion of market value within 24 hours. This case is cited in our chapter to illustrate _______.

A) the speed property of social media, where content ramps from zero to a million views in an afternoon B) the asymmetric reach property, where a creator with few followers can outdraw a brand account with millions C) the public-by-default property, where a social post with a full link and timestamp lives in a searchable database for years D) the Emotion lever, because anger is a high-arousal feeling that drives sharing E) the Law of the Few, where one influential person seeds a cascade

Answer: C

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 19.1 — The chapter uses the United case to illustrate public by default: unlike a television spot that airs once, a social post lives with a timestamp in a database that competitors, journalists, and angry customers can quote back for years.

Item 4

The STEPPS framework describes the content properties that make a piece travel through peer-to-peer sharing. The six letters stand for _______.

A) Speed, Trust, Engagement, Positioning, Platform, Scale B) Social Currency, Triggers, Emotion, Public, Practical Value, Stories C) Social Currency, Targeting, Emotion, Public, Performance, Stories D) Sharing, Triggers, Expertise, Promotion, Practical Value, Strategies E) Social Proof, Timing, Emotion, Popularity, Persuasion, Stories

Objective: LO 19.2 — The chapter defines STEPPS from Jonah Berger's research as Social Currency, Triggers, Emotion, Public, Practical Value, Stories, noting it is descriptive rather than a sequential recipe.

Item 5

A) Triggers, because the cap is encountered daily at the moment of consumption B) Public, because the bottle is visible on desks and in meetings C) Social Currency, because a surprising fact worth repeating lets the sharer look smart to their group D) Practical Value, because the fact is genuinely useful for quiz nights E) Stories, because each cap fact carries a narrative about an unusual material

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 19.2 — The chapter cites the Snapple cap as the Social Currency lever's "surprising fact worth repeating" route, specifically because repeating the fact earns the sharer status by making them look knowledgeable.

Item 6

Diff: 2

Tags: Triggers; STEPPS; Kit Kat; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 8

Item 9

A) Sadness +16%; contentment +5%; anger -12%; awe -30% B) Awe +30%; anger +34%; anxiety +21%; sadness -16% C) Positive emotions all drove sharing; negative emotions all suppressed it D) Awe and contentment drove sharing equally; anger and sadness suppressed it equally E) Articles about finance traveled more than articles about culture regardless of emotional tone

Item 10

Evian's Roller Babies ad was named the most-viewed online ad in history at 50 million views, the same year Evian's market share fell roughly 25 percent. Our chapter uses this case to illustrate _______.

A) the public-by-default risk, because the ad stayed in a searchable database and was quoted critically for years B) the Practical Value gap, because viewers enjoyed the video but had no useful reason to share it C) the low-arousal failure, where a calm, contented feeling drives views but not shares that move buyers D) platform monoculture, because the ad ran exclusively on YouTube before Instagram existed E) the Stories lever's removability test, because a viewer could retell the story without mentioning Evian

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 19.3 — The chapter classifies Evian's Roller Babies as a low-arousal positive (contentment) failure: the ad was cute and warm but the calm feeling did not carry the brand to the share button. Views and brand-moving shares are different outcomes.

Tags: arousal; Evian Roller Babies; low-arousal failure; Emotion lever; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 11

Objective: LO 19.3 — The chapter reports the TikTok trend study: the reward gap for adding a visible twist is small early, when few copies exist, and grows decisively later, once the feed is saturated with identical entries.

Item 14

Our chapter distinguishes simple contagion from complex contagion. According to this framework, a brand would choose to seed many mid-tier creators in one tight community over placing its budget behind a single macro creator when _______.

A) the product's price point is below $20, because low-cost products spread through simple contagion B) the behavior it wants to spread is costly or identity-laden and therefore requires exposure from multiple independent contacts before adoption C) the brand needs discovery reach rather than depth, because communities have a capped audience ceiling D) the algorithm is more likely to route seeded content to the brand's existing category classification E) the platform's paid-partnership disclosure requirements make macro creator deals legally riskier

Section II — Scenario / Short-Answer Items (4 items)

SA-1

A student is launching a campus coffee brand, "Grind." She has no advertising budget but a small TikTok account with 800 followers. A classmate tells her to "post more and eventually something will go viral." A second classmate tells her to score every draft on the six STEPPS letters before she posts and to aim for at least two letters at medium strength.

Identify which classmate's advice better reflects what our chapter teaches about why content travels. Name the two STEPPS letters you would recommend she prioritize for a campus coffee brand and explain the concrete tactic behind each.

Sample answer: The second classmate's advice is consistent with the chapter. The STEPPS framework is descriptive research across 6,500 brands showing that content hitting two or three letters travels reliably, while posting volume without STEPPS scores is the "volume without a voice" failure mode. Two strong letter recommendations for a campus coffee brand: (1) Triggers: coffee is a product with a natural daily cue, the morning fill or the study-break espresso. She should pair every post to a recurring student cue, such as the first lecture of the day, the 2 p.m. slump, or the Sunday-night study session, so the cue in daily life brings the brand to mind. (2) Practical Value: useful posts, such as a 30-second guide to the best study-session order or a campus map showing which buildings have outlets near the coffee shop, give students something genuinely helpful to share, and being seen as a helpful sharer is itself social currency. Both letters are accessible with zero budget, which is why they fit her constraint.

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 19.2, LO 19.7

Tags: STEPPS; Triggers; Practical Value; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Written communication

SA-2

A regional salsa brand's marketing manager is briefing her team on a new TikTok campaign. She says: "I want something heartwarming, something that brings families together. Let us make a beautiful, warm video of a grandmother cooking with her grandchildren." A team member pushes back, citing the chapter's arousal-beats-valence principle.

Using evidence from our chapter, explain the scientific basis for the team member's concern. Then propose a revised creative direction for the grandmother scene that keeps the emotional story but adds the arousal the data requires to make it travel.

Sample answer: The team member's concern is grounded in the 2012 NYT most-emailed study. That research controlled for how prominent, interesting, and well-placed each article was, then asked which emotional states still moved the share rate. Awe raised odds of going viral by about 30 percent, anger by 34 percent, and anxiety by 21 percent. Sadness ran 16 percent below baseline. Crucially, contentment and warmth are low-arousal feelings on the positive side of the valence axis, and the data show that arousal beats valence: a warm family scene carries contentment, which sits roughly where Evian's Roller Babies sat, widely viewed but not shared in a way that moved buying. The team member is not arguing the scene is bad; the argument is that the feeling it produces will not push the post toward the share button. A revised creative direction: keep the grandmother and grandchildren, but introduce a genuine stake or surprise. Options include (a) the grandmother tasting the batch and declaring it wrong, then the grandchildren scrambling to fix it before a party starts, injecting awe when it works, which is the awe-plus-stories combination; (b) the grandmother demonstrating a technique the family thought only she knew, creating a "you had no idea this was possible" awe moment for viewers; or (c) a stakes structure where the family has to finish the batch before the grandfather arrives home. Each of these pushes toward awe or excitement, which travel, while keeping the family warmth as the emotional wrapper.

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 19.3

Tags: arousal vs valence; NYT study; Emotion lever; content design; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Written communication

SA-3

A direct-to-consumer mattress brand is allocating its annual social media budget. The VP of Marketing proposes putting 80 percent on paid (boosted posts and creator fees for three macro influencers), 15 percent on owned (content studio), and 5 percent on "the earned side." A junior analyst says the leverage runs opposite to the money.

Using the chapter's CPM data and influencer-portfolio logic, explain why the analyst is right. Then recommend two specific changes to the budget allocation that would increase earned reach without increasing total spending.

Sample answer: The analyst's point is grounded in unit economics. A boosted post costs $4 to $8 CPM. A macro influencer's effective reach is followers times engagement times audience overlap, and the chapter's audit widget shows that a 1.2-million-follower creator with 15 percent audience overlap and 45 percent sponsored saturation delivers an effective reach of roughly 2,400, at a typical fee of $42,000, which is a cost per effective reach of over $17. By contrast, an earned share costs the brand nothing per additional viewer once the content is traveling. The chapter reports that most marketing budgets put 80 percent on paid, 15 percent on owned, and 5 percent on the engineering that wins the cheap channel, and the leverage runs opposite to the money. Recommendation 1: Shift the three macro-influencer budget to a portfolio of micro creators (10,000 to 100,000 followers) with high audience overlap in the brand's target. The chapter shows micro creators run two to three times the engagement of macro accounts, and a portfolio spreads the bet rather than concentrating it, which the path-dependent MusicLab result shows is the correct strategy for unpredictable virality. Recommendation 2: Invest a portion of the 5 percent earned-side budget in a STEPPS-scoring Skill and a share-card artifact, such as a sleep-data year-in-review card timed to a January-reset cue. A share-card turns the audience into the publisher and costs roughly nothing per forward, replicating the Spotify Wrapped mechanic at a fraction of the scale.

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 19.4, LO 19.5

Tags: earned media economics; CPM; influencer portfolio; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

SA-4

A startup selling zero-waste cleaning products wants to run a social campaign. It has accounts on TikTok, Instagram, and X. The founder argues the brand only needs TikTok because that is where the target audience is. A consultant recommends a two-platform minimum and a two-account approach within TikTok.

Using the chapter's rented-platform logic, the TikTok USDS case, and the two-account rule, explain the consultant's reasoning for each recommendation. Then identify which platform tier each platform in the brand's current portfolio occupies.

Sample answer: The consultant's reasoning for a two-platform minimum: every social media channel is rented infrastructure. The brand does not own TikTok, and the host can change the rules or become unavailable with little warning. The TikTok case is the chapter's clearest example: on January 18, 2025, TikTok went dark for US users for several hours under a federal law forcing its Chinese parent to sell or be banned. Even after the restructuring into TikTok USDS on January 22, 2026, the brand's entire audience strategy was a regulatory variable for a full year. Any brand whose plan rested entirely on TikTok discovered that the platform's availability was not a fixed asset. Vine's shutdown in 2017 is the harder lesson: brands built entirely there lost all their audience with no fallback. The two-platform rule means losing one channel is a setback rather than the end. The consultant's reasoning for the two-account rule within TikTok: the chapter reports that the algorithm cannot deepen one audience and reach a new one simultaneously. The harder the recommender amplifies a brand's existing category classification, the fewer new topics it shows that audience. Running a deep-engagement campaign on the primary account and a growth campaign aimed at a new target cluster on a separate account prevents the platform, rather than the brand, from deciding which goal loses. Platform tier classification: TikTok occupies Tier 1 (mass discovery), where the For You Page algorithm can push content far past the brand's follower base. Instagram sits in Tier 1 as well (Instagram Reels uses the same discovery-feed model). X occupies Tier 2 (text and journalists), the tier for quotable lines and press coverage. The brand should consider adding a Tier 3 community platform, such as a Reddit presence or a Substack, to hold its most engaged audience.

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 19.5

Tags: rented platform risk; TikTok USDS JV; two-account rule; platform portfolio; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Reflective thinking

AI-1 — The Will-It-Travel STEPPS Auditor

A student runs the "will this post travel?" widget on a campus restaurant's recent caption: "Fuel up at The Porch. Good food, good vibes, always fresh." The model scores it as Social Currency 0, Triggers 0, Emotion 1, Public 0, Practical Value 1, Stories 0 — a total of 2 out of 18.

Which option correctly identifies the two STEPPS letters the student should strengthen and provides a concrete, one-sentence rewrite of the caption that increases each score?

A) Strengthen Social Currency and Stories; rewrite: "The Porch has been Stillwater's best-kept secret since 2019 — and this week we are finally letting it out." B) Strengthen Triggers and Practical Value; rewrite: "The Porch is open every day, including weekends, with a rotating menu. Check the Instagram bio for today's specials." C) Strengthen Triggers and Social Currency; rewrite: "Next time you leave a lecture starving — The Porch. Your campus crew already knows. Now your roommate does too." (Triggers: pairs the brand to the post-lecture hunger cue; Social Currency: creates an insider-feel where knowing The Porch signals being in-the-know.) D) Strengthen Emotion and Public; rewrite: "Look at this plate. If you are not eating here right now, you are doing college wrong." E) Strengthen Stories and Practical Value; rewrite: "The Porch opened in a 400-square-foot space in 2019. Here is what is on the menu today and why it is worth the walk."

Answer: C

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 19.2, LO 19.6 — The original caption scores near zero on Triggers (no daily cue) and Social Currency (no status mechanism). Option C adds a Triggers cue (post-lecture hunger) and a Social Currency move (insider-feel: "your crew already knows"). Option A strengthens Stories and Social Currency but misses Triggers. Option D strengthens Emotion but it is generic rather than genuinely high-arousal. The chapter's will-it-travel audit is designed to identify the weakest letter and name the one edit that lifts it.

Tags: will-it-travel auditor; STEPPS; AI content audit; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

AI-2 — The cmp-tiktok-script Model-Routing Rule

A social media team drafts 200 caption replies to brand mentions every week and writes one launch script per major product drop per quarter. The team has access to a fast, low-cost model (Claude Haiku 4.5) and a deeper reasoning model (Claude Opus 4.7). According to the model-routing rule taught in our chapter, which allocation is correct and why?

A) Use the deeper model for all 200 replies and the fast model for the launch script, because replies require more brand-voice consistency than a launch script that follows a template. B) Use the deeper model for all tasks, because the cost difference is negligible at the team's weekly volume and using a weaker model for any public-facing post risks brand damage. C) Route the 200 weekly caption replies to the fast model because they are bulk tasks where trading nuance for speed is acceptable; reserve the deeper model for the launch script because reading the cultural moment wrong on a launch post can cost a quarter of audience trust. D) Use the fast model for the launch script and the deeper model for the replies, because a launch script is a templated product while replies require real-time cultural judgment. E) Alternate between both models on every task, using A/B output comparison to let the team pick the better response each time.

Answer: C

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 19.6 — The chapter states explicitly: "Cheap drafts feed the bulk work: replies, moderation, and A/B copy on posts that are already on-brand. The deeper model is held back for the launch script and the cultural-moment reaction, where reading the cue wrong costs a quarter of audience trust." The routing decision, not the model choice in isolation, is the lesson.

Tags: cmp-tiktok-script; model routing; Claude Haiku; Claude Opus; AI workflow; AACSB: Application of knowledge

AI-3 — The Find-the-Cue Triggers Loop

A student is building an AI-assisted content calendar for a local gym. She wants to find Triggers, the everyday cues in a gym member's life that would bring the brand to mind, so she can pair each post to a cue the audience encounters regularly. She runs a prompt in the chapter's widget. One model returns: "Post motivational content consistently and use hashtags like #fitness and #gym to reach your audience."

The response fails the Triggers-loop task. Which of the following describes the correct prompt structure for finding cues, and what a useful output from that prompt would look like?

A) The prompt should ask the model to identify the brand's top three competitors, because knowing competitor posting schedules reveals which cues they are already occupying. B) The prompt should ask the model to list the recurring daily and weekly activities a gym member does outside the gym that could serve as environmental cues for a hydration drink, workout supplement, or recovery habit, and the output should name specific cue events paired with a suggested post topic for each (e.g., "Monday morning commute: post a 30-second 'two-move warm-up for your lunch break' Reel"). C) The prompt should ask the model to score the brand's last ten posts on the six STEPPS letters and find the weakest, because identifying the lowest letter tells the team where to concentrate. D) The prompt should ask the model to list the top trending hashtags in the fitness category this week, because trending cues represent what is already top of mind for the audience. E) The prompt should ask the model to rewrite the gym's existing bio in the voice of its three target personas, because voice consistency is the prerequisite for any Triggers strategy.

Answer: B

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 19.2, LO 19.6 — The Triggers lever requires identifying external cues in the buyer's environment (not the brand's posting schedule). The chapter's Kit Kat case shows the cue is coffee-drinking hours, a behavior the buyer already has, which the brand then pairs its product to. The correct AI workflow is to prompt the model to enumerate the buyer's recurring activities outside the brand context, then match each to a post topic. Option A confuses competitive intelligence with cue-finding. Option D is trend-monitoring, not Triggers.

Tags: Triggers lever; find-the-cue loop; AI content calendar; STEPPS; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

AI-4 — Social-Listening Triage and the AI-Persuasion Frontier

A brand manager reads about a study showing that AI-written misinformation on X traveled farther than human-written misinformation, because the algorithm promotes content for being high-energy rather than believable. She asks her team two questions: (1) What does this mean for the brand's social-listening triage agent? (2) Does the EU AI Act's manipulation ban apply to the brand's current practice of using a generative model to write five different versions of the same product caption, each slightly tuned to a different audience segment?

Which response correctly addresses both questions?

A) (1) The finding has no operational implication for the brand; misinformation monitoring is the platform's responsibility. (2) Writing five versions of a caption is standard A/B testing and is explicitly exempt from the EU AI Act. B) (1) The triage agent should now flag any high-energy post mentioning the brand, not only posts that appear factually suspect, because high energy is the signal the algorithm amplifies regardless of accuracy. (2) Writing five tuned versions is legal as long as each version is factually accurate; the EU Act's ban applies only to false claims. C) (1) The triage agent should flag high-energy, high-share posts mentioning the brand for human review even when the content looks plausible, because a model can manufacture high-energy payload at a volume no human team can match. (2) Five audience-tuned versions are a standard personalization practice; the EU AI Act's manipulation ban applies where the personalization uses hidden techniques to materially distort choices in a way that causes harm, so the brand's legal counsel should review whether the profiling data driving the personalization meets that threshold. D) (1) The brand should shut down the triage agent because it cannot reliably distinguish AI-written posts from human ones. (2) The EU AI Act covers only advertising directed at children under 16. E) (1) The triage agent should be upgraded to a deeper model, because only a frontier model can distinguish AI-generated content from human content with confidence. (2) Five caption versions are manipulative AI per Article 5 and are illegal in all EU markets as of 2025.

Answer: C

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 19.6, LO 19.8 — On the triage implication: the chapter's frontier section notes AI-written misinformation traveled farther on high-energy payload, so a triage filter tuned only for plausibility misses the mechanism. On the EU Act: the chapter defines manipulative AI as using hidden or deceptive techniques that materially distort choices in a way that causes harm; the Act's Article 5 ban has been in force since early 2025. Standard audience-tuned copy is not per se manipulative, but the profiling data and intent matter, which is exactly what the chapter signals by pointing to Chapter 26 for the full ethics treatment.

Tags: social-listening triage; AI misinformation; EU AI Act; manipulative AI; AACSB: Ethical understanding; Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

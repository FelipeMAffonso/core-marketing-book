Chapter 13 DTC and E-Commerce Stack — Test Bank

Chapter: 13 — DTC and the E-Commerce Stack Source note: Original authoring. No Kotler MM16 parent exists for this chapter. Every item is authored from scratch against the ground-truth chapter (book/v7/chapter-13-dtc-ecommerce.html). Item content, examples, brand cases, and framework names derive entirely from OUR chapter. Kotler TB-13 and TB-16 were consulted for item-format style only, not for content.

Learning Objectives (Ch13, Our Chapter)

  • LO 13.1 Explain the three claims that priced the 2014-2020 DTC cycle and name the three events that broke each between 2021 and 2024.
  • LO 13.2 Identify the seven layers of the modern DTC stack and the primary vendor and job associated with each layer.
  • LO 13.3 Apply the four-question conversion-rate audit to a product page and identify which buyer question a drop-off answers.
  • LO 13.4 Describe the abandoned-cart recovery playbook, explain the three-message timing, and explain why the discount belongs in message three.
  • LO 13.5 Compute subscription lifetime value using the churn-rate identity and explain why churn is the most leveraged metric in a subscription business.
  • LO 13.6 Calculate the four unit-economics numbers (CAC, AOV, contribution margin, LTV), compute payback period and LTV-to-CAC ratio, and interpret the result.
  • LO 13.7 Audit a product page against the five-job framework (hero, USP, social proof, urgency, objection handler) and identify the weakest element.
  • LO 13.8 Compare the six marketplace axes (CAC, take-rate, brand control, fulfillment burden, contribution margin, scale ceiling) to select a channel mix.
  • LO 13.9 Describe how the agent-shopping frontier changes who the operator is selling to and list the five checks that make a storefront agent-readable.

Section I — Multiple-Choice Items (22 items)

Item 1

Casper went public at $12 a share in February 2020, reported roughly a dollar of marketing spend for every dollar of 2019 revenue, and was taken private in November 2021 at $6.90 a share. According to the chapter, the root cause of Casper's failure was _______.

A) poor product design that buyers rejected after the 100-night trial ended B) a retail partner that stocked competitor mattresses on the same shelf C) the cost of acquiring each new customer through paid social and search outrunning the lifetime spend that customer produced D) Apple's App Tracking Transparency update, which shut off all Facebook advertising for the mattress category E) an SEC accounting disclosure that revealed fabricated revenue figures

Answer: C

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 13.1 — The chapter's opening diagnostic is explicit: Casper spent roughly $423 million in marketing to produce $439 million in 2019 revenue and still lost $89 million, because customer-acquisition cost outran lifetime customer value. The product and the category were real; the math was broken.

Tags: DTC reckoning; Casper; unit economics; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 2

The first claim that priced the 2014-2020 direct-to-consumer cycle was that paid social on Facebook and Instagram could acquire a customer for $20 to $40 in most consumer categories. The mechanism that made the cost that low was _______.

A) a federal subsidy program that reimbursed small-business digital advertising costs B) the IDFA, a device-level identifier Apple attached to every iPhone that let Meta recognize a prior-site visitor and show her a retargeted ad when she was most likely to buy C) Shopify's built-in audience-matching tool that placed ads automatically on the seller's behalf D) a Meta policy that capped ad prices for brands under a certain revenue threshold E) influencer marketing on Instagram, which cost the brand only product samples rather than cash

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 13.1 — The chapter identifies the Identifier for Advertisers (IDFA) as the mechanism that made cheap retargeting work. An iOS user who had visited a store's site and then opened Instagram could be shown a retargeted ad because both reads the same IDFA. Retargeted ads convert at multiples of cold-audience ads, keeping acquisition cost low.

Tags: IDFA; ATT; DTC first claim; paid acquisition; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 4

The second event that broke the 2014-2020 DTC cycle arrived in October 2022, when the Federal Reserve raised the federal funds rate to 4.5 percent on its way to a 5.5 percent peak. According to the chapter, the specific mechanism by which the rate cycle hurt DTC brands was _______.

A) higher rates reduced consumer spending on discretionary items, cutting repeat-purchase rates across all DTC categories B) higher rates made it more expensive for Shopify to maintain its servers, forcing platform fee increases that squeezed merchant margins C) the working-capital cost of bridging between acquiring a customer and recovering that cost from her roughly tripled, making long paybacks structurally unprofitable D) the Federal Reserve's rate actions triggered an SEC review of DTC S-1 filings, which revealed inflated lifetime-value projections E) higher rates caused Meta's ad auction prices to rise because large financial advertisers outbid consumer brands for inventory

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 13.1 — The chapter explains that zero-interest-rate borrowing had allowed DTC brands to finance long customer paybacks cheaply. When rates reached 4.5 to 5.5 percent, the cost of carrying a customer from acquisition to repayment roughly tripled, turning previously tolerable unit economics into structural losses.

Tags: rate cycle; DTC reckoning; working capital; payback period; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 7

A) Orders rise to 2,000, a 33 percent increase on the same media budget B) Orders rise to 1,600, a 7 percent increase because half a percentage point is a small change C) Orders rise to 2,050, a 37 percent increase because the post-purchase flows multiply the lift D) Orders stay near 1,500 because most of the lift is absorbed by increased returns E) Orders rise only if the average order value rises at the same time, which a copy test cannot guarantee

Answer: A

Item 8

A) Am I in the right place (relevance) B) Is this worth my attention (value) C) What does this cost me to find out (friction) D) What could go wrong if I act (anxiety) E) What do other buyers think of this (social proof)

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 13.3 — The chapter maps each drop-off in the funnel to one of the four buyer questions. A buyer who added to cart but never started checkout has already decided the product is worth her attention, so the relevance and value questions are passing. The failure is the cost of proceeding, which is the friction question: visible price, a size selector that works, an add-to-cart that does not demand an account first.

Item 10

A candle store draws 10,000 visitors a month, converts 1.5 percent, and has a $50 average order value. The 70 percent abandonment rate leaves roughly 350 abandoned carts a month. A three-message recovery sequence recovers 25 percent of those carts. According to the chapter's recovery-economics identity, how much additional monthly revenue does the sequence generate?

A) About $875 B) About $1,750 C) About $4,400 D) About $8,750 E) About $17,500

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 13.4 — 25 percent of 350 abandoned carts is 87.5 recovered orders, approximately 88. At $50 average order value, recovered revenue equals 88 x $50 = $4,400. The chapter works this exact calculation and calls it a 59 percent lift on monthly revenue with no added acquisition spend.

Tags: recovery economics; abandoned cart; Klaviyo; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 11

The chapter describes a three-message abandoned-cart sequence timed so each message does a different job. According to the chapter, when is the correct time to offer a discount?

A) In the first message, sent about an hour after abandonment, while the buyer is still warm B) In the second message, at 24 hours, alongside the social-proof block C) Only in the third message, at 72 hours, when the buyer has likely moved on, and not before D) In all three messages at increasing discount levels to create urgency E) Discounts should never be used in recovery sequences because they train abandonment behavior

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 13.4 — The chapter states explicitly that the discount belongs in message three only. A brand that leads with a 15 percent discount in message one trains the buyer to abandon every future cart waiting for it and permanently erodes margin. Reserving the discount for message three, after the reminder and the social-proof play have failed, keeps 8 to 14 percentage points more contribution margin per recovered customer with no drop in total recovery rate.

Tags: abandoned-cart sequence; discount ladder; recovery playbook; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 12

A quarterly candle subscription contributes $30 of margin per shipment and loses 6 percent of its subscribers each month. According to the chapter's churn-versus-LTV identity, what is the lifetime value of one subscriber?

A) $180 B) $250 C) $300 D) $500 E) $720

Answer: D

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 13.5 — The chapter states the identity explicitly: lifetime value equals one shipment's contribution margin divided by the monthly cancellation rate expressed as a decimal. $30 divided by 0.06 equals $500. The chapter works this example to show that the churn rate is the single most leveraged number in the subscription model.

Tags: LTV; churn rate; subscription economics; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 13

The chapter argues that churn is the most leveraged metric in a subscription business. According to the churn-versus-LTV identity, if the candle subscription's monthly churn doubles from 6 percent to 12 percent while the $30 per-shipment margin holds constant, what happens to lifetime value?

A) It falls from $500 to $400, a 20 percent decline B) It falls from $500 to $375, a 25 percent decline C) It falls from $500 to $250, a 50 percent decline D) It falls from $500 to $150, a 70 percent decline E) It is unchanged because the per-shipment margin is the primary driver, not churn

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 13.5 — At 12 percent churn: $30 / 0.12 = $250. Doubling churn halves lifetime value. The chapter uses this asymmetry to justify pulling the churn lever first, because a 10 percent lift in order value or purchase frequency moves lifetime value only 10 percent, while halving churn doubles it.

Tags: churn rate; LTV identity; subscription leverage; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 14

Repeat orders at months three, six, and nine each add the same $11.40. At which point does the buyer cross the payback threshold?

A) After the first order at month 1, because the $11.40 margin represents a positive return B) After the second order at month 3, when cumulative margin reaches $22.80 C) After the third order at month 6, when cumulative margin reaches $34.20 D) After the fourth order at month 9, when cumulative margin reaches $45.60, just above the $45 acquisition cost E) After the fifth order, because the chapter states payback takes more than 12 months in the candle category

Answer: D

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 13.6 — The chapter works this exact calculation: the buyer starts $33.60 underwater after the first order ($45 CAC minus $11.40), then climbs at each repeat order. This is a 9-month payback, within the 12-month venture rule.

Section II — Scenario / Short-Answer Items (4 items)

SA-1

A student is launching a small-batch soy candle store on Shopify. In week one, 500 people visit the store and 8 complete a purchase. The average order value is $28. The student spent $60 on Meta ads that week.

(a) Calculate the student's conversion rate. (b) Calculate the blended customer-acquisition cost. (c) If the student's contribution margin is 40 percent of revenue per order, compute the contribution margin per order and determine whether the student is above or below the break-even on this week's ad spend alone. (d) Using the chapter's two-ratio test, what does this result suggest about the business at this stage, and what is the one lever the chapter identifies as the highest-return next move?

Sample answer:

(a) Conversion rate = 8 completed purchases / 500 visitors = 1.6 percent.

(b) Blended CAC = $60 ad spend / 8 orders = $7.50 per acquired customer. (Note: in the real chapter model, not all 8 visitors came from paid ads, but when all spend is attributed to paid acquisition in week one this is the blended CAC.)

(c) Contribution margin per order = 40 percent of $28 = $11.20. Eight orders produce $89.60 in total contribution against $60 in ad spend, so the week is above break-even on ad spend alone. The LTV-to-CAC ratio at first-order margin only is $11.20 / $7.50 = roughly 1.5 to 1, below the 3-to-1 health bar but above break-even, consistent with the chapter's candle-store baseline.

(d) At 1.5 to 1, the business needs repeat purchases to cross the 3-to-1 health bar. The chapter identifies deploying the three-message abandoned-cart recovery sequence in Klaviyo as the highest-return first deployment, because it pulls back roughly 25 percent of the abandoned-cart pool on traffic the brand already paid to acquire, lifting effective conversion and generating additional contribution margin against the same ad spend.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 13.3, LO 13.4, LO 13.6

Tags: unit economics; CAC; CRO; abandoned-cart; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

SA-2

A brand runs a quarterly subscription box for premium dog treats. Each shipment contributes $22 of margin after product, shipping, and processing costs. The brand currently loses 8 percent of its subscribers every month.

(a) Calculate the current subscription lifetime value using the chapter's churn-versus-LTV identity. (b) The brand adds a pause-and-skip flow through Recharge's standard portal, and a post-shipment Klaviyo sequence, and reduces monthly churn to 5 percent. Recalculate lifetime value at the new churn rate. (c) The chapter argues that pulling the churn lever first pays back harder than any other lever. Demonstrate this claim by computing how much a 10 percent lift in average order value (from $22 to $24.20 margin per shipment at unchanged churn of 8 percent) changes lifetime value compared to the churn reduction in part (b). (d) What does this comparison reveal about the operator's priority order?

Sample answer:

(a) LTV at 8 percent churn = $22 / 0.08 = $275.

(b) LTV at 5 percent churn = $22 / 0.05 = $440. The churn reduction lifts lifetime value by $165, a 60 percent improvement.

(c) A 10 percent AOV lift raises the per-shipment margin from $22 to $24.20 at the original 8 percent churn. New LTV = $24.20 / 0.08 = $302.50. The AOV lift adds $27.50 to lifetime value, a 10 percent improvement.

(d) The comparison is decisive. A 3-percentage-point churn reduction adds $165 to lifetime value (a 60 percent gain); a 10 percent order-value lift adds only $27.50 (a 10 percent gain). The chapter's statement that "halving churn doubles lifetime value while every other lever moves it only linearly" is confirmed by the arithmetic. The operator should pull the churn lever first, then revisit order value and frequency as separate projects.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 13.5, LO 13.6

Tags: LTV identity; churn; subscription; lever priority; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

SA-3

A junior brand manager audits a direct-to-consumer hiking-boot store's product page. She finds the following: a stunning lifestyle hero image of boots on a mountain trail; a unique selling proposition that reads "Built for the Trail. Priced for Everyone."; a five-star average from 12 reviews; a countdown timer showing "Only 3 left in stock!" that resets to the same number every time she refreshes the page; and a return policy buried in the FAQ five clicks away.

Using the chapter's five-job product-page framework, (a) evaluate each of the five elements against the buyer question it is supposed to answer, (b) identify the weakest element and explain why, and (c) propose one specific, shippable fix for the weakest element.

Sample answer:

(a) Hero: Strong. A mountain-trail lifestyle shot correctly answers "am I in the right place" for a hiking-boot buyer in about three seconds. USP: Weak. "Built for the Trail. Priced for Everyone." makes no claim a competitor could not copy. "Built for the trail" is tautological for a hiking boot. The value question goes unanswered. Social proof: Marginal. A 5-star average from only 12 reviews is thin; buyers typically look for 100 or more verified reviews before treating the average as a reliable signal. Social proof is present but underpowered. Urgency: Failing. A countdown that resets on page refresh is theater. The chapter states that honest urgency is used sparingly and only when the underlying claim is true. A timer that resets reads as theater and damages trust rather than creating urgency. Objection handler: Absent or buried. A return policy five clicks away in an FAQ fails to address the buyer's anxiety at the moment she is weighing the purchase. The return policy is the objection handler's primary content for a high-consideration, fit-sensitive product like hiking boots.

(b) The weakest elements are the fraudulent urgency and the missing objection handler, but the most urgent fix is the urgency element because it actively damages trust rather than merely failing to help. A buyer who refreshes and sees the "3 left" counter reset to "3 left" concludes the brand is dishonest and is unlikely to convert even if the other elements were perfect.

(c) Remove the countdown timer entirely unless the low-stock claim is drawn from live inventory data. If live inventory is below a real threshold (say, 5 units), display a plain in-line message: "Only 4 remaining in this size." If inventory is normal, remove the urgency element. Then, as the second shippable fix, move the return policy above the add-to-cart button with a single visible line: "Free 60-day returns. No questions." This is the objection handler addressing the primary anxiety a first-time buyer of a fit-sensitive product has.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 13.7

Tags: product-page audit; five-job framework; urgency; objection handler; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Written communication

SA-4

A student starting a personalized stationery business is choosing her first marketplace. She has the following information: her products are handmade and photo-heavy, her average order value is $45, her contribution margin after product, processing, and shipping is 38 percent, and she has a $200 monthly marketing budget. She is considering four options: Shopify, Etsy, Amazon, and TikTok Shop.

Using the chapter's six-axis comparison (customer-acquisition cost, take-rate, brand control, fulfillment burden, contribution margin left after platform fees, and scale ceiling), (a) apply each axis to help her evaluate each of the four platforms, and (b) recommend the channel-mix pattern the chapter suggests for a brand in her situation, stating which channel to launch first and when to add a second.

Sample answer:

(a) Customer-acquisition cost: Shopify delivers zero pre-existing demand, so she must spend her $200 budget entirely on ads. Etsy delivers a pre-existing buyer searching for handmade stationery, reducing her own acquisition cost. Amazon delivers broad demand but not the craft-buyer cohort pre-selected for handmade goods. TikTok Shop delivers discovery-driven demand but is creator-driven rather than search-driven, making demand less predictable. Take-rate: Shopify charges card processing only (roughly 2.9 percent plus $0.30). Etsy all-in 10 to 13 percent. Amazon 8 to 15 percent referral fee plus fulfillment. TikTok Shop roughly 7 percent before creator commissions, which can add 10 to 30 percent. Brand control: Shopify is total; the brand speaks in its own voice. Etsy is moderate. Amazon is low; the Buy Box and Amazon Basics risk undermine brand control. TikTok Shop is low; the algorithm controls which creator features the product and on what terms. Fulfillment burden: Shopify and Etsy require the brand to ship each order. Amazon FBA removes the burden but requires pre-shipping to Amazon warehouses. TikTok Shop requires Fulfilled by TikTok after March 2026. Contribution margin left after fees: At a $45 AOV and 38 percent contribution, gross margin is $17.10 per order. Shopify keeps nearly all of it minus card processing (~$1.61 per order). Etsy takes 10 to 13 percent of $45 ($4.50 to $5.85), leaving $11.25 to $12.60. Amazon takes more and adds fulfillment costs. Scale ceiling: Shopify has no ceiling but requires building all its own demand. Etsy serves handmade well at modest scale but the search algorithm becomes more competitive above certain revenue thresholds.

(b) The chapter's recommendation for a handmade, photo-heavy brand with a small budget is to launch on Etsy first. The platform delivers a buyer pre-selected for the handmade category at a modest take-rate, without requiring the student to spend her entire $200 budget on cold-audience acquisition. Once Etsy produces predictable revenue, the chapter recommends adding Shopify as the second channel to capture returning buyers in a brand-owned relationship at lower fees, consistent with the Etsy-or-Faire-primary-with-Shopify-for-retention pattern the chapter describes. Amazon and TikTok Shop are better fits after the brand has reviews, inventory depth, and a cash cushion to absorb the higher take-rate and lower brand control.

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 13.8

Tags: marketplace fit; channel mix; six axes; Etsy; Shopify; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

They test students' ability to apply the chapter's frameworks to AI-era DTC operations, evaluate AI-generated outputs against our chapter's standards, and engage with the agent-shopping frontier that the chapter identifies as the 2026 operating reality.

AI-1 — Product-Page Audit Skill: Evaluate a Vague vs. Specific Query

A student asks an AI assistant to audit a competing candle store's product page. In version A she types: "Is this a good product page?" In version B she types: "Score this page on hero, unique selling proposition, social proof, urgency, and objection handler. Name the weakest element and give me one fix I can ship this week."

Both queries go to the same model at the same cost. According to the chapter, which version produces an actionable output and why?

A) Version A, because a simple question allows the model to use its full reasoning capacity without being constrained by a prescribed framework B) Version A, because the model surfaces issues the five-element framework would miss C) Version B, because naming the specific framework gives the model a structured lens, which produces a concrete five-element scorecard the student can act on rather than generic feedback D) Both versions produce the same output; the model applies the five-element framework by default regardless of how the question is worded E) Neither version is appropriate; the student should run a live A/B test on real traffic rather than asking an AI

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 13.7, LO 13.9 — The chapter's Apply box explicitly works this contrast, stating that the vague version returns generic advice the student could have guessed while the sharp version returns an audit the student can act on, from the same assistant at the same cost. The lesson is that learning what to ask is most of the skill.

Tags: product-page audit; AI prompt quality; five-job framework; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

AI-2 — Abandoned-Cart Sequence: Evaluate an AI-Drafted Message One

A student asks an AI to draft message one of her abandoned-cart sequence for a soy candle store. The AI produces the following:

"Hi [first name], you left something in your cart! We know quality matters to you, so we're offering 15% off your order today only — use code COMEBACK15 at checkout. This offer expires in 24 hours. Don't miss out!"

Using the chapter's abandoned-cart recovery playbook, identify the specific error in this AI-generated message and explain what the correct message-one behavior should be. Then state what long-run economic harm the AI's version causes if the student deploys it unchanged.

Sample answer and rubric:

The AI has placed a 15 percent discount in message one, sent roughly one hour after abandonment. The chapter explicitly identifies this as the error the discount ladder is designed to prevent. Message one's only job is to remind the buyer of the cart she left, because the buyer is still warm and most first-message recovers (15 to 25 percent of opens) happen without any incentive at all. The correct message one is a plain reminder with no coupon, no urgency language about expiration, and no scarcity claim.

The long-run economic harm is training. A buyer who receives a discount in message one learns that abandoning the cart produces a discount. On every future visit she adds to cart, navigates away deliberately, and waits for the coupon. The chapter states this erodes margin permanently and cites an 8 to 14 percentage point contribution-margin penalty per recovered customer compared to a brand that holds the discount for message three. At scale, a message-one discount policy converts a recovery tool into a margin-destruction mechanism that grows larger as the customer base grows.

Corrected message one: "Hi [first name], you left something behind. Your [product name] is still in your cart — here's a quick link back." No incentive. No expiration language. The buyer decides on the product's merits or returns after the brand earns the next step.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 13.4 — The item requires the student to identify a specific rule violation in an AI-generated marketing artifact and articulate the downstream economic consequence, both of which the chapter teaches explicitly.

Tags: abandoned-cart sequence; discount ladder; AI-generated content; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Ethical understanding; Written communication

AI-3 — Agent-Readiness Audit: Identify the Missing Checks

A student's candle store is set up as follows: the product page has a beautiful hero image and 200 five-star reviews, the Shopify store runs a Dawn theme with a standard checkout that requires account creation before payment, there is no Product or Offer schema markup, the spec table sits below the brand-story section, and Shop Pay is disabled because the student prefers to route all payments through PayPal only.

A shopping agent (ChatGPT Shopping or a Perplexity agent) attempts to complete a purchase for a buyer who is on a phone call. According to the chapter's five-check agent-readiness framework, how many of the five checks does this store pass, and which specific checks fail? For each failure, state the one fix the student should implement.

Sample answer:

The agent-readiness framework has five checks. Evaluating each against the described store:

Check 1, Product and Offer schema: FAIL. The store has no Product or Offer structured-data markup. An agent reads those fields before it reads the rendered page; without them, the agent cannot reliably extract the product name, price, SKU, and availability. Fix: add a valid JSON-LD Product and Offer schema block to the product page template in Shopify's theme code or via a schema app.

Check 2, Machine-readable spec table above the marketing copy: FAIL. The spec table sits below the brand-story section. Agents extract fields from the spec table and compare them across competitors; placing the spec below the brand copy means the agent may never reach it or may misattribute it. Fix: move the spec table (material, dimensions, weight, country of origin, returns window) above the marketing copy in the page template.

Check 3, Express checkout: FAIL. Shop Pay is disabled and the only path is PayPal. An agentic purchase completes reliably only when at least one one-click surface (Shop Pay, PayPal express, or Apple Pay) is exposed at the product or cart page level. PayPal alone may work if the buyer has a saved PayPal account the agent can access, but Shop Pay on Shopify is the most reliable agent-compatible express path. Fix: re-enable Shop Pay via Shopify Payments settings.

Check 4, Price and stock truthfulness: PASS (assumed). No information in the prompt indicates a mismatch between listed and actual price or stock.

Check 5, Guest checkout: FAIL. The store requires account creation before payment. An agent cannot create an account on behalf of the buyer, so it will abandon the purchase at that gate. Fix: enable guest checkout in Shopify settings (Checkout and accounts: "Allow customers to check out without creating an account").

The store passes 1 of 5 checks. Three of the four failures require a one-time settings or code change, not ongoing maintenance.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 13.9 — The item requires the student to apply the five-check checklist to a described store, identify specific failures, and propose concrete fixes. It tests whether the student can translate the chapter's abstract agent-readiness framework into diagnostic practice.

Tags: agent-readiness; Agentic Storefronts; structured data; guest checkout; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

AI-4 — Sidekick as DTC Operator: Evaluate an AI-Generated Unit-Economics Report

A student connects her Shopify and Klaviyo accounts to Shopify's Sidekick AI (described in the chapter as the operator the merchant talks to in plain language). She asks: "Run the unit-economics check and tell me if I can fund my own growth."

Sidekick returns the following output:

"Your last 30 days: 142 orders, $6,390 in revenue, $1,800 in Meta ad spend. Average order value: $45. Contribution margin: approximately 38 percent, or $17.10 per order. Estimated CAC: $12.68. First-order LTV: $17.10. LTV-to-CAC ratio: 1.35 to 1. Payback period: approximately 1 month at this purchase frequency. Your ratio is below 3 to 1 and you are not yet funding growth from current margin alone. Recommend: focus retention — your 30-day repeat-purchase rate is 11 percent. Doubling it to 22 percent would move your effective LTV to roughly $34.20 and the ratio to approximately 2.7 to 1, near the health bar."

Using the chapter's four-number test and the LTV-to-CAC health standard, (a) verify that Sidekick's arithmetic is correct, (b) evaluate the accuracy and usefulness of its recommendation, and (c) identify one limitation of treating Sidekick's output as definitive for a business decision.

Sample answer:

(a) Arithmetic verification: CAC = $1,800 ad spend / 142 orders = $12.68. Correct. Contribution margin = 38 percent of $45 = $17.10. Correct. LTV-to-CAC on first order only = $17.10 / $12.68 = 1.35 to 1. Correct. Sidekick's payback period claim of "approximately 1 month" is accurate only in the narrow sense that one order's margin ($17.10) exceeds one order's acquisition cost ($12.68) — but the chapter's payback period is computed on how many months until cumulative margin covers the acquisition cost, including the timing of repeat orders. At 11 percent 30-day repeat rate the typical second order does not arrive in month one, so the payback is not literally one month for most buyers. Sidekick's framing is slightly misleading here. The projected repeat-purchase LTV at 22 percent: $17.10 x 2 purchases = $34.20. The ratio $34.20 / $12.68 = 2.70 to 1. Correct.

(b) The recommendation is well-targeted and consistent with the chapter. The chapter identifies the LTV-to-CAC ratio below 3 to 1 as the primary diagnostic failure and argues that repeat purchase is the lever that moves the ratio toward the health bar when the first-order margin alone cannot clear it. Sidekick's identification of the 11 percent repeat rate as the constraint and its suggestion to double it are consistent with the chapter's lesson that lifting repeat purchase frequency moves the ratio more effectively than cutting a CAC that already has a floor. The recommendation is useful as a first-pass read.

(c) One important limitation: Sidekick's LTV estimate uses a 30-day window, which is too short to capture the full lifetime value of customers who may make their second purchase at month 3, 6, or 9, as the chapter's candle-store model shows. Using first-order margin as a proxy for LTV understates the true economics in any category with a multi-month repurchase cycle. Before making a capital-allocation or ad-spend decision based on this output, the student should verify the repeat-purchase timeline on a cohort basis (not just 30-day repeat rate) and reconcile the attributed revenue with the Shopify order-level data her own warehouse holds, as the chapter recommends treating the brand's warehouse as the canonical truth every platform's numbers reconcile against.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 13.6, LO 13.9 — The item requires the student to verify AI-generated arithmetic, evaluate the quality of a recommendation against the chapter's standards, and identify a limitation of taking AI output at face value — all competencies the chapter's Apply box explicitly calls for.

Tags: Sidekick; AI DTC operations; unit economics; LTV-to-CAC; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

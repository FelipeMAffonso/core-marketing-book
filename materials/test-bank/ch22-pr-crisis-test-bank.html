Chapter 22 PR, Crisis Comms, and Earned Media — Test Bank

Chapter: 22 — PR, Crisis Comms, and Earned Media Source note: Original authoring. No Kotler parent exists for this chapter. Every item is authored from scratch against OUR Chapter 22 (ground truth: book/v7/chapter-22-pr-crisis.html). Framework names, cases, empirical anchors, and the running example (consumer-cup category, Bud Light, Stanley, Patagonia) are drawn exclusively from our chapter.

Learning Objectives (Ch22, Our Chapter)

  • LO 22.1 Distinguish marketing, PR, and corporate communications by who controls the message after it leaves the building, and sort any channel into the PESO model.
  • LO 22.2 Describe Grunig's four PR models (press agentry, public information, two-way asymmetric, two-way symmetric) and identify which posture a real organization is in from observable behavior.
  • LO 22.3 Explain how the Edelman trust ladder orders audience trust and why a pitch earns more credibility than a paid placement.
  • LO 22.4 Write or evaluate a personalized pitch email using the four-part template, distinguish exclusive from broad release, and explain the sender-reputation decay that follows an unscrubbed media list.
  • LO 22.5 Compute share of voice, interpret sentiment scores, and explain what message pull-through adds that volume and sentiment alone cannot.
  • LO 22.6 Apply the first-hour rule (the 60-minute / ~70% amplification anchor), write a five-component holding statement, and select the correct SCCT response family (deny, diminish, rebuild) given what the audience already believes caused the crisis.
  • LO 22.7 Classify a crisis into one of the seven types and three clusters, place it in the five-stage lifecycle, and explain the image-repair continuum from denial to mortification (Benoit).
  • LO 22.8 Describe how a 2026 AI crisis-monitoring stack works (three tiers, escalation threshold, escalation prediction) and explain the two-call AI red-team pattern for holding-statement generation.

Section I — Multiple-Choice Items (21 items)

Item 1

The key distinction between marketing communications and public relations is _______.

A) Marketing reaches consumers; PR reaches regulators only. B) Marketing is measured by revenue; PR is unmeasurable. C) Marketing runs through channels the brand controls or pays for; PR runs through channels it does not control, such as journalism and community. D) Marketing is an internal function; PR is an external agency service. E) Marketing and PR are synonyms for the same communications role.

Answer: C

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 22.1 — Marketing controls the message through owned/paid channels; PR works audiences it cannot pay to reach. The defining distinction is control, not audience type.

Tags: PESO model; marketing vs PR; earned media; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 2

A Wall Street Journal feature on the Patagonia founder, a Bud Light Super Bowl spot, a hostile TikTok clip of the brand, and the Duolingo owl's own social account represent which four PESO buckets in order?

A) Owned, paid, shared, earned B) Earned, paid, shared, owned C) Paid, earned, owned, shared D) Shared, earned, paid, owned E) Earned, owned, paid, shared

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 22.1 — The WSJ feature is earned (a third party wrote it, the brand did not pay). The Super Bowl spot is paid (the brand bought the placement and controls the message). The hostile TikTok clip is shared (the audience spreads it). The Duolingo owl account is owned (the brand runs it). The PESO sort is controlled by who makes the message and who pays.

Tags: PESO model; earned media; paid media; shared media; owned media; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 3

Patagonia gathers customer feedback on its Worn Wear repair program and then expands the program and changes its return policy in direct response to what customers said. According to Grunig's four-model framework, this is an example of _______.

A) Press agentry, where the brand talks and does not listen. B) Public information, where the brand shares accurate facts without adjusting. C) Two-way asymmetric communication, where the brand listens but only to sharpen its message. D) Two-way symmetric communication, where the brand changes its actions, not just its words, in response to its publics. E) Corporate communications, which operates above and separate from any PR model.

Answer: D

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 22.2 — Two-way symmetric is the posture where the organization both listens to its publics and actually changes its actions based on what they say. Adjusting the repair program itself on customer feedback is the operational test; adjusting only the messaging about it would be two-way asymmetric.

Tags: Grunig's four models; two-way symmetric; PR posture; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 4

A brand's communications director believes her department is fully two-way symmetric. When the chapter's diagnostic framework is applied, the department is found to run customer feedback surveys quarterly but to have changed no operational policy in four years. The most accurate description of the department's actual posture is _______.

A) Press agentry, because the department has no formal listening structure. B) Public information, because the surveys carry accurate information in one direction. C) Two-way asymmetric, because the brand collects feedback but uses it only to sharpen messaging rather than to change decisions. D) Two-way symmetric, because surveys constitute meaningful listening even if no decisions have changed. E) Corporate communications, which is a separate and superior function that does not apply the four-model diagnostic.

Answer: C

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 22.2 — The four-model diagnostic turns on two questions: whether the brand listens and whether it changes in response. Listening without changing is two-way asymmetric. The chapter notes that the most common failure pattern is a department that describes itself as symmetric while operating asymmetrically, the exact gap the Bud Light case exposed.

Tags: Grunig's four models; two-way asymmetric; PR diagnostic; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 5

The 2026 Edelman Trust Barometer reports that undergraduate students making a dining decision trust which source the most?

A) A paid campus advertisement placed by the restaurant B) A sponsored social post placed by the restaurant on Instagram C) A classmate's first-person account of eating there D) A press release on the restaurant's own website E) A review published on the restaurant's Google My Business profile

Answer: C

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 22.3 — The Edelman trust ladder orders sources by how much audiences trust them. Employees speaking to their own networks rank first, followed by known beat reporters, with paid celebrity/advertising below both. The chapter applies this to student experience: a classmate's story outranks a sponsored ad and the brand's own posts, because it is a peer voice with no financial stake.

Tags: Edelman trust barometer; trust ladder; earned media credibility; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 6

A 2026 pitch email should run under 220 words because _______.

A) Journalists open emails only when they contain under 200 words. B) PR software vendors cap pitch length at 220 words as a deliverability requirement. C) The length corresponds to roughly twelve seconds of reading on a phone, which is the window in which a reporter decides whether to continue. D) Research shows 220 words is the optimal length for AI-assisted pitch-matching scores. E) Shorter pitches receive automatic exclusives from tier-one publications.

Answer: C

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 22.4 — The chapter states that 220 words is the length a journalist can read on a phone in roughly twelve seconds and decide whether to keep going. The read-decision is fixed, and the template length is calibrated to the decision window, not to any vendor rule.

Tags: pitch email; media relations; four-part template; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 7

A PR person sends the same pitch to fifty reporters on a category list without customizing any of them. According to the chapter, the likely read rate for each of those pitches is closest to _______.

A) 40 percent B) 25 percent C) 15 percent D) 5 percent E) 0 percent, because generic pitches are automatically routed to spam

Answer: D

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 22.4 — The chapter cites a 5 percent read rate on a generic pitch. Adding a specific detail from the reporter's recent stories to the first paragraph raises that to over 40 percent, which is the difference between two reporters and twenty opening the email from a fifty-person list.

Tags: pitch personalization; read rate; media relations; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 8

A brand's PR director skips the quarterly email-verification step for the team's media list. The chapter identifies the most serious long-run consequence as _______.

A) The brand's journalists receive a small number of bounced emails, which are individually embarrassing. B) The brand's sender reputation score falls on the target publication's mail server, causing future pitches to route to spam before any reporter sees them. C) PR software vendors downgrade the brand's journalist matching scores. D) Journalists become aware of the bounces and add the brand's domain to personal blocklists. E) The brand's PESO allocation shifts toward owned media because earned media becomes inaccessible.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 22.4 — The chapter explains the compounding mechanism: bounces lower the brand's sender reputation score on the server, and once that score falls below a threshold the server routes future pitches to spam folders silently. The cost is not the bounce notice but the loss of reach for months, because reporters never see the pitches even though they arrive.

Tags: media list hygiene; sender reputation; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 9

In the consumer-cup category last quarter, 1,000 articles mentioned a brand name. Stanley was mentioned in 230 of them; Yeti in 380; Owala in 250; Hydro Flask in 140. What is Stanley's share of voice for the quarter?

A) 23 percent B) 38 percent C) 25 percent D) 14 percent E) 17 percent

Answer: A

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 22.5 — SOV = brand mentions / (brand mentions + all named-competitor mentions) x 100. Stanley: 230 / 1,000 = 23 percent. The formula is one line, and the denominator is all named-brand mentions in the category, not total articles.

Tags: share of voice; SOV arithmetic; earned media metrics; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 10

A brand's monitoring platform shows that its SOV in the fitness-app category grew from 18 percent to 28 percent last quarter. A closer read of the coverage reveals that nearly all new mentions came from a single critical investigation in a fitness trade publication. Which of the following statements best reflects how the chapter would interpret this result?

A) SOV growth always signals stronger brand health, so this is unambiguously positive. B) Volume alone does not distinguish positive from hostile coverage; sentiment scoring is required to interpret whether the SOV gain helped or harmed the brand. C) A 10-percentage-point SOV jump driven by one article is statistically unreliable and should be discarded. D) The critical investigation will boost message pull-through by increasing the percentage of coverage that carries the brand's key messages. E) Competitive SOV always falls when one brand gains, so the net effect on the category is zero.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 22.5 — The chapter defines sentiment as what SOV cannot show: whether the brand came off positively or negatively in the coverage. Volume-only tracking treats a hit piece and a product profile as identical, which is the gap sentiment scoring closes. This item tests whether students understand that SOV growth can be bad news.

Tags: share of voice; sentiment; earned media metrics; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 11

A consumer-goods brand pitches three key messages: sustainability, durability, and ownership economics. Last quarter's earned coverage ran 72 percent on durability, 23 percent on ownership economics, and 5 percent on sustainability. According to the chapter's definition, the brand's message pull-through result is telling the team that _______.

A) The overall PR program succeeded because two of three pillars exceeded 10 percent. B) The sustainability pillar has a pitch problem: the stories covering the brand are not carrying that message, so the pitching strategy for sustainability needs to change. C) The brand should eliminate the sustainability pillar from its messaging house because it is not resonating. D) Message pull-through is not a meaningful metric because it cannot be tied directly to revenue. E) A 5 percent sustainability rate is strong because most earned coverage focuses on product attributes, not sustainability claims.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 22.5 — Message pull-through measures the fraction of earned coverage carrying at least one of the brand's intended messages. A 5 percent rate on sustainability means the team's sustainability pitches are failing to land in the coverage, which is a pitch-strategy problem. The chapter states that the metric shows which pillar needs pitch reinforcement, not whether to abandon the pillar.

Tags: message pull-through; PR strategy; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 12

On April 1, 2023, the influencer Dylan Mulvaney posted a ten-second Instagram video with a custom Bud Light can. The brand's crisis communications team issued its first public statement on April 14. According to the first-hour rule and its empirical anchor, the ten-day silence had the most predictable consequence of _______.

A) Regulatory agencies opening a formal inquiry into the brand's influencer practices. B) The brand's earned media sentiment improving because the absence of statements prevents a defensive narrative from forming. C) Negative-sentiment amplification growing far beyond what a same-day response would have produced, because the early reading is the one the audience keeps. D) Competitor brands capturing the SOV spike while Bud Light stayed below detection thresholds. E) The media list for Anheuser-Busch degrading as journalists lost contact with the response team.

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 22.6 — The chapter states the first-hour rule is not folklore: brands that respond inside sixty minutes see roughly seventy percent less negative-sentiment amplification than those that wait four hours. A ten-day delay ensures the early hostile reading locks in before any correction arrives.

Tags: first-hour rule; 60-min/70% anchor; Bud Light; crisis response timing; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 13

A brand has just seen a customer-complaint video reach 800,000 views in three hours. Which of the following is the best holding statement for the first hour, assuming the brand knows only that the video is real and is still investigating?

A) "We have reviewed the video thoroughly and are confident our product met all applicable safety standards at the time of manufacture." B) "We are aware of the customer video circulating online. Our team is actively investigating. [Name], our [Title], is leading the response. We will share what we learn by 6 p.m. today at [channel]." C) "We appreciate the customer sharing their experience and invite them to contact our customer service team directly." D) "This situation is under review by our legal team and we are not in a position to comment at this time." E) "Our product has an excellent safety record across millions of units, and we remain committed to the highest quality standards."

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 22.6 — The five-component holding statement acknowledges what is happening, confirms investigation, names the response lead, commits to a next-update time, and names the channel. Answer B fills all five slots. Answers A and E are internalizing-heavy and skip instructing and adjusting information. Answer D is the "no comment" pattern the chapter identifies as the worst response. Answer C skips three of the five components.

Tags: holding statement; first-hour rule; crisis response; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 14

In November 2023, a customer named Danielle posted a TikTok of her burned car with a Stanley Quencher inside still holding ice. Global President Terence Reilly responded the same day, on TikTok, with a video promising to replace the cup and the car. The chapter describes this outcome as _______.

A) A poorly managed crisis that exposed the brand to significant product-liability risk. B) A missed opportunity to use the deny response family since no blame was attributed to the brand. C) The inverse of Bud Light point for point: the first-hour rule met on the correct platform, generating 41 percent share of voice that week and a net brand gain. D) An accidental crisis in the accidental cluster that required a diminish response family, which Reilly executed correctly. E) A brand-promise violation because replacing a car exceeded the product's express warranty obligations.

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 22.6 — The chapter states that Stanley's same-day CEO response on TikTok, inside the first-hour window, pushed SOV to 41 percent and produced a net brand gain. The chapter explicitly frames it as the inverse of Bud Light: the same template run in opposite directions produced opposite outcomes.

Tags: Stanley case; first-hour rule; crisis response; share of voice; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 15

SCCT classifies a crisis primarily by _______.

A) The financial magnitude of the potential loss to the firm. B) The number of people affected by the incident. C) What the audience has already concluded about who caused the crisis, which determines which response family is even available. D) The speed at which social media amplified the triggering content. E) Whether the incident occurred online or in a physical location.

Answer: C

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 22.7 — SCCT sorts crises by audience attribution, not by severity or speed. The cluster (victim, accidental, preventable) is what the audience has already decided, and that decision constrains which response family the brand can credibly use. The brand does not choose its family; the audience sets the constraint before the brand speaks.

Tags: SCCT; crisis classification; response family; audience attribution; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 16

In 1982 Tylenol used the deny response family effectively, while Volkswagen in 2015 could not credibly use the same family. The key structural difference is _______.

A) Tylenol was a consumer brand; Volkswagen was an industrial manufacturer. B) Tylenol acted faster than Volkswagen, so the deny window was still open. C) The Tylenol crisis was a malevolence type where an outside actor caused the harm, so the audience attributed blame externally; Volkswagen engineered the deception itself, so the audience attributed full responsibility and only rebuild was credible. D) Tylenol recalled its product, which is the prerequisite action the deny family requires. E) The EPA produced evidence against Volkswagen that would have been irrelevant if Volkswagen had acted more quickly.

Answer: C

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 22.7 — The chapter's SCCT cluster analysis makes this distinction explicitly. Tylenol was a victim cluster (malevolence type): an outside actor laced bottles, so deny was credible. Volkswagen was a preventable cluster (deception type): the company knowingly engineered the defeat device, so deny was unavailable because the EPA already had the evidence, and only rebuild was credible, which required CEO resignation first.

Tags: SCCT; deny vs rebuild; Tylenol; Volkswagen; crisis cases; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 17

The five-stage crisis lifecycle, as described in the chapter, is signal detection, prevention/preparedness, containment, recovery, and learning. Research on organizations that have experienced a crisis shows that most are competent at _____ and weakest at _____.

A) Signal detection; recovery B) Prevention/preparedness; containment C) Containment; the stages on either side (signal detection, preparedness, recovery, and learning) D) Learning; containment E) Recovery; learning

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 22.7 — The chapter states most organizations are competent at containment because the time pressure forces the work, and weak at the stages on either side. Two findings follow: preparation costs about one-tenth of post-crisis response, and organizations that skip learning repeat the same crisis class within five years.

Tags: crisis lifecycle; five stages; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 18

A brand issues a holding statement that reads: "We are deeply sorry for what has occurred. We have always stood for the safety and well-being of our customers, and we will do everything in our power to ensure this never happens again." According to Sturges's three-information-types framework and the chapter's analysis, the statement is most likely to fail because _______.

A) It is written in the first person, which the chapter identifies as inappropriate for formal statements. B) The statement is heavy on internalizing information (defending the brand's identity) but omits instructing information (what to do to be safe) and adjusting information (naming the emotion the audience is already feeling). C) The holding statement exceeds the recommended word count. D) The statement should not be issued until the SCCT cluster has been formally classified by the legal team. E) The word "sorry" triggers legal liability and should be replaced with "regret."

Answer: B

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 22.6, LO 22.7 — The chapter explains Sturges's framework: instructing (what to do to be safe), adjusting (naming the emotion), and internalizing (who the brand is). A statement heavy on internalizing and light on the other two reliably fails because it reads as the brand caring more about its image than the people affected. The sample language given reads as internalizing-heavy and skips both other types.

Tags: Sturges; three information types; holding statement; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 19

Boeing's sustained communication across the 2018-2024 period — continuous updates, software revisions, regular briefings — illustrates a failure of _______ rather than a failure of speed or transparency.

A) The deny response family, which Boeing should have used earlier. B) The five-sentence holding statement, which Boeing never issued. C) Framing: an engineering frame asks the audience to wait for a technical fix while the audience needed an accountability frame that named what was lost. D) Message pull-through, because Boeing's pillar messaging was not reflected in trade press coverage. E) The two-way asymmetric posture, because Boeing listened to regulators but not to families of crash victims.

Answer: C

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 22.7 — The chapter's Boeing analysis is precise: the company did not go silent. It communicated continuously, but every statement framed the problem as a technical fix on a timeline (engineering frame). The audience needed accountability for lost lives (accountability frame). Framing is the failure, not speed or transparency, and it illustrates how a brand can say a great deal while saying nothing the audience needed.

Item 21

In the chapter's image-repair continuum, the strategy called mortification is _____, and the chapter identifies the most reliable signal of an unworkable response as _____.

A) A public pledge to donate a percentage of profits to affected parties; announcing corrective action without apologizing. B) The explicit apology that accepts full responsibility; the absence of mortification when the audience has already attributed full blame. Section II — Scenario / Short-Answer Items (4 items)

SA-1

A regional fast-casual chain with 45 locations discovers at 9:15 a.m. on a Tuesday that a customer has posted a TikTok video showing a mouse in the kitchen of its busiest downtown location. The video is at 200,000 views and growing fast. The VP of Marketing wants to say nothing until the legal team reviews a statement. The Director of Communications argues the first-hour rule requires action by 10:15 a.m.

Using the chapter's framework, explain why the Director of Communications is correct, name the four steps the brand must complete inside the first hour, and write a holding statement that fills the five required components. Do not assign blame or speculate on cause.

Sample answer: The Director of Communications is correct because the chapter's first-hour rule is grounded in an empirical anchor: brands that respond inside sixty minutes see roughly seventy percent less negative-sentiment amplification than those that wait four hours. A 200,000-view video at 9:15 a.m. is already moving faster than the brand's legal review cycle. Silence by 10:15 a.m. allows the hostile reading to lock in before any response arrives.

The four steps inside the first hour are: (1) acknowledge that something is happening, (2) confirm the team is investigating, (3) name who is leading the response, and (4) commit to a time when the next update will come. None of the four requires the brand to know what actually happened.

Sample holding statement: "We are aware of a video circulating on TikTok showing conditions at one of our downtown locations. Our team is reviewing the situation now and we take concerns about our kitchens seriously. [Name, Title] is leading our response. We will share what we have learned by 1 p.m. today on our official social accounts and at [website]. We will have more information then."

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 22.6

Tags: first-hour rule; holding statement; five components; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Written communication

SA-2

A regional airline experiences a runway overshoot at a mid-sized airport. No passengers are injured, but the plane is damaged and the runway is closed for six hours. An external investigation later concludes the cause was a mechanical failure in the braking system, which the manufacturer had identified in a service bulletin eight months earlier that the airline had not yet addressed. Classify this crisis using SCCT (identify the crisis type, the cluster, and the available response family), explain why the deny family is not available, and identify which image-repair strategy from Benoit's continuum the airline must include in its public response.

Sample answer: Under SCCT, the crisis type is technological (a mechanical failure traceable to an unresolved manufacturer service bulletin). In cluster terms it is accidental, because the airline did not knowingly cause the failure, but the cause happened on its watch and the audience knows the service bulletin was available and unaddressed. The accidental cluster generally makes diminish available, but the unaddressed service bulletin creates a documented preventability finding that pushes the case toward the preventable cluster for at least the negligence dimension.

Deny is not available because the airline did not experience external harm (no tampering, no outside actor). The aircraft was under the airline's operational control and the service bulletin is a documented paper trail showing the vulnerability was known. An audience that sees the service bulletin will read deny as a second offense: first they failed to fix it, then they said it was not their fault. Any credible response must use rebuild: an apology accepting that the bulletin was not addressed in time, corrective action naming the full fleet inspection and the timeline, and compensation or good-faith outreach to affected passengers.

From Benoit's image-repair continuum, the airline must include mortification, the explicit acceptance of responsibility, alongside corrective action. A response that announces only the fleet inspection without acknowledging what the airline knew and did not do would read as process news covering a negligence finding, exactly the failure mode the chapter illustrates with the Boeing case.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 22.7, LO 22.6

Tags: SCCT; image repair; Benoit; accidental cluster; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

SA-3

A startup personal-care brand wants to pitch its new reef-safe sunscreen to the environmental and lifestyle beat of a national outdoor-recreation magazine. The PR intern proposes sending a generic press release to the magazine's general inbox. Using the chapter's pitch framework, identify three specific failures in the intern's approach and rewrite the process as a four-step checklist the brand should actually follow. Your answer should name the four parts of a pitch email and state the send-and-follow-up cadence.

Sample answer: Three specific failures in the intern's approach:

First, pitching to the general inbox rather than a named beat reporter. The chapter states that a 2026 pitch goes to a named human because the beat reporter who covers the category is the one who will see why the story fits. A pitch to the general inbox gets no reporter's eye before deletion.

Second, sending a press release rather than a pitch. A press release is the formal document the reporter receives after the pitch is accepted and the briefing happens. The pitch is the message that asks the reporter to consider covering the story.

Third, using a generic send with no personalization. A generic pitch produces roughly a 5 percent read rate. Adding a specific detail from the reporter's recent stories raises that to over 40 percent.

Four-step checklist the brand should follow:

  1. Find the named reporter. Search "reef-safe outdoor reporter [publication name]" or use Muck Rack to identify the beat journalist who has recently covered sustainable outdoor products. Confirm their public email and read their last three to five stories.
  1. Read the recent stories and note one specific detail from each. Write down one detail, a number, a source, or an angle from the reporter's recent work to use in the pitch's opening.
  1. Write the four-part pitch email: (a) subject line under sixty characters naming the news plainly; (b) first paragraph under fifty words naming the news and why this specific reporter should cover it, using the detail from step 2; (c) proof-point paragraph under seventy words with one verifiable fact the reporter could call to confirm; (d) next-step paragraph under sixty words offering one specific path forward with a stated time window. Total: under 220 words.
  1. Send, then wait twenty-four hours before any follow-up. If no reply comes, send one short follow-up noting the original pitch.

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 22.4

Tags: pitch email; four-part template; media relations; read rate; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Written communication

SA-4

The chapter describes two relationship types that sit underneath earned-media metrics: communal and exchange. Explain the difference between them, explain which one determines whether a brand survives a bad quarter in the press, and apply that framework to the Bud Light April 2023 case by identifying which relationship type Anheuser-Busch appears to have been relying on and why that reliance made the volume loss irreversible faster than a communal-relationship brand would have experienced.

Sample answer: A communal relationship is built on mutual care rather than a running tab. Each side helps because it cares about the other's welfare and expects nothing specific back. It survives a bad stretch because the goodwill was never conditional on the last transaction. An exchange relationship is one where each side gives in explicit expectation of getting something back, the way a gym membership works: you pay, the gym lets you in, and the relationship ends without ceremony the moment either side stops delivering.

The relationship type that determines whether a brand survives a bad quarter is the communal one. A brand running a healthy exchange relationship has no buffer. The exchange assumes the brand keeps delivering value, and the moment that assumption breaks, the audience has no reason to wait. There is no accumulated goodwill to draw down because none was ever deposited.

Applied to Bud Light April 2023: Bud Light's positioning as America's beer in the decades leading up to 2023 rested primarily on the transactional logic of mainstream accessibility, affordable price, and social ubiquity, the classic exchange relationship. The brand was in millions of hands because it was the default in its price tier and occasion, not because buyers had deep affective attachment to the brand's values or identity. When the Dylan Mulvaney video triggered a boycott infrastructure, the audience had no communal goodwill to weigh against the grievance. The relationship was: we buy Bud Light because it fits the occasion. When the occasion became contested, the reason to buy dissolved with it. A brand with a strong communal relationship, built over years of behavior-based trust rather than transaction-based ubiquity, would have had more audience patience for the explanation, just as the chapter notes a friend who breaks a laptop by accident is typically forgiven inside a semester.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 22.5, LO 22.6

Tags: communal vs exchange relationships; Bud Light; brand resilience; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

AI-1 — Evaluate an AI-Generated Crisis Statement

A junior communications manager pastes the following draft holding statement into a note for a vice president's approval:

"We are aware of the situation and are deeply concerned. Our company has a long and proud history of delivering safe and high-quality products to our customers, and we will always put safety first. We are looking into the matter and will provide an update when we have more information."

Using the chapter's five-failure-mode red-team (blame, overcommit, tone, time, posture) and Sturges's three-information-types framework, identify every failure in this draft.

A) The draft fails only on tone, because "deeply concerned" is too informal for a corporate crisis statement. B) The draft fails on time (no specific update window), posture (internalizing-heavy with no instructing or adjusting information), and blame (the phrase "safe and high-quality" implies the failure could not have been the company's fault). C) The draft is adequate for a Diff 1 crisis but would need a CEO signature for a more severe incident. D) The draft fails only on the missing response lead, which the company should add before publishing. E) The draft is complete and passes all five failure modes because it acknowledges the situation, expresses concern, and promises an update.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 22.6, LO 22.8 — Red-team analysis: TIME fails because "when we have more information" gives no specific window (the chapter requires a named time and channel). POSTURE fails because the draft is internalizing-heavy ("long and proud history," "always put safety first") and skips instructing information (nothing to do to be safe) and adjusting information (the audience's emotion is named nowhere). BLAME: "safe and high-quality products" is a pre-emptive defense that implies the failure is external; in a case where attribution is unknown, this reads as the brand defending itself before the facts are in. OVERCOMMIT: "always put safety first" is a commitment the brand cannot guarantee. Five-mode score: time, posture, blame, and overcommit all fail.

Tags: AI red-team; holding statement audit; Sturges; five failure modes; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Analytical thinking

AI-2 — Bring Your Own Crisis: SCCT Classification

A student pastes the following crisis brief into the course's SCCT classifier widget:

"A major fast-fashion retailer is facing viral news coverage revealing that its supply-chain audit team submitted false certifications to the EU's supply-chain transparency regulator, showing no forced labor in three Southeast Asian factories where investigators later documented it. The retailer's own audit team submitted the false documents. The story was broken by an investigative journalist. The retailer's first statement reads: 'We are reviewing the matter and take supply chain compliance very seriously.'"

The model returns: CLUSTER: accidental. RESPONSE FAMILY: diminish. The student disagrees. Which of the following best explains why the model's classification is wrong, and what the correct answer is?

A) The model is correct. Misreporting to regulators is unintentional and therefore accidental. B) The model is correct because the audit team, not the senior leadership, submitted the false documents, so corporate intent is absent. C) The model is wrong. The audit team submitted false certifications deliberately. This is a deception crisis in the preventable cluster, and the only available response family is rebuild, which requires an apology, structural corrective action, and likely leadership change. D) The model is wrong because the crisis involves a regulator, which moves it automatically to the malevolence cluster. E) The model is wrong. The correct cluster is victim, because the retailer did not benefit financially from the false certifications.

Answer: C

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 22.7, LO 22.8 — The chapter's SCCT taxonomy places deliberate deception in the preventable cluster with the deception type. The audit team knowingly submitted false documents; the company's employees carried out the fraud. Deception type means the audience attributes full responsibility, the audience has documentary evidence from the investigation, and deny and diminish are not available. Only rebuild is credible, and rebuild in a deception case typically requires leadership accountability first. The student's judgment about the model's cluster is the exact point where a human director's judgment earns its keep.

Tags: SCCT; bring-your-own-crisis; AI classifier; preventable cluster; deception; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Ethical understanding

AI-3 — AI Crisis-Monitoring Escalation Threshold

A communications team for a mid-sized athletic apparel brand has set up a 2026 monitoring stack. The AI-augmented layer flags a TikTok video showing a seam failure on a popular jacket model. The video has 80,000 views at the time of the alert, but the escalation-prediction model scores it 0.87 on a 0-to-1 growth-probability scale for the next 48 hours.

According to the chapter's framework, the most appropriate action for the on-call communications manager is _______.

A) Wait for the video to reach 500,000 views before pulling in senior leadership, because current volume is below typical viral thresholds. B) Treat the escalation-prediction score as the primary signal and pull in the response lead now, drafting a five-component holding statement before the cycle accelerates. C) Discard the escalation-prediction score and rely only on current view count, because predictive models in PR monitoring have not been validated in peer-reviewed research. D) Post a customer-service reply asking the user to contact the brand's support email, which satisfies the first-hour requirement without a full statement. E) Forward the alert to the brand's paid advertising team so they can begin a counter-campaign while the PR team investigates.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 22.8 — The chapter defines the AI-augmented monitoring tier as adding escalation prediction so the brand can act before the cycle peaks, not after. A 0.87 growth-probability score is designed to trigger exactly this: pull in the response lead, draft the holding statement, and be ready to publish before the algorithm surfaces the video to the next 10 million people. Waiting for the view count to cross a threshold defeats the purpose of the predictive layer.

Tags: AI crisis monitoring; escalation threshold; escalation prediction; 2026 stack; AACSB: Application of knowledge

AI-4 — AI Red-Team Pattern for Statement Generation

A student learns that the crisis-statement AI red-team pattern in the chapter uses two separate model calls. She asks why the pattern requires two calls instead of one call that generates and reviews the statement in the same turn.

Which of the following best explains the structural reason for the two-call pattern?

A) Using two separate calls reduces cost by routing the cheaper review step to a smaller model. B) The first call produces the holding statement and the second call applies a separate set of constraints; a model that drafts a statement cannot reliably red-team its own output because a drafter will systematically undercount the failures it introduced. C) Platform rate limits require multiple calls for any task longer than a single paragraph. D) The two-call pattern satisfies a legal requirement that AI-generated crisis statements receive independent review before publication. E) The first call checks the SCCT cluster and the second call writes the statement; the review happens inside the same second call.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 22.8 — The chapter states the two-call pattern is structural, not a cost or rate-limit choice. A model that drafts a statement and then reviews it will systematically undercount its own failures because a drafter cannot reliably red-team itself. The second call is set up as a separate second opinion that scores the draft on five failure modes and names the top fix without rewriting, so the human then reads draft and audit together and decides publish, revise, or escalate.

Chapter 8 Product Management — Test Bank

Chapter: 8 — Product Management

Learning Objectives (Ch8, Our Chapter)

  • LO 8.1 Decompose any market offering into its three integrated elements (product, service, brand) and identify the weak leg that drags down the value of the other two.
  • LO 8.2 Describe the nine attributes on which a product can differentiate, distinguish performance quality from conformance quality, and explain the four faces of mass customization.
  • LO 8.3 Apply the four portfolio dimensions (width, length, depth, consistency) to diagnose a firm's product mix and evaluate a proposed expansion against the two-question cannibalization filter.
  • LO 8.4 Explain the three levels of packaging as a designed tool, the four-test battery a new package must clear, and why packaging is a brand decision not a container decision.
  • LO 8.5 Distinguish line extensions from brand extensions, apply the brand-fit test to evaluate dilution risk, and explain the up-market, down-market, and two-way stretch strategies with their risk profiles.
  • LO 8.6 Identify the four PLC stages, state the strategic priority at each stage, and recognize the scalloped, cycle-recycle, and growth-slump-maturity PLC variants.
  • LO 8.7 Describe the stage-gate NPD funnel and explain why the further upstream a concept is killed, the lower the cost of failure.
  • LO 8.8 Explain Rogers' five adopter categories, Moore's chasm, and Gourville's nine-times adoption-resistance rule, and map Rogers' five innovation attributes to specific product-design moves that speed diffusion.

Section I — Multiple-Choice Items (22 items)

Item 1

A product is best understood as a market offering built from three integrated elements. According to our chapter, those three elements are _______.

A) core functionality, design aesthetics, and price tier B) the good the buyer touches, the service work around it, and the brand meaning attached before purchase C) the supply chain, the product specs, and the warranty D) the physical object, the marketing materials, and the after-sale support plan E) raw materials, manufacturing quality, and packaging

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 8.1 — The chapter defines a product as three integrated elements: the good (product), the work around it (service), and the meaning the buyer carries before opening the box (brand). A weak leg on any of the three drags the value of the other two.

Tags: three-element offering; product definition; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 2

In January 2009, PepsiCo replaced the Tropicana Pure Premium orange-with-a-straw carton with a juice-glass close-up in a cleaner typographic system. Six weeks later, sales had dropped roughly 20 percent. The most precise explanation for that drop is _______.

A) the new package failed the engineering test because it was not shelf-stable B) the price increased simultaneously, and buyers chose a competing brand instead C) buyers who could not find the brand on the shelf walked away, because the package change removed the visual cue they scanned for D) the new design was too premium and buyers in the value segment felt excluded E) PepsiCo reduced the number of SKUs and lost distribution in key accounts

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 8.4 — Tropicana is the chapter's packaging case. The redesign removed the orange-and-straw recognition cue, so loyal buyers scanning the shelf in two seconds could not locate the brand. The chapter names this a shelf-recognition failure, the one consumer test the team skipped.

Tags: Tropicana; packaging; shelf-recognition; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 3

A premium-audio startup's new wireless headphones produce exceptionally clear sound, but buyers have reported that the sound quality varies noticeably from unit to unit off the same production line. The chapter distinguishes these as separate quality problems. The startup's ceiling-level sound is a _____ issue; the unit-to-unit variance is a _____ issue.

A) performance quality; conformance quality B) conformance quality; performance quality C) durability; reliability D) reliability; durability E) form; style

Answer: A

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 8.2 — Performance quality is the level at which a product's primary characteristics operate on its best day (the ceiling). Conformance quality is whether every produced unit reaches that same ceiling. A product can have a high ceiling and poor conformance — which is the Mercedes 2003 case in the chapter.

Tags: performance quality; conformance quality; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 4

Mercedes lost the global luxury-sedan volume lead to BMW for the first time in 2003. According to the chapter, the primary cause was _______.

A) BMW's price cuts across its full range B) BMW launching more models than Mercedes that year C) conformance drift: two cars off the same line the same week arrived at dealers with different door fit, rattles, and steering feel D) Mercedes reducing its warranty coverage below BMW's E) BMW's heavy advertising campaign that moved buyer preference scores

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 8.2 — The chapter uses the Mercedes-BMW case to illustrate what conformance drift costs a brand. The recovery was a measurable production-line program, not a brand campaign, and it produced a reported 72 percent reduction in flaws per vehicle and a 25 percent drop in warranty costs.

Tags: Mercedes; conformance quality; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 5

Stanley grew its annual brand revenue from roughly $73 million in 2018 to over $750 million by 2023 without changing its core product, the Quencher tumbler, and without entering new product categories. The chapter classifies this as a _______ move.

A) width expansion, because Stanley entered the accessories category B) length expansion, because the number of shapes in the line multiplied C) depth expansion, because the same tumbler stretched into roughly thirty colorways and four capacities D) consistency reduction, because the colorways shifted the brand away from its outdoor roots E) two-way stretch, because the line added both a premium and a budget tier

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 8.3 — The chapter uses Stanley as the worked case for the four portfolio dials. Width, length, and consistency held; only depth moved (colorways and capacities). The chapter states that the four-dimension vocabulary is what lets a manager name that move precisely as a depth play, not a vague case of getting bigger.

Tags: Stanley; portfolio depth; four portfolio dials; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 6

A firm is auditing its product line and finds that one of its twelve SKUs contributes very little unit contribution and shows no segment lock-in, no halo effect on adjacent items, and no special reason to retain it. The chapter recommends that a SKU failing those three tests should _______.

A) receive a marketing spending increase to revive interest B) be repriced at a lower tier to capture the price-sensitive segment C) be removed, because it forces every remaining SKU to subsidize a slot that is not paying for itself D) be kept as a loss leader to drive traffic to more profitable items E) be converted to a licensed product to reduce production cost

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 8.3 — The chapter explains the line-review process: a SKU not paying for its warehouse slot forces every other one to subsidize it. The first-pass filter drops variants with no segment lock-in, no halo, and no recent launch reason, and the chapter notes this can be done with a spreadsheet and a chat assistant.

Tags: line review; portfolio management; cannibalization; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 7

A consumer goods firm wants to add a cheaper tier of its laundry detergent under the same brand name. The chapter's Tide Basic example illustrates the primary risk of this move, which is _______.

A) production costs rise because separate manufacturing lines are needed B) franchise damage to the premium buyer: the cheaper version may shift premium buyers to the lower-margin item without adding the new lower-tier buyers that justified the move C) regulatory risk, because lower-priced variants require separate FDA approval D) retailer resistance, because mass-market retailers will refuse to carry premium and value tiers simultaneously E) advertising inefficiency, because the same brand name splits the media budget across two price tiers

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 8.5 — The down-market stretch risk is franchise damage. Tide Basic was killed in test market in 2009 because the damage to mainstream Tide buyers outran the new lower-tier revenue. The chapter presents this as the canonical down-stretch failure.

Tags: down-market stretch; brand dilution; Tide Basic; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 8

Bic launched a five-dollar perfume in 1989 after leading the world in disposable pens, lighters, and razors. The line was discontinued within about two years despite strong distribution and a $20 million advertising campaign. The chapter identifies the failure as _______.

A) poor launch timing: the 1989 recession suppressed the premium-fragrance category B) a packaging failure: flat plastic containers could not survive perfume retail C) a brand-fit failure: the parent's disposable-cheap-functional meaning directly conflicted with what perfume buyers were purchasing D) a pricing failure: five dollars was below the category's minimum credibility threshold E) a distribution failure: Bic's convenience-store channel did not carry prestige-fragrance buyers

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 8.5 — The Bic perfume case is the chapter's worked brand-extension failure. The parent carried disposable, cheap, and impulsive meanings into a self-presentation purchase where buyers wanted lasting, premium, and considered. The campaign accelerated the failure by pushing the conflict into more buyer awareness. The fix is to run a brand-fit test before any prototype budget commits.

Tags: Bic perfume; brand-fit test; brand extension; brand dilution; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 9

A hydration startup launched its energy drink six months ago. Sales are growing fast, competitors are beginning to prepare knock-offs, and the brand has not yet locked up shelf space in major grocery chains. According to the PLC diagnostic, the startup is in the _____ stage, and the primary strategic job is _____.

A) introduction; building awareness among innovators B) growth; locking up distribution and defending the lead before rivals arrive C) maturity; defending share and extending the curve through repositioning D) decline; harvesting residual revenue from loyal early adopters E) introduction; managing price to break even against launch costs

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 8.6 — In the growth stage, sales curve upward and rivals are preparing. The chapter explicitly states the three jobs at that stage: lock up distribution, defend the lead by reinvesting in brand, and convert the later adopters. Stanley locking in Target and Starbucks before the competitor cups arrived is the chapter's worked case.

Tags: product life cycle; growth stage; PLC; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 10

A consumer appliance brand's air fryer was covered extensively in food media two years ago, drove very strong early sales, then settled at a notably lower but stable replacement-and-gifting plateau. A brand manager who reads this pattern as terminal decline and pulls advertising investment is making which PLC diagnostic error?

A) Treating a growth-stage product as if it were in maturity B) Confusing a cycle-recycle variant with a standard bell curve C) Misreading a growth-slump-maturity curve as terminal decline by treating the slump as the end D) Treating a fad as a scalloped product E) Applying a harvest strategy to a product still in introduction

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 8.6 — The growth-slump-maturity PLC variant shows a strong early-adopter peak that drops when the enthusiast pool exhausts, then stabilizes at a durable plateau. The chapter explicitly warns that a manager who reads the slump as terminal decline pulls investment too early.

Tags: PLC variants; growth-slump-maturity; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 11

DuPont's nylon ran through four successive growth phases across four decades: wartime parachutes, then hosiery, then carpeting, then tire reinforcement. Each new phase was triggered by a new use. This PLC pattern is called _______.

A) cycle-recycle, because each wave was preceded by a deliberate relaunch B) growth-slump-maturity, because adoption peaked before the mainstream entered each use C) scalloped, because multiple successive growth waves stacked on one another as new uses were found D) fad, because demand ran on social novelty in each segment E) standard bell curve, because each use moved through its own separate bell independently

Answer: C

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 8.6 — The scalloped PLC variant goes through more than one growth phase, with each wave triggered by a new use. Nylon is the chapter's canonical case, and the lesson is that a mature product is not automatically a declining one.

Tags: scalloped PLC; PLC variants; nylon; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 12

A product development manager is reviewing six new-product concepts early in the funnel, before any prototypes are built. She applies a kill criterion: any concept scoring below 3 out of 5 on desirability, feasibility, or viability does not advance. According to the stage-gate logic in the chapter, why is killing a concept at this stage preferable to killing it at the commercial-launch stage?

A) Regulatory risk is lower before prototypes are built B) The cost of failure is lowest when the bad idea is caught earliest; at concept stage the cost is weeks of analyst time, while at launch it is millions in tooling, inventory, and channel commitments C) Channel partners have not yet been approached, so there is no relationship damage D) Patent protection cannot be secured on a concept, so it is always better to kill early E) Late-stage kills violate the stage-gate contract with R&D partners

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 8.7 — The stage-gate logic is that the further upstream a bad idea is caught, the lower its cost. The chapter uses Tata Steel's 100-to-1 funnel and explicitly states: "killed at concept it costs weeks of analyst time; killed at commercial launch it costs millions in tooling, inventory, and channel commitments."

Tags: stage-gate; NPD; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 13

A new smart-home device sold 80,000 units in its first four months, almost entirely to tech-forward early adopters who were comfortable with rough edges and incomplete integrations. In the fifth month sales plateau. The product team concludes this means the device has crossed into the early-majority segment. The chapter would caution that the plateau more likely signals _______.

A) the beginning of the maturity stage B) that the product has hit the chasm: visionaries adopted but pragmatists need references, completeness, and reassurance the product has not yet delivered C) a pricing problem that a promotion can resolve D) feature fatigue caused by the device having too many settings E) the product is in decline because the early adopters have already moved to a competitor

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 8.8 — Moore's chasm is the discontinuity between early adopters (visionaries who tolerate rough edges) and the early majority (pragmatists who require references, completeness, and reassurance). The plateau after an early-adopter burst is the chapter's definition of a chasm stall. A pricing promotion does not solve a product-completion problem.

Tags: Moore's chasm; diffusion; pragmatists; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 14

Casper launched in 2014 with one mattress in a shippable box. Its first buyers were visionaries comfortable buying a mattress unseen. Then it hit the chasm. The chapter identifies three moves Casper made to cross it. Which of the following is NOT one of those three moves?

A) A 100-night return guarantee that transferred the touch-test risk back to Casper B) Line extensions into pillows and frames that made Casper a bedroom-system seller C) A heavy national TV advertising campaign targeting the early majority D) Selective showrooms through Target giving pragmatists a physical experience E) None — all three listed above were among Casper's chasm-crossing moves

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 8.8 — The chapter explicitly names Casper's three moves: the 100-night return, the bedroom-system expansion, and selective showrooms through Target. No heavy TV campaign is named. The chapter's point is that crossing the chasm requires product evolution, not more media spend.

Tags: Casper; Moore's chasm; chasm crossing; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 15

Rogers' five innovation attributes include relative advantage, compatibility, complexity, trialability, and observability. A product manager wants to speed adoption for a new B2B software tool. She learns that the tool's primary barrier is that it requires users to completely rethink how they organize their project files. According to Rogers' framework, the attribute she should focus on improving is _______.

A) Relative advantage, by demonstrating that output quality is measurably higher B) Observability, by publishing case studies of successful users C) Complexity, by redesigning the tool to match how users already organize their files D) Trialability, by offering a 30-day free trial E) Conformance quality, by reducing variance in the tool's output

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 8.8 — Complexity is the attribute to lower, not raise. When the primary adoption barrier is relearning, the fix is to redesign the product to match existing habits, which raises compatibility and lowers complexity. The chapter maps this explicitly onto the Gourville fix: behaving like the old product means the buyer gives up less.

Tags: Rogers' five attributes; complexity; adoption speed; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 16

Casper's 100-night return guarantee is a direct application of which of Rogers' five innovation attributes?

A) Relative advantage, because buyers learn the mattress is better through the trial period B) Observability, because neighbors and family members can see the buyer sleeping on it C) Compatibility, because a foam mattress is familiar to anyone who has slept on a bed D) Trialability, because the guarantee lets pragmatists test the product with their full commitment reversed before they are locked in E) Complexity, because the return process is simple enough to discourage regret purchases

Answer: D

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 8.8 — The chapter explicitly states that Casper's 100-night guarantee is pure trialability. The chapter's point is that the chapter was already using the Rogers attributes before naming them, and naming the lens turns diffusion theory into a design checklist.

Tags: Rogers' five attributes; trialability; Casper; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 17

John Gourville's nine-times rule holds that a new product faces roughly a nine-times adoption barrier because _______.

A) nine out of ten new products fail within their first year B) buyers require nine independent product reviews before they will switch from an established brand C) buyers overvalue what they already own by about three times, and innovators overvalue what they have built by about three times, so the two biases compound to roughly nine times D) a new product must reach nine percent of the target market before word-of-mouth takes over E) the stage-gate funnel filters out all but about one in nine concepts before launch

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 8.8 — Gourville's 9x rule (from his 2006 HBR article "Eager Sellers and Stony Buyers") compounds loss aversion and the endowment effect on the buyer side with innovator overvaluation on the seller side, producing a roughly nine-times mismatch. The chapter names two fixes: cut the loss by designing the new product to behave like the old one, or target non-owners who have nothing to give up.

Tags: Gourville 9x rule; loss aversion; endowment effect; adoption resistance; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 18

Apple's AirPods hearing-aid feature was announced in September 2024 and shipped by firmware to roughly 66 million AirPods Pro 2 units already in use, entering a category where dedicated devices ran $4,000 to $6,000. The chapter identifies this as an example of Gourville's fix because _______.

A) the feature was priced below competitors, removing the financial barrier to switching B) the feature demanded zero behavior change from AirPods wearers, making adoption almost pure gain with almost no felt loss C) Apple advertised the feature to non-adopters who had never tried hearing aids D) the feature was first tested on a small beta group before wider rollout, matching trialability criteria E) the hearing-aid market was in decline, so there was no established product the buyer needed to give up

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 8.8 — The chapter presents the AirPods hearing-aid case as the Gourville fix in hardware: the new feature demanded zero behavior change from existing users, so adoption was almost pure gain with almost no felt loss. This is the "cut the loss" fix, not "add more gain."

Tags: AirPods; Gourville fix; adoption; loss aversion; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 19

A new CPG brand is designing its primary package. The chapter specifies a four-test battery a new package must clear before shipping. Which of the following tests specifically checks whether buyers can locate the brand on a crowded shelf within the two seconds they actually spend scanning?

A) The engineering test B) The visual test C) The dealer test D) The shelf-recognition consumer test E) The durability test

Answer: D

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 8.4 — The four-test battery is engineering (protects the product through the supply chain), visual (legibility at arm's length), dealer (easy to stock and shelve), and consumer. The shelf-recognition test is a specific form of the consumer test that measures whether buyers can locate the brand at the shelf in real shopping conditions. This is precisely the test Tropicana skipped.

Tags: packaging; four-test battery; shelf-recognition; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 20

A direct-to-consumer mattress brand ships its product in a large cardboard box. The chapter would classify the cardboard shipping box as the _____ level of packaging, and explains that for a DTC brand this level deserves the same design investment as the primary package because _____.

A) tertiary; it protects the mattress during transport B) secondary; online, the box that arrives at the door is the shelf, and the unboxing is a brand moment C) primary; it is the first thing the buyer touches D) secondary; retailer requirements mandate a certain level of design at this layer E) tertiary; pallet packaging drives the most waste reduction at scale

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 8.4 — The chapter explains the three nested packaging levels: primary (the buyer holds and uses), secondary (the buyer carries home and usually discards, including the DTC shipping box), and tertiary (the buyer never sees). It explicitly states that for a DTC brand, the secondary box at the door is the shelf, so it deserves the same design attention as the primary package.

Item 21

The chapter describes the four dimensions of a product portfolio. The _____ dimension refers to how closely related the various product lines are in end use, production, or distribution; the _____ dimension refers to how many variants exist for each individual item.

A) width; length B) consistency; depth C) depth; length D) length; consistency E) consistency; width

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 8.3 — The chapter defines the four dimensions: width (number of lines), length (total items across lines), depth (variants per item), and consistency (how related the lines are). Consistency is about relatedness; depth is about variants per item.

Tags: portfolio dimensions; width length depth consistency; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 22

A firm is considering whether to launch a new product variant. The chapter's two-question cannibalization filter asks: (1) Does the new variant capture a buyer segment the line was losing to a competitor or to non-consumption? (2) Does the variant cannibalize existing variants without growing the total? If the answer to question 1 is "no" and the answer to question 2 is "yes," the chapter recommends _______.

A) launch the variant anyway, because the depth increase improves shelf presence B) reject the variant, because it splits the franchise into two thinner slices while capturing no competitor C) launch only if the new variant carries a higher gross margin than the existing items D) launch in a test market first to observe cannibalization in practice E) reduce the price of the existing variant to make the new one less necessary

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 8.3 — The two-question filter is: does it capture a buyer segment the line was losing? If no, then the variety pays for nothing. Does it cannibalize without growing the total? If yes, the firm pays warehouse, production, and shelf costs to move a buyer from a higher-margin to a lower-margin item under its own roof. The chapter calls the "no/yes" combination the version to refuse.

Tags: cannibalization filter; portfolio management; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Section II — Scenario / Short-Answer Items (4 items)

SA-1

Liquid Death sells sparkling and still water in a matte-black aluminum tallboy with a skull-and-bones design at about a one-dollar premium over the generic bottled water on the same shelf. Tap water covers the functional hydration job for a fraction of the price. Yet the brand reached a $1.4 billion valuation inside seven years.

Using the chapter's three-element offering framework, decompose the Liquid Death offer. Identify which element carries the most of the buyer's premium, which element is weakest, and explain why the chapter says the augmented-product view is the only lens that explains the valuation.

Sample answer: Product element: still or sparkling water in a 16.9-ounce aluminum can (functional parity with any bottled water, no meaningful differentiation on the core job). Service element: cold-cooler placement in convenience and specialty retail — present but not the driver. Brand element: the skull-and-bones design, the Murder Your Thirst tagline, the social-signaling value of the can held in public, and the recyclability claim plastic bottles cannot match. The brand element carries nearly the entire premium, because the product element is a commodity and the service element is table stakes. The augmented-product view (product plus service plus brand as one integrated offer) is the only lens that explains the valuation because any product-only analysis (water in a can) would price the company at a commodity multiple, while the brand layer is what makes the can worth $1.40 per unit instead of $0.30.

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 8.1, LO 8.5

Tags: Liquid Death; three-element offering; brand element; augmented product; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Reflective thinking

SA-2

A consumer-goods startup is launching a new reusable travel mug. The founder has run three consumer tests and is confident in the design. She plans to skip the dealer test because she sells direct-to-consumer and does not use retail stores. Her CMO argues she should run all four tests in the chapter's packaging battery.

Explain what the dealer test covers and why it remains relevant for a DTC brand even without traditional retail distribution. Then identify which test in the battery the chapter's Tropicana case proves most consequential, and explain why.

Sample answer: The dealer test asks whether retailers find the package easy to stock, stack, and shelve. For a DTC brand, this translates to whether the secondary packaging (the box that ships to the buyer's door) stacks and handles well through a fulfillment warehouse and survives last-mile delivery intact. A founder shipping via 3PL still needs to ensure the package dimensions fit standard pick-and-pack bins, stacks without collapse, and arrives undamaged. The test is about channel-handling efficiency, and a 3PL's warehouse is just as much a "dealer" environment as a retailer's back room. The most consequential test the Tropicana case proves is the shelf-recognition consumer test: the redesign cleared engineering, visual, and dealer tests and still cost $35 million in lost revenue, because it removed the orange-and-straw visual cue buyers scanned for in two seconds. The consumer test that does not measure shelf-recognition is testing the wrong thing, which is the chapter's exact conclusion.

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 8.4

Tags: packaging four-test battery; dealer test; shelf-recognition; Tropicana; AACSB: Application of knowledge

SA-3

A mid-market athleisure brand is considering two moves simultaneously: (a) launch a budget sub-brand at 40 percent below current prices under the parent name; (b) launch a premium yoga gear line priced 60 percent above current prices under the parent name.

Using the chapter's stretch framework, identify what each move is called, state the primary risk of each, and explain why the chapter's examples (Toyota/Lexus for one; Tide Basic for the other) are illustrative of those risks rather than exceptions.

Sample answer: Move (a) is a down-market stretch. The primary risk is franchise damage: the cheaper version may shift existing premium buyers to the lower-margin item without adding the new lower-tier buyers the move promised, or it may dilute the brand's quality image in the minds of existing customers. Tide Basic failed its 2009 test market because damage to mainstream Tide buyers outran the revenue from new lower-tier buyers. For the athleisure brand, launching a budget sub-brand under the same parent name risks signaling to current customers that the brand no longer stands for the quality or identity premium they paid for. Move (b) is an up-market stretch. The primary risk is lack of buyer permission: buyers may not grant the parent brand the right to operate at a higher price tier. Toyota ran this risk and responded by launching Lexus as a separate nameplate on the same production base, because research showed consumers would not accept Toyota competing with BMW and Mercedes. The athleisure brand faces the same credibility gap if buyers associate the parent name with affordable performance rather than premium yoga credentials. The solution is either a separate brand (as Toyota did) or a sub-brand boundary clear enough that the two tiers never blur. Running both moves simultaneously under the same name compounds both risks at once.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 8.5

Tags: down-market stretch; up-market stretch; brand dilution; Tide Basic; Toyota Lexus; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

SA-4

A consumer-electronics startup built a screenless AI assistant that clips to clothing and displays information on the user's palm via a laser projector. The device launched at $699 plus a $24 monthly subscription in April 2024. Returns passed gross sales by mid-2024, and HP acquired the company's assets for $116 million against $240 million raised.

Using the chapter's five failure modes (line-extension dilution, brand-stretch failure, PLC denial, poor concept testing, feature creep) and its chasm framework, identify the two failure modes most clearly present in this case. For each, cite specific evidence from the product's history and name the chapter-recommended fix.

Sample answer: The two most clearly present failure modes are poor concept testing and chasm failure (which maps to Moore's chasm from Section 8.6, closely related to the five-mode section). Poor concept testing: returns exceeding gross sales within months of launch indicates that the product did not perform as the concept promised under normal conditions. The chapter notes that the Humane Ai Pin's laser projector misfired in normal lighting, and its voice interface was unreliably slow, which means the product failed the usability gate (the BREXI rubric's Engaging and Inspiring-action criteria) in mainstream conditions. A concept test conducted with the real pragmatist target buyer, not the early-adopter enthusiast community, would have surfaced these failure points before the manufacturing commitment. The fix is to run concept validation with the pragmatist segment, score usability with a kill criterion, and do not ship if the score falls below 3 out of 5 on any dimension. Chasm failure: the product attracted about 100,000 pre-orders from tech-forward visionaries comfortable with rough edges, then effectively stalled. Visionaries tolerate an incomplete product; pragmatists need references, completeness, and a product that already works reliably in their conditions. The Ai Pin had no installed base of satisfied pragmatists to cite, no ecosystem of partners, and no "whole product" (accessories, integrations, workflow guides) that would make a pragmatist's daily work easier. The fix the chapter names is not more media spend but product-completion: building the features, partnerships, and guarantees that the pragmatist segment requires before buying — which for the Ai Pin would have meant a longer beta period, a reliability guarantee, and a narrower initial use case rather than a general-purpose device.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 8.7, LO 8.8

Tags: Humane Ai Pin; Moore's chasm; concept testing; stage-gate; failure modes; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Reflective thinking

Section III — AI-Integration Items (4 items)

These items are original to this course and do not appear in any Kotler bank. They test students' ability to evaluate AI-generated product analysis and apply the chapter's frameworks to AI-era product management decisions.

AI-1 — Identify the Framework in AI Output

A student asks an AI assistant: "Should we extend the Patagonia brand into outdoor furniture?" The AI responds:

"Patagonia is known for durability and environmental values. Outdoor furniture shares those values. The brand has strong customer loyalty, and extending into furniture would let it enter a $12 billion market. I would recommend a premium line priced at or above competitors to match the brand's positioning."

Which critical input does the AI's output omit, and what chapter framework would produce a more rigorous verdict?

A) The AI omits a price-elasticity estimate; the chapter's Van Westendorp PSM would fill the gap. B) The AI omits a brand-fit test: it confirms overlap on some values but never asks whether the parent's meaning hurts or helps in the new category, which is the question the chapter's brand-extension framework is designed to answer. C) The AI omits a stage-gate viability score; the chapter's four-risks audit would provide the missing gate. D) The AI omits Rogers' five attributes; a high-complexity product like furniture would spread too slowly. E) The AI ignores the portfolio dimensions; the recommendation should have addressed the depth dial first.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 8.5 — The chapter's brand-fit test asks: name three things the parent means, name three things the category requires, and flag every conflict. The AI output confirms one area of overlap (durability/values) but does not ask whether Patagonia's meaning in outdoor performance apparel maps cleanly onto a furniture buyer's frame of reference, or whether the extension could dilute the apparel brand (as the Bic perfume case shows). A specific prompt asking for the full brand-fit test would produce a falsifiable verdict.

Tags: brand-fit test; brand extension; AI output critique; LLM-as-judge; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

AI-2 — AI Package Design Scenario

A brand manager prompts an AI to help redesign her oat-milk creamer's primary package. Prompt A: "Design a cool package for our oat-milk creamer." Prompt B: "Generate six package-design directions for our oat-milk creamer that read as warm, clean, and premium on a crowded refrigerated shelf, then score each direction for shelf-recognition against the category leader."

According to the chapter, which prompt is more likely to produce a useful output and why? What specific packaging test from the chapter does Prompt B incorporate that Prompt A ignores?

Sample answer and rubric:

Prompt B is more likely to produce a useful output because it names the visual goals (warm, clean, premium), the competitive context (against the category leader), and the evaluation criterion (shelf-recognition). The chapter states that a vague prompt produces generic output, while a sharp prompt earns a falsifiable verdict. "Design a cool can" produces generic chrome; specifying brand meanings and scoring against shelf-recognition produces directions the team can actually test. The specific test Prompt B incorporates is the shelf-recognition consumer test, the test Tropicana skipped in 2009. Tropicana's redesign was beautiful in isolation and still dropped sales 20 percent in six weeks, because it removed the visual cue buyers scanned for in two seconds. A design brief that explicitly instructs the model to score each direction for shelf-recognition is building the most important of the four packaging tests into the AI prompt itself. The model proposes directions; the real shelf-recognition test with actual buyers still decides — but the prompt dramatically improves the quality of the options being tested.

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 8.4 — The item connects the chapter's packaging-as-AI material (Coca-Cola Y3000 as the worked case) with the shelf-recognition test and the vague-versus-specific prompt contrast from Widget 8.3.

Tags: AI package design; packaging; shelf-recognition; Coca-Cola Y3000; vague vs sharp prompt; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Written communication

AI-3 — PLC Stage Diagnostic with AI

A student uses an AI assistant to diagnose the PLC stage of a reusable water-bottle brand: Hydro Flask. The AI responds:

"Hydro Flask is in the growth stage. It is a leading brand with strong sales growth in the insulated-drinkware category."

The student accepts this verdict and recommends a growth-stage playbook: lock up distribution, reinvest in brand-building, and convert later adopters.

Using the chapter's PLC diagnostic logic, identify the two questions the student should have included in the AI prompt to get a more defensible stage diagnosis. Then explain which stage Hydro Flask most likely occupies in 2026 and what that stage's playbook actually calls for.

Sample answer and rubric:

The two questions missing from the prompt are: (1) Has the product reached most of its potential buyers? (saturation test) and (2) Are at least three direct competitors selling a similar product? (competitive-density test). The AI in this case accepted "strong sales growth" as a proxy for the growth stage, but that is insufficient — maturity can still show modest revenue growth even when unit growth is flat. The chapter's four-question diagnostic uses sales-growth rate, saturation, competitive density, and sales direction together, not any single signal alone. In 2026, Hydro Flask is almost certainly in maturity: the insulated-drinkware category is thoroughly explored, at least three direct competitors (Stanley, Yeti, Owala, plus Target private-label) compete directly, and the brand's potential-buyer universe has been largely reached. The maturity playbook calls for defending share (a one-point loss hurts more in a big mature category than a five-point loss in a small one), harvesting profit to fund the next new product, and extending the curve through repositioning or new uses — not the growth-stage moves of locking up distribution and converting adopters, which are largely behind the category.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 8.6 — The item tests whether students can use the chapter's four-question PLC diagnostic to critique an AI output, and whether they can identify the correct stage for a real brand with named evidence.

Tags: PLC diagnostic; growth vs maturity; AI output critique; Hydro Flask; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Reflective thinking

AI-4 — Gourville's 9x Rule Applied to AI Product Adoption

An enterprise software company is launching an AI-powered contract-review tool that requires users to learn a new file-tagging workflow and adopt a new review UI. The product team has run beta tests with innovator-segment legal tech enthusiasts and received very positive feedback. They are now preparing a full rollout to mainstream corporate legal teams.

Using Gourville's 9x rule and Rogers' five innovation attributes from the chapter, (a) explain why strong innovator feedback is an unreliable predictor of mainstream adoption success, and (b) identify two specific product design changes the team could make to reduce the adoption barrier for the pragmatist segment.

Sample answer and rubric:

(a) Innovators are the 2.5 percent of buyers who adopt on novelty and intrinsic interest in the technology, and they actively discount discomfort from incomplete products or new workflows. Gourville's rule predicts that the mainstream pragmatist buyer weights what they give up at three times the objective cost (loss aversion plus endowment effect), and the legal-team pragmatist is giving up a known review workflow they have used for years. Innovator beta feedback measures the innovators' tolerance, not the pragmatist's felt loss from switching, so it systematically over-predicts mainstream adoption success. Rogers' adopter-category logic reinforces this: the chasm is a segment boundary, not a preference gradient, and the visionary who ran the beta is a different psychological type than the pragmatist who must adopt during rollout. (b) Two design changes to reduce the adoption barrier: First, reduce complexity by making the new file-tagging workflow mirror the existing one as closely as possible (the Gourville fix: behave like the old product so the buyer gives up only the part they need to). This raises compatibility on the Rogers dial. Second, lower the switching cost by offering a parallel-run mode where the AI tool runs alongside the existing workflow for 30 days with no commitment, generating a side-by-side comparison before the buyer has given anything up. This raises trialability on the Rogers dial and converts the 30-day window into a de facto proof-of-value before any felt loss registers.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 8.8 — The item requires students to connect Gourville's 9x rule, Rogers' chasm and adopter categories, and Rogers' five innovation attributes to a live AI-product rollout scenario, and to generate specific design moves rather than general observations.

Tags: Gourville 9x; Rogers' five attributes; innovators vs pragmatists; Moore's chasm; AI product adoption; enterprise software; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Reflective thinking

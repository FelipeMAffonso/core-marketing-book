Chapter 1 AI for Marketers — Test Bank

Chapter: 1 — AI Foundations (AI for Marketers) Source note: Original authoring — no Kotler parent; AI-era chapter. Every item is written directly from our Ch1 chapter, which has no counterpart in Kotler MM16. No seed items were adapted or repurposed from any external test bank. Export format: Mirrors the course test-bank schema (stem, A...E options, Answer, Diff, Objective, Tags) for QTI / Canvas Common Cartridge conversion.

Learning Objectives (Ch1, Our Chapter)

  • LO 1.1 Name and sequence the five primitives of a working AI system: the file, the wire, the model, the agent, and the stack, and explain what each one does.
  • LO 1.2 Explain how file format (CSV versus PDF versus XLSX) determines what shape the AI receives and why the wrong format produces a plausible but unreliable answer.
  • LO 1.3 Define context window and token, explain what silent truncation is, and describe the one check that prevents it.
  • LO 1.4 Describe how a large language model generates its output (next-word prediction from frozen weights plus live web search) and explain the hallucination failure mode.
  • LO 1.5 Apply the five prompt moves (role, example, constraints, source material, ask-first) to sharpen a marketing request, and distinguish context engineering from prompt engineering.
  • LO 1.6 Describe retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) and explain why grounding a model in your own trusted sources reduces hallucination risk.
  • LO 1.7 Explain what an agent is, how the loop works, and why the human-review step must stay on for work that touches real customers or money.
  • LO 1.8 Describe the four rungs of the AI literacy ladder (ask, save, connect, schedule) and match a marketing task to the right rung.
  • LO 1.9 Identify AI overreliance as the cognitive trap underneath all four chapter failure modes, and state the data-handling rule about personally identifiable information (PII) and confidential inputs.

Item 1

According to our chapter, the most likely cause is _______.

Diff: 1

Objective: LO 1.3 — The opening story turns on silent truncation: the spreadsheet was sorted by date, the shipping complaints sat near the top, the model read to about row 3,800, and the 67 percent figure came from that slice.

Section II — Scenario / Short-Answer Items (4 items)

SA-1

A junior analyst at a consumer-packaged-goods brand asks an AI model to produce a market sizing estimate for a new oat-milk protein drink. The model returns a confident-sounding paragraph citing a 2024 grand-view-research study, an 8.4 percent CAGR, and a 2029 market size of $12.3 billion. The analyst drops the numbers into the executive deck without checking them. Two days later the VP of Strategy flags that the cited research firm has no record of that study.

Using the vocabulary and frameworks from our chapter: (a) name the failure mode this illustrates, (b) explain in one sentence why the calm, confident tone of the model's output is not evidence that the numbers are real, and (c) state the one-step check the analyst should have run before putting any specific number on the slide.

Sample answer: (a) This is a hallucination: the model generated fluent, plausible-looking text that is factually wrong. Hallucinations are most common with specific numbers, dates, academic citations, and named entities, all of which appear in this paragraph. (b) The model reaches for the most natural-sounding next word, not the most truthful one, so it has no built-in check asking whether a claim is true; a fluent wrong answer is the model working normally, not breaking. (c) The analyst should have clicked the link or DOI attached to the statistic, because a real citation navigates to the article and a hallucinated one either fails to resolve or lands on an unrelated paper. If no source exists or cannot be verified, the number does not go on the slide.

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 1.4, LO 1.9

Tags: hallucination; verification; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Reflective thinking

SA-2

A brand team wants to build an AI workflow that generates a weekly competitor-monitoring brief. They are debating between two options: Option A, where the analyst opens ChatGPT every Monday morning, pastes the full standing instructions, and runs the analysis manually; and Option B, where the team builds a Custom GPT or Claude Project once, uploads the brand guide as a reference file, and the analyst runs it each week with one command.

Using the four-rung AI literacy ladder from our chapter, (a) identify which rung each option represents, (b) explain what the team would have to change to reach rung 4, and (c) identify one non-technical benefit of Option B over Option A.

Sample answer: (a) Option A is rung 1 (Ask): one question per session, nothing saved, the analyst retypes everything each time. Option B is rung 2 (Save): the task is packaged in a saved specialist with standing instructions and a knowledge file, and one command runs it the same way every time. (b) To reach rung 4 (Schedule), the team would set the saved workflow on a timer, such as a cron job or a GitHub Action, so the brief is generated and posted to a shared channel every Monday at nine without the analyst typing anything during the run. (c) One non-technical benefit of Option B is consistency: because the same standing instructions and reference file run every week, the brief follows the same format and logic regardless of who is in the office, eliminating variation that arises when different analysts type their own versions of the instructions.

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 1.8

Tags: AI ladder; Custom GPT; rung 2; rung 4; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

SA-3

A startup is considering using an AI agent to handle its first-tier customer support tickets. Inbound tickets would be read by the agent, which would draft replies, and then, if it judges the issue resolved, post the reply and close the ticket automatically. A co-founder argues that turning off the human-review step entirely will make the workflow faster and cost less per ticket.

Using the agent framework from our chapter, (a) identify what the human-review step does and why removing it on customer-facing work is specifically risky, (b) explain what the chapter means by "the agent never reaches the outside world by itself," and (c) describe one guardrail the startup could keep in place to preserve speed while maintaining meaningful oversight.

Sample answer: (a) The human-review step is the safety mechanism for agent work: before the agent sends an email or closes a ticket, the app surfaces the planned action so a person can approve or cancel it. Removing it on customer-facing work means the agent can send incorrect or tone-inappropriate replies, issue credits it should not, or close unresolved tickets without anyone catching the error before the customer sees the result. The chapter states this step should never be turned off on work that touches real customers or real money. (b) The chapter's framing is that an agent works the way an assistant runs an errand while the manager stays at the desk: the model decides what tool to use and requests it, but the app around the model actually runs the tool and hands back the result. The model never directly sends email or modifies a live system; the application layer executes the action. Removing human review does not change this architecture, but it removes the last check before execution. (c) One guardrail that preserves speed without removing oversight is a confidence threshold: have the agent post and close autonomously only when its internal confidence score for the resolution is above a set bar, and route any ticket below that bar to a human queue for review before the reply goes out. This keeps the high-confidence, routine tickets fast while flagging the edge cases.

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 1.7, LO 1.9

Tags: agent; human review; safety; AACSB: Ethical understanding; Application of knowledge

SA-4

A student in the class argues: "Using AI is just about knowing which chatbot to open. Once you pick the right one, the answers are basically reliable." Using three specific concepts from our chapter, explain why this view is incomplete and what a more accurate picture of AI-assisted marketing work looks like.

Sample answer: The student's framing misses at least three things the chapter covers. First, the file matters as much as the model: handing a model a PDF when the task requires a structured CSV means the column relationships are gone before the model reads a word, and no choice of chatbot fixes a format problem the user introduced at upload. Second, the context window creates a silent failure the user has to catch: a model reading only the first 47 percent of a long file answers in the same confident voice it would use for the whole file, and the chat never warns you, so the user has to ask how many tokens the file is and verify it fit. Third, the rung the user is on changes what the task can do: opening a chatbot is rung 1, but the same task built as a Custom GPT or a Skill on rung 2 runs consistently every week without retyping, and on rung 3 or 4 it can read live company data or run itself on a schedule. Picking the right chatbot is only the first decision; the file format, the context-window check, the prompt structure, and the rung together determine whether the answer is actually reliable and whether the workflow scales.

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 1.1, LO 1.2, LO 1.3, LO 1.8

Tags: AI literacy; chapter synthesis; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Written communication

Section III — AI-Integration Items (4 items)

AI-1 — Pick the Right Rung for a Task

A marketing coordinator at a regional coffee chain needs to handle the following three tasks each week: (1) Answer one-off questions from the social media team, such as "What are the current top Instagram audio trends?" (2) Generate a standardized 200-word brief on each of the chain's five competitor brands, following the same format every time. (3) Have a competitor-pricing summary waiting in the team Slack channel every Monday at 8 a.m. with no one typing a prompt during the run.

Match each task to the correct rung of the AI literacy ladder, and identify the ONE tool or mechanism from our chapter that most clearly defines each rung.

A) Task 1 = Rung 2 (Skill); Task 2 = Rung 3 (MCP); Task 3 = Rung 4 (Scheduled agent). B) Task 1 = Rung 1 (Chat plus web search); Task 2 = Rung 2 (Skill or Custom GPT); Task 3 = Rung 4 (Scheduled agent). C) Task 1 = Rung 1 (Chat plus web search); Task 2 = Rung 1 (Chat plus web search); Task 3 = Rung 3 (MCP connector). D) Task 1 = Rung 3 (MCP connector); Task 2 = Rung 2 (Skill); Task 3 = Rung 1 (Chat plus web search), because Slack is a chat tool. E) All three tasks belong on rung 1, because all three involve the same underlying model and the rung is determined by the model version, not the workflow.

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 1.8 — Task 1 is a one-off question, the definition of rung 1: chat plus web search with nothing to set up. Task 2 follows a fixed format every week for five brands, which is exactly the rung-2 use case for a Skill or Custom GPT/Project: package the instructions once and run them with one command. Task 3 must run unattended on a timer, the definition of rung 4: a scheduled agent with no human typing during the run.

AI-2 — Spot the Hallucination Risk

A student uses an AI model to research the adoption rate of generative AI among marketing teams for a class presentation. The model replies:

"According to a 2024 McKinsey Global Survey, 84 percent of marketing teams now use generative AI daily, up from 41 percent in 2022. The report notes that the biggest use case is campaign personalization (62 percent of respondents), followed by content drafting (54 percent). The survey covered 1,200 senior marketing professionals across twelve industries."

The student copies these numbers into her slide deck. Which of the following responses is most consistent with our chapter's guidance?

A) The numbers are fine to use because McKinsey is a reputable source and the model cited a specific survey title and year. B) The student should first verify that the cited McKinsey survey exists and that these specific statistics appear in it, by locating and reading the actual report, before putting any of the numbers on the slide. C) The student should reduce the temperature to 0 before asking the same question again, because low temperature suppresses hallucination. D) The student should ask the model to rephrase the statistics in more confident language so any fabricated elements will be easier to identify. E) The student should cross-reference the 84 percent figure with the Stanford HAI AI Index from the chapter, which cites a similar number, confirming these statistics are accurate.

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 1.4, LO 1.6, LO 1.9 — The chapter's rule is clear: every specific number, name, and citation in anything you ship gets a real source clicked and read first. A reputable-sounding brand name and specific-looking numbers are exactly the hallucination pattern the chapter describes. Temperature does not suppress hallucination (it controls output variation, not truthfulness). Cross-referencing one AI output with a chapter statistic does not constitute a source check.

Tags: hallucination; source verification; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Analytical thinking

AI-3 — Choose What NOT to Paste into a Public Model

A marketing manager at a pharmaceutical company is preparing a competitive-intelligence brief. She has access to the following materials: (A) a list of her top competitor's publicly available product pages, (B) an internal spreadsheet containing the names, email addresses, purchase histories, and health conditions of 4,200 patients enrolled in a company-managed wellness program, (C) a PDF of the competitor's most recent earnings call transcript, available on the investor-relations site, and (D) a draft of her own two-page competitive summary, with no proprietary data.

Using our chapter's data-handling guidance, which of these inputs should she NOT paste into a public AI model such as ChatGPT or Claude on a standard consumer plan?

A) Material A only, because publicly available competitor pages may trigger copyright concerns. B) Material B only, because it contains personally identifiable information and confidential patient health data that the company has not approved for use in a public tool. C) Materials B and C, because both involve external parties who did not consent to having their data processed by a third-party AI. D) Materials A, B, C, and D, because no business material should ever be pasted into a public AI tool. E) None of them; all four are acceptable because the model does not store inputs between sessions.

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 1.9 — The chapter states that pasting a customer list, an unsigned contract, or anything confidential into a public chatbot can expose it the way Samsung engineers leaked internal source code in 2023, and that private data should stay out of the box unless the company has approved that tool for it. Material B contains PII (names, emails) and protected health information for 4,200 identifiable patients, which is both a privacy violation and likely a regulatory one. Materials A, C, and D are public or self-authored without confidential content.

Tags: PII; data privacy; AI overreliance; AACSB: Ethical understanding; Application of knowledge

AI-4 — Recognize the AI Overreliance Trap

A content strategist at an e-commerce brand asks an AI model to estimate the brand's net promoter score (NPS) trend over the past six months based on the description of the brand's recent launches and social media presence. The model returns a response stating: "Based on your recent activity, your estimated NPS is in the 42 to 48 range, likely trending upward." The strategist includes this in a monthly board report under "Customer Sentiment."

Which of the following best describes the situation and what the strategist should have done instead?

A) The strategist acted correctly. NPS estimates from AI are as reliable as survey-based estimates for board-level reporting. B) The AI overreliance trap is visible here: the model produced a fluent, numerical-seeming output, and the strategist accepted it without verifying against real survey data. NPS cannot be estimated from a description of launches and social posts; it requires actual customer survey responses. The strategist should have flagged to the board that real NPS data was unavailable, rather than substituting a model estimate. C) The strategist should have asked the model to lower its temperature to 0 so the NPS estimate would be more precise and repeatable before including it in the report. D) The situation illustrates a context-window error, because the brand description was too short for the model to estimate NPS accurately. The fix is to paste a longer brand history into the prompt. E) The strategist acted correctly. Using a model's estimates alongside verified data is a standard practice for board-level reporting in 2026 and is explicitly endorsed in our chapter.

Answer: B

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 1.9, LO 1.4 — AI overreliance is the documented tendency to accept a model's output because it is fluent and confident, without running the independent check that would catch its errors. NPS is a survey metric; it cannot be estimated from a description of brand activity. The fluent, numerical-sounding output is exactly the hallucination pattern the chapter describes for specific numbers. The strategist should have disclosed the absence of real NPS data rather than substituting a model estimate for a board report.

Tags: AI overreliance; hallucination; business ethics; AACSB: Ethical understanding; Reflective thinking

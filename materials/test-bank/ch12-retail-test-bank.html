Chapter 12 Retail — Test Bank

Chapter: 12 — Retail

Learning Objectives (Ch12, Our Chapter)

  • LO 12.1 Define retailing and explain why the eight retail decisions form a dependency chain, each resolved at the moment of truth at the shelf.
  • LO 12.2 Classify the major retail formats along the assortment-width-by-price-level matrix and explain how the wheel of retailing turns each format from low-cost entrant toward decline.
  • LO 12.3 Apply the assortment logic — narrow-deep, broad-shallow, and the SKU-cull based on velocity and Direct Product Profitability — to a given retail situation.
  • LO 12.4 Describe the five named store layouts (grid, diagonal, racetrack/loop, spine, free-flow) and explain how each routes shopper traffic and supports a different buying mission.
  • LO 12.5 Apply Sharp's physical-availability law: explain why each added point of weighted distribution returns more market share than the last, and why the convex distribution-to-share curve makes the final distribution points the most valuable.
  • LO 12.6 Distinguish EDLP from HiLo pricing, explain the turns-and-earns trade-off, and connect retail media networks to the EDLP structural offset.
  • LO 12.7 Distinguish omnichannel from multichannel retailing and explain how the store became a fulfillment moat when the dominant delivery promise compressed to two-hour.
  • LO 12.8 Identify the five retail failure modes and apply the measured-failure-mode test to any in-store AI or retail-tech claim.
  • LO 12.9 Evaluate an AI-assisted retail audit using the chapter's eight-decision framework, and apply the first-party-data vs. third-party-agent rule to a retailer's data strategy.

Item 1

A retailer is best defined as any business whose sales volume comes primarily from _______.

A) manufacturing goods for other businesses B) selling goods or services directly to final consumers for personal, nonbusiness use C) distributing products to other businesses that then resell them D) operating a website or app that connects buyers and sellers E) licensing intellectual property to other firms that carry out the transactions

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 12.1 — Retailing is defined by who the customer is (final consumer for personal use), not by the channel, format, or legal structure of the firm doing the selling.

Item 5

Item 7

Item 10

Sharp's physical-availability law, as applied to retail, states that the relationship between a brand's weighted distribution and its market share is _______.

A) linear: each added point of distribution returns the same share increment as the last B) convex: each added point of distribution returns more share than the last, so the final distribution points pay the most C) concave: early distribution points produce disproportionate gains and later points produce diminishing returns D) step-function: share holds constant until the brand crosses a critical distribution threshold, then jumps E) random: share and distribution are uncorrelated once a brand reaches fifty percent weighted distribution

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 12.5 — The chapter presents a convex distribution-to-share curve drawn as a reverse-L. The annotation identifies the final distribution points as the ones that pay most, which runs counter to the conventional assumption that early distribution is the high-return investment. This is the Sharp/Ehrenberg-Bass insight on physical availability.

Tags: physical availability; Sharp; distribution-to-share curve; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Section II — Scenario / Short-Answer Items (4 items)

SA-1

A regional health-food grocery chain carries 22,000 SKUs across roughly 90 categories. Sales data shows that the top 3,000 SKUs (14 percent of the line) generate 71 percent of revenue. The bottom 8,000 SKUs generate 4 percent of revenue but consume 38 percent of linear shelf footage. A new VP of merchandising proposes cutting to 12,000 SKUs. The buying team pushes back, arguing that shoppers expect selection. Using the assortment logic and the Direct Product Profitability concept from our chapter, write a 150-to-200-word recommendation for or against the proposed cut and identify the one additional piece of data the VP needs before finalizing the cull.

Sample answer: The data supports the cut. The bottom 8,000 SKUs consume 38 percent of shelf footage for 4 percent of revenue, which means the linear-foot rent those SKUs pay is almost certainly underwater on a Direct Product Profitability basis. DPP charges a SKU its full cost: receiving, moving to storage, paperwork, selecting, checking, loading, and its share of linear-foot rent. A SKU with a positive gross margin can still have a negative DPP when those handling costs are added in, and the shelf footage those 8,000 items occupy is the clearest signal that they fail the DPP test. Cutting to 12,000 frees shelf feet that can go toward deeper facings on the top-performing lines, which raises per-SKU velocity and improves supplier leverage. The buying team's "shoppers expect selection" argument applies to destination categories, not to the entire tail. The one additional piece of data the VP needs before finalizing the cull is which of the 8,000 low-performers are destination categories for a defined shopper segment, because a DPP-negative SKU that brings a high-value customer into the store every week may still earn its shelf space.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 12.3 — Assortment cull using velocity, DPP, and destination-category logic.

Tags: assortment strategy; Direct Product Profitability; SKU cull; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

SA-2

A mid-size specialty apparel chain has 140 stores. It currently prices 60 percent of its SKUs at a modest everyday price and runs promotions on the rest four to six times per year. A consultant recommends switching to full EDLP across the chain. The CFO is skeptical because gross margin on merchandise already runs at 48 percent, and the EDLP move would compress it further. Using the turns-and-earns framework and the structural offset concept from our chapter, explain what the CFO's concern is, identify one structural condition that would have to be true before EDLP makes financial sense, and describe one example of a structural offset the chain could build.

Sample answer: The CFO is right to be skeptical. Everyday-low pricing is a deliberate margin sacrifice on the merchandise: the chain sets a permanently lower shelf price, which reduces gross margin per unit. The turns-and-earns framework says specialty apparel typically runs high-earns (high gross margin) and low-turns (slow inventory velocity) because the items are not staples that the buyer replenishes weekly. Giving up the earns without a corresponding jump in turns means the chain simply makes less money per square foot. For EDLP to pay, the chain needs a structural offset that covers the lost margin. One condition that would have to be true: the chain would need to demonstrate that the promotional cycle is currently fragmenting its shopper base, meaning some shoppers cherry-pick promoted items and skip the store otherwise, so eliminating promotions actually raises average basket size and shortens the replenishment cycle. One structural offset the chain could build is a retail media network on its website and app, selling sponsored placements to the brands it carries. If the ad inventory runs at near-100-percent gross margin (as Walmart Connect does), it can cover the merchandise-margin compression while the stable, predictable pricing trains a habitual shopper.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 12.6 — Turns-and-earns trade-off; EDLP structural offset; retail media network.

Tags: EDLP; turns and earns; retail media network; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

SA-3

Costco operates roughly 900 warehouse locations with a narrow assortment of around 4,000 SKUs, no advertising budget, near-zero grocery margins, a $65 to $130 annual membership fee, and a 93 percent membership renewal rate. Apply the eight-decision framework from our chapter to explain why Costco's retail model holds together as a system rather than as a collection of separate choices. Your answer should identify at least four of the eight decisions and show how they interlock.

Sample answer: Costco's eight decisions interlock as one design, which is the chapter's core claim. Working through four of them: (1) Target market: members only, filtered by a paid annual fee, which removes the casual browser and self-selects for shoppers who buy in volume. (2) Assortment: roughly 4,000 SKUs versus a conventional grocery's 50,000. The narrow assortment concentrates velocity on every item carried, which gives Costco extraordinary supplier leverage and the ability to offer genuinely low per-unit prices without losing the earns. (3) Pricing: near-zero markup on merchandise is not a loss but a deliberate member-benefit signal. The margin sacrifice is covered by the membership fee, which runs at near-100-percent gross margin and funds roughly 70 percent of Costco's net income. The EDLP strategy only holds because the membership fee is the structural offset. (4) Atmospherics and layout: the warehouse format, the treasure-hunt rotation of limited-time SKUs, and the rotisserie chicken placed at the back-right near wine and prepared foods are all deliberate layout choices that route the member past high-margin departments before she reaches the destination item she came in for. None of these four decisions works without the others. A narrow assortment without a membership filter is a small store with no differentiation. A membership fee without a believable low-price promise does not renew at 93 percent. The eight decisions interlock because Costco made them as a system, not one at a time.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 12.1 / LO 12.3 / LO 12.6 — Eight-decision dependency chain; assortment strategy; EDLP structural offset.

Tags: Costco; eight retail decisions; assortment; EDLP; membership; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

SA-4

A startup is planning to launch a new fragrance line and must choose a retail strategy. Option A is placing products in Ulta Beauty and Nordstrom, where the brand would sit alongside established competitors. Option B is opening two company-owned pop-up stores in Dallas and Austin for six months, then evaluating whether to scale. Using Sharp's physical-availability law from our chapter, argue which option is more likely to grow market share faster and identify the one trade-off Option B creates that Option A avoids.

Sample answer: Option A is the stronger choice for growing market share faster. Sharp's physical-availability law shows a convex relationship between weighted distribution and market share: each added point of distribution returns more share than the last. A fragrance brand placed in Ulta Beauty and Nordstrom immediately reaches the buyers who are already shopping those chains, who are already in the store, and who make the majority of their brand choices at the shelf in a three-to-seven-second window. The distribution-to-share curve means that moving from near-zero weighted distribution to broad placement in two major specialty beauty chains is a very large move on the steepest part of that curve. Option B keeps weighted distribution near zero for six months while the brand collects data. The trade-off Option B creates that Option A avoids is the opportunity cost of forgone distribution during a launch window: a competitor placing in Ulta during those same six months gains compounding shelf presence and repeat purchase, while the fragrance brand is still in pilot mode. Option B does produce first-party data and format control, which is real value, but the physical-availability law says the brand that gets on more shelves first wins more share more often, and a startup fragrance brand has very few months before its launch window closes.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 12.5 — Physical-availability law; distribution-to-share curve; launch strategy.

Tags: physical availability; Sharp; distribution; retail channel choice; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

AI-1 — The Bring-Your-Own-Store Audit

A student runs the chapter's eight-decision audit on a regional outdoor sporting goods chain using an AI assistant with live web access. The AI returns the following:

"Target market: outdoor enthusiasts aged 25-50. Assortment: approximately 8,000 SKUs across camping, climbing, water sports, and cycling. Service: full-service with staff on the floor. Pricing: high-low with seasonal promotions. Location: 22 stores in the Mountain West region. Atmospherics: adventure-themed decor, product demos. Communication: Instagram and email campaigns. Loyalty: a points program with 140,000 members."

The student submits this as his retail audit. According to the chapter's framework, what is the most important analytic move the AI output skips entirely?

A) The output does not name the specific brands carried in each department, which is required to complete a full assortment audit. B) The output identifies the eight decisions by name but does not apply the dependency logic: it does not ask whether the decisions interlock as a system or whether a wrong link in the chain breaks the rest. C) The output is incomplete because it does not include financial data such as revenue per store and operating margin. D) The output fails because the AI searched the web rather than visiting the store in person, making its atmospherics reading unreliable. E) The output is fine; the student has correctly applied the eight-decision framework.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 12.1 / LO 12.9 — The chapter's eight-decision framework is a dependency chain, not a checklist. Naming eight categories is not the audit; the audit asks whether the decisions reinforce one promise or sit beside each other as separate choices. A wrong target market makes the assortment unanswerable; a mispriced assortment leaves atmospherics nothing to rescue. The student's AI output produced a list, not a systems analysis.

Tags: eight-decision audit; AI retail analysis; dependency chain; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Analytical thinking

AI-2 — Vague vs. Specific AI Question

The chapter's interactive widget demonstrates that the specificity of the question you give an AI model determines whether the output is useful. A category manager at a regional grocery chain asks an AI assistant two versions of the same question:

Version A: "What should we know about our produce section?"

Version B: "We carry 340 produce SKUs. Our top 40 by velocity generate 62 percent of produce revenue. Our bottom 120 SKUs together generate 4 percent of produce revenue and consume roughly 35 percent of produce linear footage. What is the assortment cull logic, and which SKUs would you flag as candidates for removal or consolidation?"

Which version of the question is more likely to produce output that the manager can act on, and why, according to the chapter's framework?

A) Version A, because a broader question lets the AI surface insights the manager had not thought to ask about. B) Version B, because a specific question forces the AI to name the format, apply the velocity and DPP logic, and flag the long boring middle — which is the eight-decision read the chapter just taught. C) Version A, because AI models produce more accurate answers when the question is open-ended. D) Version B, because the AI performs better with numerical inputs than with qualitative questions. E) Both versions are equivalent; the manager should ask both and compare outputs.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 12.3 / LO 12.9 — The chapter's verdict on the vague-vs-specific widget: the vague question gave back a generic answer the manager could have written herself. The specific question forced the model to apply the velocity and DPP logic, identify the long boring middle, and name candidates for the cull, which is the eight-decision read run live on a real assortment.

Tags: vague vs. specific AI question; assortment audit; AI retail tool; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

AI-3 — Evaluating an AI Retail-Tech Claim

A retail-tech vendor pitches a regional grocery chain on an autonomous planogram optimizer. The pitch deck states: "Our AI re-optimizes shelf layouts weekly using your POS data, adjusting facings and product positions without any manual review. In our 12-week pilot at a single location, we saw a 4.2 percent lift in sales per linear foot." The chain's VP of Operations wants to deploy across all 85 stores immediately.

Using the chapter's measured-failure-mode test, identify the two most important questions the VP must answer before chain-wide deployment, and explain what failure scenario each question is designed to catch.

Sample answer and rubric:

Question 1: What happens the day the model is wrong? A false positive in an autonomous planogram optimizer means the system re-faces a high-velocity item into a low-visibility position, or removes a destination category from the endcap, based on a data error or a model misread. Across 85 stores deploying simultaneously, that error fires chain-wide in the same week. The category manager does not know until the next POS cycle shows a sales drop, which is one to two weeks of compounding damage per store. The chapter's case is Just Walk Out: 700 of every 1,000 transactions needed human review, but no one realized the failure rate was that high until the system was at grocery scale. The test: does the vendor have a named human-review gate before any change deploys, and what is the rollback procedure?

Question 2: What does a false positive cost at scale? The 12-week, single-location pilot is not a chain-scale test. A 4.2 percent sales lift in one store may not reproduce at 85 stores, because the pilot location's specific layout, traffic patterns, and POS data mix drove the result. At chain scale, a model that is right 95 percent of the time makes simultaneous errors in roughly four stores per week. If each wrong reoptimization costs the chain two weeks of elevated out-of-stocks or misrouted traffic, the aggregate cost may exceed the aggregate lift. The VP needs a failure-mode rate estimate from the pilot data and a calculation of what that rate costs at 85 stores before committing to chain-wide deployment.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 12.8 — The measured-failure-mode test applied to an autonomous AI retail system. Students must name the two specific questions and connect each to a real failure scenario.

Tags: measured failure mode test; autonomous planogram optimizer; AI retail tech; chain deployment risk; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

AI-4 — The First-Party vs. Agent Rule

A national grocery chain is evaluating a partnership with a third-party AI commerce platform. The platform would integrate with the chain's app, giving customers an AI shopping assistant that recommends products, builds personalized baskets, and completes checkout. In exchange, the platform would receive a revenue share on each transaction and access to the chain's purchase transaction data to train its own models. The chain's CMO sees this as a fast path to AI-powered personalization without building the capability in-house.

According to the chapter's first-party-vs-agent rule, which of the following best describes the strategic risk the CMO is taking on?

A) The chain is accepting regulatory risk because sharing transaction data with a third party likely violates GDPR and CCPA. B) The chain is outsourcing the shopper's data relationship to a third party that will use that data to train models the third party owns, which progressively weakens the chain's first-party data asset and the retail-media network that depends on it. C) The chain is accepting brand risk because the AI assistant's recommendations will not always match the chain's private-label strategy. D) The chain is accepting margin risk because the revenue-share model will compress gross margin below the EDLP structural-offset threshold. E) The chain is accepting technology lock-in risk because switching from one AI platform to another will require re-integrating the app from scratch.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 12.9 — The chapter's first-party-vs-agent rule: a chain cannot let a third-party agent own the cart without owning the format. The data the AI commerce platform trains on is the chain's purchase history. Once that data trains a third-party model, the chain no longer has an exclusive on its own shoppers' behavioral pattern. The retail-media network the chapter describes (Walmart Connect, Amazon Ads, Instacart Advertising) derives its 70-percent gross margin precisely because the retailer owns the first-party conversion data. Outsourcing the shopping agent means outsourcing the data asset that makes the RMN valuable.

Tags: first-party data; third-party agent rule; retail data strategy; AI commerce platform; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking; Ethical understanding

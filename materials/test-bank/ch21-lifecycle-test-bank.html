Chapter 21 Lifecycle, Email, and SMS Marketing / CRM — Test Bank

Chapter: 21 — Lifecycle, Email, and SMS Marketing / CRM

Learning Objectives (Ch21, Our Chapter)

  • LO 21.1 Explain why retaining a buyer costs far less than acquiring one, name the four mechanisms that make retention pay, and compute the basic CLV formula for a subscription and a non-contractual brand.
  • LO 21.2 Distinguish contractual from non-contractual brands and explain how the contractual/non-contractual distinction changes how "retention rate" is measured and what it means.
  • LO 21.3 Describe how the three email authentication protocols (SPF, DKIM, DMARC) work together to determine inbox placement, and explain the 2024 Gmail bulk-sender rule that made them non-optional.
  • LO 21.4 Name the five drip patterns that cover the lifecycle wheel, match each to its trigger and the buyer's silent question, and explain why cadence is set by the trigger, not the brand's preference.
  • LO 21.5 Explain the TCPA's prior-express-written-consent requirement for SMS, describe the penalty structure, and apply the opt-in compliance rules to a real program design.
  • LO 21.6 Describe how RFM scoring segments a customer list into named cohorts, match each cohort to the appropriate drip, and explain the purpose of send-time optimization and the frequency cap.
  • LO 21.7 Explain the customer-base audit's decile-decomposition approach, interpret the whale curve, and use the vital-few/useful-many finding to prioritize lifecycle investment.
  • LO 21.8 Define customer equity as the sum of CLV across the customer base and connect a retention lift to its effect on the firm's underlying value and the handoff to customer-based corporate valuation (CBCV).
  • LO 21.9 Describe the four-step AI agent ladder (chat, Skill, connected, scheduled) and distinguish the clerical work the agent handles from the editorial work the marketer keeps.

Section I — Multiple-Choice Items (22 items)

Item 1

A direct-to-consumer apparel brand spends 60 dollars in social-media ads to win one new customer's first order. That order returns 35 dollars of contribution margin. On the first transaction the channel _______.

A) breaks even, because the 35-dollar margin is close enough to the 60-dollar spend B) loses money, but the investment makes sense only if the buyer comes back for repeat orders C) profits immediately, because the contribution margin is the revenue the brand keeps D) profits immediately, because paid social has no variable cost per click E) loses money and can never recover it once the first transaction closes

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 21.1 — The brand spends 60 to earn 35, a 25-dollar loss on the first order. The investment pays back only through repeat purchases that carry no fresh acquisition cost.

Tags: CAC; lifecycle economics; CLV; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 21.1 — The Bain/HBR study found a 25-to-95 percent lift in customer-lifetime profit from a five-point retention improvement, with a wide range because the four retention mechanisms differ in strength by category.

Item 3

Item 8

SPF, DKIM, and DMARC are three email authentication protocols that work as a sequence. In the chapter's hotel-security analogy, DMARC is the _______.

A) check that verifies which mail servers are authorized to send on behalf of the domain B) cryptographic signature that proves the message was not altered in transit C) instruction that tells the receiving server what to do when the first two checks fail D) carrier-registration format required before bulk SMS can be sent E) suppression list that prevents unsubscribed addresses from receiving the next campaign

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 21.3 — DMARC is the policy layer: it tells the receiving server whether to quarantine or reject messages that fail SPF or DKIM. SPF verifies the sending server; DKIM verifies message integrity. DMARC closes the loop on failure handling.

Tags: SPF; DKIM; DMARC; email authentication; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 15

A) $0, because the TCPA exempts automated marketing texts when the platform records the send B) $5,000, because the penalty applies only to the brand's total revenue, not per message C) $50,000, because class-action settlements cap individual recoveries at five dollars per message D) $5,000,000, because the TCPA statute carries damages of $500 per violation and every text is its own violation E) $500, because the FTC's Junk Fees Rule limits SMS damages to one dollar per recipient

Section II — Scenario / Short-Answer Items (4 items)

SA-1

A graduate who just joined a small DTC coffee-subscription brand as a lifecycle coordinator tells her manager that she wants to set up a "retention rate" dashboard using the same formula the chapter teaches. Her manager asks: "Does our subscription model or our DTC-subscription model change what that number actually means?" Using the chapter's contractual-vs-non-contractual distinction, explain how the coordinator should answer. Define both terms, identify which applies to a coffee subscription, and explain what the formula's retention-rate term measures in each setting.

Sample answer: A contractual brand has a subscription a buyer can cancel at any moment; the moment of cancellation is a fact the brand observes, so the retention rate is a clean count: the share of subscribers who renewed. The CLV formula's retention-rate term is a real number. A non-contractual brand has no subscription to cancel; a buyer who stops ordering never sends a departure signal, so "retention rate" is an estimate of the share who came back, derived from repeat-purchase history rather than cancellation records. For a coffee subscription — where the buyer actively cancels and the platform records it — the coordinator's formula is valid and the retention rate is the share who stayed on the plan. The chapter's caution applies to the other brands on her future resume: a DTC candle shop, an apparel label, an Amazon seller. There, "72 percent retention" is a repeat-purchase reading, not a contractual fact, and the lapsed-buyer cutoff rules (lapsed at 180 or 365 days of silence) are the workaround for a brand the buyer never tells she has left.

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 21.2

Tags: contractual vs non-contractual; retention rate; CLV formula; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

SA-2

A small outdoor-apparel brand is about to run its first email campaign to a purchased list of 15,000 addresses it has never mailed before. The founder says: "We have SPF and DKIM set up — that's all we need." Using the chapter's inbox-placement framework, explain what is missing and why the gap matters. Name the three authentication protocols, identify which one the founder is missing, and explain what will happen to bulk sends without it after February 2024.

Sample answer: The founder has SPF (which verifies the authorized sending server) and DKIM (which cryptographically signs the message to prove it was not altered in transit), but is missing DMARC. SPF and DKIM each close one hole; DMARC is the policy layer that tells the receiving server what to do when either check fails: reject the message, quarantine it, or let it through while reporting the failure. Without DMARC, the receiving mail server has no clear instruction on failure, and mailbox providers fill that silence with their own defaults, which almost always route the message to spam or block it. After February 2024, Google's rule for bulk senders — anyone sending more than 5,000 messages per day to Gmail addresses — explicitly requires a published DMARC record or the mail is rejected at the gateway. A purchased, unwarmed list of 15,000 addresses is exactly the scenario that triggers this rule, and Yahoo and Outlook followed Google's lead within months of the February announcement. The founder's brand will have its first campaign blocked or bulk-spammed before a single creative decision matters, and the platform's dashboard will report a stable "open rate" on the delivered fraction without showing the founder how few messages actually arrived.

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 21.3

Tags: SPF; DKIM; DMARC; deliverability; Gmail bulk-sender rule; AACSB: Application of knowledge

SA-3

A Stanley-scale DTC brand runs a popular SMS list through Postscript for drop launches on new colorways. The brand's head of growth argues that because their opt-in rate is strong — 18 percent of online buyers check the SMS checkbox at checkout — TCPA compliance is handled and there is nothing else to worry about. A new marketing director disagrees. Using the chapter's TCPA framework, identify two specific risks the head of growth is overlooking and explain what the brand should do to reduce each one.

Sample answer: Risk 1 — Broken opt-in flow: even if the checkbox is correctly configured today, a platform update, A/B test, or developer push could accidentally pre-check the box or remove the consent language, quietly making every opt-in collected after that moment non-compliant. The fix is to audit the opt-in flow at every checkout-page change and keep timestamped records of each confirmed opt-in event inside Postscript. Risk 2 — The private right of action: the TCPA allows individual buyers, or a class of buyers, to sue the brand directly for $500 to $1,500 per unauthorized message without waiting for a regulator. At 10,000 non-compliant texts the floor exposure is $5 million. A brand running a large list at Stanley's scale is precisely the type of defendant TCPA class-action litigators target. The opt-in box alone does not protect against this if any batch is sent to a number collected before proper consent was recorded. The defense is: (a) route all sends through a platform that records consent automatically; (b) use a double opt-in (a reply-YES confirmation text) as industry-standard risk reduction; (c) honor STOP requests within 24 hours, the standard the carriers enforce; and (d) never send to a purchased or imported list without verifying each number's consent record.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 21.5

Tags: TCPA; prior express written consent; statutory damages; opt-in compliance; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Ethical understanding

SA-4

A local meal-prep subscription service has 8,000 subscribers and a 68-percent annual retention rate. A junior analyst proposes three ideas to grow revenue: (1) run more paid-social ads to add 500 new subscribers per month, (2) improve the onboarding drip to raise retention to 72 percent, and (3) add a frequency cap and a send-time optimization setting to protect deliverability. Using the CLV formula and the customer-equity logic from our chapter, explain which of the three proposals produces the largest long-run lift in the brand's underlying value, and show the arithmetic behind your answer.

Sample answer: The meal-prep service is a contractual brand (a subscription buyers can cancel), so the CLV formula applies directly. Assume each subscriber contributes $22 per month in gross margin, or $264 per year, and use a 1 percent monthly discount rate for simplicity. At 68 percent annual retention the CLV formula gives $264 / (1 - 0.68) = approximately $825 per subscriber. Lifting retention to 72 percent gives $264 / (1 - 0.72) = approximately $943 per subscriber, a $118 increase. Across 8,000 current subscribers the 4-point retention lift adds roughly $944,000 to the customer equity total — the combined lifetime value of the whole base — with no new acquisition spending. Proposal 1 adds 500 subscribers per month at a CAC the analyst does not specify, but at $825 each that is $412,500 of new CLV, less the acquisition spend. The retention improvement (Proposal 2) lifts the value of 8,000 existing subscribers immediately and persistently; the acquisition campaign must fund each new buyer before a net gain appears. Proposal 3 protects deliverability (the frequency cap prevents list burn, and send-time optimization improves click-through), which supports the retention lift but does not by itself change the CLV figure. The chapter's framework shows that lifting the retention side of the CLV formula compounds across the entire existing base, which is why Proposal 2 almost always has the highest long-run lift per dollar spent.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 21.1, LO 21.8

Tags: CLV formula; customer equity; retention lift; CBCV; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

Section III — AI-Integration Items (4 items)

These items are original to this course and do not appear in the Kotler bank. They test students' ability to apply the chapter's frameworks to AI-era lifecycle tools, the four-step agent ladder, and the customer-base audit's AI workflow.

AI-1 — Four-Step AI Agent Ladder

A lifecycle marketer's current workflow: every Monday morning she opens Klaviyo, exports last week's flow metrics to a spreadsheet, computes the change in click-through rate for each drip, copies the numbers into a chat assistant, types "here are our metrics — flag anything unusual," and reads the reply before writing a summary email to her team. On a good morning this takes 90 minutes.

According to the chapter's four-step AI agent ladder, this marketer is at Step 1 (paste into chat). Which of the following changes would move her to Step 4 (scheduled agent), and what single piece of clerical work does Step 4 remove?

A) She switches from Claude to GPT-4o, which processes spreadsheets faster. B) She connects Klaviyo to her AI assistant via an MCP connector, schedules the audit to run every Monday at 7 a.m., and configures the agent to post the diff directly to the team channel — removing the need for a human to remember to start the task. C) She hires an analyst to run the export while she focuses on writing the diff. D) She packages the Monday audit as a Skill so it runs in one command instead of a paste. E) She sets up a frequency cap and send-time optimization inside Klaviyo, which automates the metrics collection side of the task.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 21.9 — Step 4 is the scheduled agent: the tool pulls live data (Step 3's MCP connector), runs the audit, and posts the diff on a cron schedule with no human trigger. The clerical task removed is "remembering to start it," which the chapter names explicitly.

Tags: four-step AI ladder; scheduled agent; MCP; lifecycle automation; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Reflective thinking

AI-2 — Critique an AI-Drafted Lifecycle Audit

A marketing student uses an AI assistant to audit a win-back email draft. She gives the assistant this prompt: "Make this email better." The assistant replies with three suggestions: (1) use a stronger call to action, (2) shorten the subject line, and (3) add social proof. The student accepts all three and sends the campaign. Response rates are lower than the brand's prior win-back average.

Using the chapter's framework for AI-assisted lifecycle work and the lesson from the widget comparison, identify the primary failure in how the student used the assistant, and rewrite the prompt in a way that would have produced a more useful audit.

Sample answer and rubric:

Primary failure — Vague prompt: the student asked a general copywriting question with no context about the buyer's lifecycle stage, the brand's no-discount rule, the subject-line character limit, or the re-engagement goal. The chapter makes this distinction explicit with its widget comparison: a vague prompt ("make this email better") returns generic advice the student could have guessed; a sharp prompt that names the buyer's stage and the constraints returns a rewrite that can go directly into the platform.

Rewrite: "This is a win-back email going to buyers who have not purchased in 180 days. The brand does not use discounts in win-back campaigns. The goal is a soft reintroduction, not a hard sell. The subject line must fit under 80 characters to survive AI inbox summary previews. The buyer's silent question at this stage is 'is there a reason to come back that has nothing to do with a sale?' Review the draft against those constraints and rewrite the subject line and opening sentence."

What the rewrite produces that the original could not: a response matched to the win-back stage's specific constraints, a subject line designed to survive Gmail's AI summary feature (which the chapter names as the reason to front-load the value reason in the first 80 characters), and copy that avoids the discount that the chapter says trains the wrong behavior in a lapsed buyer.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 21.9, LO 21.4

Tags: AI lifecycle audit; vague vs specific prompt; win-back drip; AACSB: Written communication; Application of knowledge

AI-3 — Customer-Base Audit Decile Decomposition

A student downloads a synthetic transaction log from her firm's Shopify account and asks a spreadsheet-enabled AI assistant to run a customer-base audit. The assistant returns the following summary:

"The top 10 percent of buyers (by annual profit) generated 38 percent of total profit. The top 20 percent generated 57 percent. The bottom 25 percent operated at a loss to serve. Decomposing top-decile vs bottom-decile buyers: the top group beats the bottom on all three levers — average order frequency (6.8 vs 1.1 orders per year), average order value ($94 vs $41), and gross margin per order (37% vs 31%). The single largest gap is in order frequency."

Using the chapter's customer-base audit and vital-few/useful-many framework, answer: (1) Is this pattern unusual or normal? (2) What does the decomposition tell the brand about which lever to prioritize in its lifecycle program? (3) What does the whale curve for this brand look like, and what does it reveal that the average-customer metric hides?

A) The pattern is unusual; the brand should investigate whether its top decile is gaming a loyalty program. The decomposition says to prioritize margins. The whale curve slopes downward from the start. B) The pattern is normal; the vital-few/useful-many principle appears in almost every customer base. The decomposition says to prioritize order frequency (the biggest gap), with lifecycle drips designed to trigger a second and third order. The whale curve climbs steeply on the vital few, crosses 100 percent of total profit before the last buyer is added, and bends back down as the unprofitable tail subtracts from the total — revealing that the average-customer figure is a blend of radically different groups that treating everyone equally ignores. C) The pattern is normal, but the action is to cut the bottom 25 percent from all lists immediately, which will raise average profitability automatically. D) The decomposition says to prioritize average order value because it is the most controllable lever in email marketing. E) The whale curve shows that the brand's customer base is healthy and no lifecycle changes are needed until the bottom-25-percent segment turns profitable.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 21.7 — The vital-few/useful-many pattern is described as near-universal in the chapter. The decomposition points to frequency as the largest gap, which maps to activation and re-engagement drips. The whale curve visual is explained in detail in the chapter, and the "average customer" problem is named as the reason flat-average metrics hide this shape.

Tags: customer-base audit; decile decomposition; whale curve; vital few and useful many; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

AI-4 — The AI Lifecycle Agent Loop

A brand's lifecycle team is evaluating whether to build a scheduled AI agent that runs every Monday and produces a weekly diff for the team. The team's current manual process takes one analyst 90 minutes every Monday. The agent would use an MCP connector to pull live Klaviyo segment and flow data, a packaged Skill (the /lifecycle-audit skill the team wrote) to run the audit, and a cron trigger to post the diff to Slack at 7 a.m. The agent's estimated running cost is $3 per week. The analyst's loaded hourly cost is $55.

Which of the following correctly describes the editorial work the agent cannot do — and therefore the work the team still needs the analyst to perform?

A) The agent cannot pull data from Klaviyo because MCP connectors require a developer to maintain them, so the analyst still runs the export manually. B) The agent handles the clerical audit reliably but cannot review the draft before it goes live, approve a campaign launch, or catch the moment when a segment definition produces a result the brand can see is wrong. Those editorial judgments stay with the analyst. C) The agent can do everything described and the analyst's role in lifecycle marketing is eliminated entirely once the agent is running. D) The agent cannot produce a diff because it lacks natural-language reasoning, so the analyst still writes the Monday summary. E) The agent cannot audit the TCPA consent records because that requires human legal review, so the analyst still checks the opt-in list manually.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 21.9 — The chapter's central AI distinction is between the clerical half (which the agent handles: draft copy, build segments, pick send times, post the diff) and the editorial half (which the marketer keeps: review the draft before it ships, approve the launch, catch the quietly wrong segment). The work was relocated, not eliminated.

Tags: four-step AI ladder; clerical vs editorial; lifecycle agent; MCP; Skill; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

Learning Objectives (Ch24, Our Chapter)

  • LO 24.1 Explain why a marketing plan is a closed loop and identify the five G-STIC elements that must all be present for the loop to close.
  • LO 24.2 Describe the seven-row one-page marketing plan document and explain why writing it in G-STIC order prevents the budget-plus-calendar collapse.
  • LO 24.3 Define customer lifetime value (CLV) and customer asset value, distinguish CLV from revenue as a goal-layer metric, and apply the CLV formula to rank customer segments.
  • LO 24.4 Explain the AER (acquisition, expansion, retention) framework and describe how the three structural positions (cost leadership, differentiation, focus) set different AER budget postures.
  • LO 24.5 Describe the five-stage analytical-maturity staircase and the DELTA Plus capability model, and explain why marketing audit differs from the operational dashboard.
  • LO 24.6 Classify marketing metrics on the six-rung hierarchy, define vanity metrics, and distinguish leading from lagging indicators.
  • LO 24.7 Compare the four attribution methods on the spectrum (last-click, multi-touch, marketing-mix modeling, incrementality) by cost, causal accuracy, and appropriate use case.
  • LO 24.8 Identify the six common planning failure modes, match each to the G-STIC element it breaks, and apply the corresponding fix.
  • LO 24.9 Apply the AI customer-base-audit Skill, the metrics-classifier, and the draft-my-plan capabilities to a real marketing planning problem.

Item 6

The simplified contractual CLV formula in our chapter is written as CLV = (M minus C) / (1 + d minus r) minus A. Which of the following is the correct CLV calculation?

A) $100 minus $10 = $90; stop here B) ($100 minus $10) / (1 + 0.05 minus 0.65) minus $65 = approximately $235 C) ($100 minus $65) / (1.05 minus 0.65) = $87.50 D) ($100 / 0.65) minus $10 minus $65 = approximately $79 E) ($100 minus $10) / 0.05 minus $65 = approximately $1,735

Section II — Scenario / Short-Answer Items (4 items)

SA-1

A regional outdoor-gear retailer has a customer file showing 280,000 active buyers, $42 million in annual revenue, and a reported churn rate of 28 percent per year. The marketing director plans to budget next year's retention spend based on the 28 percent figure, which implies an average customer lifetime of 3.6 years.

A junior analyst splits the file by transaction behavior and finds three distinct groups: a low-risk 65 percent of the base that churns at 12 percent per year (average lifetime 8.3 years), a mid-risk 25 percent that churns at 40 percent (2.5 years), and a high-risk 10 percent that churns at 75 percent (1.3 years).

(a) Compute the weighted-average customer lifetime using the three segments. Show your work. (b) Identify the failure mode the director's single-rate approach represents from our chapter's six failure modes. (c) State one concrete change the director should make to the goal-layer number in the marketing plan.

Sample answer:

(a) Weighted average = (0.65 × 8.3) + (0.25 × 2.5) + (0.10 × 1.3) = 5.395 + 0.625 + 0.130 = 6.15 years. The single aggregate rate implies 3.6 years; the segmented read implies 6.15 years, which is 71 percent longer. The same customer file read two ways produces a materially different goal-layer number before a new dollar is spent.

(b) This is failure mode 1, the naive retention curve: the firm fits one attrition rate to a base whose underlying rates vary. The aggregate rate collapses the survival dynamic that the segmented rates respect. Once the high-risk tenth churns out, the survivors are dominated by the long-stay majority, which is what makes the average lifetime nearly double when read correctly.

(c) The director should replace the single-rate CLV in the plan's goal row with a segment-weighted customer-asset value number. Specifically, the goal layer should state the customer-asset value computed from the three risk groups' retention rates, not from the aggregate rate, so that the plan's spending ceiling per customer reflects the value the base actually holds.

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 24.3, LO 24.8

Tags: naive retention; CLV; customer asset value; Vodafone; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

SA-2

A direct-to-consumer skincare brand runs its weekly marketing operations from a dashboard that shows revenue by product, spend by channel, cost per acquisition, and email open rates. The CMO considers this sufficient instrumentation. On a Tuesday standup call, she notes that total revenue is up 8 percent year-over-year and active-customer count is up 12 percent.

A consultant proposes adding a quarterly customer-base audit. The CMO asks: "What would the audit show that the dashboard cannot?"

Write a response the consultant could give, drawing on the audit framework from our chapter. Your answer should name at least two specific types of information the audit would surface, explain why the dashboard cannot surface them, and name the G-STIC element the audit feeds.

Sample answer: The dashboard reports operational metrics at a product, channel, or campaign level, and none of those units is the customer. It can tell you how many customers you have and what they spent in aggregate this week, but it cannot reorganize the base by how often each customer buys or how much profit each customer produces. Two things the audit surfaces that the dashboard cannot are:

First, the decile distribution of profit. The audit would show how much of the brand's profit comes from the top 10 percent of customers versus the bottom half. A base where 40 percent of profit comes from the top decile and the bottom 10 percent produces 1 percent is a very different planning problem from a homogeneous base, yet the dashboard reports all active customers identically.

Second, the shape of the retention curve and the mix of cohort types. The audit reads acquisition cohorts separately, showing whether the 2024 cohort is retaining better or worse than the 2023 cohort, and whether acquisition through different channels is producing structurally different buyers. The dashboard's active-customer count cannot show that a 12 percent increase in active customers is driven by a new cohort retaining at half the rate of older ones.

The G-STIC element the audit feeds is the goal layer: it checks whether the customer-asset-value number the plan was sized against is still correct for next year, which is a different question from what the control-layer dashboard tracks week to week.

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 24.5, LO 24.6, LO 24.8

Tags: customer-base audit; dashboard; G-STIC; instrumentation; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Written communication

SA-3

A mid-size sports-nutrition brand is preparing its annual marketing plan. The strategy team has sketched the following attribution setup: the firm credits every conversion to the last paid touch the customer clicked. The top channel by this measure is paid search, which receives 58 percent of the next-year budget.

A new marketing scientist joins and argues the firm should switch to marketing-mix modeling as its budget spine and run one incrementality test per quarter on the highest-spend channel.

(a) Name one systematic bias the current last-click setup introduces into the budget allocation. (b) Explain why marketing-mix modeling is the chapter's recommended budget spine rather than incrementality testing, even though incrementality is described as the "gold standard." (c) State one way the quarterly incrementality test serves as a cross-check even when MMM is the primary method.

Sample answer:

(a) Last-click systematically over-credits the bottom-funnel channels (paid search, retargeting) that merely close a customer the upper-funnel channels (social video, sponsorship, brand advertising) already produced. A buyer who saw an Instagram video three weeks earlier and then searched for the brand the day she bought is credited entirely to the search click under last-click. Over time, reallocating by last-click starves the demand-generation work that fed the search funnel, compressing next quarter's pipeline before anyone notices.

(b) Incrementality testing is the only method that measures causality directly, but it is too slow and too narrow to run on every budget line. A single geo-holdout test takes four to twelve weeks and measures only one channel at a time. The firm cannot run an incrementality test on every item in its budget without either taking years or foregoing the revenue from held-back markets. MMM can estimate an elasticity coefficient for every channel simultaneously from aggregate weekly data, making it the practical budget spine. The chapter describes MMM as the 2026 budget-allocation standard.

(c) An incrementality test on the highest-spend channel each quarter gives the firm a causal ground truth it can compare against the MMM coefficient for that channel. If the two methods agree, the MMM model is calibrated well; if they diverge significantly, the analyst knows the MMM coefficient for that channel needs to be revisited. The incrementality test does not replace MMM as the spine, but it provides an empirical check on the model's most consequential estimate.

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 24.7

Tags: attribution spectrum; last-click bias; MMM; incrementality; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

SA-4

A subscription fitness-app brand launches a six-month loyalty program. After six months, it reports that loyalty program members buy 2.3 times more per month than non-members. The VP of Retention uses this figure to justify a budget increase for the program.

Using concepts from our chapter, explain why "members buy more than non-members" is not reliable evidence that the program caused the additional purchases. Name the two effects that contaminate the comparison and state what type of evaluation would produce an unbiased estimate of the program's causal effect.

Sample answer: The comparison between members and non-members reflects two effects that are not the program's causal effect. The first is a selection effect: customers who join a loyalty program are already heavier buyers. They enrolled because they intended to continue buying frequently, not because the program converted them into heavier buyers. The membership pool is self-selected from the upper portion of the base, so comparing them to the full non-member population is comparing two structurally different groups.

The second is buyer moderation: a base tends to revert toward its long-run mean between periods. The heaviest buyers at the time of enrollment buy somewhat less in the next period independent of any marketing decision, and the lightest buyers buy somewhat more. The chapter names this the law of buyer moderation. This means the loyalty program may be taking credit for purchases that would have happened anyway as the top buyers moderated toward their long-run level.

The evaluation that produces an unbiased causal estimate is an experimental holdout: randomly assign eligible customers to a treatment group that receives the loyalty program and a matched control group that does not. The difference in purchasing behavior between the two randomly assigned groups, after the program runs for a meaningful period, is the program's true lift. The chapter specifies that a working plan stratifies treatment by decile, expects baseline churn even when the loyalty program appears to perform well, and runs experimental holdouts rather than reading a members-buy-more comparison as a program effect.

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 24.8, LO 24.4

Tags: buyer moderation; selection effect; loyalty programs; holdout testing; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Analytical thinking

AI-2 — Classify Metrics with AI

A student pastes the following six metrics from a brand's monthly report into an AI assistant and asks it to classify each on the chapter's six-rung metrics hierarchy:

  2. Total impressions across all channels

  5. Average trust score from the quarterly customer survey (shown to predict renewal rate six months ahead)
  6. A model-generated forecast of next-quarter renewal rate based on current cohort behavior

The AI responds that metrics 1, 2, and 3 are "efficiency metrics" and 4, 5, and 6 are "effectiveness metrics." A classmate says this response is incomplete because it does not distinguish among the efficiency rungs or among the effectiveness rungs.

Which of the following responses most accurately completes the AI's classification?

A) Efficiency metrics: 1 (activity), 2 (output), 3 (operational); Effectiveness metrics: 4 (outcome), 5 (leading indicator), 6 (predictive). The classmate is correct that the AI collapsed two distinct layers on each side. B) All six are operational metrics differing only in unit of measurement. C) Metrics 1-4 are lagging indicators and 5-6 are leading indicators, which is a complete classification. D) Efficiency metrics: 1, 2, 3, 4; Effectiveness metrics: 5, 6. The four efficiency rungs include outcomes because outcomes reflect past spending. E) The classification is impossible without knowing the brand's goal-layer number.

Answer: A

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 24.6 — The chapter's six-rung metrics ladder assigns: activity (posts published), output (impressions), operational (cost per click), outcome (new subscribers), leading indicator (trust score that predicts renewal), and predictive (model forecast). The AI's two-bucket efficiency/effectiveness split is correct in direction but collapses the detail that matters for planning decisions. Each rung implies a different intervention and a different cadence.

AI-3 — Draft-My-Plan and the One-Page Plan Document

A student uses an AI assistant to generate a one-page marketing plan for a fictional direct-to-consumer tea brand targeting health-conscious millennial women. The AI produces a document with the following sections: Brand Overview, Target Audience, Social Media Strategy, Budget Breakdown, and KPIs.

A classmate compares this output to the chapter's seven-row plan template and identifies two critical omissions that would make the plan a "budget-plus-calendar collapse wearing a template." Which of the following correctly names both omissions?

A) The plan lacks a mission statement and a vision statement. B) The plan lacks a product-market objective with a measurable number and date (row 3) and a positioning statement that serves as the hinge between strategy and tactics (row 4). C) The plan lacks a competitive analysis section and a SWOT grid. D) The plan lacks a customer acquisition cost figure and a retention-rate benchmark. E) The plan lacks both a brand identity row and a budget row, which are rows 1 and 7.

Answer: B

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 24.2, LO 24.9 — The chapter states that a plan whose objective row has no number or whose positioning row is blank is the budget-plus-calendar collapse wearing a template. The AI output has tactics (social media strategy), a budget, and KPIs, but without a measurable product-market objective with a date (the row the rest of the plan is sized against) and a positioning statement (the hinge between the strategic and tactical rows), the plan cannot close the loop. The chapter is specific: writing the seven rows in order is what stops the collapse.

AI-4 — Attribution Audit with AI

An e-commerce brand connects its Google Ads, Meta Ads, and email platform to an AI agent through MCP data connectors. The agent runs an attribution audit and returns the following finding:

"Under your current last-click setup, Google Search receives 61 percent of attributed revenue. Running the same conversion events through a marketing-mix model using 18 months of weekly data, Google Search accounts for 38 percent of the lift, while Meta Brand Awareness receives 27 percent (currently credited with 4 percent under last-click) and email receives 19 percent (currently credited with 8 percent)."

Based on this finding and our chapter's attribution framework, which channel is most likely being systematically undervalued by the current setup, and what does the chapter recommend the brand do with this information?

A) Google Search is undervalued; the brand should double its paid-search budget based on the MMM coefficient. B) Meta Brand Awareness is most likely undervalued, because last-click cannot see the upper-funnel work that brand awareness channels produce; the brand should use the MMM finding to reconsider its budget allocation, run an incrementality test on Meta Brand Awareness as the cross-check, and treat the gap between last-click and MMM as a flag for reallocation. C) Email is undervalued; the brand should eliminate Meta Brand Awareness and shift its budget entirely to email and search. D) The 38 percent MMM estimate for Google Search is unreliable because MMM requires at least three years of data to produce stable coefficients. E) The finding is inconclusive because the two methods use different base periods and cannot be compared directly.

Answer: B

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 24.7, LO 24.9 — The chapter states that last-click over-credits bottom-funnel channels (paid search, retargeting) that merely close customers the upper-funnel channels produced. Meta Brand Awareness, as a brand-building channel that generates awareness but rarely produces the final click, is the classic victim of this bias: credited with 4 percent under last-click but assigned 27 percent by the MMM model. The recommended approach is to make MMM the budget spine, use the new allocation as the starting point for reallocation, and run an incrementality test on the highest-spend channel as a causal cross-check. The chapter does not recommend eliminating a channel based on a single method's output alone.

Tags: attribution audit; MMM vs last-click; AI agent; MCP; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

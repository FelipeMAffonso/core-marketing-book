Chapter 14 Mobile and App Marketing — Test Bank

Chapter: 14 — Mobile and App Marketing Source note: Original authoring. No Kotler MM16 parent exists for this chapter. Every item is authored directly from OUR chapter 14 (ground truth: book/v7/chapter-14-mobile-app.html). Stems, scenarios, and answer rationales trace back to the chapter's frameworks, named concepts, running examples (Duolingo, Stanley, OSU senior's flashcard app), and AI-era material. No item is adapted from Kotler or any other publisher's bank.

Learning Objectives (Ch14, Our Chapter)

  • LO 14.1 Explain why mobile is its own marketing surface and how the five-stage loop (found, installed, opened, retained, re-monetized) differs from web marketing.
  • LO 14.2 Describe the two-number unit economics of a mobile business (LTV minus CPI) and explain how retention connects the two.
  • LO 14.3 Explain how App Store Optimization (ASO) works as two mechanically separate jobs — keyword indexing and conversion-rate optimization — and apply the three-axis keyword-research framework.
  • LO 14.4 Describe the engagement surface (push notifications, onboarding, paywall) and identify the decisions that win or lose retention after the install.
  • LO 14.5 Explain why Apple's App Tracking Transparency broke deterministic attribution in 2021, and describe how brands measure installs now using MMPs, postbacks, and media mix modeling.
  • LO 14.6 Map the paid-acquisition mix from highest-intent channels (Apple Search Ads, Google App Campaigns) to lower-intent channels (Meta, TikTok, programmatic) and explain the logic of that ordering.
  • LO 14.7 Identify how AI assistants accelerate mobile-growth work across keyword triage, screenshot critique, push-copy generation, and MCP-connected audits.
  • LO 14.8 Describe the no-code and coded paths to shipping a working app and explain the store-commission structure (15/30 percent) that sets the LTV ceiling.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 14.1 — The chapter opens with this exact case. The Duolingo PM's lesson is that two-thirds of new learners arrived through the App Store listing, not the website the team had optimized for a decade.

Item 2

The chapter defines mobile marketing as the coordinated set of decisions about how a product is _______.

Item 3

A flashcard app pays $3 per install and earns $9 of lifetime value from a $4.99 monthly subscription that lasts on average two months. Retention then slips and the average subscription lasts only one month. What happens to the unit economics?

A) The LTV-to-CPI ratio rises to 4.5-to-1 because the subscriber pays more per month to compensate. B) The ratio holds at 3-to-1 because the subscription price is unchanged. C) The ratio falls from 3-to-1 to 1.5-to-1, converting a profitable campaign into a break-even one. D) The ratio falls to zero because the install never recouped its cost. E) The ratio is unaffected because LTV is calculated over the total lifetime, not a single month.

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 14.2 — The chapter's worked case holds CPI at $3. When subscription length drops from two months to one, LTV falls from $9 to $4.50, cutting the ratio from 3-to-1 to 1.5-to-1. The lesson is that retention, not ad price, is the dial that decides the business.

Tags: LTV; CPI; unit economics; retention; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 5

App Store Optimization (ASO) has two mechanically separate jobs. The first gets the listing to appear when a buyer searches a relevant term. The second turns a buyer who has already landed on the listing into an installer. Which pair correctly names the two jobs?

A) Paid search and organic traffic B) Keyword indexing and conversion-rate optimization C) Discovery and monetization D) Impressions and click-throughs E) Title metadata and review management

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 14.3 — The chapter names the two ASO jobs explicitly: keyword indexing (visibility) and conversion-rate optimization (the screenshots and ratings that turn viewers into installs). The most common mistake is optimizing one and ignoring the other.

Tags: ASO; keyword indexing; conversion-rate optimization; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 6

An OSU senior is researching keywords for her flashcard app. The term "study tools" has high traffic, high difficulty, and high relevance. The term "flashcards for organic chemistry" has low traffic, low difficulty, and high relevance. The term "workout tracker" has high traffic, low difficulty, and zero relevance. According to the chapter's three-axis keyword model, which term is the best asymmetric bet for her early organic ASO program?

A) "study tools," because high traffic maximizes impressions B) "workout tracker," because low difficulty means she can rank without paid support C) "flashcards for organic chemistry," because low difficulty and high relevance together produce installs that stay D) Any of the three, because early apps should test all keywords before narrowing down E) "study tools" combined with "workout tracker," to maximize coverage

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 14.3 — The chapter says the asymmetric bet is a term with enough traffic to matter, low enough difficulty to rank without paid support, and tight enough relevance that the installs actually stay. A high-traffic irrelevant term wins installs that churn on day one; "workout tracker" is exactly that trap. The long-tail relevant term is the bet an early app can win.

Tags: three-axis keyword model; traffic, difficulty, relevance; ASO; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 7

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 14.3 — The chapter's ASO weight figure shows title at roughly 30 percent, subtitle at 25 percent, hidden keyword field at 20 percent, and description at 15 percent, with the remaining 10 percent in developer name, URL, and in-app purchases.

Tags: ASO index weights; title; keyword field; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Difficulty: 1

Tags: push notifications; opt-in timing; post-delight prompt; Duolingo; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 9

A push notification campaign broadcasts "Come back to Lingua!" to every installed user regardless of where they are in their journey. According to the chapter, a generic broadcast like this converts at approximately _______ the rate of a message written for a specific cohort.

Objective: LO 14.4 — The chapter states a generic message broadcast to everyone converts at about a third the rate of a message written for a specific cohort. The fix is distinct copy per cohort, such as new installs in the first 48 hours, lapsed seven-day users, paying subscribers, and power users.

Item 10

The chapter describes three paywall placements and their typical conversion outcomes. Ranked from lowest to highest conversion, which order is correct?

A) After onboarding, at-limit, at first open B) At first open, at-limit, after onboarding C) At first open, after onboarding, at-limit D) At-limit, at first open, after onboarding E) All three placements convert at the same rate; position does not matter

Difficulty: 2

Item 11

Apple released iOS 14.5 in April 2021 with a framework that permanently changed mobile measurement. The framework _______.

A) required every app to display a full privacy policy before download B) mandated explicit user opt-in before an app could track the user across other companies' apps and websites, cratering iOS cross-app opt-in to roughly 25 percent C) forced ad networks to share click data directly with brands rather than through third-party measurement partners D) introduced a mandatory 72-hour delay between an install and the first push notification E) banned all retargeting of users who had previously uninstalled an app

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 14.5 — App Tracking Transparency (ATT) required apps to ask explicit permission before tracking users across other apps. Cross-app opt-in settled at roughly 25 percent in the United States, meaning three of four iOS users are effectively without an IDFA from the brand's perspective.

Tags: App Tracking Transparency; ATT; iOS 14.5; privacy; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 12

A) 0 percent, because ATT made all tracking illegal B) 50 percent, because ATT is opt-out and most users leave the default C) 75 percent, because only users who actively deny permission are hidden D) Roughly 25 percent, the share who tapped "Allow" when the prompt appeared E) 100 percent, because the MMP works around ATT using fingerprinting

Objective: LO 14.5 — The chapter states roughly 25 percent of iOS users tapped "Allow," which is the share still trackable deterministically. Section II — Scenario / Short-Answer Items (4 items)

SA-1

An OSU student ships a hydration-tracking app in week three of the semester. She prices it at $3.99 per month and acquires the first 400 installs through a mix of Apple Search Ads ($2.80 average CPI) and word-of-mouth (zero paid cost). Her day-30 retention rate is 12 percent, and paying subscribers stay for an average of 2.5 months. The platform commission is 15 percent because her revenue is well under $1 million.

(a) Calculate the lifetime value per install (assume all installs eventually subscribe, for simplicity). (b) Calculate the blended CPI across the 400 installs, given that 200 came from paid search and 200 came from word-of-mouth. (c) Is the unit economics sustainable? State the LTV-to-CPI ratio and interpret it using the chapter's benchmark.

Sample answer:

(a) LTV per install. The $3.99 subscription nets the developer $3.99 x 0.85 = $3.39 per month at the 15 percent Small Business Program rate. Average subscription length is 2.5 months, so LTV = $3.39 x 2.5 = $8.48 per install (rounded).

(b) Blended CPI. Paid installs cost $2.80 each (200 x $2.80 = $560 total). Word-of-mouth installs cost $0 each. Total ad spend = $560 across 400 installs. Blended CPI = $560 / 400 = $1.40 per install.

(c) LTV-to-CPI ratio = $8.48 / $1.40 = approximately 6.1 to 1. The chapter's benchmark says a healthy consumer-app business runs 2.5 to 4 times CPI and anything below 1.5 times is a unit-economics problem. At 6.1-to-1 the app is comfortably above the healthy range, largely because the word-of-mouth channel cuts the blended CPI in half. If word-of-mouth dries up and the student scales to pure paid acquisition at $2.80 CPI, the ratio falls to $8.48 / $2.80 = 3.0 to 1, which is still healthy but tighter.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 14.2, LO 14.8

Tags: LTV; CPI; unit economics; Small Business Program; commission arithmetic; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

SA-2

A subscription fitness app currently asks users for push permission at the very first screen after install (first open, before any workout has been completed). The team is debating moving the prompt to one of two alternative moments: immediately after the user completes their first workout, or the moment the user's free-tier daily workout limit is hit for the first time.

Using the chapter's push-permission timing principle and the paywall-placement evidence, recommend which moment the team should choose and explain why. Then identify one additional lever from the chapter's three-lever push framework that the team should address once opt-in rate is fixed.

Sample answer:

The first-open prompt is the weakest of the three options because the user has done nothing inside the app yet and has no reason to trust the brand with their lock screen. Both alternatives are better. The post-first-workout moment is closest to the chapter's "post-delight" principle, because the user has just experienced value and "would you like reminders to keep going?" reads as a favor rather than an interruption. That framing is why Duolingo waits for a seven-day streak and earns an 18-point lift over the first-open prompt. The at-limit moment is typically the trigger the chapter recommends for the paywall (where it converts three to four times the first-open rate), not specifically for the permission prompt, but it also represents a moment of felt pain, which could work. On balance, post-first-workout is the cleaner choice because it is the permission equivalent of the post-delight principle; the at-limit moment risks pairing two friction points (the limit screen and the permission prompt) at once. Once opt-in rate is fixed, the second lever to address is segmentation: a generic "Come back to your workout!" broadcast converts at about a third the rate of a message written for a named cohort (new installer, lapsed user, paying subscriber), so writing distinct copy per cohort is the next highest-impact move. The third lever is the frequency cap, which the chapter places at three to five pushes per week; above roughly seven, unsubscribes climb.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 14.4

Tags: push notification; opt-in timing; segmentation; frequency cap; paywall; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Reflective thinking

SA-3

Stanley, the drinkware brand, has historically relied on retail placement and search advertising for growth. In 2026, the brand is launching a Stanley Care app that lets owners register products, access care guides, and shop Stanley accessories directly. The head of e-commerce argues the app growth team should start with Meta before touching Apple Search Ads, because "Meta has a bigger audience and better targeting data." The head of mobile disagrees.

Using the chapter's paid-acquisition logic (the intent ladder), explain who is right and why. Then name the two specific performance benchmarks the chapter cites for Apple Search Ads that support the correct position.

Sample answer:

The head of mobile is correct. The chapter's intent-ladder logic says the growth team should buy first where the buyer is already shopping, then layer lower-intent channels on top once the in-store auction saturates. A buyer searching the App Store for "Stanley" or "drinkware care" has already decided they want this type of app, so their intent is higher than an Instagram scroller who has not yet expressed that need. The install produced by App Store intent is worth more per dollar precisely because the buyer is already in the right room. Meta should layer on top once the Apple Search Ads auction saturates, using lookalike audiences built from the highest-LTV Stanley owners, because that is Meta's documented strength (targeting depth). The two benchmarks the chapter cites for Apple Search Ads: the tap-through rate runs 4 to 9 percent, far above the roughly 0.5 percent of a web banner, and about 2 percent of users who see the ad ultimately install the app, at a cost per install near four dollars in the United States (higher on more competitive keywords).

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 14.6

Tags: paid acquisition; intent ladder; Apple Search Ads; Meta; tap-through rate; CPI benchmark; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Reflective thinking

SA-4

A brand manager asks why the chapter spends a full section on "shipping an app" in a marketing course, rather than leaving that to computer science. She says: "That is an engineering task, not a marketing decision."

Drawing on the chapter's argument for why shipping an app matters to a marketer, write a two-to-three-paragraph response that also names the two specific platform fees the chapter cites and the two AI development tools the chapter identifies for the coded path.

Sample answer:

The chapter argues that the marketer who has shipped an app reads every benchmark in the chapter differently, because the install graph, the retention curve, and the review queue stop being abstractions and become a system she has operated from the inside. That judgment is not transferable from a textbook or a colleague's description; it comes from having watched a real retention curve form in a real dashboard. A marketer who has never shipped an app can still use the chapter's frameworks, but one who has built the thing can spot an onboarding problem in a day-7 retention drop rather than needing an analyst to name it. In that sense shipping is not the deliverable; the judgment it builds is.

The two platform fees the chapter names are the $99 per year Apple Developer Program membership and the one-time $25 Google Play Console registration, for a total of roughly $124 in store fees plus the cost of a builder subscription. The chapter's point is that a working app in front of real users is now a weekend-scale project for a marketer, not a months-long engineering engagement.

On the coded path the chapter identifies Cursor, an AI-augmented code editor built on Visual Studio Code where the marketer confirms each suggestion at line level, and Claude Code, Anthropic's terminal-first coding agent where the marketer names an outcome and the agent does the multi-file work and reports back. The distinction matters because neither tool requires the marketer to know how to write code; the chapter explicitly says the bottleneck across the weekend is decision-making rather than coding throughput.

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 14.1, LO 14.8

Tags: shipping an app; App Store Connect; Apple Developer Program; Google Play Console; Cursor; Claude Code; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Written communication

Section III — AI-Integration Items (4 items)

These items are original to this course. They test students' ability to evaluate AI-generated mobile-growth analysis, apply the chapter's frameworks to AI-era measurement constraints, and understand the four-tier AI-cadence the chapter teaches in Section 14.6.

AI-1 — Evaluate AI Keyword-Research Output

A student asks an AI assistant to find keywords for her fitness tracking app. The AI responds:

"Here are five keywords I recommend for your App Store listing: 'workout,' 'fitness,' 'exercise,' 'health,' and 'gym.' These are the most popular terms in the fitness category and will maximize your impressions."

Using the chapter's three-axis keyword-research model (traffic, difficulty, relevance), identify the specific failure in this AI output and explain what the student should ask instead to get a more actionable answer.

A) The output is correct. High-traffic terms in the relevant category are exactly what the listing needs to maximize visibility. B) The output optimizes for traffic but ignores difficulty. All five terms are head keywords with near-ceiling difficulty, so a new app cannot rank for any of them without significant paid support, and the real gain is zero installs at high cost. C) The output optimizes for relevance but ignores traffic. The terms are too specific and will produce very few impressions. D) The output is fine for Android (Google Play) but fails on iOS, because the iOS hidden keyword field does not index these common terms. E) The output fails only because it does not include the exact brand name, which is the highest-weight field in the App Store index.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 14.3, LO 14.7 — The chapter's three-axis model scores every candidate on traffic, difficulty, AND relevance simultaneously. The AI output focuses only on traffic and relevance while completely ignoring difficulty. "Workout" and "fitness" are dominated by category leaders with hundreds of thousands of reviews, so the difficulty is near the ceiling and a new app cannot rank without paid acquisition behind every term. The correct follow-up question is: "Propose ten candidate keywords for this fitness tracking app, score each from 0 to 100 on traffic, difficulty, and relevance, sort by traffic divided by difficulty among only the high-relevance rows, and name the three I should actually target and why."

AI-2 — Interpret a Post-ATT Measurement Report

A growth team's AI-connected MMP dashboard shows this summary for the past week:

"iOS installs: 3,200. Deterministic attribution: 812 installs (25.4 percent). SKAdNetwork postbacks: 2,388 installs (74.6 percent). Average CPI deterministic: $3.10. Estimated CPI from media mix model: $4.20."

A junior analyst says: "The MMM number is higher, which means the media mix model is overestimating our costs. We should rely only on the deterministic CPI of $3.10."

Identify the flaw in the analyst's reasoning, using the chapter's explanation of post-ATT measurement.

A) The analyst is correct. The deterministic number is always more accurate than a model estimate. B) The analyst is wrong. The deterministic $3.10 covers only the 25 percent of installs that opted into tracking. That group may be systematically different from the remaining 75 percent of users who denied tracking. The MMM estimate of $4.20 reflects a population-level average across all installs and is likely the more representative number for decision-making. C) The analyst is wrong because SKAdNetwork is more accurate than deterministic attribution in 2026. D) The analyst is correct, but only for iOS campaigns; the deterministic figure should not be used for Android. E) Both numbers are equally valid; the team should average them.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 14.5, LO 14.7 — Post-ATT, deterministic attribution covers only the roughly 25 percent who tapped "Allow." That group may systematically skew toward more privacy-comfortable or more engaged users. The MMM estimate uses aggregate data across all installs and is the framework the chapter says the "brands that kept growing" learned to run alongside deterministic. A brand that optimizes only on the deterministic 25 percent will misallocate budget.

AI-3 — Evaluate an AI-Generated Push Notification

A brand asks an AI to write a push notification for users who installed a Spanish-learning app, finished their first lesson, and have not opened the app in seven days. The AI produces two options:

Option A (cheap model brief): "Your Spanish lessons are waiting! Come back and keep learning today."

Option B (detailed brief with cohort context): "You finished Lesson 1 last Tuesday, your streak is still alive. Lesson 2 takes four minutes — pick it up before it breaks."

Which option better applies the chapter's push-personalization principle, and what does the difference reveal about prompt quality?

A) Option A is better because it is shorter and more likely to fit the iOS notification character limit. B) Option B is better because it names what the user did, which the chapter says earns the open a generic broadcast loses. The difference reveals that output quality is downstream of question quality, the same discipline Chapter 1 introduced. C) Both options are equivalent because the same AI produced them; model capability drives the difference, not the brief. D) Option A is better for retention because emotional urgency ("waiting!") outperforms factual framing. E) Option B would violate GDPR by referencing user behavior without explicit consent.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 14.4, LO 14.7 — The chapter states that a push that names what the user did earns the open a broadcast loses, and that campaign after campaign the gap accumulates into the difference between a retention curve that holds and one that crumbles. The chapter also explicitly teaches that output quality is downstream of question quality, citing the vague-vs-sharp comparison in Widget 14.6. Option B embeds the cohort context (finished Lesson 1, streak still alive) that the chapter says is required for the message to read as relevant.

Tags: push personalization; cohort messaging; question quality; AI push copy; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Written communication

AI-4 — Apply the Four-Tier AI Cadence

A mobile growth manager currently asks a general-purpose AI "what should I check in my app's store listing this week?" every Monday morning. The AI returns a generic checklist of ASO best practices. She wants to get from that generic answer to an answer that pulls her app's live install counts and retention curves from her MMP without any copy-paste step.

According to the chapter's four-tier AI cadence (Section 14.6), which tier is she currently using, and which two steps must she take in sequence to reach the live-data audit?

A) She is using Tier 2 (Skill). She must first upgrade to Tier 4 (scheduled agent), then add the MCP connection. B) She is currently at Tier 1 (chat search with a generic question). She must first move to Tier 2 (package the recurring question as a Skill so the context and format are preserved), then move to Tier 3 (connect the MMP through MCP so the Skill pulls live install and retention data with no copy-paste). C) She is using Tier 3 (MCP). She must add a Skill wrapper and then schedule the agent to run weekly. D) She is at Tier 1. She can skip directly to Tier 4 (scheduled agent) without building a Skill or MCP connection. E) The four tiers apply only to social media analytics; mobile measurement partners require a separate integration framework not covered in the chapter.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 14.7 — The chapter maps the four tiers explicitly: Tier 1 is built-in chat search (generic question, generic answer); Tier 2 packages the recurring question as a Skill so the context and format are preserved across runs; Tier 3 connects the measurement partner through MCP so the Skill pulls live numbers without copy-paste; Tier 4 schedules an agent to run the audit weekly and post the diff to a team channel. The correct path is sequential: Tier 1 to Tier 2, then Tier 2 to Tier 3.

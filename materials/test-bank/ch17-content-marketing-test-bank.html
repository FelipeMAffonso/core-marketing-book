Chapter 17 Content Marketing Strategy — Test Bank

Chapter: 17 — Content Marketing Strategy Source note: Original authoring. No Kotler MM16 parent exists for this chapter. Every item is authored from scratch against our Chapter 17 ground truth (book/v7/chapter-17-content-marketing.html). Items reflect our learning objectives, our framework names (pillar/cluster architecture, the content operating system, BADASS, repurposing cascade, TOFU/MOFU/BOFU, the AI-era allocation shift, the Magic Words headline levers, SUCCESs Unexpected and Curse of Knowledge, and the long-form application of STEPPS), and our running example (insulated drinkware, Patagonia, Duolingo, Liquid Death, CMI). STEPPS coordination note: STEPPS is taught canonically in Chapter 19 (Social Media). Chapter 17 applies the six levers to long-form content and owns the content operating system / BADASS material. Items here test the long-form application (scoring a pillar post, fixing the two weakest sections); items that define the six levers individually belong to the Chapter 19 bank. Export format: Mirrors Kotler markdown schema (stem, A…E options, Answer, Diff, Objective, Tags) for QTI / Canvas Common Cartridge conversion.

Learning Objectives (Ch17, Our Chapter)

  • LO 17.1 Explain why content marketing is an asset, not a campaign, and calculate the inbound cost and close-rate advantage on the correct measurement horizon.
  • LO 17.2 Describe the pillar-and-cluster architecture, explain how bidirectional internal linking earns topical authority with both classical search engines and 2026 generative-engine retrievers, and apply product-led SEO.
  • LO 17.3 Build an editorial calendar using quarterly themes, a realistic publishing cadence, and batch production economics.
  • LO 17.4 Apply the repurposing cascade to turn one long-form piece into five channel-native derivatives, respecting the native format of each platform.
  • LO 17.5 Distinguish TOFU, MOFU, and BOFU content types, explain how AI Overviews are shifting the classical sixty-thirty-ten budget split, and select the right gating threshold for B2B content.
  • LO 17.6 Apply the BADASS delivery system and the long-form STEPPS application (scoring a pillar post on six levers, fixing the two weakest) to diagnose and improve a piece's distribution potential.
  • LO 17.7 Write a more effective headline by applying the six Magic Words levers (concreteness, identity and agency, confidence, a question, emotion, similarity or difference) and the Ogilvy specifics-over-generalities principle.
  • LO 17.8 Distinguish vanity metrics from business metrics, explain the attribution-lag and asset-compounding-curve timelines, and identify the confounder that distorts content-ROI comparisons.

Section I — Multiple-Choice Items (20 items)

Item 1

The Content Marketing Institute spent eleven years publishing weekly research, a benchmarks report, a podcast, and a conference before media holding company UBM acquired it in 2016. UBM's primary purchase was _______.

A) the CMI brand name and domain authority B) the back catalogue of past issues and podcast episodes C) the permission-based audience: the email list, the speaker contacts, and the sponsor relationships D) the content-marketing software CMI had developed E) the eleven-year publishing archive as a search-ranking asset

Answer: C

Diff: 1

Objective: LO 17.1 — Content marketing builds a permission-based audience that returns on a schedule. The buyer paid for the audience, not any software or archive in isolation.

Tags: content as asset; CMI; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 2

A content manager at a mid-market B2B software firm reports that inbound-sourced leads cost about $39 per lead while outbound-sourced leads cost $100. She also notes that inbound leads close at 14.6 percent versus 1.7 percent for outbound. Her director says he sees none of those advantages in the monthly reporting. The most likely explanation is _______.

A) the firm is using the wrong lead-scoring model B) the inbound cost and close-rate advantages require roughly twelve to eighteen months for search rankings to mature before they become a steady-state result C) the inbound and outbound programs are generating leads from the same audience segment D) the content the firm publishes is not original enough to earn search ranking E) the director's CRM is attributing inbound leads to the wrong source

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 17.1 — The inbound advantage emerges on a twelve-to-eighteen-month horizon when search rankings have matured and clusters are linking to the pillar. A thirty-day read sees only the first-month number and misreads it as failure.

Tags: inbound marketing; attribution lag; measurement horizon; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 3

A content team builds one long 7,000-word reference page on insulated drinkware mechanics, then publishes twenty narrower articles each covering one sub-topic (vacuum versus single-wall construction, 18/8 versus 304 stainless, cup-holder fit in standard vehicles). Every short article links back to the long page, and the long page links out to all twenty. The primary purpose of that bidirectional linking pattern is to _______.

A) reduce the server load by spreading the content across many pages instead of one B) ensure every cluster article ranks independently on its own sub-topic query C) signal to a search engine and to a 2026 generative-engine retriever that the site has a coherent body of work on the broader topic, earning topical authority D) allow the brand to gate each cluster article separately and collect twenty separate lead-capture forms E) prevent any single page from becoming too long for mobile readers

Answer: C

Diff: 1

Objective: LO 17.2 — The bidirectional link structure, every cluster linking to the pillar and the pillar linking back to every cluster, is what a search engine reads as topical authority. A 2026 retrieval-augmented-generation system reads the same structure when deciding which sites to cite in an AI Overview.

Tags: pillar-and-cluster architecture; topical authority; SEO; generative-engine retrieval; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 4

Stripe's developer documentation targets specific developer queries like "how do I issue a refund" and ranks on Google for those queries. The same page also shows the Stripe API solving that exact problem in copy-paste code. This illustrates _______.

A) gated B2B content, where the developer must register before accessing the solution B) product-led SEO, where each page both ranks for a search query and demonstrates the product solving the underlying job C) the 95-5 rule applied to developer audiences D) the repurposing cascade, where the same page serves multiple channel formats E) earned media, where Stripe's documentation is independently cited by reviewers

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 17.2 — Product-led SEO (Eli Schwartz) means each page targets a real query and also demonstrates the product solving that query, so the page ranks and converts in the same place.

Tags: product-led SEO; Stripe; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 5

A quarterly theme on the editorial calendar is best described as _______.

A) a list of keywords the team plans to target in that quarter's cluster pages B) a single-paragraph statement of what the next twelve weeks of content is collectively trying to teach the audience, specific enough to let the team say no to out-of-scope requests C) the publishing schedule showing which pieces ship each week D) a budget allocation for the quarter's paid amplification spend E) a brand-safety checklist run before any piece is published

Answer: B

Diff: 1

Objective: LO 17.3 — The quarterly theme is the boundary that gives the team language to decline ad-hoc requests. Without it every request looks equally plausible and the team ships whatever the loudest stakeholder asked for that week.

Tags: editorial calendar; quarterly theme; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 6

Patagonia's journal The Cleanest Line has published two to four posts per week for nineteen years, while a junior marketer's Substack commits to one essay every two weeks. According to the chapter, the deciding factor when setting a publishing cadence is _______.

A) the frequency at which the target audience expects new content B) the cadence that would be ideal in a fully-staffed world C) the frequency the team can hold for at least a year without burning out or collapsing D) the minimum posting rate the platform's algorithm requires to maintain reach E) the publishing frequency of the brand's closest competitor

Answer: C

Diff: 1

Objective: LO 17.3 — The honest cadence is not the ideal-world rate but the rate the current team can sustain for at least a year. A sprint that collapses after six weeks reads to the audience as the operation having quit.

Tags: publishing cadence; editorial calendar; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 7

A one-person content team produces one piece per week by writing on Monday, editing on Wednesday, and publishing on Friday. In month four the team reports that the schedule feels unsustainable. According to the chapter, batch production would address this by _______.

A) outsourcing the editing step so the writer only has to draft the piece B) reducing the total word count of each piece so the writing time per piece falls C) booking one full production day to write and record four pieces at once, paying the setup cost one time for the output of four, then publishing one per week over the following month D) switching from a weekly cadence to a biweekly cadence so each piece gets more polish time E) using AI to draft the first version of each piece so the human time per piece drops by half

Answer: C

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 17.3 — Batch production inverts the per-piece setup cost. Paying that cost four separate times is what grinds the team down; booking one day and producing four pieces pays it once while the audience still experiences a weekly cadence.

Tags: batch production; editorial calendar; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 8

A brand manager writes a 3,000-word essay on insulated drinkware mechanics and publishes it on the brand blog. She then reposts the link with a one-line comment on LinkedIn, a copy-paste to Instagram, and the same text in the email newsletter. According to the chapter, the primary error is _______.

A) publishing on too many channels at the same time B) not gating the piece to capture leads before distributing it widely C) cross-posting the same content unchanged across channels instead of rewriting each derivative for its channel's native format D) failing to include a quarterly theme statement in the original piece E) publishing the piece on a blog before uploading it to social platforms

Answer: C

Diff: 1

Objective: LO 17.4 — Each derivative in the repurposing cascade must be rewritten for its channel's native format. A LinkedIn post is a 500-word excerpt rewritten for someone who will not click away; a reel is a 60-second sound-off format with captions. The same clip or text posted everywhere unchanged tracks the engagement floor of the platform it fits worst.

Tags: repurposing cascade; native format; channel strategy; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 9

Gary Vaynerchuk's content model books a 90-minute recording session in which the principal records a keynote or a long conversation. That single session is called the pillar capture, and afterward editors cut it into 30 to 60 shorter pieces. The primary economic rationale is _______.

A) each short piece can be gated separately to capture more leads than a single long piece would B) recording is the expensive part of the process, so paying for it once and then producing many cheap derivatives maximizes the return on the production investment C) the algorithm on every platform favors short-form content over long-form, so short pieces reach more people per dollar D) having 30 pieces published simultaneously signals to search engines that the site is actively updated E) one recording session avoids the scheduling complexity of multiple different production formats

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 17.4 — The pillar capture concentrates the costly step (the recording or writing session) into one block, then the cheap editing and formatting steps repeat for weeks. This is the same economics as batch production, applied to video and audio.

Tags: pillar capture; GaryVee content model; repurposing cascade; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 10

Duolingo's 14-million-follower TikTok account publishes 20-to-40-minute turnaround short posts because the framework is fixed: the same green owl character, voiced and costumed the same way, drops into a trending moment or sound. The chapter cites this as an illustration of _______.

A) the three-jabs-one-right-hook ratio, because Duolingo earns three free-value pieces before any paid post B) a distinctive asset cutting the per-piece production cost, because without the recurring character every post would have to reintroduce the brand from scratch C) the 95-5 rule, because the owl character reaches the 95 percent of buyers not currently in the learning-app market D) product-led SEO, because each TikTok post also demonstrates the Duolingo product E) gated content, because the owl content drives downloads to a paid app rather than serving a free audience

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 17.4 — A recurring distinctive asset (the Duolingo owl) reduces the per-piece cost across the entire content motion, because the character framework is already established, so each post only needs to drop the owl into a new moment rather than rebuild the brand identity each time.

Tags: distinctive asset; Duolingo; repurposing cascade; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 11

A B2B software company classifies its content by buyer journey stage. Which of the following best matches the TOFU, MOFU, and BOFU labels to their primary purpose and the content type that serves each?

A) TOFU: close the deal; ROI calculators and comparison guides. MOFU: help the shortlist; benchmark reports and case studies. BOFU: refresh memory; ungated essays and podcast episodes. B) TOFU: refresh memory in the 95 percent not in market; ungated essays, podcasts, and SEO blog clusters. MOFU: help the shortlist in the 5 percent considering; gated benchmark reports and framework templates. BOFU: close the in-market buyer; ROI calculators, comparison guides, and implementation documentation. C) TOFU: capture form fills; gated whitepapers. MOFU: build social following; short-form video. BOFU: drive brand awareness; thought-leadership essays. D) TOFU: generate paid traffic; sponsored posts and display ads. MOFU: capture organic traffic; SEO blog posts. BOFU: retain customers; email newsletters. E) TOFU: measured by close rate. MOFU: measured by unaided brand recall. BOFU: measured by social shares and page views.

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 17.5 — TOFU (awareness) refreshes memory in the out-of-market majority, measured by reach and recall. MOFU (consideration) helps the shortlisting minority, often through gated assets that capture leads. BOFU (decision) closes the in-market buyer with proof-of-value content.

Tags: TOFU; MOFU; BOFU; B2B content funnel; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 12

In 2026, the classical B2B content budget split, which allocated roughly 60 percent to awareness, 30 percent to consideration, and 10 percent to decision content, is shifting toward the middle and bottom of the funnel because _______.

A) B2B buyers are completing more of their journey on social media than on branded websites B) AI Overviews and generative-engine retrievers now answer many top-of-funnel awareness queries in-engine, so awareness pieces earn a citation rather than a click, while comparison and decision content the buyer still leaves the engine to read earns the durable traffic return C) awareness content is more expensive to produce than consideration content on a per-piece basis D) gating awareness content behind forms reduces its reach, making consideration content a more efficient use of budget E) the rise of short-form video has made awareness content easier to produce, so teams can achieve awareness goals with less budget

Answer: B

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 17.5 — The AI-era allocation shift flows directly from the AI Overview click-through compression: when the engine summarizes a topic at the top of the results, the awareness piece earns a brand mention rather than a visit, while decision-stage content the buyer actively seeks still earns the page visit and the conversion signal.

Tags: TOFU/MOFU/BOFU shift; AI Overviews; generative-engine retrieval; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 13

A B2B demand-generation team wants to gate every new piece of content behind an email form to hit its monthly form-submission target. The chapter's gating test says the team should gate only when _______.

A) the piece is longer than 2,000 words, because longer pieces carry more perceived value B) the piece answers a question that no other source on the open web answers for free, because the scarcity itself is what the visitor pays for C) the team has already published an ungated teaser version of the piece to warm up the audience D) the piece was produced in collaboration with a named industry expert whose audience it can tap E) the form is kept short, with no more than three fields, to minimize the abandonment rate

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 17.5 — Gating cuts reach by roughly two-thirds, so the lead-capture benefit only pays for that loss when the asset is genuinely scarce. The right test is not depth but scarcity: does this exist anywhere else for free?

Tags: gating vs ungating; B2B lead generation; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 14

The BADASS framework names six structural reasons a piece travels through the channels a brand arranges. Which of the following correctly identifies all six factors?

A) Blog, Automation, Data, Analytics, SEO, Syndication B) Brand recognition, Authority, Demographics, Advertising, Social reach, Shares C) Brand recognition, Alpha-audience targeting, Distribution time, Paid amplification, Shareability, Social proof D) Brand visibility, Audience size, Depth of content, Advertising budget, SEO ranking, Stakeholder endorsement E) Build quality, Audience relevance, Design, Algorithmic reach, Specificity, Style

Answer: C

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 17.6 — BADASS: Brand recognition (pre-read credibility), Alpha-audience targeting (the 5-10 percent who pass things on), Distribution time (the 80-20 flip, per piece), Paid amplification (boost only the top fifth), Shareability (engineered in during production), Social proof (a trusted endorsement in place before publication).

Tags: BADASS framework; distribution; content ignition; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 15

The chapter describes the "paid amplification" component of BADASS as extending only the top fifth of pieces. What signal should the team use to identify which fifth to amplify?

A) The piece's word count, because longer pieces typically earn higher lifetime traffic B) The engagement the piece earns in its first 72 hours on the brand's owned and earned channels, before any paid boost C) The quarterly theme alignment, because on-theme pieces earn higher organic performance D) The production cost of the piece, because higher-cost pieces require paid recovery of their investment E) The number of cluster articles it links to, because more links indicate higher topical authority

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 17.6 — The early organic signal from owned and earned channels (typically the first 72 hours) tells the team which pieces are actually resonating before money goes behind them. Amplifying anything before that read wastes budget on pieces the audience has already shown it does not want to pass along.

Tags: BADASS; paid amplification; distribution timing; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 16

A content team asks an AI to evaluate a 3,000-word pillar post on insulated drinkware using the six STEPPS principles. The model scores the piece 0-3 on each lever, identifies Practical Value (score 1) and Stories (score 0) as the two weakest, and rewrites the opening paragraphs of those two sections. According to the chapter, this AI move is most useful because _______.

A) the model's 0-3 scores are accurate measures of how often the piece will be shared on social media B) the model can rewrite the piece automatically so no human editing is needed before publishing C) the rubric converts a vague feeling that the piece is flat into two named levers the team can actually fix, turning a subjective editorial judgment into an actionable task D) the model detects whether the piece was AI-drafted and flags it for FTC disclosure compliance E) the model scores the piece against competitors' content to produce a relative ranking

Answer: C

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 17.6 — The STEPPS scorer is useful not because the score is a precise measurement but because it forces the model to commit a number to each principle, surfacing the two that are weakest and giving the team two concrete sections to rewrite rather than a vague instruction to "make it more shareable."

Tags: STEPPS long-form application; AI-assisted content; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 17

The chapter warns about the Curse of Knowledge as a reason why the people closest to a product often write the least shareable content. The Curse of Knowledge means _______.

A) a brand that has published for many years can no longer see its own content from a beginner's perspective B) once a person knows something deeply they can no longer easily imagine not knowing it, so their writing is dense with assumed context that a newcomer cannot follow C) AI-drafted content carries more assumed context than human-drafted content because the model was trained on expert sources D) brands in technical categories earn lower search rankings because their content uses vocabulary most buyers do not type into search engines E) long-form content performs worse than short-form because experts tend to write longer pieces

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 17.6 — The Curse of Knowledge (Heath and Heath, Made to Stick) explains why subject-matter experts write drafts newcomers cannot follow: expertise strips away the ability to imagine not-knowing, so the content fails the reader it was meant to reach. The chapter applies this to long-form content drafts and to the reason an outside read, or an AI score against a named rubric, catches what the author cannot.

Tags: Curse of Knowledge; SUCCESs; long-form content craft; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 18

A marketer is writing a headline for a pillar essay reporting that, under an eleven-hour real-desk cold-retention test, a $25 cup and a $50 cup differed by only 45 minutes of ice retention. Which headline lever does the chapter identify as the most reliable starting point, and which phrase in the following options best illustrates it?

A) The question lever: "Is your expensive insulated cup really worth the price?" B) The identity lever: "Be the commuter who tests claims before spending $50." C) The concreteness lever: "Your $50 insulated cup outlasts the $25 one by just 45 minutes in an 11-hour desk test." D) The emotion lever: "The truth about insulated drinkware will change how you shop forever." E) The confidence lever: "This is definitely the most honest insulated drinkware review you will find."

Answer: C

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 17.7 — The most reliable headline lever is concreteness, because specifics out-pull generalities. The headline in option C includes the real test duration (11 hours), the real price points ($50 and $25), and the real performance gap (45 minutes), all of which the Ogilvy specifics-beat-generalities test rewards.

Tags: Magic Words; headline craft; concreteness; Ogilvy; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 19

A brand content director reads a monthly dashboard showing 220,000 page views, 8,400 social shares, and a 34 percent email open rate. She presents these to the CMO as evidence the content program is working. The chapter argues this presentation is incomplete because _______.

A) the three numbers are too small to demonstrate a significant content marketing effect B) all three numbers measure surface behaviors rather than whether the brand stays in memory for the moment a buyer enters the market, and none of them is correlated with business outcomes the way unaided recall, share of category-entry-point, or content-influenced revenue is C) email open rates and social shares double-count the same audience D) page views include search-engine bot traffic, which inflates the number E) the open rate of 34 percent is below the industry average and reflects a content-quality problem

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 17.8 — Page views measure reach without memory, shares measure surface shareability without recall, and open rates measure subject-line performance. The slow business metrics (unaided recall, share of category-entry-point associations, content-influenced revenue) are the ones that actually move with the outcomes the brand cares about.

Tags: vanity metrics; business metrics; content measurement; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 20

A brand published a 5,000-word pillar essay in April. By May the piece had generated 1,800 organic visits. By the following March, eleven months later, it was generating 22,000 organic visits per month. The chapter's explanation for this pattern is _______.

A) the search engine penalizes new content for the first few months before granting full ranking B) the brand's paid amplification budget was higher in February and March than in the spring C) the asset-compounding curve: around month six the search-ranking ramp begins once the engine has weighted the piece's link structure, and by month eighteen the cluster pages link back to the pillar and traffic compounds further D) social-media shares of the piece grew exponentially through word-of-mouth referrals E) the brand refreshed the page content each month, and the algorithm rewards frequent updates

Answer: C

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 17.8 — The asset-compounding curve has three windows: the month-one launch spike from the existing audience, the month-six search-ranking ramp once the engine indexes the page and weighs its links, and the month-eighteen cluster-link compounding once the bidirectional structure is mature. The cost was paid in April; the return compounds for years.

Tags: asset-compounding curve; attribution lag; content ROI; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Section II — Scenario / Short-Answer Items (4 items)

SA-1

A junior at Oklahoma State wants to build a content portfolio for a marketing internship. She is choosing between two plans:

  • Plan A: Post on LinkedIn every day about general marketing topics, with no fixed theme, publishing whatever feels timely.
  • Plan B: Write one 4,000-word pillar essay on "How DTC (direct-to-consumer) drug advertising actually converts," host it on Substack, then publish one 1,000-word cluster post per week for the next ten weeks, each exploring one tactic named in the pillar.

Using the chapter's pillar-and-cluster architecture and the 95-5 rule, explain which plan is more likely to build the portfolio asset the recruiter is hiring for and why. Name at least two structural reasons Plan B outperforms Plan A.

Sample answer: Plan B applies the pillar-and-cluster architecture. The pillar essay is the long reference page that anchors the cluster articles, and the bidirectional linking, every cluster pointing back to the pillar and the pillar out to the clusters, is what a search engine and a 2026 AI retriever read as topical authority. Plan A produces volume without structure: no pillar anchors the content, no cluster links reinforce any theme, and the posts disappear down a feed rather than accumulating as a searchable body of work. Two structural reasons Plan B wins: (1) the pillar compounds in value over time as cluster pages link back to it, so the production cost paid once keeps earning, while daily LinkedIn posts earn nothing after a few days in the feed; (2) the cluster map covers the range of sub-topics a recruiter or a search engine would use to verify the author's knowledge, demonstrating depth, not just opinion. The 95-5 rule also applies: the recruiter who sees the Substack URL during an interview was almost certainly in the 95 percent who were not actively looking at marketing portfolios when the daily posts ran, so the recruiter finds the pillar only because it is still ranking and searchable, which is the advantage the permanent indexed record provides over the ephemeral daily post.

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 17.1, LO 17.2

Tags: pillar-and-cluster; personal brand; 95-5 rule; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Written communication

SA-2

A content team at a B2B analytics company has just published its best pillar essay of the year: a 6,000-word review of how mid-market CFOs actually read marketing-mix models. The essay was published on a Tuesday and received good organic engagement in the first 72 hours. The team's current weekly plan is to write the next piece.

Using the BADASS framework from the chapter, identify three specific actions the team should take before moving to the next piece, and for each action name the letter of BADASS it corresponds to. Then explain why the chapter describes this distribution emphasis as the top-quartile move.

Sample answer: Three BADASS actions before moving on: (1) Distribution time (D): the team should block the remaining time in the week to push the essay actively through the channels it can reach, rather than switching to writing. The chapter describes the 80-20 flip, where top-quartile teams spend a larger share on distribution than production for each piece, because a brand that becomes the place readers return to on a schedule has a moat paid ads cannot copy. (2) Paid amplification (A): the first-72-hour organic signal showed good engagement, so this piece qualifies for the top fifth the team promotes with paid budget. The team should boost the essay on LinkedIn and any relevant newsletters now, while the early momentum is real, rather than waiting until the next planning cycle. (3) Social proof (S): the team should identify two or three named CFOs or industry figures whose endorsement would carry weight with the target audience and route the essay to them now, with a personal note, before the launch window closes. A named endorsement in place at publication carries more lift than one bolted on after the essay has already aged. The chapter says top-quartile teams flip the 80-20 ratio because distributing a proven piece costs less per new reader than writing and producing a new piece from scratch, and the two-in-three touchpoints that consumer-driven channels carry can only be reached by designing for travel, not by publishing more.

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 17.6

Tags: BADASS; distribution; paid amplification; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Reflective thinking

SA-3

A brand manager at a regional grocery chain runs a quarterly content program. At the end of Q1 she presents the following dashboard to the VP of Marketing: organic visits up 18 percent quarter-over-quarter, social shares up 22 percent, and email open rate up from 28 percent to 31 percent. The VP asks, "Do you know whether any of this is driving more customers into our stores?"

Using the chapter's distinction between vanity metrics and business metrics, explain why none of the three numbers presented answers the VP's question. Then name the two business metrics the chapter identifies as the closest proxies for the outcome the VP is asking about, and explain what each measures.

Sample answer: The three dashboard numbers are vanity metrics. Organic visits measure reach without measuring memory, so a visit to a recipe article does not show whether the reader associated the brand with a category-entry point the next time she planned a grocery run. Social shares measure surface shareability without measuring recall, so a share of a holiday recipe reaches friends who might share it further but does not measure whether any of them later walked into the store because the brand came to mind at the right moment. Email open rates measure only that the subject line was clicked, not that the content built any mental availability or influenced a purchase. None of the three tracks whether the brand has stayed in memory for the moment a shopper decides where to buy. The two business metrics the chapter identifies as closest proxies are: (1) Unaided brand recall: a periodic survey asking in-category buyers to name a grocery brand without being shown a list. The share who name this brand, tracked quarter over quarter, is the direct measure of mental availability the content program is trying to build. (2) Share of category-entry-point associations: the share of buyers who name this brand first when they encounter the trigger that brings the grocery category to mind, such as planning a dinner or seeing a weekly ad. This drives shortlist inclusion more reliably than general recall.

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 17.8

Tags: vanity metrics; business metrics; unaided brand recall; category-entry-points; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Written communication

SA-4

A B2B marketing manager is planning content for Q3. She has a 30-week backlog of topic ideas but no quarterly theme. Her colleague argues the team should publish as many topics as possible to maximize coverage. The manager disagrees and wants to structure the quarter around one theme, one pillar piece, and twelve weekly cluster articles.

Using the chapter's three editorial-calendar concepts (quarterly theme, publishing cadence, and batch production), explain the manager's argument in your own words. Address each of the three concepts, and include in your answer the specific benefit the quarterly theme provides that no tool or calendar system can substitute for.

Sample answer: The manager is applying the three concepts that make a content calendar sustainable. The quarterly theme provides the benefit no tool can substitute for: the capacity to say no. When the team faces the constant stream of last-minute requests from sales, product, and leadership, a theme gives them language to decline, stating that a piece is interesting but falls outside the Q3 theme and goes to Q4 planning instead. Without a theme every request looks equally plausible and the team ships whatever the loudest stakeholder asked for. A 30-item backlog without a theme is a mood board, not a plan. The publishing cadence follows from the theme: once the boundary is set, the team commits to the rate it can hold for twelve months, not the ideal rate. One pillar plus twelve clusters is the top-quartile benchmark from the CMI 2026 report, but only if the team can actually hold it. The manager is right to pick a weekly cluster cadence if the team can sustain it through a busy quarter, because a sprint that collapses is worse than a slower rate held consistently. Batch production makes that cadence achievable: rather than writing one piece each week at a fresh setup cost each time, the team books two production days a month and produces six pieces each time, paying the setup cost twice instead of twelve times. The audience still experiences a weekly cadence; the team experiences a far lower production grind.

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 17.3

Tags: editorial calendar; quarterly theme; batch production; cadence; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Written communication

AI-1 — Diagnose a Vague AI Repurpose Request

A marketing coordinator pastes a 3,000-word pillar essay into an AI assistant and types: "Repurpose this for social."

The AI returns:

"Here are some ideas for your social channels: 1. Post a quote from the essay on Instagram. 2. Share the link on LinkedIn with a brief comment. 3. Tweet a key takeaway. 4. Mention the essay in your next newsletter."

According to the chapter's repurposing-cascade principles, this response fails on two counts. Which answer correctly names both failures?

A) The response fails because it does not include a TikTok derivative and because the word count of each derivative is not specified. B) The response fails because it recommends only four derivatives instead of five, and because it does not specify a posting schedule with dates. C) The response fails because it does not respect each channel's native format (cross-posting a link on LinkedIn and a link-share tweet are the same idea in different words), and because the output contains no actionable format difference between channels (an Instagram quote, a LinkedIn link, and a tweet are all the same cross-post pattern with no rewriting for each channel's native shape). D) The response fails because it recommends using the newsletter, which is an owned channel, before using earned and paid channels first. E) The response fails because the AI did not search the live web to find out which content format currently performs best on each platform.

Answer: C

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 17.4 — The vague question returns vague cross-posts. The cascade requires a rewrite for each channel's native format: a LinkedIn post is a 500-word excerpt for someone who will not click away, an X thread is a tweet-by-tweet story, and an Instagram reel is a 60-second sound-off format. The AI's response treats every channel as a different venue for the same link, which is the pattern the chapter explicitly identifies as tracking the lowest platform's engagement floor.

Tags: AI content cascade; vague vs sharp question; native format; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

AI-2 — Apply the Long-Form STEPPS Scorer

A content manager pastes this opening paragraph of a pillar post into an AI assistant and asks it to score the piece on the six STEPPS and rewrite the two weakest sections:

"Insulated drinkware is a growing product category with many options for consumers. This guide covers the key features to consider when purchasing an insulated cup, including material type, insulation method, and lid design. We hope this overview is helpful."

The model scores Practical Value at 1, Stories at 0, Social Currency at 0, Triggers at 1, Emotion at 0, and Public at 1. It identifies Stories and Social Currency as the two weakest levers and offers rewrites.

A student reads the two rewritten sections and says, "The model's score is probably wrong. The original paragraph is fine." According to the chapter's guidance on the long-form STEPPS scorer, which response most accurately describes how to use the model's output?

A) The student is right. A model cannot score creative content reliably, so the 0-3 numbers should be discarded. B) The student should trust the model's rewrite completely and publish it without editing, since the model applied the rubric correctly. C) The model's score is useful as a second-reader diagnostic, not a precise measurement. The 0s in Stories and Social Currency name two specific levers the opening paragraph is not earning, and the rewrites show one way to feed them. The manager should read the rewrites critically, decide whether the revisions fit the brand voice and the reader's expectations, and edit them before publishing. D) The score is only useful if the model also identifies which of the six levers is strongest, so the manager knows what to protect while fixing the weak ones. E) The model should have been asked to compare the paragraph against competitors' content before scoring it, so the score reflects relative performance rather than an absolute rubric.

Answer: C

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 17.6 — The STEPPS scorer converts a vague editorial feeling into two named levers. The scores are the model's judgment, not a measurement, and the rewrites are a starting draft, not a finished piece. The manager reads the diagnosis, decides whether the named weaknesses are real, and edits the rewritten sections before publishing.

Tags: AI STEPPS scorer; long-form content; vague vs sharp AI question; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Reflective thinking

AI-3 — The Magic-Words Headline Move

A marketer types into an AI assistant: "Give me some headline ideas for a post about insulated drinkware."

The model returns twelve near-identical options, all variations of "The Ultimate Guide to Insulated Drinkware" and "Everything You Need to Know About Your Next Insulated Cup."

The chapter identifies a sharper version of this request that would return a more useful result. Which of the following correctly states the sharper request and explains why it produces a different outcome?

A) "Give me headlines that will rank on Google for the keyword 'insulated drinkware.'" This is sharper because it forces the model to apply keyword-research principles rather than generic headline patterns. B) "Draft one headline per lever: a concrete-number version, an identity version, a question, a confident claim, an emotion-based version, and a curiosity-gap version. Then pick the one with the most specific, concrete promise and say why." This is sharper because it forces the model to work a different lever for each variant rather than generating twelve flavors of the same vague title, and the Ogilvy specifics test identifies which one earns the open. C) "Write headlines that are shorter than ten words so they read well on mobile." This is sharper because length is the primary predictor of headline performance. D) "Draft headlines that include the brand name and the main keyword." This is sharper because branded headlines earn higher click-through rates in Google search. E) "Give me headline ideas that test well with a B2B audience." This is sharper because audience specificity focuses the model on the right register.

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 17.7 — The Magic Words move (Berger) asks for one variant per lever, forcing the model to explore six distinct strategies rather than generating variations on one pattern. The Ogilvy specifics-beat-generalities principle then identifies the winner from the set, because the headline with the most concrete specific promise earns the open at a higher rate than any abstract or generic option.

Tags: Magic Words; headline craft; AI content generation; Ogilvy; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

AI-4 — Evaluate an AI-Drafted Content Strategy Recommendation

A student asks an AI assistant to evaluate their content plan: "I want to build a content program. I plan to post every day on five different platforms and write a new topic each day based on whatever is trending."

The AI responds:

"That sounds like a great strategy! Daily posting maximizes your visibility and trending topics ensure relevance. Make sure to use strong hashtags and post at peak engagement times."

Using the chapter's frameworks, identify the three most significant errors the AI's advice contains and name the chapter concept each error violates.

A) The AI fails to recommend a paid advertising budget (violates paid amplification), does not mention keyword research (violates product-led SEO), and recommends hashtags (violates the native-format rule). B) The AI's advice violates three chapter principles: (1) it recommends a cadence the student almost certainly cannot sustain for a year, which violates the honest-cadence principle (a rate held for twelve months beats an ambitious rate that collapses in six weeks); (2) it recommends posting whatever is trending each day, which means no quarterly theme, no pillar, and no cluster structure, so volume accumulates with no topical authority and no compounding asset; (3) it defines success as "visibility," which is a vanity-metric framing rather than a business-metric framing tied to an actual outcome the student is trying to drive. C) The AI fails to recommend batch production (violates editorial calendar principles), does not mention the 95-5 rule (violates B2B awareness strategy), and does not address attribution lag (violates ROI measurement). D) The AI recommends five platforms (too many for a solo creator, violating the alpha-audience targeting principle), does not mention the repurposing cascade (violates derivative economics), and ignores the gating decision (violates B2B lead-capture principles). E) The AI recommends posting at peak times (contradicts the BADASS distribution-time principle), does not address the Curse of Knowledge (violates SUCCESs), and uses trending topics (violates the content-saturation thesis).

Answer: B

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 17.1, LO 17.3, LO 17.8 — The AI's advice, which is encouraging but structurally wrong, violates three chapter principles. An unsustainable cadence produces a sprint and a silence. No theme and no pillar-cluster structure produces volume without authority. Framing success as visibility rather than a business outcome means the student has no signal tied to what they are actually trying to achieve.

Tags: AI content advice; honest cadence; quarterly theme; vanity metrics; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Analytical thinking

Chapter 3 — The Marketing Environment Test Bank

Chapter: 3 — The Marketing Environment

Learning Objectives (Ch3, Our Chapter)

  • LO 3.1 Explain why a brand that locks to a single snapshot of the world loses ground over time, and distinguish the two planning modes (snapshot vs. continuous read).
  • LO 3.2 Distinguish the microenvironment from the macroenvironment using the negotiate-vs-read split, and apply the 5C and 5W frameworks to a brand scenario.
  • LO 3.3 Apply PESTEL to name and score the six macro forces on a real category, identify the binding force this quarter, and read each force early enough to act while cheap options remain.
  • LO 3.4 Use Porter's Five Forces to assess category attractiveness, name the binding force, classify rivals using the four-competitor taxonomy and the four market positions, and identify standard leader defenses and the role of complements.
  • LO 3.5 Conduct a scored SWOT analysis using the magnitude-times-importance method, run the three-read overlay (right-side-up, upside-down, outside-in), and convert the top-scored items into a strategic match.
  • LO 3.6 Explain how environmental scanning differs from one-shot research, apply the five-stage peripheral-vision loop (Scope, Scan, Interpret, Probe, Act), use scenario planning to build four futures from two critical uncertainties, and distinguish deliberate from emergent strategy.
  • LO 3.7 Apply the chapter's frameworks to three real brand cases (Stanley, Patagonia, Liquid Death) and explain what each rhythm of environmental reading teaches.
  • LO 3.8 Identify the five failure modes (snapshot strategy, ignoring weak signals, over-reading noise, judging a new force by old rules, vendor-concentration blindness) and name the fix for each.

Section I — Multiple-Choice Items (25 items)

Item 1

Of the 500 largest U.S. companies on the 1955 Fortune 500, our chapter reports that only 51 were still on that list seventy years later. The most common reason the other 449 dropped off is best described as _______.

A) government regulation that forced them to break up B) missing one significant environmental shift that their existing strengths could not absorb C) internal financial fraud that erased shareholder value D) voluntary exit from the public markets to become private firms E) foreign competition from lower-cost producers replacing them in every industry

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 3.1 — The snapshot trap claims most Fortune 500 exits. The firms that dropped off had typically missed one shift, not many.

Tags: snapshot trap; environmental change; Fortune 500; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 2

Blockbuster filed for bankruptcy in September 2010 with a billion dollars of debt. Netflix, which Blockbuster had a chance to acquire in 2000, passed Disney's market capitalization by 2018. According to our chapter's opening case, Blockbuster's failure is best explained by _______.

A) Netflix's superior technology that could not have been anticipated B) Blockbuster betting that the world would sit still and locking in a plan built on 9,000 stores while the streaming environment moved C) a regulatory change that prohibited Blockbuster from offering online services D) Netflix's deep financial backing from Silicon Valley investors that Blockbuster could not match E) a pricing error that made Blockbuster's late fees uncompetitive against Netflix's monthly subscription

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 3.1 — Blockbuster defended a snapshot of the world (physical rental) while Netflix read the shift to streaming a year or two before competitors. The failure was planning mode, not technology alone.

Tags: Blockbuster; Netflix; snapshot vs. continuous read; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 3

The marketing environment divides into two layers, and the split is defined by _______.

A) whether the force is domestic or international B) whether the force moves fast or slowly C) whether the brand can negotiate with the party directly or can only read and react to it D) whether the force is driven by consumers or by regulators E) whether the force can be measured with survey data or only with secondary sources

Answer: C

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 3.2 — The microenvironment holds parties the brand can call, contract, sue, or buy lunch for. The macroenvironment holds forces no brand can bargain with. The split is about negotiating power, not speed or geography.

Tags: micro vs. macro environment; negotiate-vs-read; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 4

Stanley can call its retail buyer at Target and negotiate for better shelf space. According to the chapter's framework, that relationship sits in the _______.

A) macroenvironment, because retailers are too large for a single brand to influence B) microenvironment, because Stanley can sign a contract with Target and renegotiate its terms C) macroenvironment, because shelf placement depends on category-wide forces D) microenvironment only when the transaction exceeds a minimum dollar threshold E) neither layer, because retail relationships require a separate channel-management framework

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 3.2 — A party the brand can call, negotiate with, and sign contracts with is by definition in the microenvironment, regardless of the counterparty's size.

Tags: microenvironment; 5C framework; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 5

Stanley cannot phone TikTok and bargain over the recommendation algorithm that pushed its Quencher into millions of feeds in 2018. This illustrates that TikTok's algorithm is part of Stanley's _______.

A) microenvironment, because TikTok is a named platform the brand can monitor B) supplier network, because social platforms supply distribution to the brand C) macroenvironment, because no brand can negotiate the algorithm's terms D) competitive set, because TikTok competes for the same consumer attention E) legal environment, because platform rules are enforced by the FTC

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 3.2 — The macroenvironment contains forces a brand can read and react to but cannot bargain with. A recommendation algorithm fits that definition exactly.

Tags: macroenvironment; TikTok; Stanley; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 6

PESTEL is best described as _______.

A) a one-time audit run annually before strategy is written B) a six-factor checklist that ensures a marketing team walks all six macro forces on a regular clock so none becomes an undetected blind spot C) a framework for scoring the five competitive forces in an industry D) a financial model that converts macro signals into revenue forecasts E) a regulatory filing required by public companies in the United States

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 3.3 — PESTEL names six macro forces (Political, Economic, Sociocultural, Technological, Environmental, Legal) and its value is the habit of scanning all six, not any single scan.

Tags: PESTEL; macro forces; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 7

A marketing strategist reads a draft trade-policy rule buried in a Federal Register notice in January. Another strategist at the same firm waits until the rule is finalized and published with an effective date in September before beginning to act. According to the chapter's early-signal logic, the primary cost of waiting until September is _______.

A) the fine that regulators impose on firms that do not comply before the effective date B) the brand that acted early already locked up the cheap moves that are no longer available C) the risk of having acted on a draft rule that the government later reversed D) the reputational damage from being seen to comment on pending regulation E) the loss of supplier relationships that formed during the open-comment window

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 3.3 — The chapter's early-signal principle states that the clearer a signal gets, the fewer cheap options remain. A draft rule buried in trade press costs almost nothing to prepare for; by the final effective date, the cheap contingencies are gone.

Tags: PESTEL; early signal; cheap options; Legal force; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 8

A canned-water brand reads that headline U.S. inflation rose to 3.8 percent in April 2026 on an energy spike, while consumer inflation expectations sit near 4.8 percent for the next year. The chapter argues that the more strategically important number for pricing decisions is _______.

A) the 3.8 percent headline figure, because that is what supply chains and commodity prices respond to B) the 4.8 percent expectation, because pricing runs against what buyers expect next year rather than against the past C) the gap between the two numbers, which signals impending deflation D) neither, because canned water is a commodity with inelastic demand E) the Federal Reserve's official target rate, because that determines cost of capital

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 3.3 — The Economic force: the chapter explicitly states that "the lever most teams misread is inflation, because what matters is not the headline figure but the gap between it and what buyers expect next year, since pricing runs against the expectation, not the past."

Tags: PESTEL; Economic force; inflation expectations; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 9

A marketing team reads a sociocultural signal that roughly one in eight U.S. adults reported using a GLP-1 medication by late 2025, and that traditional snack purchases in those households fell approximately 31 percent. Which PESTEL force does this primarily represent, and why?

A) Economic, because pharmaceutical spending crowds out grocery spending B) Legal, because GLP-1 drug pricing is subject to Medicare negotiation under the Inflation Reduction Act C) Sociocultural, because GLP-1 adoption is changing what buyers want and how they use food categories, creating a new baseline consumer behavior D) Political, because drug approval decisions are made by the FDA E) Technological, because GLP-1 drugs are a biotechnology innovation

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 3.3 — The Sociocultural force covers shifts in what buyers want and how they signal identity through consumption. A medication that structurally changes the basket is a sociocultural force because it alters a long-run behavior, not a temporary one.

Tags: PESTEL; Sociocultural force; GLP-1; consumer behavior; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 10

The EU AI Act's Article 50 binds any brand whose content reaches European users starting 2 August 2026. It requires disclosure of AI-generated image, audio, or video that could pass for real. For a brand that runs AI-generated campaign assets, this represents which PESTEL force, and what is the chapter's recommended response?

A) Technological force; upgrade the AI tools used for content generation before August B) Legal force; prepare a one-page disclosure specification for every campaign asset this quarter, well before the August effective date C) Legal force; stop all AI-generated content to eliminate the compliance risk entirely D) Political force; lobby the EU Commission to extend the enforcement window E) Environmental force; offset the carbon footprint of the computing used to generate the assets

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 3.3 — The EU AI Act is a Legal force. The chapter's prescribed action is a one-page disclosure spec for every campaign asset this quarter, not waiting until the week of the August date, because creative pipelines have to disclose months before, not the week of the effective date.

Tags: PESTEL; Legal force; EU AI Act; Article 50; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 11

Porter's Five Forces is best used to answer which question?

A) What macro forces are pushing on every brand in the market simultaneously? B) Which of a brand's internal capabilities should receive the most investment this year? C) Is this category structurally attractive, and which single force is compressing margin the hardest right now? D) How should the brand segment its target market across different geographies? E) What promotional budget will produce the highest return on investment?

Answer: C

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 3.4 — Porter's Five Forces diagnoses whether a category makes good money for participants and identifies the primary structural force squeezing profit, which then tells a brand where to position or whether to exit.

Tags: Porter's Five Forces; industry attractiveness; binding force; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 12

A marketing team scores all five of Porter's forces for insulated drinkware and averages them. The average comes out to "moderate." According to the chapter's binding-force principle, this result _______.

A) correctly identifies the category as medium-attractiveness and supports a middle-ground strategy B) buries the single force pressing hardest right now, which is the information a strategy needs C) confirms that no single force dominates, so the brand should diversify across categories D) means the team should run the analysis again quarterly until one force clearly dominates E) indicates that the category is stable and requires no immediate strategic adjustment

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 3.4 — The chapter states explicitly: "What squeezes a category in any quarter is usually a single hand pressing harder than the rest, the binding force you have to name. Find the hand pressing hardest, then ask whether it is tighter than last quarter or letting up. That answer, not the average, tells you where the next move goes."

Tags: Porter's Five Forces; binding force; averaging trap; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 13

In insulated drinkware, a twelve-dollar Amazon look-alike that keeps drinks cold through a workday threatens Stanley's margin. In Porter's framework, this is best classified as the force of _______.

A) rivalry, because Amazon is a direct competitor to Stanley on the same platform B) buyer power, because online buyers can compare prices in a single tab C) supplier power, because Amazon controls the fulfillment infrastructure D) the threat of substitutes, because a cheaper product does the same job and could take the sale when the style premium weakens E) the threat of new entrants, because the look-alike brand is entering the drinkware category

Answer: D

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 3.4 — Substitutes are products from outside the category (or at a very different price point) that do the same job. The chapter makes this specific: "If the look you were paying for stops being the look everyone wants, the cheap copy takes the sale, because you were buying the style and not the steel."

Tags: Porter's Five Forces; threat of substitutes; Stanley; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 14

Before running Porter's Five Forces, our chapter says the first step is to "draw the box," meaning to define the industry boundary. The reason this step matters is that _______.

A) regulators require a formal industry definition before approving mergers B) the boundary determines which rivals appear in the analysis, and drawing it too narrowly is how a brand misses the category-defining entrant C) a narrower boundary reduces the number of forces the team has to score D) the boundary must match the SIC code the firm reports on its Form 10-K E) a wide boundary inflates the threat of new entrants and makes the category look less attractive than it is

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 3.4 — The chapter introduces "define the industry" as step zero of the Five Forces analysis, describing it as marketing myopia to draw it too narrowly. Stanley scoped to tumblers would never have surfaced Cirkul.

Tags: define the industry; marketing myopia; Porter's Five Forces; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 15

Cirkul, a brand whose bottle takes swappable flavor cartridges, has raised over $100 million and carries a valuation around $1 billion. Stanley's standard competitor dashboard never flagged Cirkul. Which type of competitor from the chapter's four-competitor taxonomy does Cirkul represent for Stanley?

A) Brand competitor, because Cirkul sells hydration products at a similar retail price B) Product competitor, because Cirkul occupies the same drinkware shelf section C) Generic competitor, because Cirkul solves the hydration job with a different form factor that bypasses the tumbler category D) Total-budget competitor, because Cirkul fights for the same discretionary forty-dollar spending decision E) Substitute, because Cirkul's cartridge system makes plain water obsolete

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 3.4 — The chapter introduces the four-competitor taxonomy. Cirkul is a generic competitor: it solves the same hydration occasion with an entirely different product, so it never appears on a tumbler-scoped dashboard even though it draws the same budget from the same buyer.

Tags: four-competitor taxonomy; generic competitor; Cirkul; Stanley; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 16

In the insulated-drinkware market in 2026, Stanley holds the largest market share and defends its position in women's hydration. Yeti holds real scale but trails Stanley and pushes from its men's outdoor-utility base. A twelve-dollar Amazon look-alike copies Stanley's proven design at a lower price. Cirkul serves a flavor-cartridge niche too small for Stanley to chase. The chapter's four market positions, in order, are _______.

Objective: LO 3.4 — The chapter's market-positions frame names leader (largest share, defends), challenger (real scale, attacks), follower (imitates proven moves at a lower price), and nicher (serves a slice too small for the leader). Stanley, Yeti, the Amazon look-alike, and Cirkul map onto these four positions exactly.

A market leader launches a separate, cheaper brand specifically designed to absorb a price attack from a low-cost follower, without ever discounting the flagship product. The chapter calls this defensive tactic a _______.

Item 18

Porter's original five forces do not include complements. The chapter's sidenote adds complements as a potential sixth force because _______.

A) complements increase competitive rivalry by making it easier for customers to switch between brands B) complements raise buyers' willingness to pay for the original product and make substitutes less tempting C) complements create supplier concentration that raises input costs for the brand D) complements are the primary driver of buyer power in platform-based categories E) complements weaken the threat of new entrants by requiring higher capital investment

Answer: B

Item 19

A brand manager uses the chapter's SWOT scoring method. She rates the brand's proprietary distribution network as magnitude plus three (a defining asset rivals cannot copy) and importance two (buyers value it but it is not the top decision criterion). She rates a key product weakness as magnitude minus two and importance three (buyers walk away over it). Which item earns priority funding?

A) The distribution network strength at plus six B) The product weakness at minus six C) Both earn equal priority because the absolute scores are the same at six D) Neither, because both fall below the chapter's threshold of four E) The distribution network, because strengths always outrank weaknesses in the scoring model

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 3.5 — Magnitude times importance produces the score. Distribution: 3 x 2 = 6. Product weakness: 2 x 3 = 6 in absolute value, but the weakness is a minus-six. The chapter says the brand funds "only what crosses four in either direction" and the decision is driven by the magnitude of the score, not the sign alone. In a resource-allocation decision, a minus-six weakness that makes buyers walk away earns the first move because it is destroying value that the plus-six strength cannot recover.

Tags: SWOT; magnitude times importance; scoring; strategic priority; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 20

Stanley's brand assets and cultural relevance each score plus nine in the chapter's scored SWOT. Trend dependence and cheap Amazon substitutes each score minus nine. International expansion scores plus six. According to the chapter's SWOT-to-strategy conversion, the prescribed move is _______.

A) invest in defending against the Amazon look-alike because it is the largest threat B) invest in another color-drop product launch because brand assets are the strongest strength C) cross the top-scored strength (brand assets, plus nine) with the top-scored opportunity (international, plus six) and lead the UK and Australia launch with the distinctive-asset playbook that already works at home D) exit the insulated-drinkware category because two minus-nine threats signal structural decline E) spend the budget equally across all items above the threshold of four

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 3.5 — The chapter states: "Ranking is only half the method; the move comes from matching the highest-scored strength to the highest-scored opportunity."

Tags: SWOT; matching step; strategic move; Stanley; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 21

The "upside-down" read in the chapter's three-SWOT-read overlay asks the team to _______.

A) rank opportunities above threats to emphasize a growth mindset B) read the SWOT from a competitor's perspective, in the competitor's voice C) ask of every current strength: under what environmental shift would this turn into a liability? D) invert the magnitude scores so weaknesses become opportunities E) reweight importance scores based on next year's predicted market conditions

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 3.5 — The chapter's three reads: right-side-up (the default scored grid), upside-down (ask of every strength under what shift it flips into a weakness), and outside-in (write how a named rival describes the brand in its own voice).

Tags: SWOT; three-read overlay; upside-down read; Flip Test; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 22

Environmental scanning differs from one-shot market research in three key ways. Which of the following is one of those differences, as described in our chapter?

A) Scanning focuses on competitor intelligence, while market research focuses on consumer behavior B) Scanning produces a quarterly diff against the previous scan rather than a standalone profile, so the comparison itself tells the team a new strategy is needed C) Scanning uses primary data collection; market research uses only secondary data D) Scanning is performed by outside consultants; market research is performed internally E) Scanning tracks financial metrics; market research tracks brand equity metrics

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 3.6 — The chapter defines environmental scanning as running PESTEL, Porter, and SWOT on a clock, producing a quarterly diff, filed in a repository the executive team actually reads. The diff, not the standalone deck, is the deliverable.

Item 23

In the chapter's five-stage peripheral-vision loop, the Probe stage comes between Interpret and Act. Its specific role is to _______.

A) submit a regulatory comment letter during a public review period B) run a small, inexpensive test, under $5,000, that separates two genuinely different stories about a signal in about thirty days before committing budget to act C) hire a research firm to conduct a nationally representative survey before making a channel decision D) brief the executive team on the two competing interpretations of the signal E) publish the scan results to the market to signal competitive intent

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 3.6 — The chapter's five-stage loop: Scope, Scan, Interpret, Probe, Act. "The common failure is jumping straight from scan to act." The Probe stage is a small, fast, cheap test, with Stanley's 5,000-unit order that sold out in five days as the worked example.

Tags: peripheral-vision loop; Probe; weak signal; environmental scanning; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 24

Scenario planning crosses two critical uncertainties rather than two predetermined (locked) forces. The reason to use critical uncertainties as the axes is that _______.

A) locked forces are too speculative to include in any formal planning document B) critical uncertainties are the forces where the plan actually differs across the four futures, making the exercise strategically useful C) locked forces generate four identical strategies, which makes the planning exercise vacuous D) both B and C accurately describe the chapter's rationale E) critical uncertainties always carry higher probability than locked forces

Answer: D

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 3.6 — The chapter states: "A 2x2 built on locked forces returns four nearly identical strategies, so the discipline is to cross the two forces where a plan that survives both directions actually matters, the critical uncertainties."

Tags: scenario planning; critical uncertainties; predetermined elements; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 25

Patagonia's environmental stance, written down and held for decades, is the chapter's example of _____ strategy. Stanley's Quencher repositioning, which emerged from a buyer base the marketing team never planned for on a platform it did not control, is the chapter's example of _____ strategy.

A) emergent; deliberate B) reactive; proactive C) deliberate; emergent D) planned; opportunistic E) blue-ocean; red-ocean

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 3.6 / LO 3.7 — The chapter draws explicitly on Mintzberg and Waters (1985): deliberate strategy is articulated before the action and realized as intended; emergent strategy is realized without prior intention, with consistency visible only in hindsight.

Tags: deliberate strategy; emergent strategy; Patagonia; Stanley; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Section II — Scenario / Short-Answer Items (4 items)

SA-1

A new campus coffee-shop chain operates five locations near large state universities. The owner is preparing her first environmental scan before expanding to three more campuses. She lists the following items in her analysis:

  • City health-permit requirements and annual inspection risk
  • A national coffee chain opening on one of her target campuses
  • Diesel fuel prices (her delivery trucks) rising 15 percent
  • An app allowing mobile pre-orders, which 60 percent of her current customers now use
  • An informal survey showing her regulars care most about speed and WiFi, not price

Classify each item by PESTEL force (some may fit more than one), then identify which of the five Porter forces the competing national chain represents. Explain in one sentence why the national chain is not a substitute.

Sample answer: Health-permit requirements and inspection risk are a Legal force. The national chain entry is a Technological threat — no, it is a competitive/rivalry threat, or more precisely the threat of new entrants (a rival entering the same local market). Diesel prices are an Economic force. The mobile pre-order app is a Technological force. Customer preferences for speed and WiFi are a Sociocultural force. In Porter's framework, the national chain represents the threat of new entrants (it is entering this campus market), not a substitute. A substitute would be a different category meeting the same need, such as a meal-prep service or a vending machine selling espresso drinks. The national chain sells the same product (coffee) through the same channel, so it is a new entrant that will intensify rivalry once established.

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 3.3, LO 3.4

Tags: PESTEL; Porter's Five Forces; new entrants; substitutes; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

SA-2

Stanley's strategic team is preparing a SWOT for the brand entering the UK market. Two items are on the table: (a) Stanley's distinctive brand identity with women aged 25 to 44 and (b) the brand's single tumbler aesthetic that may not resonate in the UK market. Another team member adds a third item: (c) growing UK wellness culture and active hydration behavior.

For each item, state which SWOT box it belongs in and the two scores it would receive (magnitude and importance) using the chapter's method. Explain the difference between magnitude and importance in one sentence, then name the one matchup the chapter prescribes as the strategy-generating step.

Sample answer: (a) Distinctive brand identity with women 25 to 44 is an internal Strength. Magnitude: plus three (a defining asset that took years to build and rivals cannot fake). Importance: three (buyers in the wellness category treat brand identity as a top choice criterion). Score: plus nine. (b) Single aesthetic that may not resonate in the UK is an internal Weakness. Magnitude: minus two (a real constraint but fixable with minor product adaptation). Importance: two (UK buyers are sensitive to aesthetic fit but not the primary criterion). Score: minus four. (c) Growing UK wellness culture is an external Opportunity. Magnitude: plus three (a structural, growing trend). Importance: three (directly drives category demand). Score: plus six. Magnitude measures how big the item is on its own terms; importance measures how much buyers in the category care about it, and the two are multiplied because an impressive strength nobody pays for scores near zero. The prescribing step is to match the top-scored strength to the top-scored opportunity: the plus-nine brand identity crossed with the plus-six wellness opportunity says the UK launch should lead with the established brand identity, with aesthetic adaptation as a secondary product decision.

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 3.5

Tags: SWOT; magnitude times importance; strategic matching; Stanley; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

SA-3

A direct-to-consumer cold-brew coffee brand is running its first environmental scan. Its marketing director asks: "What is the difference between environmental scanning and just doing market research once a year?" Write a three-sentence answer in the chapter's terms, then describe what the quarterly deliverable should look like and what it should contain.

Sample answer: Environmental scanning differs from one-shot market research in that it runs on a quarterly cadence rather than as a single project, it produces a diff against the previous scan rather than a standalone profile, and it is filed where the executive team actually reads it so it drives decisions rather than decorating a shared drive. Running PESTEL, Porter, and SWOT once a year ships a polished deck in January and leaves the team running on assumptions that started going stale by February. The quarterly deliverable is a one-page diff in three columns: the axis (which of the six PESTEL forces or five Porter forces moved), what changed since last quarter in dated, sourced terms, and the recommended move. The diff, not the deck, is the deliverable because the comparison tells the team a new strategy is needed.

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 3.6

Tags: environmental scanning; quarterly diff; G-STIC; AACSB: Written communication; Reflective thinking

SA-4

Liquid Death launched in 2017 in a category that conventional analysis called unenterable: bottled water, a commodity with incumbent giants at low-single-digit margins. The chapter explains Liquid Death's opening as a convergence of three forces. Name all three, classify each by PESTEL axis, and explain in one sentence why reading three forces converging at once revealed an opening that reading any single force would have missed.

Sample answer: The three converging forces are: (1) the collapse in Facebook-video production costs, a Technological force, where a $1,500 video could buy measurable attention overnight instead of the $50,000 and months it would have cost in 2007; (2) the Gen Z buyer's shift away from corporate brand voices toward authenticity and non-alcoholic options, a Sociocultural force; and (3) rising concern about single-use plastic with aluminum recycling at far higher rates than plastic, an Environmental force. Reading any single force alone would not have identified a viable category entry: cheap video alone does not explain why canned water wins, the anti-corporate sentiment alone does not explain the packaging advantage, and the plastic concern alone does not explain why a death-metal aesthetic succeeds. The opening lived precisely in the overlap where all three arrived at the same window, which is what turns a six-axis scan into the instrument that finds a category before incumbents can defend it.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 3.3, LO 3.7

Tags: Liquid Death; PESTEL; convergence; environmental scanning; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

Section III — AI-Integration Items (4 items)

These items are original to this course and do not appear in any Kotler bank. They test students' ability to evaluate AI-generated environmental analysis and apply the chapter's frameworks to AI-era scanning tools.

AI-1 — Identify the AI Scanning Output

A student runs the chapter's sharp PESTEL prompt on Liquid Death in a web-searching AI assistant. The assistant returns this output:

"Liquid Death faces a general trend toward healthier beverages. Social media is important for the brand. Sustainability is also a concern for consumers. The company should watch out for competition. Overall, the environment is moderately challenging."

According to the chapter's distinction between a vague and a sharp question, which specific failure does this output demonstrate, and what is missing?

A) The output combines too many PESTEL forces, which should be separated into individual reports B) The output is a vague-question response: it returns trend salad from memory rather than dated, sourced signals on each of the six PESTEL axes with an impact score and a named binding force C) The output demonstrates that AI assistants cannot perform PESTEL analysis reliably D) The output is acceptable because PESTEL analysis is inherently qualitative and cannot be scored E) The output fails only because it did not use a live-search model; with web search enabled it would be fully adequate

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 3.3 / LO 3.6 — The chapter's sharp PESTEL prompt asks for "the two most important dated 2026 signals on each PESTEL axis, each with a source link and a 1-to-10 impact score, then name the single binding force and say whether it is tightening or loosening." The output above is trend salad: undated, unsourced, no axis discipline, no binding force named. The failure is the vague question, not the model.

Tags: AI PESTEL scan; vague vs. sharp question; binding force; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

AI-2 — Evaluate the Flip Test Output

A student pastes the following SWOT for Patagonia into the chapter's Flip Test widget and runs the upside-down and outside-in reads:

Strengths: authentic environmental mission; loyal customer base; repair and resale programs Weaknesses: high price point limits mass-market reach Opportunities: growing ESG investing community; expansion into outdoor-adjacent wellness Threats: fast-fashion brands launching sustainability lines; greenwashing accusations

The AI returns this response:

"Patagonia's environmental mission could flip into a liability if a major supply-chain scandal exposed unsustainable practices they had not publicly disclosed. Rival perspective (Nike): Patagonia owns the environmental credibility space, but its high price and niche aesthetic keep it from the mainstream outdoor segment. Nike would target millennial outdoor runners who want sustainability without the premium, launching a lower-priced recycled-materials line and using athlete ambassadors Patagonia cannot afford to sign."

The student says: "This output is useless because it just told me things I already knew." Using the chapter's three-SWOT-read logic, explain in two to three sentences why the student's judgment is wrong, and identify the one principle the Flip Test is designed to surface.

Sample answer: The student's judgment is wrong because the Flip Test is not designed to reveal new facts, but to reveal failure modes a comfortable right-side-up SWOT hides. The upside-down read showing that Patagonia's mission flips into a liability under a supply-chain scandal is precisely the kind of reasoning most teams skip because it is uncomfortable, yet it is the assumption they need to write down and monitor on a quarterly cadence. The outside-in read showing Nike's likely move gives Patagonia's team a dated, specific competitive hypothesis to probe with data rather than a vague "competition is a threat," which is the principle the Flip Test surfaces: a SWOT becomes a working instrument only once you make it argue against you.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 3.5 — The item requires students to apply the three-read overlay, defend why adversarial reads add strategic value even when the output seems familiar, and name the Flip Test's teaching principle.

Tags: Flip Test; SWOT; upside-down read; outside-in read; Patagonia; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Written communication

AI-3 — Critique an AI-Generated PESTEL Scan

A brand manager asks an AI assistant to run a PESTEL scan for a national craft-beer brand entering the ready-to-drink (RTD) canned cocktail category. The AI returns a well-formatted scan with two signals on each axis, but all signals are dated 2022 to 2023 and the output contains no source links. The manager approves the scan for the strategy deck.

According to the chapter's PESTEL discipline, identify the two specific problems with using this scan for a 2026 strategy decision and explain the organizational risk each creates.

A) The scan covers too many forces; a strategy deck should focus on the two or three most important axes only B) The signals are stale (2022-2023 data in 2026 is the same as running the scan on last year's photo) and unsourced (the team cannot trace the claims or update them when conditions change), so any strategy built on them bets on a snapshot of the world that may have reversed C) AI assistants are legally prohibited from providing regulatory analysis, making the Legal axis unreliable D) The scan lacks a binding-force ranking, which is a formatting problem that can be fixed by adding a summary row E) The scan was generated by an AI rather than a human analyst, which introduces inherent bias into every axis

Answer: B

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 3.3 / LO 3.6 — Stale signals violate the chapter's principle that a scan must carry dated, sourced evidence: a 2023 signal in a 2026 deck is a snapshot, not a scan. Unsourced signals cannot be verified, updated, or traced to the original force-the two organizational risks are building strategy on reversed conditions and losing the audit trail for the assumptions. The chapter's sharp prompt explicitly requires "dated 2026 signals" and "a source link."

Tags: PESTEL; stale signals; sourcing; AI scanning discipline; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Ethical understanding

AI-4 — AI Environmental Scanning in Practice

The chapter's "Take This Away" callout teaches a PESTEL sharp-question prompt a student can run in any free web-searching assistant. The prompt asks for the two most important dated signals on each PESTEL axis, a source link, a 1-to-10 impact score, and the single binding force. A student runs this prompt for a campus food truck launching next fall and receives a well-structured, sourced, dated response. She then asks the model: "What should I do about this?"

According to the chapter's five-stage peripheral-vision loop, which stage does the student's follow-on question most closely represent, and what is the error in jumping from it to action?

A) The scan itself represents the Probe stage; the error is that the student has not yet Scoped the three to seven domains most relevant to the food truck B) The model's response represents the Scan stage; the student's "what should I do" question tries to jump from Scan to Act, skipping the Interpret and Probe stages, which is the common failure the chapter names C) The student has completed the Act stage; the only remaining step is to revisit the scan after six months D) The model's response is the Scope stage; the student should next search for more signals before interpreting E) The "what should I do" question is the Interpret stage; the Probe stage is unnecessary when the scan is already sourced

Answer: B

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 3.6 — The chapter states: "The common failure is jumping straight from scan to act." Interpret requires writing two genuinely different stories for any signal that moves; Probe runs a small, cheap test (under $5,000) to separate those stories before committing budget. A sharp AI scan output is the Scan stage; the model's strategic recommendation does not replace the Interpret and Probe steps because it cannot distinguish a genuine long-arc signal from noise without the team's local context and a test.

Tags: peripheral-vision loop; Scope Scan Interpret Probe Act; environmental scanning; AI scanning tool; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Reflective thinking

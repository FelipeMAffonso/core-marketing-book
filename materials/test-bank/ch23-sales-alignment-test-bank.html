Chapter 23 Sales Force and B2B / Sales-Marketing Alignment — Test Bank

Chapter: 23 — Sales and Marketing Alignment

Learning Objectives (Ch23, Our Chapter)

  • LO 23.1 Explain why sales and marketing fight and identify the structural cause — divergent incentive design — rather than treating it as a personnel or culture problem.
  • LO 23.2 Describe the four distinctive features of B2B buying (fewer/larger/concentrated buyers, derived demand, professional multi-person buying, long cycles) and explain how the three buy classes (straight rebuy, modified rebuy, new task) predict how much qualification apparatus a deal needs.
  • LO 23.3 Map the six-role modular sales pipeline (demand generation, lead, qualified lead, prospect, closed-won, retention/expansion) and identify the two structurally disputed handoffs where revenue leaks.
  • LO 23.4 Define MQL, SQL, and BANT and explain why both functions must share one written definition of a qualified buyer for the pipeline to work.
  • LO 23.5 Apply the four tactical-empathy moves (mirror, label, calibrated question, recognized summary) and the JOLT and Ackerman frameworks to a stuck-buyer scenario.
  • LO 23.6 Describe the marketing-sales SLA, the four comp levers, and the Kotler-Rackham-Krishnaswamy four-rung alignment ladder; explain why most firms plateau at the Defined rung.
  • LO 23.7 Define account-based marketing (ABM), explain when it outperforms broad-funnel demand generation, and identify the five failure modes that break alignment.
  • LO 23.8 Describe the CRM philosophy-vs-database distinction and the 2026 AI sales stack (Agentforce vs Breeze pricing models, the /bant-qualifier and /alignment-audit Skills, the four-step AI cadence), and explain why the buyer's AI now ranks shortlists before any rep makes contact.

Section I — Multiple-Choice Items (22 items)

Item 1

Sales and marketing fight about lead quality most directly because _______.

A) one function has more budget than the other and sets the agenda B) the two functions are paid for different outcomes, so each behaves rationally inside its own formula C) sales reps have more experience and know better than marketers what a qualified buyer looks like D) marketing produces too few leads for a typical salesforce to work E) CRM software has not yet been good enough to track lead quality accurately

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 23.1 — The chapter's opening principle: incentive design is the structural cause. A marketer paid per lead lowers the quality bar; a rep paid for closed-won rejects soft leads. Both are rational. The fix is a shared metric, not a motivational talk.

Tags: incentive design; sales-marketing alignment; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 2

A tire manufacturer notices that its orders rise and fall almost exactly in line with consumer sales of automobiles, even when the tire company's sales team runs no promotions. This relationship is best described as _______.

A) inelastic demand, because business buyers do not adjust volume when price changes B) fluctuating demand, because a small consumer-side swing amplifies into a large supplier-side swing C) derived demand, because the business buyer's orders are set by its own customers downstream D) geographic concentration, because both industries cluster in the same industrial regions E) modified rebuy behavior, because the automakers change their specs rather than reorder identically

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 23.2 — Derived demand is business demand driven by a downstream consumer market rather than by the buyer the seller is talking to. The tire-to-auto link is the chapter's worked example. The practical implication: a B2B seller must watch its customer's customers.

Tags: derived demand; B2B fundamentals; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 3

A SaaS company's BDR receives a lead from a company that has never used this type of software before and is evaluating four vendors for the first time. According to the buyclass framework, this deal is a _____, and the BDR should expect _____.

A) straight rebuy; a single purchasing agent who handles the order alone B) modified rebuy; a moderate-risk evaluation by the affected departments C) new task; a large buying committee, the highest risk, and the longest evaluation window D) straight rebuy; a routine reorder that needs almost no qualification apparatus E) modified rebuy; a quick yes/no from the economic buyer without a committee

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 23.2 — The buyclass predicts apparatus needed. A new task is a first-time purchase at the highest risk, which pulls in the largest committee and the longest evaluation. It needs the full discovery-and-stakeholder map.

Tags: buyclass; new task; B2B buying process; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 4

In the chapter's six-role modular pipeline, the BDR's primary job is to _______.

A) generate top-of-funnel demand by running ads, content, and events B) run the demo, negotiate the contract, and carry quota for the deal C) onboard the signed customer and reduce churn before renewal D) screen leads from marketing and decide which are worth an account executive's time E) manage the CRM database and produce the weekly forecast report

Answer: D

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 23.3 — The BDR screens marketing's leads through short discovery calls and produces qualified leads the AE develops. The BDR role sits at the disputed marketing-to-sales seam.

B) A 10-percentage-point lift at every handoff produces roughly a 2.5-times revenue multiplier on the same top-of-funnel spend. C) Pipeline length does not matter as long as the final close rate is above 50 percent. D) The BDR stage is the most important because it sits at the top of the funnel. E) Adding more stages to the pipeline always improves conversion by creating additional checkpoints.

Objective: LO 23.3 — The chapter's conversion-arithmetic illustration: a 10-point lift at every handoff is a 2.5x revenue multiplier. This is the mathematical case for the SLA and the written handoff definitions.

Item 6

A buyer fills out a form on a software vendor's website, downloads a whitepaper, and attends a webinar, earning a lead score of 81 out of 100. No salesperson has spoken with her yet. This buyer is best classified as _______.

A) a sales-qualified lead, because she has cleared the 75-point threshold B) a marketing-qualified lead, because she cleared marketing's scoring threshold but has not yet been vetted in a live discovery conversation C) a prospect, because she has expressed active buying intent through three separate actions D) a qualified lead, because BANT criteria are implicitly met by her engagement behavior E) a closed-won opportunity, because she has invested this much time in the vendor's content

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 23.4 — An MQL clears marketing's lead-scoring threshold. It becomes a SQL only after a BDR runs a discovery call and confirms the buyer against shared criteria, typically BANT. Clearing the score with no live conversation is the definition of MQL, not SQL.

Tags: MQL; SQL; lead qualification; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 7

A BDR screens a prospective customer and confirms that the company has an allocated budget for the purchase, that the contact on the call can sign the contract without additional approval, that the software addresses a documented operational pain, and that the company intends to decide in the next 45 days. Which qualification framework does this screening map to, and does the lead pass?

A) CHAMP; the lead passes because Challenges and Money are confirmed B) BANT; the lead passes because all four criteria — Budget, Authority, Need, and Timing — are confirmed C) MEDDIC; the lead passes because Metrics and Economic Buyer are confirmed but the full six criteria are not D) FAINT; the lead passes because Funds are confirmed but Authority has not been fully tested E) ORDER; the lead fails because the Decision-process has not been mapped

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 23.4 — BANT scores four criteria: Budget (can they pay), Authority (can they sign), Need (the problem is real), and Timing (the decision falls this quarter or next). All four are confirmed in this scenario.

Tags: BANT; qualification framework; SQL; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 8

A prospect says to a rep, "Our IT team has concerns about the integration timeline." The rep responds, "Integration timeline?" and then stays silent. What technique is the rep using, and what is the expected result?

A) Labeling; the rep is naming the prospect's emotion and waiting for a correction B) Mirroring; repeating the last few words as a question prompts the buyer to elaborate and volunteer the real concern C) A calibrated question; the stem "integration timeline" is a how/what construction that hands the problem back D) The Ackerman anchor; the rep is opening at the lowest number to set a reference point E) A recognized summary; the rep has summarized all the buyer's constraints in three words

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 23.5 — Mirroring means repeating the buyer's last one to three words back as a question, then staying silent. The buyer almost always elaborates, revealing the real obstacle.

Tags: tactical empathy; mirroring; sales conversation; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 9

A sales rep says to a hesitant buyer, "It seems like the last vendor burned you on the delivery timeline." The buyer pauses and then opens up about a specific past failure the rep had not known about. Which tactical-empathy move produced this result?

A) Mirroring, because the rep repeated the buyer's words back as a question B) The Ackerman model, because the rep anchored at a low offer to create space C) Labeling, because the rep named the buyer's emotion out loud with a tentative third-person phrase, reducing defensive posture D) A calibrated question, because the phrase "it seems like" is a how/what construction E) The recognized summary, because the buyer replied "that's right" to the rep's full summary

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 23.5 — Labeling names the buyer's emotion out loud using a tentative third-person opener ("it seems like," "it sounds like"). It lowers defensive posture so the buyer can correct or confirm rather than defend.

Tags: tactical empathy; labeling; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 10

A rep about to negotiate contract terms wants to use calibrated questions rather than yes/no questions. Which of the following is a calibrated question?

A) "Can you approve this contract at the current price?" B) "Is the timeline flexible if we sweeten the terms?" C) "Will your CFO sign off on this?" D) "How am I supposed to make that work within your budget?" E) "Do you have final authority to close this deal today?"

Answer: D

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 23.5 — Calibrated questions use how and what stems that give the buyer a sense of control while steering toward the rep's objective. Yes/no stems (can, is, will, do) train the buyer to say no.

Tags: calibrated questions; tactical empathy; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 11

The Ackerman bargaining model opens at 65 percent of the target price, climbs to 85 percent, then 95 percent, and closes on a precise non-round number. The decreasing size of each step is designed to communicate _______.

A) that the seller is willing to negotiate indefinitely and has no firm floor B) that the seller is moving closer to the buyer's position with each concession C) that each successive concession is more costly, signaling that a real floor has been reached D) that the seller uses a formula and cannot exercise human judgment E) that the final price was derived from a standard industry markup

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 23.5 — In the Ackerman model, shrinking concession steps signal a real floor. The non-round final number signals a price that was computed rather than picked arbitrarily.

Tags: Ackerman model; negotiation; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 12

A 2022 study coded roughly 2.5 million B2B sales conversations and found that 40 to 60 percent of losses end in no-decision rather than a competitive loss. According to the chapter, what most commonly causes a buyer to be in "no-decision" and what is the appropriate rep response?

A) The buyer lacks authority; the rep should escalate to the economic buyer immediately B) The buyer has a better competitive offer; the rep should counter with a deeper discount C) The buyer suffers from customer indecision, agreeing the problem is real and the solution would work but unable to commit because the choice feels too risky; the rep should take risk off the table with a pilot or rollback clause D) The buyer's budget was cut; the rep should offer a smaller initial scope to fit the remaining budget E) The buying committee cannot agree; the rep should identify the most senior stakeholder and close only with that person

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 23.5 — Customer indecision is the no-decision case where the buyer accepts the status quo is broken and the solution would fix it but cannot commit. The JOLT playbook prescribes removing risk, not pushing harder on features or price.

Tags: customer indecision; JOLT; no-decision; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 13

A marketing team commits in a written agreement to deliver 600 marketing-qualified leads per month at a lead score above 75, and a sales team commits to make a first sales call on each lead within 24 hours and to keep its lead rejection rate below 8 percent. Both numbers are audited monthly on the same schedule the quarterly bonus runs on. This document is a _______.

A) North Star Metric declaration B) buyclass specification C) marketing-sales service-level agreement D) BANT qualification scorecard E) revenue-operations maturity audit

Answer: C

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 23.6 — An SLA is a written two-way commitment naming what marketing owes (volume and quality) and what sales owes (follow-up speed and rejection rate cap), audited at the same cadence the bonus runs on.

Item 14

According to the chapter's four-compensation-lever framework, a firm selling enterprise medical imaging equipment to hospital systems should lean toward a _____ pay mix, because _____.

A) variable-heavy mix; the sales cycle is short and the rep's individual effort is measurable and directly tied to each close B) fixed-heavy mix; the sale is long, complex, and team-run, and no single rep can be cleanly credited for the outcome C) fixed-heavy mix; hospital procurement committees respond better to salaried reps than to commission-driven ones D) variable-heavy mix; commissions motivate reps on capital equipment better than base salary does E) recognition-only mix; intangible rewards are more effective than monetary compensation for medical professionals

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 23.6 — The design rule: fixed-heavy plans suit long, complex, team-based selling where individual effort cannot be cleanly credited. Variable-heavy plans suit measurable, transactional, short-cycle selling. Enterprise medical equipment fits the fixed-heavy case.

Tags: compensation levers; fixed vs variable; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 15

On the Kotler-Rackham-Krishnaswamy four-rung alignment ladder, a firm has a written SLA naming what both functions owe each other, but marketing and sales still report to separate dashboards with separate scorecards. This firm is on the _____ rung, and the next rung costs _____.

A) Undefined; the cost is writing the first SLA B) Defined; the cost is merging at least one metric across both scorecards, which means changing the compensation plan C) Aligned; the cost is building a full revenue-operations function to integrate the two systems D) Integrated; there is no next rung to climb E) Aligned; the cost is adopting account-based marketing across the named-account list

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 23.6 — Defined means a written SLA exists but two separate scorecards. The climb to Aligned requires a shared scorecard with weighted metrics on both sides, meaning a comp-plan change, the lever firms least want to touch.

Tags: KRK ladder; alignment maturity; comp plan; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 16

Account-based marketing (ABM) is most appropriate when _______.

A) a firm wants to maximize the volume of inbound leads from search and content at low per-lead cost B) deals are above roughly $50,000 in annual contract value with five or more people on the buying committee, where broad-funnel demand generation lacks the leverage to mobilize consensus C) the sales cycle is short and the buyer can make a unilateral purchase decision quickly D) the firm has no marketing department and relies entirely on direct outreach from its salesforce E) the firm's product is a commodity where price is the only differentiating criterion

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 23.7 — ABM picks a finite list of named high-value accounts and makes sales and marketing co-own each one. It outperforms broad-funnel demand generation for large, complex, multi-stakeholder deals where one-to-many content lacks the leverage to build committee consensus.

Tags: ABM; account-based marketing; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 17

A firm runs CRM software that perfectly logs every sales call, email, and cart event. Its dashboards show green across all metrics. At the same time, the firm is not retaining customers at the rate it expects. Which problem does the chapter identify as the most likely explanation?

A) The CRM software has a data-quality problem that prevents accurate logging B) The firm bought the CRM database but skipped the philosophy, so the dashboard tracks the wrong number perfectly rather than the number the customer relationship actually requires C) The salesforce is not entering data into the CRM consistently, creating gaps the dashboard cannot detect D) The North Star Metric has not been defined, so the CRM has no objective to optimize toward E) Account-based marketing has not been implemented, so the CRM cannot distinguish high-value from low-value accounts

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 23.8 — CRM names two things: a philosophy of keeping the right customers, and the database that records interactions. A firm with only the database tracks the wrong number perfectly, which is the Wells Fargo failure at every scale. The fix is to design the dashboard metric jointly to reflect the relationship the firm actually intends.

Item 18

A March 2026 survey of 1,076 business-software buyers found that 51 percent began their vendor research with an AI chatbot and 33 percent bought from a vendor they had never heard of before the chatbot named it. According to the chapter, what is the primary implication for the marketing function?

Section II — Scenario / Short-Answer Items (4 items)

SA-1

A SaaS startup has a marketing team and a three-person BDR team. The CMO reports that marketing delivered 400 MQLs last month, all scoring above 80 on the lead-scoring model. The VP of Sales reports that the BDR team touched only 280 of them, and of those, 190 were rejected as "not really qualified." The CMO says sales is lazy; the VP of Sales says marketing fills the funnel with noise. Neither knows what the other's "qualified" means.

Using the chapter's framework, (a) identify the root cause of this fight without blaming either function; (b) describe the single document that would close the definitional gap; and (c) explain why lead volume and follow-up speed are both part of that document rather than just the quality definition.

Sample answer: (a) Root cause: the two functions are paid for different metrics and have never written a shared definition of a qualified buyer. The CMO is paid for lead volume and score, so she has every reason to lower the bar to hit the count. The VP is paid for closed-won revenue against a quota, so he has every reason to reject anything that does not look ready to close. Both are acting rationally against their own formulas. The fault is in the design, not the people. (b) The document is a marketing-sales service-level agreement (SLA). It names what marketing owes per period (lead volume above a quality threshold, usually a BANT-shaped lead-score cap), what sales owes in return (a first-touch window, often 24 hours, and a capped rejection rate, often 8 percent), and both numbers are audited on the same schedule the bonus runs on. (c) Volume is necessary because of the Law of Replacement: every closed, lost, or disqualified deal frees a pipeline slot, and today's prospecting refills the slot the rep closes from in 30 to 90 days. Under-delivering leads in one month books a quota miss two to three months later. Speed is necessary because conversion compounds across the pipeline: a 10-point slip in first-touch rate multiplies into a much larger gap at closed-won. Both are part of the same SLA because marketing owes the volume and sales owes the speed, and neither number works without the other.

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 23.4, LO 23.6

Tags: SLA; MQL/SQL; incentive design; Law of Replacement; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Written communication

SA-2

A B2B software rep is on a discovery call with a prospect at a mid-size logistics company. The prospect says: "We've looked at three other vendors. Honestly, we're not sure any of them can actually integrate with our TMS in under six months." The rep's instinct is to immediately pitch her firm's integration timeline. Explain why the chapter argues this is the wrong move at this moment, and walk the first three tactical-empathy moves the rep should make instead, including the exact language pattern for each.

Sample answer: The chapter argues that pitching immediately is wrong because the prospect has revealed an emotional concern (doubt about every vendor, including this one) that has not been acknowledged. A pitch before the concern is named closes the prospect down rather than opening her up, and the rep loses the chance to surface the real obstacle, which may be a prior vendor failure (a Black Swan) the lead score never captured. Move 1: Mirror. Repeat her last few words as a question and stay silent. Rep: "Six months?" Then wait. She will almost certainly elaborate on what went wrong before, giving the rep the real constraint. Move 2: Label. Name the emotion she just revealed with a tentative third-person opener, so she can correct rather than defend. Rep: "It seems like the last integration project left your team skeptical of timelines that sound too good." This lowers her limbic load and signals the rep is listening rather than selling. Move 3: Calibrated question. Replace any yes/no stem with a how or what construction that hands the problem back. Rep: "What would it take for a timeline commitment to actually feel credible to your team?" This surfaces her success criteria without the rep having to guess them. Only after these three moves has the rep earned the right to speak about her firm's integration timeline.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 23.5

Tags: tactical empathy; mirror; label; calibrated question; Black Swan; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

SA-3

A B2B capital equipment company reviews its compensation structure. Its sales cycle runs 9 to 14 months, involves four to seven stakeholders across engineering, procurement, and finance, and the key success factor is long-term customer success rather than volume closed per quarter. Currently, the sales plan pays a 5 percent commission on every dollar closed, with no base salary component, and the rep who closes the most in a quarter wins a public recognition award.

Using the chapter's four compensation levers, (a) identify which levers are currently active; (b) explain why the current variable-heavy structure is a design mismatch for this firm's selling task; and (c) describe the adjustment the chapter's design rule prescribes.

Sample answer: (a) Currently active levers: variable (commission-only), and recognition (quarterly public award). Fixed base salary is absent, and intangible reward (titles, advancement, job satisfaction tied to long-cycle relationship work) appears underused. (b) The design mismatch: the chapter's rule is to lean variable-heavy when individual effort is measurable and the sales cycle is short and repeatable, and to lean fixed-heavy when the sale is long, complex, and team-run. This firm's 9-to-14-month, 4-to-7-stakeholder selling task is the archetype for fixed-heavy: no single rep closes the deal alone, the relationship spans multiple departments, and a pure commission rewards whoever happens to be the rep of record at signing rather than the team that built the relationship over fourteen months. That misattribution damages internal cooperation and drives short-cycle deal cherry-picking over long-cycle relationship investment. (c) The adjustment: the chapter prescribes a fixed-heavy plan for this context, placing most compensation in a base salary that pays for the non-selling work, the relationship management, the multi-stakeholder coordination, and the customer-success follow-through that is invisible in a commission-only structure. A smaller variable component (bonus against a quota, not commission per close) can preserve effort motivation without rewarding the wrong moment. A centrality-adjusted recognition tier on top of the quota bonus would acknowledge the connecting work that high-centrality reps do but that raw quota ignores.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 23.6

Tags: compensation levers; fixed vs variable; comp design rule; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

SA-4

A founder is launching a B2B productivity platform and has been told by an investor that "ABM is the only way to sell enterprise software." The founder's platform costs $12,000 per year per seat, has a two-week implementation, and a single VP-level buyer can approve the purchase. Evaluate whether ABM is the right go-to-market motion for this product.

Sample answer: The chapter identifies ABM as the right motion specifically for deals above roughly $50,000 in annual contract value with five or more stakeholders on the buying committee, where one-to-many demand generation lacks the leverage to build consensus. This platform at $12,000 per year is below the ACV threshold, and the description of a single VP-level approver means the buying committee is small, likely one or two people rather than five or more. ABM would be the wrong default here for three reasons. First, ABM requires sales and marketing to co-own a finite list of named accounts, which is expensive overhead for a deal at this price point, since the cost of the coordinated ABM motion would likely exceed the deal economics. Second, ABM trades breadth for depth, so a firm that runs ABM rather than broad-funnel demand generation will reach far fewer total prospects at a given budget, which is a poor trade for a $12,000/year deal that closes in two weeks. Third, the chapter argues the modal ABM failure is running the program without genuine buy-in, meaning sales and marketing both committing to the same account list. A founder-stage firm with no established sales motion will struggle to execute ABM correctly before either function is staffed adequately. The investor's recommendation is a pattern-match error: ABM is the right answer for a different class of deal. For this product, broad-funnel inbound demand generation with a short BANT-qualified discovery call is likely the more appropriate motion.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 23.7

Tags: ABM; go-to-market; ACV threshold; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

AI-1 — Evaluate a /bant-qualifier Output

A student uses the chapter's /bant-qualifier Skill on a discovery call note. The Skill returns the following output:

BUDGET (2/3): The contact mentioned a "dedicated software budget" but no dollar figure was given. AUTHORITY (1/3): The contact is the Director of Operations; she said she "will need to loop in finance before anything is signed." NEED (3/3): She described a specific operational bottleneck that the product directly addresses; quoted: "we lose six hours of team time every week to this problem." TIMING (1/3): She said they are "thinking about solving this sometime this year." TOTAL: 7/12. VERDICT: Nurture. MISSING PIECE: Confirm who signs and when the decision window opens.

Which of the following most accurately describes the right next step, and which criterion should the rep prioritize in the next call?

A) The rep should advance the deal immediately, because 7/12 is above 50 percent and the need score is the highest possible. B) The rep should disqualify the lead, because Authority is only 1/3 and no deal can close without confirmed signing authority. C) The rep should nurture the lead, prioritizing Authority (confirm who must sign) and Timing (establish a concrete decision window), because the Skill correctly identified these as the gaps that prevent advancing. D) The rep should re-score the lead manually, because AI qualification scores are unreliable and the rep should override the Skill. E) The rep should escalate directly to the CFO, skipping the Director of Operations who cannot sign.

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 23.4, LO 23.8 — The /bant-qualifier Skill produces a scored verdict the rep uses to decide next steps, not a replacement for the rep's judgment. The Skill correctly identified Authority and Timing as the two underscored criteria and named the missing piece. The rep's job is to run the next discovery interaction against those specific gaps.

Tags: BANT; /bant-qualifier Skill; AI sales tools; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

AI-2 — Critique an AI-Drafted SLA Communication

A marketing director asks an AI assistant to draft a message announcing the new sales-marketing SLA to both teams. The AI produces:

"Effective immediately, the Marketing team will be held accountable for delivering 500 MQLs per month with a lead score above 75. The Sales team will be required to make first contact within 24 hours and may not reject more than 8 percent of the leads they receive. Failure to meet these standards will be reviewed by leadership. We hope both teams will comply with these new requirements."

Using the chapter's alignment framework, identify two specific weaknesses in this communication and rewrite the opening two sentences in a way that reflects the chapter's framing of an SLA as a two-way commitment rather than a unilateral mandate.

Sample answer:

Weakness 1: The framing is unilateral and compliance-oriented rather than a two-way commitment. The word "required" applied to sales and "held accountable" applied to marketing treats the SLA as a top-down mandate, not a negotiated contract both sides wrote together. The chapter defines an SLA as a written two-way commitment; a communication that frames one side as holding the other accountable reproduces the power-imbalance dynamic the SLA is meant to dissolve.

Weakness 2: The tone of "we hope both teams will comply" simultaneously signals the document is optional and that leadership does not fully endorse it. An SLA without audit teeth is a memo. The communication should name the cadence at which both numbers are reviewed and what the review process looks like, not imply hoped-for compliance.

Revised opening: "Starting this quarter, Marketing and Sales are committed to a shared standard for the lead handoff, one that both teams helped design. Marketing commits to 500 leads per month above a quality score of 75, and Sales commits to a first touch within 24 hours and a rejection rate no higher than 8 percent. Both numbers are reviewed together each month on the same schedule the bonus runs on."

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 23.6, LO 23.8 — The item requires identifying when an AI-generated alignment communication violates the chapter's SLA principles (two-way commitment, audited on the bonus cadence, designed jointly) and rewriting toward a correct framing.

Tags: SLA communication; AI output critique; two-way commitment; AACSB: Written communication; Reflective thinking

AI-3 — AI SDR and Pipeline Scoring

A B2B firm deploys an AI system to handle initial outreach to the 1,400 leads on its monthly list. The AI sends personalized first-touch emails, schedules discovery calls with interested leads, and records a preliminary BANT score in the CRM before any human rep speaks to the prospect. By end of Q1 the firm has cut its BDR headcount from six to two and reduced first-touch costs by 60 percent. Three months later, the AE team reports that SQL-to-close rates have dropped from 34 percent to 19 percent, and the forecast is consistently missing.

Which of the following most likely explains the performance drop, and what does the chapter's framework suggest as the structural fix?

A) The AI system is too expensive for the firm's deal economics; the firm should switch to a per-outcome pricing model like HubSpot Breeze. B) The remaining two BDRs are overloaded with call volume; the firm should restore full headcount immediately. C) The AI's preliminary BANT scores are being treated as SQLs without a human discovery call confirming readiness, so leads that were auto-scored as qualified are entering the AE pipeline without the shared criteria the SLA requires; the fix is to use AI scoring as MQL-screening and require a human BDR discovery call before any lead becomes an SQL. D) The North Star Metric has not been updated to reflect the new AI-driven pipeline, causing misalignment between the AI's scoring logic and the AE's close criteria. E) The firm has moved to ABM without telling the AEs, so the AI is targeting accounts outside the shared named-account list.

Answer: C

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 23.4, LO 23.8 — An AI system can efficiently screen and score at scale (the MQL function), but the chapter defines an SQL as a lead that has additionally passed a live BDR discovery call confirming BANT. Treating AI BANT scores as SQLs skips the shared definitional criterion that makes the handoff credible to the AE team. The drop in SQL-to-close confirms the leads entering the AE pipeline are not meeting the criteria the AEs expect.

Tags: AI SDR; pipeline scoring; MQL/SQL; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

AI-4 — Salesforce Agentforce vs HubSpot Breeze Pricing

A VP of Revenue is evaluating two AI agent platforms for her 25-rep B2B salesforce. Salesforce Agentforce charges approximately $500 per user per month, with the firm paying whether or not deals close. HubSpot Breeze charges $1.00 per qualified lead. The VP's team closes an average of 40 deals per month at an average contract value of $18,000, and the BDR pipeline generates roughly 350 qualified leads per month.

(a) Calculate the approximate monthly platform cost under each model at current lead volume. (b) Explain how the chapter's framing of outcome-based pricing connects Breeze's model to the SLA concept the chapter already teaches. (c) Identify one scenario in which the seat-priced model would be the better economic choice.

Sample answer:

(a) Agentforce: 25 users x $500 = $12,500 per month. Breeze: 350 qualified leads x $1.00 = $350 per month at current volume. At the firm's stated deal flow, Breeze is dramatically cheaper at comparable usage.

(b) The connection to the SLA is direct: an SLA names what marketing owes (qualified-lead volume above a quality bar) and makes that deliverable auditable. HubSpot Breeze prices the same artifact, the qualified lead, as the unit of billing. A seat-priced contract charges for capacity (a rep has the tool regardless of deal flow), while an outcome-priced contract charges only for the artifact the SLA already names as the deliverable. The chapter explicitly ties Breeze's $1.00-per-qualified-lead model to the 2026 trend toward outcome-based pricing for AI-agent products, where the invoice is tied to completed work rather than to consumption of capacity. This aligns the platform's commercial model with the same alignment logic the SLA enforces between marketing and sales.

(c) The seat-priced model would be a better economic choice when deal flow is high and the per-outcome cost would compound rapidly. For example, a firm generating 15,000 qualified leads per month at Breeze's $1.00 rate would pay $15,000, exceeding the $12,500 Agentforce cost at the same headcount. More broadly, Agentforce's seat price becomes competitive when the AI handles a large volume of non-lead tasks (call summaries, forecast updates, email drafts) that the per-qualified-lead model does not capture, because the firm is paying for the whole agent's scope of work, not just one artifact type.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 23.8 — The item tests the chapter's AI stack section: the two contrasting pricing models, the connection to outcome-based pricing, and the conditions under which each model is more economical.

Tags: Agentforce; HubSpot Breeze; AI pricing; outcome-based; SLA alignment; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

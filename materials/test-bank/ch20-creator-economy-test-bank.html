Chapter 20 Creator Economy — Test Bank

Chapter: 20 — The Creator Economy Source note: Original authoring. No Kotler parent chapter exists for this content. All items authored directly from our Chapter 20 ground truth (book/v7/chapter-20-creator-economy.html). No item adapts or seeds from a Kotler MM16 test-bank file. Export format: Mirrors the course test-bank markdown schema (stem, A...E options, Answer, Diff, Objective, Tags) for QTI / Canvas Common Cartridge conversion.

Learning Objectives (Ch20, Our Chapter)

  • LO 20.1 Explain why creators became a paid channel and place brand-creator spending on the paid/owned/earned trichotomy.
  • LO 20.2 Distinguish the four creator tiers by audience size, engagement rate, and per-impression economics, and select the right tier for a given marketing goal.
  • LO 20.3 Apply CPM and cost-per-engagement analysis to choose the right metric for a campaign goal, and explain the CPM-versus-cost-per-engagement flip.
  • LO 20.4 Explain the FTC two-element disclosure standard and the four brand-safety contract clauses, and identify compliance failures in a real post.
  • LO 20.5 Define cost-per-attention-second, explain how the two-step flow and parasocial relationships ground the channel's effectiveness, and describe the de-influencing counter-current.
  • LO 20.6 Describe how affiliate marketing and social commerce (TikTok Shop / shoppable video) work mechanically and explain the cookie-window trade-off.
  • LO 20.7 Map the four-tier UGC rights ladder (organic, whitelisting, repurposing, perpetual) and select the right tier for a given brand scenario.
  • LO 20.8 Apply the five-check AI creator audit, write a specific brief, and distinguish vague from specific AI prompts in the creator-selection context.

Section I — Multiple-Choice Items (22 items)

Item 1

The structural reason creators became a paid channel in the 2020s, according to our chapter, is best described as _______.

A) celebrity endorsement costs rising faster than inflation, pushing brands toward cheaper alternatives B) the marginal cost of producing and distributing video falling to near zero at the same time audience willingness to watch traditional advertising also fell to near zero C) a Federal Trade Commission ruling that required brands to disclose paid placements, which legitimized the category D) the rise of smart-TV platforms that blocked third-party tracking and forced brands to find attention elsewhere E) social media platforms paying brands a revenue share to keep ad budgets from leaving the ecosystem

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 20.1 — The chapter names three simultaneous collapses: cost to produce, cost to distribute, and willingness to watch ads. Creators filled the gap because all three approached zero at once.

Tags: creator economy; marginal cost; structural shift; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 2

A brand manager at a regional snack company places its TikTok integration fee in the marketing budget. Which media type does that line item represent?

A) Earned media, because the creator produced the content voluntarily B) Owned media, because the brand controls the brief C) Paid media, because the brand is renting the creator's audience for a fee D) Shared media, because the creator and brand co-own the distribution E) Hybrid media, because TikTok blends paid and organic reach in the same feed

Answer: C

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 20.1 — Paid, owned, and earned is the organizing vocabulary. A creator integration fee is rented contact, which is the definition of paid media. Earned is the unpaid Stanley burned-car clip; owned is Duolingo's in-house account.

Tags: paid/owned/earned; media trichotomy; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 3

According to the chapter's 2026 benchmarks, which creator tier typically posts the highest engagement rate per impression?

A) Mega-creators, because their celebrity premium commands more attention B) Macro-creators, because the lifestyle audience is actively engaged C) Micro-creators, because niche authority drives category-specific trust D) Nano-creators, because the audience is close to friends-and-family distance and treats the recommendation like a personal referral E) All tiers post the same engagement rate; only the follower count differs

Answer: D

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 20.2 — Nano-creators (1,000-10,000 followers) post engagement rates of 5-9 percent on Instagram and 8-14 percent on TikTok, the highest of the four tiers, because the relationship reads like a friend's recommendation.

Tags: creator tiers; engagement rate; nano-creator; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 4

A direct-to-consumer running-shoe brand wants to launch a new colorway to as many pairs of eyes as possible in a single week. Which creator tier does the chapter identify as the right match for this goal?

A) Nano-creators, because their engagement rate is the highest of the four tiers B) Micro-creators, because the niche running-shoe audience is self-selected and deeply engaged C) Macro-creators, because their lifestyle audience spans the right age range for the shoe D) Mega-creators, because raw reach and a low CPM are the correct metrics when the goal is maximum impressions on a launch E) Multiple nano-creators running simultaneously, because stacking is always more efficient than a single large buy

Answer: D

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 20.2 / LO 20.3 — The chapter states: when the goal is sheer reach, the mega-creator wins on the 50-dollar CPM. Nano and micro win on cost per engagement when the goal is consideration. The goal here is maximum launch impressions, not consideration.

Tags: creator tiers; CPM; reach goal; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 5

A skincare brand pays a micro-creator 5,000 dollars for a single Instagram post. The creator has 60,000 followers and a post typically reaches the full follower base. What is the CPM on this buy?

A) 5 dollars B) 50 dollars C) 83 dollars D) 120 dollars E) 300 dollars

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 20.3 — CPM = (spend / impressions) x 1,000 = (5,000 / 60,000) x 1,000 = $83.33. The chapter works this exact example in §20.2.

Tags: CPM arithmetic; creator tiers; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 6

The chapter shows that a mega-creator paid 200,000 dollars for a post reaching 4 million followers has a CPM of about 50 dollars, lower than the micro-creator's 83-dollar CPM. Yet the chapter argues the micro-creator may still be the better buy. The concept that explains this reversal is _______.

A) the Van Westendorp optimal price point applied to audience pricing B) the cost-per-engagement flip, where the micro-creator's 4 percent engagement rate on 60,000 followers produces a lower cost per action than the mega-creator's 1 percent on 4 million C) the dual-entitlement principle, where the audience grants a smaller creator more latitude than a celebrity D) the parasocial anchor effect, where familiarity with the mega-creator actually lowers persuasion E) the Bass diffusion law, where smaller audiences adopt products earlier and drive imitation

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 20.3 — The CPM-versus-cost-per-engagement flip is the chapter's named concept. Mega wins on CPM; micro wins on cost per engagement. The right metric matches the brand's goal (reach vs. consideration).

Tags: CPM vs cost-per-engagement; metric flip; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 7

Under the FTC's 2023 updated Endorsement Guides, which of the following posts fully satisfies the "clearly and conspicuously" disclosure standard for a paid Instagram integration?

A) A post with #ad buried as the twelfth item in a hashtag stack at the end of the caption B) A post with only the Instagram Paid Partnership label in the header, no written disclosure in the caption C) A post that opens the caption with #ad in the same language as the rest of the post, combined with the Instagram Paid Partnership label D) A post that mentions "in partnership with [brand]" in the third sentence of a long caption E) A post marked #collab at the start of the caption, which the FTC's 2023 update confirmed as sufficient

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 20.4 — The chapter names the two-element standard explicitly: the platform's structured tag (Instagram Paid Partnership label, TikTok Branded Content tag, or YouTube Includes Paid Promotion banner) AND a written #ad or #sponsored at the front of the caption, before the line break. Neither works alone. #collab and #partner are specifically listed as banned workarounds.

Tags: FTC disclosure; Endorsement Guides; two-element standard; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 10

Item 11

A CPM-based media plan shows that a mega-creator's 2-million-impression post costs 50 dollars per thousand impressions, while a nano-creator's 60,000-impression post costs 83 dollars per thousand. The chapter argues the CPM comparison overpays the wrong creators when it ignores _______.

A) the creator's posting frequency and the number of posts in the contracted window B) the fact that an impression is not a unit of attention, and different platforms deliver widely different seconds of real attention per impression C) the FTC disclosure penalty exposure, which is higher for mega-creators who have more followers at risk D) the affiliate cookie window, which credits more sales to larger creators than to smaller ones E) the four-tier UGC rights ladder, which makes mega-creator content more expensive to repurpose

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 20.5 — The chapter's attention section introduces cost-per-attention-second as the fix, grounded in Amplified Intelligence's cross-platform eye-tracking data showing attention per impression varies by a factor of two to four across platforms. CPM calls two impressions on two platforms the same unit when they are not.

Tags: cost-per-attention-second; attention; CPM critique; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 12

A 2025 counter-current found creators building credibility by telling their audiences not to buy certain products, often items everyone else promotes. This practice is called _______, and according to the chapter it works for the same reason parasocial trust does.

Difficulty: 1

Item 14

Amazon's Associates affiliate program uses a 24-hour tracking-cookie window. A competing independent merchant on Impact uses a 30-day cookie. The chapter's trade-off analysis says the 30-day cookie _______.

Section II — Scenario / Short-Answer Items (4 items)

SA-1

A regional outdoor-apparel startup with a 10,000-dollar quarterly creator budget is launching a new trail-running vest. The founder proposes spending the entire budget on one macro-creator with 400,000 followers and a 1.5 percent engagement rate, arguing that the big name will give the brand legitimacy. Her co-founder argues for spreading the budget across eight micro-creators averaging 50,000 followers each at a 4 percent engagement rate for about 1,250 dollars each.

Using the chapter's CPM-versus-cost-per-engagement analysis, identify which strategy is more appropriate for the brand's goal of driving trial purchases rather than awareness, and explain how the metric flip justifies your answer. Then name the one other factor from the chapter's attention framework that further supports the lower-tier strategy.

Sample answer: For trial purchase, the relevant metric is cost per engagement, not CPM, because trial requires the audience to interact with the post (like, save, click), not merely see it. The macro-creator at 1.5 percent engagement on 400,000 followers produces 6,000 actions at a cost of roughly 1.67 dollars each ($10,000 / 6,000). The eight micro-creators at 4 percent on 50,000 followers each produce 2,000 actions per creator, or 16,000 actions total, at a cost of about 0.63 dollars each ($10,000 / 16,000). The micro strategy produces more than twice as many engaged actions per dollar. This is the CPM-versus-cost-per-engagement flip: CPM favors scale, but cost per engagement favors the micro tier when the goal is consideration. The additional factor is cost-per-attention-second: micro-creators' TikTok and Instagram content typically earns more seconds of real attention per impression than a macro-creator post does (per Amplified Intelligence's cross-platform data), so the micro strategy also wins on the metric that predicts memory encoding.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 20.3, LO 20.5

Tags: CPM vs cost-per-engagement; micro-creator strategy; attention-seconds; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

SA-2

A direct-to-consumer pet-food brand wants to run a paid integration with a dog-lifestyle creator who has 85,000 Instagram followers, a 3.2 percent engagement rate, and a clean posting history. The brand is considering three contract terms: (a) organic post only, flat fee; (b) organic post plus 12-month repurposing right; (c) organic post plus whitelisting. Explain what each tier lets the brand do, then state which tier the chapter identifies as the 2026 default for a CPG brand and explain the reasoning. Finally, identify the one contract clause the brand should always include regardless of rights tier and explain what it protects against.

Sample answer: (a) Organic post only: the creator publishes on their own channel, the brand pays the fee, and the post sits on the creator's grid. The brand has no right to repost, edit, or run the content as an ad. This is the cheapest tier and the default when no additional rights are negotiated. (b) Twelve-month repurposing right: the brand may re-edit the creator's content and publish it on the brand's own channels, its Instagram grid, email, website, and paid social. The creator keeps underlying ownership but grants a usage license. Cost typically adds 50-150 percent to the organic fee. (c) Whitelisting: the brand runs the creator's content as paid media from the creator's account, so the ad appears to come from the creator's handle but reaches audiences the brand selects, which may never appear in the creator's main feed. TikTok calls this Spark Ads; Instagram calls it Partnership Ads. The chapter identifies the 12-month repurposing right as the 2026 default for a typical CPG buy, because a video that performs well on the creator's feed is exactly the asset the brand wants to re-run as an ad, and that right costs far less bought up front than re-shot later. Perpetual rights are rarely worth the 3-to-10x multiplier. The one clause the brand should always include, regardless of rights tier, is the takedown clause, which lets the brand pull the content within five business days if it ages badly, triggers a backlash, or the creator is involved in a brand-safety incident after publication.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 20.7, LO 20.4

Tags: UGC rights; repurposing; whitelisting; takedown clause; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Reflective thinking

SA-3

A Stillwater coffee shop hires a 7,500-follower nano-creator for a 350-dollar TikTok integration. The creator's TikTok posts typically reach 6,750 viewers and earn an average of 7 seconds of attention per impression. A boosted Instagram-feed ad from the same shop reaches 18,000 people at an average of 1.3 seconds of attention per impression for the same 350-dollar spend. Using cost-per-attention-second as the denominator, which option produces more total attention-seconds and which produces cheaper attention per dollar? Show the arithmetic. Then explain why the chapter argues attention-seconds is a more decision-relevant metric than raw impression count in this context.

Sample answer: TikTok nano integration: 6,750 impressions x 7 seconds = 47,250 total attention-seconds. Cost-per-attention-second = $350 / 47,250 = $0.0074 per attention-second (about 0.74 cents). Instagram boosted post: 18,000 impressions x 1.3 seconds = 23,400 total attention-seconds. Cost-per-attention-second = $350 / 23,400 = $0.0150 per attention-second (about 1.5 cents). The nano TikTok integration produces 47,250 attention-seconds versus 23,400 for the boosted Instagram post, so the TikTok buy produces more than twice as many total attention-seconds at less than half the cost per second. The reason attention-seconds is more decision-relevant than raw impression count is that memory encoding depends on actual seconds of engaged attention, not the moment a platform served the post to a feed. A buyer who glances at a static Instagram feed image for 1.3 seconds encodes almost nothing; a buyer who watches a 7-second TikTok clip earns a real memory trace. The chapter cites Amplified Intelligence's cross-platform eye-tracking research as the basis for this claim. A media plan that compares only impression counts would say the Instagram buy is the better deal (18,000 vs. 6,750), which is the opposite of the correct result.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 20.5, LO 20.3

Tags: cost-per-attention-second; Amplified Intelligence; attention; AACSB: Analytical thinking

SA-4

A brand manager at a beverage startup wants to run a first affiliate program. She is choosing between Amazon Associates and Refersion as the payment network. She also asks whether TikTok Shop's in-app checkout is a better fit for the brand's target audience of 18-to-24-year-olds who primarily consume content on short-form video. Using the chapter's affiliate and social commerce content, compare the two network options on cookie window and commission structure, then explain the structural difference between the classic tracked-link funnel and the TikTok Shop in-app checkout, and state which the chapter identifies as the place where most creator-driven sales closed in 2026.

Sample answer: Amazon Associates uses a 24-hour cookie and pays 1-10 percent commission by category. The short window means the brand loses attribution for buyers who click and then wait longer than one day to purchase. Amazon's advantage is the largest catalog, the highest buyer trust, and the easiest install. Refersion uses a 30-day cookie and pays commissions the brand sets (typically 5-15 percent for consumer products), with a starting cost of 39 dollars a month plus 3 percent of affiliate-driven sales. The 30-day window credits more sales to creators including slow-consideration purchases, which is especially valuable for a product an 18-to-24-year-old might want to think about for a week. The structural difference between the classic funnel and TikTok Shop: in the classic tracked-link funnel the creator puts a link in the caption or bio, the viewer clicks through to an external website, and the brand's checkout captures the cookie to credit the creator. Every hop out of the platform is a place buyers drop. In the TikTok Shop model the creator tags a product in a shoppable video, the viewer taps the tag, and the sale closes inside the same screen the video is playing on, so the checkout never leaves the app. The chapter reports TikTok Shop's United States GMV reached the high-teens-to-low-twenties billions in 2025 and identified it as where most creator-driven sales now close, citing Tarte's case where 88 percent of TikTok Shop revenue came from affiliate creators. For an 18-to-24-year-old audience already on TikTok, the in-app checkout removes the biggest abandonment point in the classic funnel.

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 20.6

Tags: affiliate marketing; social commerce; TikTok Shop; cookie window; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

AI-3 — AI Brief-Generator and Human Red-Line

A brand-side analyst asks an AI assistant to draft a one-page creative brief for a nano-creator Instagram post, using the chapter's frameworks. The AI produces the following draft:

"Creator: @everydaydine (8,200 followers, food niche). Deliverable: one Instagram Reel, 30-60 seconds. Goal: drive trial for the brand's new plant-based pasta sauce. Fee: $250 flat. Usage: brand may share the post organically on its own account. Timeline: post live by June 10."

A student is asked to red-line the draft against the chapter's four contract clauses and the FTC disclosure requirement. Identify the three most important gaps in the brief and state which clause or standard each gap corresponds to.

Sample answer and rubric (any three of the following earn full credit):

Gap 1 — Missing disclosure language. The brief does not require the creator to use the Instagram Paid Partnership label AND a written #ad at the start of the caption before the line break. Under the FTC's 2023 Endorsement Guides (two-element standard), the brand is liable for undisclosed posts, not only the creator. Every brief must include the exact disclosure instruction.

Gap 2 — Missing morality clause. The brief contains no termination language covering creator misconduct, indictment, or hate speech. Without it, the brand has no contractual exit on day 30 if old posts resurface or a public incident occurs after signing.

Gap 3 — Missing content pre-approval window. The brief names a publication date but gives the brand no right to review the script or final cut before it goes live. A 48-to-72-hour pre-approval window is standard and catches off-tone copy before publication.

Gap 4 — Missing takedown clause. The brief has no provision allowing the brand to pull the post within five business days if the content triggers a backlash or becomes a regulatory issue after publication.

Gap 5 — Rights ambiguity. "Brand may share the post organically on its own account" does not map to a named rights tier. Sharing from the brand's own account while re-running it as paid social requires the repurposing right, not organic re-posting. The brief should specify the tier (organic, whitelisting, or repurposing) and the duration.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 20.4, LO 20.7, LO 20.8 — This item tests the AI brief-generator + human red-line move from the Apply box. The AI produces a plausible but legally incomplete brief; the student supplies the compliance check the model omits.

Tags: AI brief-generator; contract clauses; FTC disclosure; UGC rights; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Written communication

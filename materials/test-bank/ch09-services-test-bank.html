Chapter 9 Services Marketing — Test Bank

Chapter: 9 — Services Marketing

Learning Objectives (Ch9, Our Chapter)

  • LO 9.1 Explain what a service is and describe the four IIVP characteristics (intangibility, inseparability, variability, perishability) that distinguish it from a physical good.
  • LO 9.2 Place a purchase on the search-experience-credence spectrum and explain how a buyer's evaluation strategy shifts as a purchase moves from search toward credence.
  • LO 9.3 Apply the seven Ps of the services marketing mix and explain why process, people, and physical evidence are added to the standard four.
  • LO 9.4 Identify the five gaps in the SERVQUAL model, locate where a service quality problem originates, and distinguish the five dimensions of service quality.
  • LO 9.5 Describe a service blueprint, distinguish it from a customer-journey map, and use the line of visibility to identify where a frontstage failure has a backstage cause.
  • LO 9.6 Explain the Tax-Brown-Chandrashekaran recovery effect, its three boundary conditions (severity, frequency, locus), and how to size a recovery using the three justice dimensions.
  • LO 9.7 Apply AI-assisted tools to build a service blueprint, diagnose a SERVQUAL gap, score a queue on the three justices, and route a recovery ticket by the buyer's service goal.

Section I — Multiple-Choice Items (22 items)

Item 1

A massage, a therapy session, and a flight share one property no bag of potato chips has: the buyer cannot inspect, taste, or test the product before paying. This defining property of services is called _______.

A) inseparability B) variability C) perishability D) intangibility E) heterogeneity

Answer: D

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 9.1 — Intangibility means a service cannot be sensed before purchase. All four IIVP traits matter, but intangibility is the one that covers the inability to pre-inspect.

Tags: intangibility; IIVP; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 2

A campus coffee cart cannot make six hundred cups in advance Sunday night and sell them during Monday morning rush. The reason is that every cup is produced and consumed at the same moment, with the buyer in line as part of the process. This trait is called _______.

A) intangibility B) perishability C) variability D) inseparability E) standardizability

Answer: D

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 9.1 — Inseparability means production and consumption happen simultaneously, with the buyer inside the production process.

Tags: inseparability; IIVP; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 3

A hotel that cannot fill its rooms on a Thursday night loses that revenue permanently; the unsold rooms cannot be stored and sold Friday. This characteristic of services is called _______.

A) inseparability B) intangibility C) variability D) standardizability E) perishability

Answer: E

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 9.1 — Perishability means service capacity that goes unsold is lost forever. A hotel room is the standard teaching case; Chapter 10 connects this to yield pricing.

Tags: perishability; IIVP; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 4

A Starbucks barista in Oklahoma City produces a different latte from one in Miami, even following the same recipe, because the quality depends on who provides the service, when, and to whom. This trait is called _______.

A) intangibility B) perishability C) variability D) inseparability E) heterogeneity

Answer: C

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 9.1 — Variability (also called heterogeneity) means service quality varies across providers, times, and customers. Starbucks' operational standardization is designed to suppress this trait.

Tags: variability; heterogeneity; IIVP; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 5

A new patient scheduling an elective consultation with a specialist she has never seen before cannot tell whether the physician is competent until well after the appointment, and even then she may not know whether the diagnosis was correct. The purchase sits at the _______ end of the search-experience-credence spectrum.

A) search, because the physician's credentials are publicly available B) experience, because the buyer can evaluate quality after a single visit C) credence, because the buyer lacks the expertise to judge quality even after consumption D) search, because price is visible before the appointment E) experience, because the buyer can compare outcomes across visits over time

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 9.2 — A specialist medical consultation is high in credence qualities because the buyer lacks the technical expertise to evaluate the service even after receiving it. Credentials are a search cue, not a quality evaluation.

Tags: credence qualities; search-experience-credence spectrum; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 6

A restaurant meal sits in the middle of the search-experience-credence spectrum because _______.

A) buyers can read menu descriptions before ordering, so the purchase is fully evaluable on search qualities B) buyers need to eat the meal before they can judge whether it was good, placing it in the experience zone C) food chemistry is too complex for the average buyer to evaluate even after eating, placing it in the credence zone D) price signals quality perfectly for restaurants, eliminating the need for experience or credence cues E) restaurant meals are hybrid goods, so the spectrum does not apply to them

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 9.2 — Experience qualities are those the buyer can evaluate after purchase but not before. A restaurant meal is the textbook midpoint because the buyer can read a menu but must eat the meal to judge it.

Tags: experience qualities; search-experience-credence spectrum; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 7

Singapore Airlines spends roughly seventy million dollars a year training its cabin crew, which is near the advertising budget of a mid-size consumer brand. Our chapter says this investment is what produces the airline's position in global rankings. Which IIVP trait does this investment most directly address?

A) Intangibility, by giving passengers visible evidence of quality before the flight B) Perishability, by ensuring full planes through better onboard hospitality C) Variability, by suppressing the service-quality differences that arise from differences in staff skill and mood D) Inseparability, by separating the production of the service from the consumption of it E) All four traits equally, because training has no single primary effect

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 9.1 / LO 9.3 — The chapter uses Singapore Airlines as the canonical example of variability solved by investing in people. The investment addresses variability because quality depends on who provides the service; reducing that dependence through deep training is the mechanism.

Tags: variability; Singapore Airlines; people in services; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 8

The seven Ps framework expands the traditional four Ps for services by adding people, process, and physical evidence. Which of the following best explains why physical evidence is added?

A) Physical goods require physical marketing, and services are really just goods delivered differently. B) Because a service is intangible, buyers look for tangible cues in the environment, the equipment, and the staff's appearance to infer service quality before they can experience it. C) Physical evidence replaces pricing as the primary demand driver in service categories. D) It covers the physical product that always accompanies a service in a hybrid offering. E) It is added only for service providers in capital-intensive industries like hospitality and healthcare.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 9.3 — Physical evidence is the third added P because buyers face uncertainty about intangible services and reduce that uncertainty by reading physical cues. The chapter calls this strategy "tangibilize the intangible."

Tags: physical evidence; seven Ps; intangibility; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 9

Costco's operational model runs on three reinforcing moves: a membership fee that screens for committed buyers, a markup cap near fourteen to fifteen percent that keeps the shelf price visibly below retail, and a curated assortment of roughly 3,800 SKUs. The chapter treats Costco as the textbook answer to which IIVP trait?

A) Intangibility, because the membership card is a tangible cue for an intangible service B) Inseparability, because the buyer shops the warehouse and produces the purchase at the same time C) Variability, because the curated assortment guarantees consistent quality across product categories D) Perishability, by converting the service into a renewable annual product the buyer pays for once a year E) Variability, because fewer SKUs means fewer quality-control decisions for staff to manage

Answer: D

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 9.1 / LO 9.3 — Costco converts perishability from a weakness into a business model by selling the membership as the service: each annual fee is a renewal that gives the buyer the right to walk in, so the capacity is not truly perishable but rather repackaged as an annual subscription.

Tags: perishability; Costco; membership model; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 10

The SERVQUAL model identifies five gaps between what customers expect and what they receive. Which gap does a manager close when she writes a detailed operating standard specifying that a barista must greet a customer within thirty seconds and deliver a drink within four minutes of payment?

A) Gap 1 — the knowledge gap B) Gap 2 — the standards gap C) Gap 3 — the delivery gap D) Gap 4 — the communication gap E) Gap 5 — the perception gap

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 9.4 — Gap 2 is the standards gap: management understands what the customer wants but has not translated that understanding into a measurable specification the front line can hit. "Greet quickly" is a wish; "greet within 30 seconds" is a standard.

Tags: SERVQUAL; Gap 2; standards gap; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 11

A regional hotel chain runs a Sunday newspaper insert featuring a staged suite at the flagship property. Bookings spike, but so do complaints because guests at the smaller-market properties find rooms that do not match the photograph. The primary SERVQUAL gap this represents is _______.

A) Gap 1 — management misread what guests wanted from a hotel ad B) Gap 2 — no operating standard was written for room photography C) Gap 3 — front-desk staff failed to deliver on the promise because they lacked authority D) Gap 4 — marketing lifted guest expectations faster than operations could lift delivery E) Gap 5 — guest perception differed from guest experience through no management error

Answer: D

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 9.4 — Gap 4 is the communication gap: external communications set expectations the operation cannot meet. The chapter uses this hotel case as the worked example of Gap 4 in the wild.

Tags: SERVQUAL; Gap 4; communication gap; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 12

A hospital watched its satisfaction scores drop after physicians added extra check-in calls to patients recovering at home. The calls were introduced as a care upgrade, but patients read an extra physician call as a warning sign that something was wrong. The primary SERVQUAL gap this illustrates is _______.

A) Gap 1 — management misread what patients wanted from post-discharge care B) Gap 2 — no standard specified how often follow-up calls should be made C) Gap 3 — physicians did not follow the new check-in protocol consistently D) Gap 4 — marketing materials advertised the new follow-up program incorrectly E) Gap 5 — the service was delivered on-spec but perceived negatively

Answer: E

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 9.4 — Gap 5 is the perception gap: the service was delivered as designed and met the standard, but the patient's interpretation of the extra call differed from management's intent. A framing script ("stopping in to make sure you're recovering on schedule, not because anything is unusual") closes this gap without changing the delivery.

Tags: SERVQUAL; Gap 5; perception gap; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 13

A service blueprint differs from a customer-journey map primarily because the blueprint _______.

A) focuses only on the buyer's emotional response at each step rather than the operational steps B) adds the line of visibility, separating what the buyer sees from the backstage work that enables it C) removes the buyer's perspective entirely and focuses on back-office processes D) is produced after a service failure to document what went wrong, while the journey map is prospective E) applies only to high-contact services like hospitality and healthcare, not to self-service categories

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 9.5 — The journey map captures frontstage experience; the blueprint keeps that frontstage row and adds the line of visibility plus the backstage processes below it. The line of visibility is the seam that reveals why a frontstage emotion dip often has a backstage cause.

Tags: service blueprint; customer-journey map; line of visibility; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 14

Our chapter names the five swimlanes of a service blueprint in order from top to bottom. Which of the following correctly lists all five?

A) Customer actions, front-stage staff, back-stage staff, support processes, physical evidence B) Physical evidence, customer actions, on-stage staff, back-stage staff, support processes C) Management, front-stage staff, customers, support processes, physical evidence D) Customer actions, physical evidence, on-stage staff, information systems, management E) Physical evidence, on-stage staff, customer actions, back-stage staff, support systems

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 9.5 — The chapter specifies the five swimlanes as physical evidence (row 1), customer actions (row 2), on-stage staff (row 3), back-stage staff (row 4), and support processes (row 5), with the line of visibility drawn between rows 3 and 4.

Tags: service blueprint; swimlanes; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 15

A student-run smoothie pop-up runs out of a pre-ordered flavor on homecoming Saturday for a buyer who drove in from out of town. The chapter says the recovery that fits this failure is _______.

A) a free cup of the unavailable flavor the next time the buyer visits B) a verbal acknowledgment from the server, since the flavor was out of stock and not the firm's fault C) a refund with a sincere acknowledgment, because the magnitude and the controllable locus require more than the free-cup-later template D) a significant over-compensation such as a full-day credit, because repeat customers need large gestures E) no recovery, because running out of a pre-ordered item is a supply issue, not a service failure

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 9.6 — The chapter works this example explicitly. The failure is moderate-to-high in severity (the buyer made a special trip), controllable (the firm could have managed inventory), and one-time, so the calibrated move is a refund plus a genuine acknowledgment. A free-cup-later offer reads as dismissive at this magnitude.

Tags: service recovery; severity; locus; recovery calibration; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 16

Research on the Tax-Brown-Chandrashekaran recovery effect shows that customers whose service failures are resolved well sometimes end up more loyal than customers who never had a problem. Our chapter notes this effect is conditional. Which combination of failure properties makes a recovery most likely to recover and even increase loyalty?

A) Severe, first-time, stable-incompetence locus B) Mild, first-time, controllable locus C) Mild, repeated, stable-incompetence locus D) Severe, repeated, controllable locus E) Any severity, any frequency, any locus — the recovery effort level is all that matters

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 9.6 — The recovery effect is conditional on severity (mild failures reverse, severe ones often do not), frequency (first-time failures recover, repeats read as a pattern), and locus (controllable lapses forgive because the buyer reads the cause as discrete; stable-incompetence lapses forgive almost not at all).

Tags: recovery effect; Tax-Brown-Chandrashekaran; severity; frequency; locus; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 17

A buyer calls a service firm after a billing error. The firm refunds the correct amount, but the agent reads from a policy script, never acknowledges the inconvenience, and ends the call in under ninety seconds without asking whether anything else is needed. Post-interaction satisfaction scores show the buyer felt less satisfied than before the call, despite receiving the correct refund. The justice dimension this most directly illustrates is _______.

A) distributive justice — the refund was sized incorrectly B) procedural justice — the process was too fast and should have involved more steps C) interactional justice — the conversation lacked empathy and acknowledgment, which matter more than the dollar amount D) substantive justice — the outcome did not match what was promised E) outcome justice — the buyer received what was due but nothing extra

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 9.6 — Interactional justice is the empathy, honesty, and explained effort of the recovery conversation. The chapter states that interactional justice carries more weight in post-recovery satisfaction than the compensation amount itself, so a buyer who receives a correct refund delivered cold ends up less loyal than one who receives a smaller refund delivered with genuine acknowledgment.

Tags: interactional justice; three justices; service recovery; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 18

The Ritz-Carlton authorizes every employee to spend up to $2,000 per guest per day to resolve a problem, without manager approval. The chapter says the primary strategic value of this rule is _______.

A) controlling service costs by setting a cap below which employees are not required to escalate B) enabling quick responses to luxury-tier complaints, which are generally worth the investment C) the empowerment itself: the floor cook, valet, and housekeeper can fix a failure at the moment it happens, before it compounds D) reducing the liability the hotel faces from guests who might otherwise pursue legal remedies E) marketing differentiation, because the $2,000 cap appears in advertising that raises expectations

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 9.6 / LO 9.7 — The chapter explicitly says the cap is rarely hit and its strategic value is the empowerment: the floor knows it exists, which means any employee can fix a failure immediately, without waiting for manager approval, and before a minor failure compounds into a major one.

Tags: Ritz-Carlton; empowerment; service recovery; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 19

The five SERVQUAL service-quality dimensions are reliability, responsiveness, assurance, empathy, and tangibles. Research consistently finds buyers weight them in that order, with reliability first and tangibles last. Which of the following best explains why reliability ranks first?

A) Reliability is the cheapest SERVQUAL dimension for firms to deliver, so it is the most widely available baseline. B) Buyers cannot evaluate most services before purchase, so the ability to deliver on a promise consistently is the primary substitute for pre-inspection. C) Reliability correlates most strongly with price, so buyers weight it first as a price-justification mechanism. D) The other four dimensions are sub-components of reliability and therefore ranked lower. E) Regulatory standards in most service categories require reliability audits, making it the legally required baseline.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 9.4 — Because services are intangible and variable, buyers cannot pre-inspect quality. Reliability, which means delivering the promised service dependably and accurately, fills that gap by giving the buyer a basis for trusting the next encounter. The chapter notes that tangibles rank last because they are the easiest to fake without delivering underlying quality.

Tags: SERVQUAL; reliability; service quality dimensions; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 20

Patagonia's Worn Wear program repaired 174,799 garments across more than 110 repair centers in fiscal 2025, with the line growing about forty percent year over year. The chapter notes the Worn Wear buyer runs roughly ten years younger than the typical Patagonia customer. What does this demographic finding imply about the program's strategic value?

A) Worn Wear is a cost-saving measure that avoids new-product manufacturing for older customers. B) The repair program serves as a younger-buyer acquisition channel that reaches a price point the new-jacket margin cannot. C) It demonstrates that younger buyers are more willing to pay a premium for sustainability credentials. D) It shows that the goods-versus-service distinction breaks down for fashion brands appealing to younger demographics. E) The program is primarily a brand-image investment with no direct revenue implication.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 9.3 / LO 9.1 — The chapter explicitly states that "the Worn Wear buyer runs roughly ten years younger than the typical Patagonia customer, so the repair operation is less a sustainability gesture than a younger-buyer acquisition channel that reaches a price point the new-jacket margin cannot." The service layer addresses a gap the product alone cannot close.

Tags: Patagonia; Worn Wear; service as product layer; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 21

Hospital administrators saw quarterly satisfaction scores rating the food poorly and authorized a two-million-dollar kitchen renovation. Scores barely moved. An exit survey later revealed patients actually disliked nurses taking ten to fifteen minutes to answer the call button at night. The chapter uses this story to illustrate which SERVQUAL gap?

A) Gap 2 — the call-button standard was a wish with no measurable number B) Gap 3 — nurses had the standard but could not hit it during overnight shifts C) Gap 1 — management was wrong about what patients wanted most D) Gap 4 — the hospital marketed its food upgrades but not its nursing responsiveness E) Gap 5 — patients perceived poor care despite receiving objectively good nutrition

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 9.4 — Gap 1 is the knowledge gap: management believed it understood the primary driver of dissatisfaction (food) and was wrong. The chapter uses this case as the worked example of Gap 1 in the wild, noting that the named complaint is rarely the breakdown.

Item 22

A buyer can evaluate the cleanliness of a hotel room's bathroom only after check-in, not from a website photo. This quality is most accurately described as _______.

A) a search quality, because the website photo provides advance visual evidence B) an experience quality, because the buyer can only evaluate it through direct contact during the stay C) a credence quality, because professional cleaning standards are too complex for a guest to judge D) an intangibility quality, because physical spaces cannot be truly evaluated at any point E) an assurance quality within the SERVQUAL framework

Section II — Scenario / Short-Answer Items (4 items)

SA-1

A student-run campus gym that charges $15 per month wants to run the chapter's service audit on its own operation. Using the four IIVP traits as the framework, identify one specific operating challenge the gym faces for each trait and one discipline (operating tactic) that each challenge calls for. Your answer should reflect the chapter's reasoning, not generic management advice.

Sample answer: Intangibility challenge: a prospective member cannot try a workout before signing up, so the quality of the training staff and equipment is unknown at purchase. The discipline intangibility calls for is tangibilizing the intangible, which means managing the physical-evidence cues a visitor reads as quality proxies: clean equipment, credentialed trainers visibly at work, and a tour before signup. Inseparability challenge: every class requires the member to show up, so the gym produces and consumes at the same time with the buyer inside the production; if the trainer is absent or the class is cancelled, the service cannot be redelivered from inventory. The discipline is internal marketing: investing in the front-line people who deliver the service, because the staff is the service. Variability challenge: one trainer may be excellent and another mediocre on the same schedule, so quality varies with the person and the day. The discipline is the service blueprint and standardization: writing a protocol for every class format so a substitute trainer runs the same experience. Perishability challenge: a class slot with two members and a capacity of twelve wasted ten slots tonight that cannot be sold tomorrow. The discipline is yield management: differential pricing (early-bird or off-peak rates) to smooth demand and fill the class before the slot expires.

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 9.1, LO 9.3

Tags: IIVP; four characteristics; services marketing mix; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

SA-2

Klarna, the buy-now-pay-later company, launched an AI chatbot in 2024 that it announced would handle the work of 700 customer-service agents. By mid-2025 the company had walked back the program and restored a significant human escalation tier. Using the chapter's framework for AI-assisted recovery, explain (1) what the company likely got right in the initial launch, (2) what it got wrong, and (3) what the SR-CX routing rule recommends for firms deploying AI in service recovery contexts.

Sample answer: What Klarna got right: routing routine, high-volume, low-stakes contacts to an AI tier is consistent with the chapter's recommendation. The Marriott Bonvoy rebuild found that roughly 70 percent of front-line phone minutes were occupied by inquiries an AI could handle, and the Brynjolfsson-Li-Raymond co-pilot study found a 14 percent productivity gain from AI assistance concentrated in the least-experienced agents. Klarna was applying the same efficiency logic to a digital-first category. What it got wrong: replacing the human tier entirely rather than routing to it. The chapter says the AI co-pilot study found the gain held only where a human tier sat underneath the AI, and Klarna's 2024 full-bot push removed that tier. High-stakes or layered failures (such as a buyer whose refund has gone wrong across four prior contacts) require a recovery conversation an AI cannot safely handle, because interactional justice, the empathy and acknowledged effort in the conversation, carries more weight in post-recovery satisfaction than the compensation amount itself. A bot that recites policy without acknowledging the layered failure violates interactional justice even when the procedural outcome is correct. The SR-CX rule: route by the buyer's service goal at intake. Utilitarian goals (process my refund, update my address) suit mechanical or thinking AI because conversational warmth reads as unwanted friction when the buyer wants speed. Hedonic or high-emotional-stakes goals (resolve a layered dispute, recover a meaningful purchase) require feeling AI or a human tier, because the buyer wants to feel heard, not just processed.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 9.6, LO 9.7

Tags: Klarna; AI service delivery; SR-CX; human escalation tier; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Reflective thinking

SA-3

A casual-dining chain tells its servers to "greet quickly and serve fast." Customer satisfaction scores remain mediocre despite the directive. Identify which SERVQUAL gap this represents, explain why the directive fails as an operating standard, and write two revised standards that would close the gap. Then explain which of the five SERVQUAL dimensions the new standards most directly improve.

Sample answer: This is Gap 2, the standards gap: management knows what customers want (fast, friendly service) but has not translated that into a measurable specification the front line can hit. The directive fails because neither "quickly" nor "fast" can be coached, measured, or enforced. A server who greets in sixty seconds and one who greets in ninety seconds are both inside "quickly." Any standard the firm cannot put a stopwatch on is a wish, not a standard. Two revised standards: (1) "Greet the table within thirty seconds of seating, making eye contact and stating your name"; (2) "Deliver drinks within four minutes and entrees within twelve minutes of the order being entered." Both are behavior-bound and time-bound, meaning a manager can observe, coach, and measure them on any shift. The dimensions most directly improved are reliability (delivering the promised service accurately and consistently) and responsiveness (willingness to help customers promptly). Reliability ranks first in SERVQUAL's importance hierarchy, so closing Gap 2 with measurable time standards is the highest-leverage operating investment the chain can make.

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 9.4

Tags: SERVQUAL; Gap 2; standards gap; reliability; responsiveness; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Written communication

SA-4

Patagonia, Costco, Singapore Airlines, and Mayo Clinic are the four brand applications in our chapter. Each one is treated as the best available answer to a different one of the four IIVP characteristics. Match each brand to its primary IIVP trait, explain the specific operating move the brand uses to address that trait, and identify the SERVQUAL dimension the move most directly strengthens.

Sample answer: Singapore Airlines addresses variability through investment in people. A four-month cabin-crew training induction (about twice the industry norm) and an annual training budget near $70 million suppress the quality differences that arise from differences in staff skill and day-to-day variation. The SERVQUAL dimension most directly strengthened is reliability, since consistent delivery across flights and crew members is exactly what that investment buys. Patagonia addresses inseparability by converting the service layer into a product layer on top of the jacket: the Worn Wear repair program means the buyer re-enters the production process on every repair or resale transaction, but now the service is packaged in a way the buyer pays for deliberately. The dimension strengthened is assurance, because the lifetime-repair commitment signals that Patagonia will stand behind the transaction indefinitely. Costco addresses perishability by making the membership itself the renewable annual service: the fee converts the perishable access right (a warehouse visit that expires if not used) into a product the buyer purchases once a year, so capacity is not wasted but is paid for in advance. The dimension strengthened is reliability: the markup cap and curated assortment together make the shelf price a promise the buyer can count on, scored by the 92.3 percent renewal rate. Mayo Clinic addresses intangibility by anchoring an otherwise uncertain medical service to one institutional value, "the needs of the patient come first," expressed through salary-based physician compensation, integrated multi-specialty team structures, and facility design that signals respect. The dimension most directly strengthened is assurance, because the consistent institutional frame gives the buyer confidence the service will behave the same way each time, even when the outcome itself is uncertain.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 9.1, LO 9.3, LO 9.4

Tags: Singapore Airlines; Patagonia; Costco; Mayo Clinic; IIVP; SERVQUAL; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

Section III — AI-Integration Items (4 items)

These items are original to this course and do not appear in any Kotler bank. They test students' ability to evaluate AI-generated service analysis, apply chapter frameworks to AI-era service delivery, and use AI tools the chapter teaches.

AI-1 — Identify the SERVQUAL Gap in AI Output

A student asks an AI assistant to help diagnose why a national coffee chain is receiving complaints about inconsistent drink quality across locations. The AI responds:

"Based on a web search, customers are most frustrated by drinks that taste different at different locations. The problem is that the company has not trained its baristas consistently. I recommend more training, better equipment, and improved quality-control procedures across all locations."

Which aspect of the chapter's diagnostic approach is missing from the AI's output, and what is the most likely SERVQUAL gap the AI should have identified before recommending solutions?

A) The AI correctly identified all relevant gaps; its recommendations match the chapter's framework. B) The AI skipped the backward diagnostic: it jumped to solutions without tracing the complaint to the specific gap that produced it. The most likely gap is Gap 3 (the delivery gap), where a measurable standard exists but the operation is not built to consistently hit it — not a training gap, which is Gap 2. C) The AI correctly identified Gap 2 (standards gap) by noting that baristas are inconsistently trained, so more training is the correct solution. D) The AI diagnosed a Gap 4 failure, since inconsistent drinks likely result from marketing materials that promise a standard the operation cannot deliver. E) The AI's response is incomplete because it failed to recommend a customer-journey map rather than a service blueprint.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 9.4, LO 9.5 — The chapter's backward diagnostic says to trace the complaint to the gap that produced it before recommending a fix, because the named complaint is rarely the breakdown. Inconsistent drink quality could be Gap 2 (no measurable standard), Gap 3 (a measurable standard the operation cannot consistently hit due to workflow or staffing conflicts), or Gap 1 (management's assumption about the root cause is wrong). The AI skipped the diagnostic and went straight to the most available fix, which is how the hospital spent $2 million on the kitchen before discovering the real gap was the call button.

AI-2 — Score a Recovery Queue on the Three Justices

A student pastes fifteen one-star reviews of a streaming service into an AI assistant and runs the "score a queue on the three justice dimensions" move from the chapter. The AI returns the following summary:

"Distributive justice mean: 2.1 out of 5. Procedural justice mean: 3.8 out of 5. Interactional justice mean: 4.1 out of 5. Leak: the firm pays out poorly and slowly, but when a customer does reach a human, the conversation is handled with warmth and professionalism."

Using the chapter's framework, interpret these results and recommend the single highest-leverage change the streaming service should make. Explain why that change rather than any improvement to procedural or interactional justice.

Sample answer and rubric:

Interpretation: The firm's recovery system leaks on distributive justice, the tangible outcome of the recovery. A mean of 2.1 out of 5 means the refunds or credits being offered are, on average, sized well below what buyers feel they lost. Procedural justice at 3.8 is adequate, meaning the process is reasonably fast and accessible. Interactional justice at 4.1 is a strength, meaning front-line agents are doing their job well in terms of empathy and explanation. Highest-leverage change: raise the empowerment cap or the refund authorization level so that front-line agents can offer a compensation amount that matches the magnitude of the loss. The chapter says interactional justice carries more weight in post-recovery satisfaction than the compensation amount, which might suggest leaving distributive as-is. But a 2.1 out of 5 is below the threshold where any amount of conversational warmth can make up the shortfall, because the buyer who receives a $2 credit on a $15 monthly loss is insulted by the outcome regardless of how nicely the agent explained it. The chapter's calibration rule is that distributive justice must first meet the floor of fairness before interactional quality can compound it. The reason to fix distributive before procedural or interactional is that procedural and interactional are already performing adequately, so the marginal loyalty gain from improving the already-strong dimensions is smaller than fixing the dimension that is actively damaging trust.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 9.6, LO 9.7

Tags: three justices; distributive justice; AI recovery queue; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

AI-3 — Journey Map to Blueprint Apply Loop

A student uses the chapter's AI Apply loop to build a service blueprint for a campus health clinic. The AI-generated frontstage customer-journey map shows the following steps and emotions:

Step 1: Find and schedule an appointment online — Emotion: anxious, confused by the booking form Step 2: Arrive and check in at the front desk — Emotion: uncertain, long wait Step 3: Wait in the waiting room — Emotion: frustrated, more than thirty minutes Step 4: See a nurse for vitals — Emotion: calm, quick and professional Step 5: Wait for the physician — Emotion: frustrated, another fifteen minutes Step 6: Physician visit — Emotion: reassured, thorough and clear

The student then runs Step 2 of the apply loop ("Drop the line of visibility") and the AI adds backstage causes for each frontstage emotion. According to the chapter's logic, which backstage cause most likely explains the Step 3 frustration (more than thirty minutes in the waiting room)?

A) A Gap 1 failure: management does not know that patients dislike waiting B) A backstage scheduling bottleneck such as a single physician seeing back-to-back appointments with no buffer time, causing every patient to wait while the prior patient's notes are finalized C) A Gap 4 failure: the clinic's website promises same-day appointments but operations cannot deliver them D) A Gap 5 failure: patients perceive a wait as longer than it actually is because there is no visible progress indicator E) An interactional justice problem: the wait room staff are not empathetic enough

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 9.5, LO 9.7 — The chapter's blueprint lesson is that a frontstage emotion dip almost always has a backstage cause the buyer cannot see. A thirty-minute wait in a clinic waiting room is almost never caused by the waiting room itself but by a scheduling system that creates a queue through no-buffer back-to-back appointments. The fix is below the line of visibility (the scheduling protocol), not at the counter.

AI-4 — Routing a Recovery Ticket by SR-CX Logic

A service manager at a regional airline is setting up an AI-assisted recovery system. She has two types of inbound contact arriving in the queue:

Type A: Passengers requesting a refund for a flight cancelled due to weather. The refund amount is fixed by policy and the process takes under three minutes to complete online.

Type B: Passengers whose checked luggage was delayed three days, including medication needed daily, who have called twice before and received no update.

Using the chapter's SR-CX routing rule (which routes by the buyer's service goal — utilitarian versus hedonic), which model tier does each contact type call for, and why?

A) Both types call for a human agent, because any airline recovery requires empathy. B) Both types call for an AI tier, because efficiency is the primary goal in airline service recovery. C) Type A calls for a fast-tier AI model (utilitarian goal: process my refund quickly and accurately); Type B calls for a human tier or deep-tier model (hedonic or high-emotional-stakes goal: feel heard, receive an honest explanation, and be treated as a person with a specific situation, not a policy case). D) Type A calls for a human agent because weather cancellations are high-stakes failures; Type B calls for an AI tier because the luggage delay is a routine operational problem. E) Type A calls for a deep-tier AI model because policy-based refunds require precise language; Type B calls for a fast-tier AI model because repeated contacts indicate the process already failed and speed is now the priority.

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 9.7, LO 9.6 — The chapter presents the SR-CX (Service Robot Customer Experience) rule from Lariviere et al. 2025: utilitarian service goals suit mechanical or thinking AI because conversational warmth reads as friction when the buyer wants speed; hedonic or high-emotional-stakes goals require feeling AI or a human tier because the buyer wants to feel heard. Type A is a clear utilitarian goal, correctly routed to the fast tier. Type B has high emotional stakes (health risk, repeated failure, trust damage), correctly routed to the human or deep-model tier. The chapter also cites the Marriott Bonvoy 2024 rebuild as the worked case: AI front door for the 70 percent routine, human tier reserved for the 25 to 30 percent with high stakes.

Tags: SR-CX; AI routing; utilitarian vs hedonic; service recovery tiers; Marriott; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

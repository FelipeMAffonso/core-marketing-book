Chapter 2 What Marketing Is — Test Bank

Chapter: 2 — What Marketing Is And Why It's Hard Source note: Adapted from Kotler MM16 Test Bank Chapter 1 (Kotler/Keller/Chernev, Marketing Management 16e) + original AI-era items authored against OUR chapter. Kotler seed items have been rewritten into this course's voice, updated to 2026 context, and re-tagged to our learning objectives. No item is a verbatim lift. Export format: Mirrors Kotler markdown schema (stem, A...E options, Answer, Diff, Objective, Tags) for QTI / Canvas Common Cartridge conversion.

Learning Objectives (Ch2, Our Chapter)

  • LO 2.1 Define marketing using the AMA's 2007 definition and explain what an exchange requires.
  • LO 2.2 Name the five marketing decisions (the coordinated-decisions definition) and explain why they multiply rather than add.
  • LO 2.3 Distinguish the marketing concept from the selling concept, and explain how marketing myopia traps firms.
  • LO 2.4 Trace the five business orientations (production, product, selling, marketing, holistic) and connect each to a 2026 example.
  • LO 2.5 Apply the four Ps, seven Ts, and 4Cs frameworks to a real brand, and explain what the 4Ps-to-7Ts split fixed.
  • LO 2.6 Explain the four value types (economic, functional, experiential, social) and apply the four-part positioning statement.
  • LO 2.7 Explain how penetration, customer lifetime value, and retention determine whether a marketing configuration pays on repeat.
  • LO 2.8 Analyze the Bud Light case using the coherence principle and Situational Crisis Communication Theory.
  • LO 2.9 Evaluate AI output about a brand's marketing decisions, identify which framework an AI-drafted positioning or strategy applies, and flag what the output leaves out.

Section I — Multiple-Choice Items (20 items)

Item 1

The American Marketing Association's 2007 definition describes marketing as the activity, set of institutions, and processes for creating, communicating, delivering, and exchanging offerings that have value for customers, clients, partners, _______.

A) and competitors in the firm's category B) and the company's shareholders C) and society at large D) who have been targeted by the firm's strategy E) in the domestic market the firm currently serves

Answer: C

Diff: 1

Objective: LO 2.1 — The 2007 AMA definition extends "value" to customers, clients, partners, and society at large. The addition of "society at large" is the upgrade over the earlier managerial wording and is re-approved by the AMA's standing panel on a roughly three-year cadence.

Tags: AMA definition; marketing scope; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 2

An exchange, in the sense the chapter uses the term, requires that each party _______.

A) pay money rather than barter goods or services B) be a legally registered business entity C) hold something the other wants and be free to walk away D) have already established a prior purchase relationship E) use a market intermediary to reduce transaction risk

Answer: C

Diff: 1

Objective: LO 2.1 — An exchange is a voluntary, value-creating transfer between two parties. Each side gives up something and gets something it values more, and neither is forced into it. The chapter's definition of exchange names "two parties who each hold something the other wants" and "each free to walk away" as two of the five conditions.

Tags: exchange; marketing fundamentals; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 3

According to Peter Drucker's 1973 formulation, the true aim of marketing is to _______.

A) maximize the volume of advertising reach per dollar spent B) make selling unnecessary by understanding the customer so well that the offer fits and sells itself C) find the right buyers for the products the firm has already decided to make D) build internal alignment between the sales and marketing departments E) generate demand for offerings buyers would not otherwise seek

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 2.3 — Drucker's 1973 line is: "The aim of marketing is to make selling superfluous." When the offer, price, place, and message all agree on one buyer, she arrives already willing and the salesperson only confirms a choice made long before. The chapter uses Spotify Wrapped as the everyday illustration.

Tags: Drucker; marketing vs selling; marketing concept; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 4

A regional coffee-roasting startup makes a high-quality single-origin bean, charges a fair price, and runs striking social media ads. However, it sells only through its own website and ships once a week with long delays. Which of the five marketing decisions is broken, and what makes that one failure costly?

A) The "what to make" decision is broken; the quality commitment raises expectations the firm cannot meet. B) The "how to talk about it" decision is broken; striking ads attract buyers the product cannot retain. C) The "who to serve" decision is broken; targeting online buyers is the wrong choice for a coffee brand. D) The "where to sell it" decision is broken; an inconvenient channel delivers buyers into a friction the other four decisions paid to create. E) The "what to charge" decision is broken; a fair price undercuts the premium brand story.

Answer: D

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 2.2 — The chapter's core rule: the five decisions multiply. Four good decisions deliver the buyer straight to the friction the fifth one created. Here the channel decision (where) breaks the chain the other four built. The correct option names this precisely.

Tags: five decisions; coherence; multiplicative not additive; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 5

Patagonia's founder gave the entire company to a climate trust in September 2022 — roughly three billion dollars — and the brand kept selling jackets the next morning with no break in revenue. The chapter argues this happened because _______.

A) the giveaway generated so much press coverage that new buyers rushed in B) Patagonia had already built the largest outdoor-apparel customer base in the US C) the company's premium-gear, durable-build, repair-program, and story-telling decisions already pointed at the same buyer, so the ownership change was coherent with all five D) Patagonia's marketing team ran a preemptive campaign to reassure existing customers E) the gift was structured so that day-to-day product decisions remained unchanged

Answer: C

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 2.2 — The chapter's opening illustration: a move that would have killed almost any other clothing company did not break Patagonia because every other choice for forty years already pointed the same way. Coherence across all five decisions is what survives almost anything.

Tags: coherence; five decisions; Patagonia; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 6

In 1960 E. Jerome McCarthy introduced a four-word mnemonic to teach the marketing program. The "seven Ts" expansion the chapter describes fixed two specific problems with that original frame. Those two problems were _______.

A) the original frame excluded digital channels and did not account for services B) "Product" was hiding three separate decisions, and "Promotion" was hiding two C) the original frame lacked a profitability metric and ignored the competitive environment D) "Price" and "Place" were bundled into a single cost concept the buyer could not evaluate E) the original frame was designed for industrial goods and did not apply to consumer brands

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 2.5 — Product hid the artifact, the service wrapped around it, and the brand memory the buyer carries. Promotion hid the long-term work of building the brand and the short-term work of running a sale. Folding each pair into one word lets a weekend coupon eat the budget meant to build the brand.

Tags: four Ps; seven Ts; marketing mix; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 7

Theodore Levitt's 1960 concept of marketing myopia describes a trap where a firm _______.

A) invests too heavily in advertising at the expense of product development B) defines itself by the product it makes rather than the job that product does for the buyer, and misses substitutes C) targets too narrow a buyer segment and fails to achieve the scale needed for profitability D) confuses short-term promotional tactics with long-term brand strategy E) over-relies on market research and slows its ability to react to competitors

Answer: B

Diff: 1

Objective: LO 2.3 — Marketing myopia is Levitt's 1960 name for defining the firm by the product rather than the buyer's job. The US railroads thought they were in the railroad business when buyers hired them for transportation, so they kept perfecting locomotives while cars and planes took the trips.

Tags: marketing myopia; Levitt; marketing orientation; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 8

A firm that concentrates on driving cost down, output up, and wide distribution because it assumes buyers will favor whatever is cheapest and easiest to find is operating under the _______ orientation.

A) marketing B) selling C) product D) production E) holistic

Answer: D

Diff: 1

Objective: LO 2.4 — The production orientation holds that buyers will pick whatever is cheap and on a nearby shelf, so the firm pours effort into driving cost down and output up. Henry Ford's Model T is the chapter's case, with the price falling from $850 to about $260 by the mid-1920s.

Tags: production orientation; five orientations; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 9

A life-insurance company trains its agents to follow up with prospects multiple times, uses newspaper ads and direct mail to generate leads, and runs regular promotional offers to encourage immediate applications. Its operating assumption is that buyers, left alone, will not seek out coverage on their own. This describes the _______ orientation.

A) marketing B) holistic C) product D) production E) selling

Answer: E

Diff: 1

Objective: LO 2.4 — The selling orientation assumes the buyer, left alone, will not take enough of what the firm decided to make, so it pushes hard with advertising and short-term deals. Life insurance is the chapter's own example of a product nobody goes looking for.

Tags: selling orientation; five orientations; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 10

The marketing orientation, which arrived in the 1950s, flipped the firm's starting question. Which framing best captures that flip?

A) From "how do we cut production cost?" to "how do we hire the most effective sales force?" B) From "how do we find buyers for the products we have made?" to "how do we find products for the buyers we already have?" C) From "how do we build the best-performing product?" to "how do we communicate its superiority?" D) From "how do we dominate distribution?" to "how do we dominate the consumer's mind?" E) From "how do we achieve the lowest landed cost?" to "how do we achieve the highest profit margin?"

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 2.3 — The chapter states this flip precisely. The marketing orientation asks how to find products for the buyers the firm already has, rather than buyers for the products it has already made. Jobs to be Done is named as the 2016 reframing that turns this flip into an actionable tool.

Tags: marketing concept; marketing orientation; marketing vs selling; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 11

Robert Lauterborn proposed the 4Cs in 1990 as a buyer-side mirror of the 4Ps. The reframe matters most on two of the four Cs. Which pair, and why?

A) Consumer wants and convenience, because these reveal the distribution and product gaps most firms overlook B) Cost-to-satisfy and communication, because the buyer pays in time, search effort, and switching cost as well as money, and in 2026 she talks back without the firm in the room C) Convenience and communication, because digital channels have made both the easiest to improve quickly D) Consumer wants and cost-to-satisfy, because these two together define the buyer's net perceived value E) All four Cs matter equally; the reframe is most useful as a total system, not for any two levers in particular

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 2.5 — The chapter calls out cost and communication as the two most consequential Cs in the 2026 context. Cost-to-satisfy includes time, search, and switching cost beyond the tag price. Communication is asymmetric because the buyer now talks back to other buyers without the firm in the room, changing the communication landscape entirely.

Tags: 4Cs; Lauterborn; buyer-side frameworks; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 12

Liquid Death built its brand on a heavy-metal-themed tallboy can of mountain water and priced it near imported beer, far above bottled-water alternatives, without discounting. Which of the four value types from our chapter does this premium price most directly defend?

A) Economic value, because buyers save money relative to buying the same water in a plastic bottle at a higher total-cost-to-satisfy B) Functional value, because the aluminium can performs better than plastic on taste preservation C) Experiential and social value, because the can is an identity signal the buyer displays, and carrying it is a statement in front of the concert crowd D) Economic and functional value together, because tallboy cans are more cost-efficient per ounce than standard bottles E) Only functional value; the social meaning is a by-product of distribution in Whole Foods, not a decision by the firm

Answer: C

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 2.6 — The chapter names four value types: economic, functional, experiential, and social. Liquid Death and Patagonia are described as leading on experiential and social value, selling an experience and a tribe, while Costco leads on economic and functional. The premium price for Liquid Death makes no sense on functional grounds (water is water) but perfect sense on experiential and social grounds.

Tags: four value types; Liquid Death; value-based positioning; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 13

Penetration, in the Sharp/Ehrenberg-Bass sense the chapter uses, measures _______.

A) how deeply a brand has embedded itself in a buyer's daily routine over the past year B) the share of category buyers who bought the brand at least once in a given time window C) the number of distribution points where the brand is physically available D) the average number of times existing buyers repurchase in a year E) the brand's share of the advertising spend in its category

Answer: B

Diff: 1

Objective: LO 2.7 — The chapter defines penetration as "how many different people bought you in a given stretch of time," and explains that when a brand gets bigger, penetration is the number that moves first, because growth comes from people who were not buying you starting to.

Tags: penetration; brand growth; Sharp; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 14

Costco charges an annual membership fee of $65 before a member has bought anything. Viewed through the chapter's 4Cs lens, which statement correctly explains why this additional money cost does not push most buyers away?

A) The fee signals premium quality, raising the buyer's perceived product value and more than offsetting the extra money cost B) The fee buys lower per-unit prices, fewer required trips, and reduced search effort, so it drops more in the buyer's total cost-to-satisfy than it adds C) The fee creates a sunk-cost commitment that makes buyers visit more often, which Costco counts as a behavioral loyalty metric D) Compared to competing memberships like Amazon Prime, the Costco fee is positioned as the cheapest option in the warehouse-retail segment E) The fee has no rational justification but persists because buyers have habituated to it over decades of shopping

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 2.5 — The chapter states explicitly: "The firm adds one money cost so four other costs in the buyer's stack fall by more than the fee." Lower per-unit prices, bigger packs requiring fewer trips, and a tight assortment cutting search effort are all buyer-side cost reductions within the 4Cs framework's "cost-to-satisfy."

Tags: 4Cs; Costco; cost-to-satisfy; membership fee; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 15

Bud Light was the best-selling beer in the United States for more than twenty consecutive years. According to our chapter's analysis, the April 2023 partnership that began the brand's collapse represented a failure of which specific marketing decision?

A) The "what to make" decision, because the product formula was altered to appeal to a new demographic B) The "how to talk about it" decision, because the ad copy was poorly written and tone-deaf C) The "who to serve" decision, because one promotion pointed at a buyer the other four decisions were not serving D) The "what to charge" decision, because the price was raised at the same time as the partnership launched E) The "where to sell it" decision, because distribution was shifted toward channels that the core buyer did not use

Answer: C

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 2.8 — The chapter names the failure precisely: the partnership aimed the "who to serve" decision at a buyer the other four were not serving. The price, product, distribution, and voice had all been built for conservative working-class men for twenty years, and one promotion aimed elsewhere. The decisions multiply, so one misaligned slot sinks the configuration.

Tags: Bud Light; coherence; five decisions; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 16

After the April 2023 Bud Light misstep, Anheuser-Busch InBev responded by pointing to the brand's history of supporting veterans and the patriotism of its workforce, without directly acknowledging the harm its core buyers felt. According to Situational Crisis Communication Theory, why did this response fail?

A) The company should have issued a formal apology instead of a public statement, because formal apologies satisfy legal obligations B) The company chose a Bolstering response (reciting past good deeds) when the dominant buyer emotions were broken trust and lost control, which require a Rebuild response that acknowledges the harm and announces a concrete fix C) The company involved the marketing team in the response instead of the legal team, which created a message that was too emotional D) The response was delivered too slowly, which is the primary driver of crisis damage regardless of which response type is chosen E) No response would have worked, because social media makes recovery impossible once a boycott reaches one billion dollars in lost sales

Answer: B

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 2.8 — The chapter cites the peer-reviewed Iachizzi (2025) study that coded the angry Instagram posts: 52.63 percent loaded on broken trust and 44.74 percent on lost control. Coombs's SCCT says those two emotions call for a Rebuild response (acknowledge the harm, announce a concrete fix). The company substituted Bolstering, which the framework flags as the worst-case choice for that combination of emotions.

Tags: Bud Light; SCCT; Coombs; crisis response; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 17

A Bain and Company study across roughly a hundred service firms found that cutting the share of customers lost each year by five percentage points lifted profit by _______.

A) approximately 5 to 10 percent, tracking roughly one-to-one with the reduction in churn B) approximately 10 to 20 percent, because retained customers gradually upgrade to higher-margin products C) anywhere from 25 to 95 percent, because each retained buyer adds years of margin the firm never has to spend to win again D) approximately 100 percent, because service firms carry 100 percent variable-cost structures that scale with volume E) no measurable amount, because service firms' margins are set by labor costs, not customer retention rates

Answer: C

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 2.7 — The chapter cites Reichheld and Sasser (1990) directly: cutting customer losses by five points lifted profit anywhere from 25 to 95 percent across roughly a hundred service firms. The mechanism is the compounding of years of margin on buyers the firm already paid to win, not a one-for-one arithmetic relationship.

Tags: retention; Reichheld; customer lifetime value; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 18

Clayton Christensen's "jobs to be done" reframing of the marketing orientation holds that buyers do not buy products, they hire products to do a job. Which of the following examples from our chapter best illustrates this principle in action?

A) Costco prices groceries near cost and earns its profit on membership fees, so the economic job the buyer hires it for is saving money on the total household bill B) The US railroads kept perfecting locomotive engines while buyers hired them for transportation, so cars, trucks, and planes eventually took the hire C) Liquid Death built its brand on a can, so the can performs the job of delivering water to a thirsty consumer D) Patagonia charges a premium for gear that lasts a decade, so it performs the job of durable apparel for an outdoor enthusiast E) The AMA definition covers all ten entity types, because buyers hire different entities to do different jobs across categories

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 2.3 / LO 2.4 — The railroad case is the chapter's own worked example of JTBD in the context of marketing myopia. The buyers hired the railroads for transportation, and when cars and planes did that job better, the hire went elsewhere. "The buyer was loyal to the job, so the railroads kept perfecting locomotives while cars and planes took the trips."

Tags: jobs to be done; Christensen; marketing orientation; marketing myopia; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 19

The chapter introduces the four-part positioning statement as a tool that forces the firm to name its buyer and benefit, not its features. The standard form fills four slots. Which set of four slots is correct?

A) Product category, price tier, distribution channel, and key message B) Target customer, statement of the want, product category, and key benefit C) Competitive frame, point of difference, reason to believe, and brand essence D) Value proposition, points of parity, points of difference, and brand promise E) Customer segment, customer insight, brand personality, and brand purpose

Answer: B

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 2.6 — The chapter states the four-part positioning statement exactly: "For a target customer who has a want, the brand is a product category that delivers a key benefit." It cites Dolan's Framework reading and Kotler's point that solving the positioning problem is what lets the firm solve the marketing-mix problem. The test a useful position passes is that it names the buyer and the benefit, not the firm and the feature.

Tags: positioning statement; four-part statement; targeting; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 20

A firm's marketing configuration can only pay on repeat if three numbers stack favorably. Which set of three numbers does our chapter identify?

A) Gross margin, revenue growth rate, and market share B) Share of voice, share of wallet, and net promoter score C) Penetration, customer lifetime value, and retention rate D) Brand awareness, brand preference, and brand loyalty index E) Cost per acquisition, cost per click, and conversion rate

Answer: C

Diff: 1

Objective: LO 2.7 — The chapter names these three numbers explicitly and explains how they stack: penetration tells you how many different people buy you; customer lifetime value tells you what one of those buyers is worth; retention tells you how long you can keep her. A five-point improvement in retention swings lifetime value disproportionately because average tenure scales with the inverse of churn.

Tags: penetration; CLV; retention; marketing metrics; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Section II — Scenario / Short-Answer Items (4 items)

SA-1

A software startup is trying to set a direction for its go-to-market strategy. The CEO says: "We have great engineering. Once we finish the product, sales will take care of itself." A co-founder argues: "We should start by talking to ten potential buyers, understand the job they are currently hiring a workaround to do, and build the product around that." Identify which business orientation each co-founder reflects and explain which better fits the marketing concept. Use at least two framework terms from our chapter in your answer.

Sample answer: The CEO reflects the product orientation, which assumes the buyer rewards whichever firm builds the most refined or highest-performing thing, so the engineering bench becomes the center of the firm. The risk is marketing myopia: defining the firm by the product it makes rather than the job buyers hire it for. If a competitor or a simpler workaround does that job better or more cheaply, the excellent product loses the hire regardless of quality. The co-founder reflects the marketing orientation (and specifically a jobs-to-be-done framing), which flips the starting question from "how do we find buyers for the product we made?" to "how do we find a product for the buyers who already have a problem?" The marketing concept holds that the firm should find the right products for its customers, not the right customers for its products. For a startup with limited resources, the co-founder's path also targets the smallest viable audience, deliberately picking the tightest group of buyers the firm can serve well enough to generate word-of-mouth, instead of building a product and hoping a mass audience finds it.

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 2.3, LO 2.4

Tags: marketing concept; product orientation; jobs to be done; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

SA-2

A direct-to-consumer candle brand has built strong Instagram engagement and earns favorable reviews for its scent quality. Its founder calculates that it costs $28 to acquire a new customer but that buyers only repurchase once on average before churning. The brand is currently losing money despite growing sales. Using the chapter's three-number framework (penetration, customer lifetime value, and retention), diagnose the specific failure point and propose one marketing action that would address it at the root cause rather than the symptom.

Sample answer: The three numbers reveal the diagnosis clearly. Penetration is working: the Instagram following and positive reviews suggest the brand is adding new distinct buyers. CLV is the failure point, and retention is the root driver. If buyers repurchase only once on average, the average tenure is roughly two purchase cycles. With a $28 acquisition cost and only two cycles of margin, the brand is spending more to acquire buyers than they return. A posted price increase or aggressive ad spend (addressing symptoms) would not fix the root problem. The chapter's logic shows that cutting churn even slightly swings CLV hard, because average tenure is approximately one divided by the share who leave. The action that addresses root cause is something that improves retention: a subscription model that smooths repurchase (lowering the behavioral friction of remembering to buy), a scent-subscription flight that creates a product-discovery reason to stay, or a loyalty reward for the third purchase that moves the average tenure by one cycle. Once retention improves, the acquisition cost ceiling rises and the configuration can become profitable without changing the acquisition spend.

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 2.7

Tags: penetration; retention; CLV; acquisition cost; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

SA-3

Stanley was a century-old brand that sold stainless-steel thermoses primarily to construction workers and hunters. In roughly thirty-six months its 40-ounce Quencher drove annual sales from about $73 million to about $750 million, with its buyer base flipping to approximately 98 percent women aged 35 to 44. Using the chapter's five-decision framework, identify which single decision changed first and explain how the other four had to realign behind it.

Sample answer: The targeting decision (who to serve) changed first. Stanley's product team recognized an underserved buyer in the women's wellness and aesthetics community after observing organic social sharing by women who had discovered the Quencher independently. That single targeting shift cascaded through the other four. What to make: the product line was expanded with seasonal colorways and collaborations (Target, Starbucks) relevant to the new buyer's identity-driven purchasing. What to charge: prices held at a premium tier that fits the aspirational lifestyle positioning the new buyer was willing to pay. Where to sell: distribution shifted toward Target and its online presence, the channels the new buyer already used. How to talk about it: the communication moved to TikTok-native social proof, influencer gifting, and limited-release drops that create scarcity and social sharing rather than the functional durability messaging used for the original construction-worker buyer. The Quencher case shows the chapter's principle in action: targeting lines the other four decisions up behind it, so a deliberate shift in the who-to-serve decision requires a coherence check across all five.

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 2.2, LO 2.6

Tags: five decisions; targeting; Stanley; coherence; AACSB: Application of knowledge

SA-4

The chapter describes the 5Cs situation analysis (Customer, Company, Collaborators, Competitors, Context) as the input that precedes the five marketing decisions. A regional fast-casual chain is planning to launch a plant-based protein bowl. A marketing intern runs a 5C analysis and finds: strong customer demand from 25- to 35-year-olds; the company has a well-staffed kitchen but no supply chain for plant-based proteins; existing collaborators (produce distributors) do not carry the needed inputs; the three nearest direct competitors already offer plant-based options at a lower price point; and the regulatory context around labeling claims for plant-based proteins is tightening. Identify the weakest C and explain which of the five marketing decisions it most directly exposes.

Sample answer: The weakest C is Collaborators. The firm's existing produce distributors do not carry plant-based protein inputs, which means the supply chain for the new menu item does not yet exist. This directly exposes the "what to make" decision: the product cannot be consistently produced at quality and volume without a reliable ingredient supply. It also creates a secondary exposure in the "where to sell it" decision, because without the right supply chain, menu availability across all locations cannot be guaranteed and the rollout may be forced to begin in select units only. Note also that the Competitors C is concerning (existing lower-priced alternatives already in market), but the supply-chain gap is more fundamental because it threatens the ability to produce the item at all, which is upstream of pricing, positioning, and distribution choices. The correct response is to resolve the collaborator gap first by sourcing a new distribution partner or direct supplier for plant-based proteins before committing to a launch date or finalizing the price.

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 2.2, LO 2.6

Tags: 5Cs; situation analysis; five decisions; supply chain; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

Section III — AI-Integration Items (4 items)

These items are original to this course and do not appear in any Kotler bank. They test students' ability to evaluate AI-generated marketing analysis, identify which framework an AI output applies, and flag what is missing or misleading.

AI-1 — Identify the Orientation in AI Output

A student asks an AI assistant: "How should my new organic soap brand approach marketing?" The AI responds:

"Focus on making the best possible product. Invest in sourcing the highest-quality organic oils, develop a distinctive scent profile no competitor has, and your product will naturally attract customers who appreciate craftsmanship. Great products speak for themselves."

Which business orientation does this AI output reflect, and what critical perspective does it fail to include?

A) It reflects the production orientation; it fails to include distribution strategy. B) It reflects the selling orientation; it fails to include a promotional plan for gaining awareness. C) It reflects the product orientation; it fails to ask who the buyer is, what job she is hiring the soap to do, or whether anyone has already named the brand's target customer. D) It reflects the marketing orientation; it fails to specify what price the buyer is willing to pay. E) It reflects the holistic orientation; it fails to address social-responsibility commitments.

Answer: C

Diff: 2

Objective: LO 2.3, LO 2.4 — The AI output is a textbook product orientation: it centers the firm's effort on building the most refined product and trusts that quality will attract buyers without identifying the buyer, the job, or the competitive alternatives she is already using. The chapter's lesson is that marketing myopia lives inside "great products speak for themselves." An AI tool trained on marketing cliches will reproduce this orientation fluently unless the student's prompt specifies the buyer and the job first.

Tags: AI output; product orientation; marketing myopia; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

AI-2 — Evaluate an AI-Drafted Positioning Statement

A marketing student asks Claude to draft a positioning statement for a sustainable athletic shoe brand targeting college women. The AI produces:

"For health-conscious consumers who care about the planet, GreenStride is a premium athletic footwear brand that combines style and sustainability."

Using the chapter's four-part positioning-statement framework, identify which slot is underfilled and explain why the current wording fails the buyer-and-benefit test.

A) The "product category" slot is underfilled, because "premium athletic footwear brand" is not a specific enough category description. B) The "key benefit" slot is underfilled, because "combines style and sustainability" is buzzword filler that any competitor could claim word for word without changing a single fact about its product. C) The "target customer" slot is underfilled, because "health-conscious consumers who care about the planet" is vague enough to apply to almost any buyer in any category. D) Both B and C are underfilled, and the statement would collapse if you asked whether either phrase is a real, differentiated choice the brand has made. E) The statement is complete as written; all four slots are specific and defensible.

Answer: D

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 2.6 — The chapter's acid test for a positioning statement is whether each slot is a real choice or buzzword filler a competitor could claim word for word. "Health-conscious consumers who care about the planet" applies to half the sustainable-goods market. "Combines style and sustainability" is the generic sustainable-brand claim used by every entrant in this space. A good AI-generated first draft surfaces these gaps immediately when you ask "which of these four elements is a real choice and which is filler a competitor could match?" The item tests whether the student can run that check rather than accept a plausible-sounding output as done.

Tags: AI positioning; positioning statement; four-part statement; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Written communication

AI-3 — Diagnose AI Coherence Analysis

A brand manager asks an AI assistant to evaluate a proposed marketing change for a grocery store chain known for low prices, large packs, and a members-only shopping model. The proposed change: eliminate the membership fee to remove the barrier to first visit and attract more casual shoppers. The AI responds:

"Removing the fee will lower the barrier to entry, increase the potential shopper pool, and is consistent with the trend toward frictionless retail experiences. The change aligns with the broader marketing goal of maximizing customer reach."

Which framework from our chapter does the AI apply, and what critical problem in the five-decision system does it overlook?

A) The AI applies the 4Cs framework and overlooks that convenience-to-buy is already the brand's strength, making the change redundant. B) The AI applies the selling orientation and overlooks that pushing more buyers into the store requires a corresponding increase in advertising spend. C) The AI applies a penetration-growth argument and overlooks that the membership fee is the profit engine of the entire configuration, so removing it breaks the coherence of all five decisions — the price, the product assortment, the distribution model, and the no-advertising posture all depend on the fee funding them. D) The AI applies the four A's framework and overlooks that affordability, not accessibility, is the real barrier to first visit for casual shoppers. E) The AI is correct; maximizing customer reach is the right strategic priority for a retailer competing against Amazon.

Answer: C

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 2.2, LO 2.7 — The chapter's Costco analysis shows that the membership fee is not a price the buyer pays before shopping but the profit engine that makes the whole configuration run: it funds near-cost grocery pricing, the tight SKU assortment, the no-ad posture, and the high-retention renewal. The AI output has taken a legitimate general principle (lower barriers to entry) and applied it without checking whether the proposed change breaks the coherence of the other four decisions. This is the most common error an AI marketing tool makes: optimizing one variable without modeling the system.

Tags: AI coherence analysis; five decisions; Costco; membership fee; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

AI-4 — Evaluate an AI-Drafted Crisis Response

A fast-casual restaurant chain made a social media post that unintentionally appeared to mock a group of loyal customers. Within twenty-four hours the brand received thousands of angry comments, with the dominant emotions coded (by a brief qualitative scan) as broken trust and embarrassment at having publicly endorsed the brand. A junior marketer uses an AI assistant to draft a response. The AI produces:

"We have been serving communities across the country for fifteen years and are proud to be a local partner in hundreds of towns. Our team reflects the diversity we celebrate, and we remain committed to bringing people together over great food. We appreciate our fans' passion for our brand."

Using the chapter's Situational Crisis Communication Theory framework, identify the response family this AI draft reflects and explain the specific failure.

Sample answer: The AI draft reflects a Bolstering response, the fourth option in Coombs's SCCT framework, which recites the firm's past good deeds and general values without acknowledging the harm the audience is actually feeling. The chapter (and the peer-reviewed Bud Light study it cites) identifies Bolstering as the worst-case choice when the dominant buyer emotions are broken trust and lost control, because it addresses neither. The Rebuild response family is what the dominant emotions call for: a direct acknowledgment that a group of loyal buyers was hurt by the post, a statement of why that matters, and a concrete change to prevent recurrence, even a small one such as a new review process for social content. The AI draft makes the situation worse by leading with the brand's past service, which reads as "we have been good before, so this doesn't count" rather than "we understand why you feel betrayed." The Bolstering move was exactly what Anheuser-Busch InBev chose after the April 2023 Bud Light misstep, and the chapter documents that it allowed the break to spread to all five decisions over the following months.

Diff: 3

Objective: LO 2.8 — The item requires students to recognize the Bolstering response family in an AI-drafted output, diagnose why it is the wrong choice for the specific emotional state of the audience (broken trust), and prescribe the Rebuild alternative. It extends the chapter's Bud Light analysis to a new, lower-stakes scenario where the same framework applies, which is the AI-integration skill this course builds toward: knowing how to evaluate what a general-purpose AI produces against the specific analytical frameworks the chapter teaches.

Tags: AI crisis response; SCCT; Coombs; Bolstering vs Rebuild; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Written communication

Chapter 6 STP — Test Bank

Chapter: 6 — Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning

Learning Objectives (Ch6, Our Chapter)

  • LO 6.1 Explain why customer heterogeneity makes segmentation necessary and identify the three ways a firm can respond to it (mass marketing, segment-based, one-to-one).
  • LO 6.2 Apply the four segmentation bases (geographic, demographic, psychographic, behavioral) and identify the demand-space concept as the most actionable behavioral cut.
  • LO 6.3 Evaluate candidate segments using the five-criteria usability filter (measurable, accessible, substantial, differentiable, actionable).
  • LO 6.4 Distinguish target compatibility from target attractiveness and explain why compatibility is evaluated before attractiveness.
  • LO 6.5 Describe positioning as work done in the buyer's mind, use the mental-ladder metaphor, and apply the three positioning moves (claim the rung, find the creneau, reposition the competition).
  • LO 6.6 Define points of parity (POP) and points of difference (POD), apply the three POD tests (desirable, deliverable, differentiating), and distinguish the three types of POP (category, correlational, competitive).
  • LO 6.7 Build and interpret a perceptual map, apply the empty-quadrant rule, and explain why an empty quadrant requires buyer ideal-point confirmation.
  • LO 6.8 Explain the double-jeopardy law and duplication-of-purchase law, and reconcile the narrow-targeting tradition with the broad-penetration tradition.
  • LO 6.9 Define mental availability and physical availability as the two market-based growth assets, and connect them to the Distinctiveness Grid and the 7W category-entry-point audit.

Section I — Multiple-Choice Items (22 items)

Item 1

A seller who ignores differences in what buyers want and serves the average buyer of an imagined crowd is falling into what the chapter calls _______.

A) the creneau trap, where an empty market position draws in too many rivals B) the everybody trap, where refusing to choose a target means the brand stands for nothing specific to anyone C) mass customization, where one platform is tailored to each individual buyer D) undifferentiated segmentation, where segment cuts are drawn too broadly E) the correlational parity failure, where brand strengths imply weaknesses the firm never corrected

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 6.1 — The everybody trap is the failure mode of a firm that refuses to choose. The buyer's head holds room for three to seven brands per category, and a brand with no clear claim never makes the list.

Tags: customer heterogeneity; everybody trap; mass marketing; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 2

Two customers reach for the same forty-ounce insulated cup on the same shelf. One needs it for a construction worksite; the other likes that the color matches her car's interior. These two buyers illustrate _______.

A) the duplication-of-purchase law, which shows buyers of one brand also buy others B) customer heterogeneity, the variation in what buyers want that makes segmentation necessary C) the demand-space framework, which cuts the market by occasion rather than by person D) the Pareto distribution, where a small share of buyers drives most category volume E) the five-criteria usability filter, which evaluates whether a slice is worth serving

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 6.1 — Customer heterogeneity is the formal name for the variation in what buyers want. Two people reaching for the same shelf can want completely different things from the same product, and that variation is the precondition that makes segmentation necessary.

Tags: customer heterogeneity; segmentation rationale; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 3

Stitch Fix assembles a clothing box against a single customer's style file, building a unique selection for each individual buyer rather than for a group. This is best described as _______.

A) undifferentiated targeting, where one offer goes to everyone B) differentiated targeting, where several segments each receive a tailored offer C) one-to-one marketing, where each individual buyer gets an offer built for them D) concentrated targeting, where one small segment is served deeply E) behavioral segmentation, where observed usage patterns drive the offer

Answer: C

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 6.1 — One-to-one marketing pushes segmentation to its limit, where each buyer gets an offer built for them. The chapter notes it is usually a retention tactic rather than a strategy for the whole market, because it only pays off when each buyer's lifetime value is high enough to cover the cost.

Tags: one-to-one marketing; targeting levels; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 4

A brand wants to cut its market using a variable that reads what buyers actually do rather than who they are or what they say. The chapter identifies this as the most predictive base because it _______.

A) is the only base that captures demographic variation across income groups B) reads observed behavior instead of taking a buyer at their word, since stated intent overstates loyalty C) requires only publicly available data and is therefore the cheapest base to use D) measures the geographic density of category buyers rather than individual purchase patterns E) focuses on attitudes and lifestyle variables that drive brand preference directly

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 6.2 — Behavioral segmentation cuts buyers by what they actually do, and it is the most predictive of the four bases because it reads behavior instead of taking a buyer at their word. Stated intent overstates loyalty by a known margin in every category studied.

Tags: behavioral segmentation; segmentation bases; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 5

In beer, the heaviest-drinking fifth of buyers accounts for nearly ninety percent of the volume. A brand that cannot identify that fifth is targeting blind. This concentration pattern is called _______.

A) the everybody trap B) benefit segmentation C) the Pareto distribution D) the double-jeopardy law E) the duplication-of-purchase law

Answer: C

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 6.2 — The Pareto distribution, the 80/20 rule, is the pattern most often found in behavioral segmentation. In beer the heaviest-drinking fifth accounts for close to ninety percent of volume. A brand that cannot name that fifth targets blind.

Tags: behavioral segmentation; Pareto distribution; 80/20 rule; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 6

Knorr reframed its packet soup in India from a Western dinner starter to a healthy pre-dinner snack for children, the occasion Indian mothers were already trying to fill. Sales rose 127 percent in six months on a product that was never reformulated. This result illustrates the concept of _______.

A) repositioning the competition, where the firm reframes a rival's strength as a liability B) the demand space, where cutting the market by the occasion that triggers need drives growth without acquiring new buyers C) the differentiating test, where a point of difference is confirmed as one no rival can match D) the five-criteria usability filter, where the segment proved actionable at scale E) benefit segmentation, where survey-identified benefit clusters are layered onto demographic descriptors

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 6.2 — The demand space is the intersection of a usage occasion and the needs that occasion carries. Knorr's reframe moved the same product into an occasion Indian mothers were already trying to fill, which is the practical application of demand-space thinking. Growth came from owning the right occasion, not from acquiring new buyers.

Tags: demand space; behavioral segmentation; occasion-based marketing; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 7

A 1968 study divided the toothpaste category into four groups: Worriers (decay prevention), Sociables (bright teeth), Sensories (flavor), and Independents (lowest price). Two buyers in the same age and income bracket fell into different groups. The segmentation base these groups represent is _______.

A) geographic, because the groups were separated by store proximity and shopping distance B) demographic, because age and income were the primary sorting variables C) psychographic, because the groups reflect different attitudes toward health and appearance D) benefit segmentation, where the cause of the choice, the benefit wanted, is the root variable E) behavioral, because the groups were separated by brand loyalty and usage rate

Answer: D

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 6.2 — Benefit segmentation cuts the market by the benefit buyers seek, which is the cause of the choice. The four toothpaste groups share overlapping demographics but want different outcomes, so a demographic cut collapses them and misses the whole structure. Benefit segmentation belongs at the root of any segmentation analysis.

Tags: benefit segmentation; segmentation bases; toothpaste study; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 8

A startup identifies a global luxury-watch segment it believes is large and growing. But the firm has no distribution, no retail presence, and no budget to reach those buyers. Which criterion of the five-criteria usability filter does this segment fail?

A) Measurable, because the startup cannot estimate the segment's size B) Substantial, because the segment is too small to justify the investment C) Differentiable, because luxury buyers respond the same as mass-market buyers D) Actionable, because the firm cannot design and execute a program for the segment E) Accessible, because the segment has no identifiable media channels

Answer: D

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 6.3 — Actionable means the firm has the resources to design and execute a program for the segment. A slice that fails this test is research, not a target. The chapter notes that a small firm identifying a global luxury-watch segment may lack the distribution, budget, and presence to act.

Tags: five-criteria usability filter; actionable; segment evaluation; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 9

A chain coffee brand wants to target "college freshmen in large US cities." The firm knows the size and purchasing power of this group from campus enrollment data, can reach them through TikTok and campus visits, and has confirmed they respond differently to its campus menu than older buyers do. However, the segment is so narrow that the revenue it generates would not cover the cost of a separate marketing program for it. Which criterion does this segment fail?

A) Measurable B) Accessible C) Substantial D) Differentiable E) Actionable

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 6.3 — Substantial means the segment is large and profitable enough to repay the cost of serving it. The chapter notes that 2,000 households is substantial for Wagner Custom Skis but not for a national brand. A segment that is measurable, accessible, and differentiable but too small to recoup a separate program's cost fails the substantial test.

Tags: five-criteria usability filter; substantial; segment evaluation; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 10

A large consumer-goods firm is evaluating two segments. Segment A is the largest in the category, growing fast, and full of profitable buyers. Segment B is smaller but matches the firm's existing brand, distribution, and skill set well. According to our chapter, which segment should the firm evaluate first and why?

A) Segment A, because attractiveness is the primary criterion and a larger prize justifies any fight B) Segment B, because smaller segments are always easier to win and less expensive to serve C) Segment B, because compatibility comes before attractiveness: a segment you cannot outcompete a stronger rival in is the wrong fight regardless of its size D) Segment A, because market share growth in a fast-growing segment outweighs the cost of the competitive fight E) Both segments simultaneously, because compatibility and attractiveness are weighted equally and neither is evaluated first

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 6.4 — The chapter states that targeting hunts for the segment you can win, not the biggest one. Target compatibility, the ability to create more value for those buyers than rivals can, is evaluated before target attractiveness. Segment A fails compatibility if a stronger rival already serves those buyers better.

Tags: target compatibility; target attractiveness; targeting sequence; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 11

In 1962 Avis stopped arguing with buyers who ranked it second behind Hertz and instead built a campaign on the line "We try harder." The first year the firm cleared $1.2 million in profit. What the chapter describes here is _______.

A) the creneau move, where the firm invented a new positioning frame the leader had not claimed B) claiming the rung the buyer had already handed it and asking buyers to value the effort behind second place C) repositioning the competition, where the firm changed what buyers believed about Hertz D) a correlational point of parity, where Avis neutralized the assumption that second place meant inferior service E) straddle positioning, where the firm competed in the rental-car and logistics frames at once

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 6.5 — The first and cheapest positioning move is to take the rung the buyer has already handed you and claim it out loud. Avis had the second-place rung; the campaign named it and asked buyers to value the extra effort that came with being number two.

Tags: mental ladder; positioning moves; Avis; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 12

The buyer's memory holds brands in a ranked order, one per category. In cola, most American buyers report Coke on the top rung, Pepsi on the second, and few can name a fourth without help. The chapter calls this structure a _______.

A) perceptual map, which plots brands on two attribute axes to reveal empty positioning spaces B) mental ladder, where buyers carry a ranked list of brands in each category and the top rung wins most decisions by default C) Distinctiveness Grid, which scores each brand asset on prevalence and uniqueness D) positioning statement template, which defines the target, need, category, difference, and reason to believe E) demand space, which links a usage occasion to the functional and emotional needs it carries

Answer: B

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 6.5 — A mental ladder is the picture positioning theorists use for how a buyer's memory ranks brands in a category. One ladder per category, three rungs is typical, and the brand on the top rung wins most decisions by default.

Tags: mental ladder; positioning; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 13

In the early 1970s Tylenol ran print ads that listed aspirin's side effects before the Tylenol name even appeared, forcing buyers to separate the molecule from the pain relief it provided. The chapter calls this move _______.

A) finding a creneau, where an empty position is identified and claimed B) straddle positioning, where the brand competed in two frames at once C) repositioning the competition, where the firm changes what buyers believe about a rival D) a competitive point of parity, where the firm matches a rival's point of difference just enough to neutralize it E) claiming the rung the buyer already handed the brand and defending it against challengers

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 6.5 — Repositioning the competition is the hardest of the three positioning moves; it changes what the buyer values so a rival's strength stops looking like one. Tylenol listed aspirin's side effects until the rung dissolved under Bayer, then stepped into the space.

Tags: repositioning the competition; positioning moves; Tylenol; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 14

A coffee shop must serve coffee to be considered a legitimate option in the category. This is an example of a _______.

A) competitive POP, which matches a rival's point of difference just enough to neutralize it B) correlational POP, which defends against a bad inference a brand's strength would otherwise trigger C) point of difference, because no rival can claim the same attribute at the same level D) category POP, which is the price of admission every brand needs to be considered at all E) differentiating test failure, because the attribute is held by every brand and fails the test

Answer: D

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 6.6 — A category POP is the price of admission that earns a brand consideration in its category. A coffee shop must serve coffee. The absence of a category POP disqualifies the brand before any brand comparison starts.

Tags: points of parity; category POP; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 15

Trader Joe's prices its private-label items far below a conventional grocer and simultaneously emphasizes unusual food quality. The chapter describes the quality claim as a _______ point of parity, because buyers assume cheap means low quality and the firm has to say out loud that it has not sacrificed quality to get to the low price.

A) category B) competitive C) correlational D) deliverable E) differentiating

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 6.6 — A correlational POP defends against a bad assumption a brand's own strength would otherwise trigger. Buyers assume quality and price trade off, so a brand that wins on low price has to address the inferred quality sacrifice. Trader Joe's quality claim is the chapter's case.

Tags: points of parity; correlational POP; Trader Joe's; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 16

Before building a campaign on a point of difference, a brand team runs it through three tests. One test asks: do buyers in the target actually want this attribute, and do they name it on their own without prompting? This is the _______ test.

A) Deliverable B) Differentiating C) Desirable D) Defensive E) Distinctive

Answer: C

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 6.6 — The desirable test asks whether buyers in the target want the attribute and name it unprompted when asked what matters about the category. A POD that buyers have to be coached to care about is not a real point of difference.

Tags: POD three tests; desirable; points of difference; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 17

A brand manager plots three competing insulated-drinkware brands on a perceptual map with price on the x-axis and ruggedness/heritage on the y-axis. She finds all three brands clustered in the upper-left quadrant (heritage, mid-price) and an empty quadrant in the lower-right (premium price, sleek aesthetic). She proposes going after the empty quadrant. What question does the chapter say she must answer before treating it as an opportunity?

A) Whether the firm has the production capacity to enter a new segment B) Whether buyer ideal points actually cluster in that quadrant, because without buyers in it the open space is dead air, not an opening C) Whether the other three brands will reposition into the quadrant once they notice it D) Whether the empty quadrant falls on the correct side of the competitive-compatibility grid E) Whether the axes she chose are the ones the firm's media buyer can actually purchase

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 6.7 — The empty-quadrant rule states that an empty quadrant is an opening only when buyer ideal points cluster in it. The chapter states: "Without the buyers on the map, dead air and real demand look identical." Plotting brand positions alone is the first step, but adding ideal-point data is the confirmation step.

Tags: perceptual map; empty-quadrant rule; positioning; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 18

Coca-Cola is bought by about three-quarters of soda category buyers, roughly nine times a year each. Dr Pepper is bought by about a quarter of category buyers, roughly seven times each. The chapter uses this pattern to illustrate the _______, which shows that a smaller brand gets hit twice: fewer people buy it, and the buyers it keeps purchase slightly less often.

A) duplication-of-purchase law B) everybody trap C) double-jeopardy law D) Distinctiveness Grid penalty E) five-criteria accessibility failure

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 6.8 — The double-jeopardy law is the four-decade finding that smaller brands suffer two penalties at once: a smaller buyer pool and a slightly lower purchase rate among that pool. Both move together, and the loyalty gap is always too small to close the penetration gap.

Tags: double-jeopardy law; Sharp; brand growth; penetration; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 19

A UK chocolate bar ran the line "It's not for girls," which cut off half of every buyer pool it could have reached. Sales fell for years afterward. The chapter uses this case to illustrate _______.

A) the creneau strategy gone wrong, where a new category frame attracted the wrong buyers B) the cost of narrow targeting when the double-jeopardy and duplication-of-purchase laws are at work: cutting off half the category costs exactly that in lost reach C) a failed competitive POP attempt that left the brand without category membership D) the everybody trap applied in reverse: serving too small a target rather than too large a one E) a perceptual map error, where the brand occupied a position no buyers wanted

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 6.8 — The Yorkie case is the chapter's proof that narrow targeting has a direct cost when the penetration evidence is staring back at you. The double-jeopardy and duplication-of-purchase laws together mean that growth comes from reaching more category buyers, not fewer, and a campaign that cuts off half the pool pays a measurable penalty.

Tags: Yorkie; narrow targeting cost; double jeopardy; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Item 20

The two market-based growth assets in Byron Sharp's model are _____ and _____, where the first is how easy a brand is to bring to mind in a buying situation and the second is how easy a brand is to find and buy.

A) brand equity and brand loyalty B) points of parity and points of difference C) mental availability and physical availability D) target compatibility and target attractiveness E) prevalence and uniqueness

Answer: C

Difficulty: 1

Objective: LO 6.9 — Mental availability is how easily a brand comes to mind in a buying situation, built from the breadth and freshness of links to buying occasions. Physical availability is how easy the brand is to find and buy: distribution breadth and shelf presence. A brand wins only when it is both easy to think of and easy to buy.

Tags: mental availability; physical availability; Sharp; brand growth; AACSB: Reflective thinking

Item 21

The Distinctiveness Grid scores each brand asset on two axes. The horizontal axis is prevalence: the share of category buyers who attribute the asset to the brand. The vertical axis is uniqueness: one minus the share who would attribute it to a competitor. An asset in the upper-right corner of the grid is _______.

A) one the brand should retire, because it is recognized but generic B) one the brand should build, because it is unique but invisible C) one the brand should refresh, because it is neither unique nor widely attributed D) an icon the brand should defend, because category buyers attribute it to this brand and not to a rival E) a competitive POP, because rivals already share the asset and it only earns category membership

Answer: D

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 6.9 — Assets in the upper-right of the Distinctiveness Grid are icons: high prevalence (most category buyers attribute it to this brand) and high uniqueness (most buyers would not attribute it to a rival). These are the assets the brand defends. The other three corners sort into build (high uniqueness, low prevalence), refresh (high prevalence, low uniqueness), and retire (low on both).

Tags: Distinctiveness Grid; mental availability; brand assets; AACSB: Analytical thinking

Item 22

The 7W category-entry-point audit asks buyers to spontaneously connect a brand to occasions across seven prompts: why, when, where, while doing what, with or for whom, with what, and how they feel. A brand a buyer links to nine or more occasions scores at the top of the category. The audit is a diagnostic for _______.

A) physical availability, because it measures how many distribution channels carry the brand B) mental availability, because it counts how broadly a brand is linked to the buying occasions buyers carry in memory C) the five-criteria usability filter, because it measures whether the segment is accessible through multiple media D) perceptual mapping, because it tracks how buyers rank the brand on key attributes relative to rivals E) target attractiveness, because each occasion represents a potential revenue opportunity for the firm

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 6.9 — The 7W audit is a tool for building and measuring mental availability: the breadth of links between the brand and the occasions buyers carry in memory. A brand linked to one narrow occasion is exactly the brand the duplication-of-purchase law catches first, because it shows up in fewer buying situations than a brand linked to many.

Tags: 7W audit; category entry points; mental availability; AACSB: Application of knowledge

Section II — Scenario / Short-Answer Items (4 items)

SA-1

A startup is launching a portable espresso maker aimed at "coffee lovers who travel frequently." The founder wants to skip the five-criteria filter because the target "obviously works" given the team's personal experience. As the brand's first marketing hire, walk the segment through all five criteria and identify which one or two are most likely to create problems. Then explain what the founder would need to verify before committing a launch budget.

Sample answer: The five criteria are measurable, accessible, substantial, differentiable, and actionable. Measurable: the firm needs to estimate how many US travelers make espresso at home and how many are willing to buy a portable device, from sources like market research reports or travel behavior surveys. If the team is guessing from personal experience, this criterion is not yet met. Accessible: can the target be reached through identifiable media or channels at a cost the launch budget can cover? "Frequent travelers who love espresso" is a strategic sentence, not a media buy; the team needs to translate it into platform-purchasable descriptors (TikTok coffee content, Delta SkyMiles frequent-flyer lists, travel retail buyers). Substantial: is the segment large and profitable enough to cover the cost of a dedicated product launch? A few thousand buyers is substantial for a boutique, not for a funded startup projecting growth. Differentiable: do frequent-traveling espresso lovers respond differently to a portable device than casual travelers or home coffee drinkers? If the same message works for all three, there is no benefit to treating them as a separate segment. Actionable: does the team have the manufacturing, distribution, and marketing capacity to serve this segment? The two most likely problem criteria are substantial (the team needs market-size data, not gut feel) and accessible (the strategic sentence needs a translation into buyable descriptors before a media plan can be written). Until both are verified, the launch budget is a bet, not a plan.

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 6.3, LO 6.4

Tags: five-criteria usability filter; segment evaluation; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Analytical thinking

SA-2

Stanley's brand team in 2018 read low Quencher tumbler sales as a signal that the product was weak, and the firm nearly discontinued it. The actual buyer, a woman aged 25 to 50 drinking from her desk all day, appeared on a shopping blog rather than in the company's own dashboard data. Using the chapter's targeting framework, identify the specific targeting failure Stanley made and explain how the five-criteria filter and the compatibility-before-attractiveness rule would have caught it earlier.

Sample answer: Stanley's failure was a target-compatibility error hidden behind a target-attractiveness read. The firm's dashboard was measuring the segment it had chosen (outdoorsmen aged 35 to 55), where the Quencher's unit sales looked weak. But it was not asking whether a different segment existed that the firm was also well positioned to serve. Run the new buyer through the five-criteria filter: measurable (yes, census data and blog analytics could estimate the size), accessible (yes, the same shopping blog that drove the wholesale order was the channel), substantial (yes, the sales spike after the blog post showed the group was large enough), differentiable (yes, this buyer's need, a 40-ounce desk companion in pastel colors, was different from the outdoor buyer's need for a rugged worksite bottle), and actionable (yes, Stanley's existing manufacturing, heritage, and retail presence transferred directly). Every criterion passed. The compatibility-before-attractiveness rule would have caught it by asking: can we create more value for this buyer than rivals can? Stanley's existing product, packaging, and brand story matched the new buyer's job exactly. The chapter's lesson is that a firm's data system can miss a segment entirely if the segment was never in the dashboard's question list. The refit rule, comparing each quarter's segment table to the last and treating stability below 70 percent as a signal to re-cut, would have flagged the new buyer pool within a month of the blog traffic spike.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 6.3, LO 6.4

Tags: Stanley; target compatibility; five-criteria filter; segment discovery; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Reflective thinking

SA-3

Liquid Death entered a mature, low-margin bottled-water category by leaving the product ordinary and putting all the work on the meaning: canned Alpine water under the tagline "Murder Your Thirst," aimed at buyers for whom the wellness look had stopped feeling aspirational. A 2025 price test found the labeled Liquid Death can earned about 27 percent more revenue per unit than the same water sold unbranded at the same shelf price. Using the chapter's POP/POD framework, identify Liquid Death's category POPs and its main point of difference, then run that POD through the three tests.

Sample answer: Category POPs: Liquid Death must hold the same category-membership attributes every water brand needs. The water is real Alpine water, so it passes the product category test. It is sold in a standard retail bottle aisle, so distribution POPs are met. The category's expected attributes (safe, potable, available) are present. Main point of difference: the brand meaning, the heavy-metal/punk identity carried by the can design, the tagline, and the entire visual language. No other bottled-water brand offers identity in the anti-wellness, aggressive-humor frame. Now run the three tests. Desirable: yes, the 27 percent price premium at the same shelf price shows buyers are willing to pay for the meaning, and the 1.4-billion-dollar valuation demonstrates demand. Deliverable: yes, the meaning is delivered every time the can is held in public. The visual cues (the can design, the wordmark) prove it repeatable and verifiable. Differentiating: yes. The chapter notes that no rival can already credibly claim the punk frame for water. Smartwater, Evian, and Voss all compete on the wellness/prestige frame. Liquid Death owns the only position a twenty-to-thirty-five-year-old buyer can use as an identity prop against wellness culture. All three tests pass. The chapter's broader point is that this is a creneau move: the wellness frame was a red ocean of incumbents, and the punk frame was an empty ladder rung no one had bothered to claim.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 6.6, LO 6.5, LO 6.7

Tags: Liquid Death; POPs/PODs; three tests; creneau; AACSB: Application of knowledge; Reflective thinking

SA-4

A brand manager argues: "We have a small but intensely loyal buyer base. We should focus all our spending on making those loyal buyers buy more often, because chasing new buyers is expensive and they are not as committed." Using the chapter's double-jeopardy law and duplication-of-purchase law, explain what is wrong with this argument and describe what the spending priority should actually be.

Sample answer: The argument has the math backwards, and the double-jeopardy law names exactly why. A smaller brand gets hit by two penalties at once: fewer category buyers purchase it at all, and the buyers it keeps purchase slightly less often than the big brand's buyers do. The loyalty gap in the chapter's Coke/Dr Pepper example is seven versus nine annual purchases, which sounds fixable but is not, because the real penalty is that Dr Pepper reaches only a quarter of category buyers at all while Coke reaches three-quarters. Spending to push the seven to eight does almost nothing when the problem is reaching the other three-quarters. The duplication-of-purchase law reinforces this: people who buy any brand also buy others in the same category at rates roughly proportional to each rival's market share. There is no loyal tribe that refuses to cross over; there is one shared pool of category buyers that every brand fishes. A brand grows by catching more of that pool, not by deepening its hook in the fish it already has. The practical implication is that the brand should reallocate spending toward reach, toward finding category buyers who do not currently buy the brand, rather than toward loyalty programs that serve buyers it already has. Building mental availability (more occasions the brand comes to mind) and physical availability (more channels through which a buyer can find and buy it) are the two durable growth levers. The manager's instinct toward loyal buyers is understandable but is exactly the spending pattern that keeps a small brand small.

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 6.8, LO 6.9

Tags: double-jeopardy law; duplication of purchase; brand growth; mental availability; physical availability; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Reflective thinking

Section III — AI-Integration Items (4 items)

These items are original to this course and do not appear in any Kotler bank. They test students' ability to evaluate AI-generated segmentation and positioning output, apply the chapter's frameworks to AI-era tools and their limits, and recognize when AI output is well-specified versus vague.

AI-1 — Identify the Segmentation Base in AI Output

A marketing analyst asks an AI assistant: "Segment our customer base for the insulated-drinkware category." The AI returns three groups:

Group 1. Female buyers, ages 25 to 45, household income $60,000 to $100,000, US metro areas with population above 500,000. Group 2. Male buyers, ages 35 to 60, household income $50,000 to $90,000, suburban and rural areas in the US Sun Belt and Mountain West. Group 3. Buyers of any gender, ages 18 to 30, household income under $50,000, near a college campus.

What segmentation base(s) did the AI primarily use, and what critical information is missing from the output?

A) The AI used psychographic bases; it is missing lifestyle and values data. B) The AI used primarily demographic and geographic bases; it is missing any behavioral or benefit-based information about what each group actually wants from an insulated cup. C) The AI used behavioral bases; it is missing purchase frequency and occasion data. D) The AI used a benefit battery; it is missing demographic descriptor profiles to make the groups reachable. E) The AI used all four bases correctly; no critical information is missing.

Answer: B

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 6.2, LO 6.3 — The AI's output cuts entirely on demographic (age, income, gender) and geographic (metro area, region) variables. It says nothing about what any of these buyers wants from an insulated cup, which occasion they use it for, or what benefit they are seeking. The chapter states that benefit segmentation belongs at the root, because the benefit is the cause of the choice. Two people in Group 1 can want completely different things, the desk-hydration buyer and the coffee-to-worksite buyer, but the AI's cuts collapse them into one group.

Tags: segmentation bases; AI segmentation output; benefit segmentation; AACSB: Analytical thinking; Application of knowledge

AI-2 — Critique an AI-Drafted Positioning Statement

A student uses an AI tool to draft a positioning statement for a new campus meal-kit delivery brand. The AI returns:

"For busy college students who want healthy and convenient meals, CampusEats is the best meal-kit service that delivers fresh ingredients fast and affordably, because we care about student nutrition and believe food should be simple."

Using the chapter's positioning statement template (For [target] who [need], [brand] is the [category] that [biggest difference] because [reason to believe]) and the three POD tests (desirable, deliverable, differentiating), identify the two weakest clauses in this statement and rewrite them to pass the tests.

Sample answer and rubric:

The template maps onto the statement as follows: Target = "busy college students who want healthy and convenient meals"; category = "meal-kit service"; biggest difference = "delivers fresh ingredients fast and affordably"; reason to believe = "because we care about student nutrition and believe food should be simple."

Weak clause 1 — "delivers fresh ingredients fast and affordably": This fails the differentiating test. Every meal-kit brand can say fast and affordable and fresh. No competitor is branding itself as slow, expensive, or stale. A claim any rival could say word-for-word is not a point of difference.

Weak clause 2 — "because we care about student nutrition and believe food should be simple": This fails the deliverable test. "We care" and "we believe" are not provable claims. There is nothing in the reason to believe that the firm can demonstrate or repeat. A reason to believe must be something the buyer can check.

Rewrite example: "For busy college students who do not have a car to reach a grocery store, CampusEats is the on-campus meal-kit service that lets you cook a full dinner in one dorm burner in under 20 minutes, because every kit is designed around a single burner and sells at the campus dining plan rate."

This version survives the three tests: desirable (a single-burner constraint is a real need for a dorm cook), deliverable (the 20-minute time and the dining-plan-rate price are provable), and differentiating (no other meal-kit service brands on single-burner dorm cooking).

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 6.5, LO 6.6 — The item requires students to map the AI output onto the chapter's positioning template, apply the three POD tests clause by clause, identify the two weakest clauses, and rewrite them toward claims that are desirable, deliverable, and differentiating.

Tags: AI positioning output; positioning statement template; POD three tests; AACSB: Written communication; Application of knowledge

AI-3 — Synthetic Respondent Limits

A brand team uses a synthetic-respondent panel to pre-test three positioning concepts for a new plant-based protein bar aimed at Gen Z women in US cities. The AI panel returns strong positive responses to all three concepts, with the wellness concept scoring highest. The team decides to skip the real-consumer validation step and move directly to a media buy. The chapter identifies five ways simulated buyers depart from real ones that rewording the prompt cannot fix. Which departure is most likely to have inflated the wellness concept's scores in this scenario?

A) Hyper-rationality, where the AI panel chooses the concept whose claim sounds most evidence-based B) Stereotyping, where everyone in the Gen Z women demographic answers the same way, while real buyers in that group disagree substantially about what "wellness" means to them C) Representation bias, where thinly-covered subgroups come back as caricatures D) Ideological bias, where the model nudges responses on charged questions E) Insufficient individuation, where all three concepts scored identically because the personas blurred together

Answer: B

Difficulty: 3

Objective: LO 6.4 — The chapter notes that simulated buyers depart from real ones in five predictable ways. Stereotyping is the most directly relevant here: everyone in a demographic cell answers the same way, where real people disagree. Gen Z women are not a monolith on wellness, and a model that has learned an "average Gen Z woman attitude" will return suspiciously uniform high scores for wellness without the within-group disagreement a real 200-person survey would surface. The team should use the simulated run to find the top two or three candidates, then validate those against real humans before spending.

Tags: synthetic respondent panel; AI segmentation limits; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

AI-4 — AI and the Segment-of-One

Klaviyo's Marketing Agent (launched 2025, expanded March 2026) personalizes every email send for a brand on its own, adjusting timing, subject line, and offer to each individual subscriber based on their purchase and engagement history. In March 2026, Sephora put its Beauty Insider program data inside ChatGPT so a shopper's own history shapes the recommendation in real time.

A student argues: "If AI can now do segment-of-one personalization for every buyer, companies no longer need to do segmentation. They should just let the AI figure out each buyer individually."

Which response best captures what the chapter says about this argument?

A) The student is correct: segment-of-one personalization replaces segmentation, because the AI can generate a perfect individual offer without any prior grouping. B) The student is mostly correct, but companies still need geographic segmentation to ensure the right messages reach the right regions. C) The student is incorrect. The segment is the model's starting prior, the rough first guess about what a buyer probably wants, and the per-person tailoring is the correction the model makes once individual data flows in. Skipping the segment makes the model relearn the whole category from scratch on every buyer, which costs more and works worse. D) The student is incorrect. AI personalization only works within a pre-defined demographic segment and cannot operate without one. E) The student is partially correct: segmentation is still needed for new customers who have no behavioral history, but it is obsolete for returning customers.

Answer: C

Difficulty: 2

Objective: LO 6.1, LO 6.2 — The chapter states directly: "The segment still does the first piece of work as the rough guess you hand the model about what a buyer probably wants, and the per-person tailoring is the model correcting that guess once the buyer's own data arrives. Skip the segment and you ask the model to relearn the whole category from scratch on every customer, which costs more and works worse." Klaviyo and Sephora are the chapter's 2026 worked cases, and both use segments as the starting prior, not as a replacement.

Tags: segment-of-one personalization; AI marketing tools; Klaviyo; Sephora; AACSB: Reflective thinking; Application of knowledge

# Grading Skill: The Pricing-Tactics Scavenger Hunt

*Chapter 10 group assignment. This file lets you grade a submission with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste the block below into Claude or ChatGPT, paste the team's packet under it, and read back a score for each rubric row with a sentence of feedback. The block already knows what each tactic looks like in the wild and what the AI usually gets wrong on a leading question, so it can tell whether the team's catches were real. You stay the grader of record; the AI gives a first pass. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom.*

---

## Part 1: If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install or pay for anything. This takes about five minutes the first time, and under two minutes per team after that.

1. Open a web browser and go to **claude.ai** (or **chatgpt.com**, which works the same way).
2. Make a free account: click Sign up, enter your email, follow the steps. A free account is enough.
3. Start a new chat. The box that asks how it can help is where you type.
4. Copy the grader instructions in Part 2 below (everything in the gray box) and paste them into the chat. Do not send yet.
5. Press Enter twice for space, then paste the team's packet underneath, in the same message: the six-row table, the screenshots' captions, the AI transcript summary, and the team's individual paragraphs.
6. Send it. The AI replies with a score for each of the four rubric rows, a total out of 100, and a short note per row.
7. Read the score and decide. It is a first draft for you to approve or override; you are the grader of record.
8. Click New chat for the next team, so submissions do not blur together.

The ten-second check you can always run yourself, no AI needed: did the team use **six different sellers** (no two rows on the same retailer), and did they write **at least one specific catch** on the AI's read (an invented anchor, a sycophantic confirmation, a tactic confused)? Both yes is Excellent across rows 1 through 3.

## Part 2: The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a second-year marketing team's submission for a Chapter 10 scavenger-hunt on psychological pricing tactics. The team found one real online example each of six chapter mechanisms (charm pricing, reference pricing, the decoy effect, price-quality inference, bundle pricing, and loss-leader pricing) from six different sellers, asked an AI assistant to read each example, and judged the AI's reads. Score the team's packet against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback. Reward catches on the AI's reads over polished writing.
>
> What a strong example looks like, by tactic:
>
> 1. Charm pricing: a regular retail price ending in 99 or 95 where the seller could have rounded up. Weak if it is a sale price ending in 99 (that is reference pricing) or a luxury price ending in 00 (that is price-quality inference).
> 2. Reference pricing: a "compare at" anchor or a strike-through ($80 to $52, "Was $89, now $59"). Weak if it is a charm-priced number with no anchor next to it.
> 3. The decoy effect: a three-tier lineup where the top two prices are close enough that the middle option is the obvious pick. Weak if it is any three-tier menu without that asymmetric closeness.
> 4. Price-quality inference: a premium price as the quality signal (a $400 candle, a $58 jar of honey). Weak if there is a cheaper alternative next to it that turns it into reference pricing.
> 5. Bundle pricing: two or more items sold at a price below the sum of the individual prices, with both prices shown. Weak if the bundle price is not clearly below the parts sum.
> 6. Loss-leader pricing: a popular item near or below cost (Costco's $0.99 rotisserie chicken, a printer below cost). Weak if it is a generic sale price the team labels a loss leader without evidence the price is at or below cost.
>
> The chapter's tested AI failures on this kind of task:
>
> - Sycophancy on a leading question (the AI confirms whatever tactic the team led with).
> - An invented anchor (the AI states the strike-through reflects the seller's actual previous list price, which it cannot verify from a screenshot).
> - Confusion between charm pricing and reference pricing (the AI calls a $19.99 with no anchor next to it a reference-pricing example).
>
> A strong team catches at least one of these on a specific row, and writes the catch in the rightmost column of the table.
>
> Score these four rows and add them for a total out of 100. For each, give a number, one sentence on why, and one sentence the team could act on.
>
> 1. Six examples found, one per tactic (36 points). Full marks: all six rows filled with a seller, a product, a public URL, a screenshot attached, and a one-sentence shelf-cue read that ties the price to the named tactic. Lower if shelf cues read as labels rather than evidence.
> 2. The AI's read judged on each example (32 points). Full marks: six AI-read columns filled with at least one specific catch (an invented anchor, a confused tactic, a sycophantic confirmation), and the catch named with what the team corrected. Lower if catches are stated as "the AI was mostly right."
> 3. Different sellers across the six (8 points). Full marks: six different sellers, no repeats, no two prices from the same product category at the same retailer. Lower for repeats.
> 4. Individual verdict, each member (24 points). Full marks: every member's paragraph is their own, names the strongest and the weakest tactic in the team's read, names the single best catch the team made on the AI, and adds one sentence on what surprised them. Lower if verdicts are near-duplicates.
>
> Output format:
> - Six examples found: __ /36 (why) (one tip)
> - AI's reads judged: __ /32 (why) (one tip)
> - Different sellers: __ /8 (why) (one tip)
> - Individual verdict: __ /24 (why) (one tip)
> - Total: __ /100
> - One-line summary for the team
>
> The team's packet follows below this line.

## Part 3: Reading and trusting the result

- Skim the notes first; they tell you whether the AI understood the packet. The note on the AI-judged row is the heart of the grade, so check that it names whether the team caught at least one specific failure (an invented anchor, a sycophantic confirmation, a tactic confused).
- You can push back. Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example "the team did catch the AI calling the $19.99 ending reference pricing on row 1, look again at the AI-judged row," and it will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- The seller-diversity row is the easiest to double-check yourself: scan the seller column for repeats. If two rows say "Amazon" the row drops at least one band.
- Keep a fresh chat per team. The grade is yours; the AI only assists.

## Part 4: Grade by hand instead (no AI at all)

Read the packet against the rubric below; the full answer key is in `ch10-pricing-tactics-scavenger-hunt-grading-guide.md`.

| Row | Points | What earns full marks |
|---|---|---|
| Six examples found | 36 | Six rows, six different sellers, public URLs, screenshots attached, shelf cues that tie the price to the tactic |
| AI's reads judged | 32 | At least one specific catch on the AI's read (an invented anchor, a confused tactic, a sycophantic confirmation), named with the correction |
| Different sellers | 8 | No two rows on the same retailer |
| Individual verdict | 24 | Each member's own paragraph naming the strongest and weakest tactic, the best catch, and what surprised them |
| **Total** | **100** | |

Fast bands: 90 to 100, six real examples on six different sellers, real catches on the AI, distinct member paragraphs; 80 to 89, five of six strong with one thin spot and a real catch; 70 to 79, shelf cues read as labels or AI's reads accepted on most rows; 60 to 69 or below, repeated sellers or no catches at all. The single fastest signal of a strong submission is a specific catch on at least one of the AI's reads, written in the team's own words with the correction.

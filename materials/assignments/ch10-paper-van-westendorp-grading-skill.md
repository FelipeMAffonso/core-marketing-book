# Grading Skill: A Paper Van Westendorp Reading for a Portable Guitar Amp Launch

*Chapter 10 individual assignment, no AI account used by students. This file lets you grade a student's hand-plotted analysis of a provided Van Westendorp dataset with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste a block of instructions into Claude, paste the student's work under it, and read back a score for each rubric row with a sentence of feedback. The student used no AI; the only AI here is yours, and only to speed your grading. Because the key artifact is a hand-drawn graph the AI may not see well, you stay especially in charge here: the AI gives a first-pass score, you verify the plot and decide the grade. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom in case you would rather not use AI at all.*

---

## Part 1: If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install anything or pay anything. This takes about five minutes the first time and a few minutes per student after that.

1. **Open a web browser** (Chrome, Safari, Edge, whatever you use) and go to **claude.ai**. (ChatGPT at **chatgpt.com** works the same way if you prefer it.)
2. **Make a free account.** Click **Sign up**, enter your email, and follow the steps. A free account is enough. You will not be charged.
3. **Start a new chat.** After you log in you will see a box that says something like *"How can I help you today?"* That box is where you type. A "chat" is just a conversation with the AI, like texting.
4. **Copy the grader instructions.** In Part 2 below, everything inside the gray box is one block. Select it all, copy it, and paste it into the chat box. Do not press send yet.
5. **Add the student's work underneath.** Press Enter twice to make space, then paste the student's hand-computed cumulative table (typed out from the photo if needed), the four crossing values they read off the graph, the band as a dollar range, the recommended launch price, the rival defense against Vox amPlug 2 at forty-five dollars, Boss Waza-Air Mini at eighty-nine dollars, and Fender Mustang Micro at one hundred and twenty-nine dollars, and the elasticity line, right below the instructions, in the same message.
6. **Handle the graph.** The key artifact is a hand-drawn graph, and an AI often cannot read a photo of graph paper well. You have two choices: **upload the photo** of the graph if your tool lets you attach images, or **describe the plotted crossings to the model in words** (for example, "the too-cheap and too-expensive curves cross at about seventy-three dollars, the marginal points sit at about fifty dollars and seventy-nine dollars, the recommended price is seventy-four dollars and ninety-nine cents"). Either way, plan to verify the plot yourself in Part 3, because this is the one thing the AI cannot judge reliably.
7. **Send it.** Press Enter (or click the arrow). The AI will reply with a score for each of the five rubric rows, a total out of one hundred, and a short note for each row.
8. **Read the score and decide.** The AI's score is a **first draft for you to approve, change, or override.** You are the grader of record. If a score looks off, trust your own read; the grading guide (`ch10-paper-van-westendorp-grading-guide.md`) carries the answer key and tells you what each row is really worth.
9. **Grade the next student.** Click **New chat** and repeat from step 4. A fresh chat each time keeps one student's work from bleeding into the next.

That is the whole process. The block in Part 2 does the work; you paste, read, verify the plot, and sign off.

> **The one thing to check yourself, no AI needed.** On the graph, the *too expensive* curve should rise as price rises, the *a bargain* curve should also rise, the *too cheap* curve should fall, and the *getting expensive* curve should fall. The OPP sits where too cheap and too expensive cross at about seventy-two dollars and fifty cents. If a curve slopes the wrong way, the cumulative arithmetic was done wrong. The AI may not see this; you can, in under a minute.

## Part 2: The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a second-year marketing student's Van Westendorp price-sensitivity analysis. The student was given a pooled survey dataset for the PracticeKit, a USB-C headphone amp for guitar made by Coyle Audio, a small Norman audio shop. The dataset has sixteen real-feeling respondent rows with four price columns (too cheap, a bargain, getting expensive, too expensive). The student plotted the four cumulative curves by hand on graph paper (no AI, no software) and recommended a launch price defended against three named real competitors: Vox amPlug 2 at forty-five dollars, Boss Waza-Air Mini at eighty-nine dollars, and Fender Mustang Micro at one hundred and twenty-nine dollars. Your job is to score the submission against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback.
>
> **Important: you may not be able to see the hand-drawn graph clearly.** If the graph is a photo you cannot read, say so plainly in your notes and base the band-reading score on what the instructor described and on whether the student's cumulative table and stated crossings are consistent with the dataset. Tell the instructor to verify the plot themselves.
>
> **Background you need (the chapter's framework and the answer key for this dataset):**
>
> - **Van Westendorp's four questions** are indirect: the price at which the product is too expensive, the price at which it is too cheap (so cheap the buyer doubts the quality), the price at which it is getting expensive, and the price that would be a bargain.
>
> - **The four cumulative curves are built off the pooled table** with the sixteen respondents as the denominator. Each respondent counts for one over sixteen of the cumulative percent, which is six and one quarter percent per respondent.
>
>   1. **Too cheap, descending.** At price P, the share whose too-cheap value is greater than or equal to P. Formula in words: count of too-cheap values greater than or equal to P, divided by sixteen, times one hundred.
>   2. **A bargain, ascending.** At price P, the share whose a-bargain value is strictly less than P (the share for whom P has climbed above their bargain zone). Formula in words: count of a-bargain values strictly less than P, divided by sixteen, times one hundred.
>   3. **Getting expensive, descending.** At price P, the share whose getting-expensive value is strictly greater than P (the share for whom P has not yet entered the getting-expensive zone). Formula in words: count of getting-expensive values strictly greater than P, divided by sixteen, times one hundred.
>   4. **Too expensive, ascending.** At price P, the share whose too-expensive value is less than or equal to P. Formula in words: count of too-expensive values less than or equal to P, divided by sixteen, times one hundred.
>
>   Two curves rise (a bargain and too expensive), and two curves fall (too cheap and getting expensive). Each of the four crossings is a meeting of one rising and one falling curve.
>
> - **The four crossings on this dataset (the answer key):**
>   - **PMC, the point of marginal cheapness** (too cheap meets a bargain): exactly fifty dollars (where the too-cheap and a-bargain curves both pass through the eighteen-to-twenty-five percent range). The lower bound of the acceptable band. Acceptable range forty-eight dollars to fifty-three dollars.
>   - **IPP, the indifference price point** (a bargain meets getting expensive): sixty-five dollars. Roughly the median willingness to pay.
>   - **OPP, the optimal price point** (too cheap meets too expensive): approximately seventy-two dollars and fifty cents. The center of the band. Acceptable range seventy dollars to seventy-five dollars.
>   - **PME, the point of marginal expensiveness** (too expensive meets getting expensive): approximately seventy-nine dollars. The upper bound of the acceptable band. Acceptable range seventy-five dollars to eighty dollars.
>
> - **The acceptable band** runs from PMC at about fifty dollars up to PME at about seventy-nine dollars. A defensible recommended launch price sits inside the band, near OPP or IPP. Natural landing zones: sixty-nine dollars and ninety-nine cents, seventy-four dollars and ninety-nine cents, seventy-eight dollars and ninety-nine cents.
>
> - **The three named competitors:** Vox amPlug 2 at forty-five dollars (sits below the band's floor), Boss Waza-Air Mini at eighty-nine dollars (sits just above the band's ceiling), and Fender Mustang Micro at one hundred and twenty-nine dollars (sits well above the band). The recommendation should reference all three.
>
> - **The elasticity read:** the band runs from about fifty dollars to seventy-nine dollars, a width of about twenty-eight dollars on a center of sixty-five dollars (about forty-three percent of the center). That is a moderately wide band, which says music-student buyers are moderately price-tolerant on this small accessory.
>
> - **No AI was used by the student.** The plot is by hand on graph paper. Do not penalize the student for the absence of software output, and do not reward it.
>
> **How to score.** Give a number for each of the five criteria below, then add them for a total out of one hundred. For each, write one sentence saying why, and one sentence the student could act on. Be specific and encouraging. If something required is missing, say so plainly and score it low; do not invent credit.
>
> 1. **The hand-plotted four-curve chart and the cumulative arithmetic (thirty-five points), the entry condition.** Are the four cumulative curves plotted by hand on graph paper from the sixteen-respondent table, with the cumulative percent computed correctly at each price tick (running, off sixteen as the denominator, with two rising curves and two falling curves), and is the chart legible enough that the four crossings can be read off it? Full marks: the hand-drawn plot is present and the cumulative table is correct. Low marks: hand-drawn graph missing or replaced by software output; the plotting requirement is not satisfied and the analytical rows are capped low. If you cannot see the graph, score what you can verify and flag the plot for the instructor.
>
> 2. **The four crossings and the band (twenty-five points).** Are all four crossings (OPP, PMC, PME, IPP) labeled at the right intersections on the graph, and is the acceptable band shaded between PMC and PME? Full marks: PMC about fifty dollars, OPP about seventy-two dollars and fifty cents, IPP at sixty-five dollars, PME about seventy-nine dollars, all within about three dollars of the answer key. Deduct for a crossing mislabeled at the wrong two-curve pair or a band shaded outside the marginal points.
>
> 3. **Recommended launch price defended against the named competitors (twenty points).** Is a price set inside the band (about fifty dollars to seventy-nine dollars), with two or three sentences defending it against Vox amPlug 2 at forty-five dollars, Boss Waza-Air Mini at eighty-nine dollars, and Fender Mustang Micro at one hundred and twenty-nine dollars? Deduct for a partial defense (only two competitors named) or a price set outside the band.
>
> 4. **The four Van Westendorp questions and the elasticity read (ten points).** Are the brief's four indirect questions reproduced or referenced, the four crossings correctly named (OPP at too cheap meets too expensive, PMC at too cheap meets a bargain, PME at too expensive meets getting expensive, IPP at a bargain meets getting expensive), and is the one-line elasticity read sound on the band the student read (wide band and price-tolerant, narrow band and price-sensitive)?
>
> 5. **Writing, presentation, and the reflection (ten points).** Is the prose plain and clear, with the chapter vocabulary used correctly, and are the two insights at the close specific to what the student read off the data? Deduct for disconnected writing or a generic reflection.
>
> **Watch for these failure patterns and score them down (with a kind note):** hand-drawn graph missing or replaced by software output (the plotting requirement is the entry condition); one curve sloping the wrong direction (the cumulative math was off the wrong convention); cumulative percentages computed off the wrong denominator (twelve or twenty instead of sixteen); a band reported as one number without curves; a crossing labeled at an impossible point (PMC above PME, the band shaded outside the marginal points); a recommended price outside the band with no acknowledgment; a recommended price inside the band but with no reference to all three named competitors.
>
> **Do not penalize the student for not using AI.** This analysis is meant to be plotted by hand. The student is graded on the hand-drawn plot, the band read off it, and the recommendation defended against the three named competitors.
>
> **Output format:**
> - **Criterion 1, The hand-plotted four-curve chart and the cumulative arithmetic: __/35**, (why), (one tip)
> - **Criterion 2, The four crossings and the band: __/25**, (why), (one tip)
> - **Criterion 3, Recommended launch price: __/20**, (why), (one tip)
> - **Criterion 4, The four Van Westendorp questions and the elasticity read: __/10**, (why), (one tip)
> - **Criterion 5, Writing, presentation, and the reflection: __/10**, (why), (one tip)
> - **Total: __/100**
> - **One-line summary for the student:** (warm, specific, what to do next time)
>
> The student's submission follows below this line.

## Part 3: Reading and trusting the result

- The AI returns five scores, a total, and short notes. **Skim the notes first**; they tell you whether the AI understood the survey. If the notes match what you saw, the total is trustworthy, with one exception below.
- **Verify the plot yourself.** This is the one row the AI cannot grade reliably, because it may not read a photo of graph paper well. Look at the graph: the too-expensive curve should rise with price, the a-bargain curve should rise, the too-cheap curve should fall, and the getting-expensive curve should fall. The PMC and PME should sit at the two bargain-related crossings, the OPP at the too-cheap meets too-expensive crossing, and the IPP at the a-bargain meets getting-expensive crossing. If the curves slope right and cross where the student says, the band-reading row is sound; if not, override the AI's score on that row.
- **Compare the student's crossings to the answer key in the grading guide.** PMC about fifty dollars, OPP about seventy-two dollars and fifty cents, IPP at sixty-five dollars, PME about seventy-nine dollars. Within three dollars of those values is correct.
- **You can push back.** Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example *"One of the curves slopes the wrong way; look again at Criterion 1"* or *"The recommended price sits outside the band, re-check Criterion 3."* The AI will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- **Keep a fresh chat per student** so submissions do not blur together.
- Remember the framing: *the instructor grades; the AI assists.* The student used no AI at all; your optional AI use is only to speed the grading, and on this card the plot is yours to verify. The score is yours.

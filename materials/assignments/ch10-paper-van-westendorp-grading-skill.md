# Grading Skill: A Paper Van Westendorp Survey, Plotted by Hand

*Chapter 10 individual assignment. This file lets you grade a student's hand-run, hand-plotted survey with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste a block of instructions into Claude, paste the student's work under it, and read back a score for each rubric row with a sentence of feedback. The student did this with no AI; the only AI here is yours, and only to speed your grading. Because the key artifact is a hand-drawn graph the AI may not see well, you stay especially in charge here: the AI gives a first-pass score, you verify the plot and decide the grade. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom in case you would rather not use AI at all.*

---

## Part 1: If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install anything or pay anything. This takes about five minutes the first time and a few minutes per student after that.

1. **Open a web browser** (Chrome, Safari, Edge, whatever you use) and go to **claude.ai**. (ChatGPT at **chatgpt.com** works the same way if you prefer it.)
2. **Make a free account.** Click **Sign up**, enter your email, and follow the steps. A free account is enough. You will not be charged.
3. **Start a new chat.** After you log in you will see a box that says something like *"How can I help you today?"* That box is where you type. A "chat" is just a conversation with the AI, like texting.
4. **Copy the grader instructions.** In Part 2 below, everything inside the gray box is one block. Select it all, copy it, and paste it into the chat box. Do not press send yet.
5. **Add the student's work underneath.** Press Enter twice to make space, then paste the student's four questions, their response table, the recommended price, and the elasticity line right below the instructions, in the same message.
6. **Handle the graph.** The key artifact is a hand-drawn graph, and an AI often cannot read a photo of graph paper well. You have two choices: **upload the photo** of the graph if your tool lets you attach images, or **describe the plotted crossings to the model in words** (for example, "the too-cheap and too-expensive curves cross at about $6, the marginal points sit at about $4.50 and $8, the recommended price is $7"). Either way, plan to verify the plot yourself in Part 3, because this is the one thing the AI cannot judge reliably.
7. **Send it.** Press Enter (or click the arrow). The AI will reply with a score for each of the four rubric rows, a total out of 100, and a short note for each row.
8. **Read the score and decide.** The AI's score is a **first draft for you to approve, change, or override.** You are the grader of record. If a score looks off, trust your own read; the grading guide (`ch10-paper-van-westendorp-grading-guide.md`) tells you what each row is really worth.
9. **Grade the next student.** Click **New chat** and repeat from step 4. A fresh chat each time keeps one student's work from bleeding into the next.

That is the whole process. The block in Part 2 does the work; you paste, read, verify the plot, and sign off.

> **The one thing to check yourself, no AI needed.** On the graph, the "too expensive" curve should rise as price rises and the "too cheap" curve should fall, and they cross at the Optimal Price Point. If a curve slopes the wrong way, the cumulative arithmetic was done wrong. The AI may not see this; you can, in under a minute.

## Part 2: The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a second-year marketing student's Van Westendorp price-sensitivity survey. The student ran the survey on paper, surveyed real people, and plotted the acceptable price band by hand on graph paper, with no AI and no software. Your job is to score their submission against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback. This is a first-year-friendly course; reward a survey that was really run and a band that is really read off the curves over neat presentation.
>
> **Important: you may not be able to see the hand-drawn graph clearly.** If the graph is a photo you cannot read, say so plainly in your notes and base the band-reading score on what the instructor described and on whether the response table and the stated crossings are consistent. Tell the instructor to verify the plot themselves.
>
> **Background you need (the chapter's framework):**
> - **Van Westendorp uses four indirect questions about price points, not a direct "what would you pay":** (1) at what price is it so expensive you would not buy it (too expensive); (2) at what price is it so cheap you would doubt its quality (too cheap); (3) at what price is it getting expensive but you would still consider it (getting expensive); (4) at what price is it a bargain (a bargain). If the questions are written as a direct price ask, that is a wording error to flag.
> - From the responses you build **four cumulative curves** with price on the horizontal axis: the "too expensive" and "getting expensive" curves rise as price rises, and the "too cheap" and "bargain" curves fall as price rises.
> - The curves cross at four points: the **OPP** (Optimal Price Point, where "too cheap" meets "too expensive"), the **PMC** and **PME** (the marginal cheapness and marginal expensiveness points, at the bargain crossings), and the **IPP** (Indifference Price Point, where "bargain" meets "getting expensive"). The **acceptable price band** runs between the marginal points (PMC and PME).
> - The point is to read the band from where the curves cross, not to report a single number, and to recommend a price inside the band. The elasticity read is whether the band is wide (price-tolerant buyers) or narrow (price-sensitive buyers).
>
> **How to score.** Give a number for each of the four criteria below, then add them for a total out of 100. Check whether the four questions are worded as the indirect Van Westendorp forms (not direct price asks) and whether the recommended price sits inside the stated band. For each criterion, write one sentence saying why and one sentence the student could act on. Be specific and encouraging. If something required is missing, say so plainly and score it low; do not invent credit.
>
> 1. **The band read off the hand-plotted curves (42 points).** Were the four cumulative curves plotted correctly from the data, the four crossings (OPP, PMC, PME, IPP) found and labeled, the acceptable band shaded correctly, and is the elasticity read sound? Full marks: a band read off correctly plotted curves with the crossings labeled. Low marks: no band, or a band asserted without the curves. If you cannot see the graph, score what you can verify and flag the plot for the instructor.
> 2. **The four questions and real responses (28 points).** Are all four Van Westendorp questions worded correctly (indirect, not direct), did ten or more real people answer, and are the answers tabulated cleanly? Deduct for fewer than ten respondents or a tabulation slip; score low if one or two questions are written as direct price asks.
> 3. **Recommended price (18 points).** Is a price set inside the band with a reason tied to where the crossings landed? Deduct for a partial reason; score low for a price with no reason or no price.
> 4. **Cumulative-percent arithmetic and writing (12 points).** Are the running cumulative percentages computed correctly (running, off the right denominator), and is the writing plain and clear with correct terms? Deduct for muddled cumulative math.
>
> **Do not penalize** the student for not using AI; this survey is meant to be run and plotted by hand. Penalize only wrong question wording, a band not read from the curves, a missing recommendation, or muddled cumulative math.
>
> **Output format:**
> - **Criterion 1, The band read off the curves: __/42**, (why), (one tip)
> - **Criterion 2, The four questions and real responses: __/28**, (why), (one tip)
> - **Criterion 3, Recommended price: __/18**, (why), (one tip)
> - **Criterion 4, Cumulative-percent arithmetic and writing: __/12**, (why), (one tip)
> - **Total: __/100**
> - **One-line summary for the student:** (warm, specific, what to do next time)
>
> The student's submission follows below this line.

## Part 3: Reading and trusting the result

- The AI returns four scores, a total, and short notes. **Skim the notes first**; they tell you whether the AI understood the survey. If the notes match what you saw, the total is trustworthy, with one exception below.
- **Verify the plot yourself.** This is the one row the AI cannot grade reliably, because it may not read a photo of graph paper well. Look at the graph: the "too expensive" curve should rise with price, the "too cheap" curve should fall, and they cross at the labeled OPP. If the curves slope right and cross where the student says, the band-reading row is sound; if not, override the AI's score on that row.
- **You can push back.** Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example *"One of the questions is a direct price ask, look again at Criterion 2"* or *"The recommended price sits outside the band, re-check Criterion 3."* The AI will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- **Keep a fresh chat per student** so submissions do not blur together.
- Remember the framing: *the instructor grades; the AI assists.* The student used no AI at all; your optional AI use is only to speed the grading, and on this card the plot is yours to verify. The score is yours.

## Part 4: Grade by hand instead (no AI at all)

If you would rather not use AI, grade straight from the rubric, starting with the graph. Read each submission once, then score the four rows.

| Criterion | Points | What earns full marks |
|---|---|---|
| **The band read off the hand-plotted curves** | 42 | Four cumulative curves plotted correctly from the data, the four crossings (OPP, PMC, PME, IPP) found and labeled, the band shaded correctly, and a sound elasticity read |
| **The four questions and real responses** | 28 | All four Van Westendorp questions worded correctly (indirect), ten or more real respondents, answers tabulated cleanly |
| **Recommended price** | 18 | A price set inside the band with a reason tied to where the crossings landed |
| **Cumulative-percent arithmetic and writing** | 12 | Running cumulative percentages computed correctly, plain clear writing with correct terms |
| **Total** | **100** | |

**The one-minute plot check:** the "too expensive" curve rises with price, the "too cheap" curve falls, and they cross at the OPP; the band runs between the marginal points (PMC and PME) and the recommended price sits inside it. If a curve slopes the wrong way, the cumulative arithmetic was done wrong.

**Fast bands:** 90 to 100, four correct questions, ten or more real respondents, a clean cumulative table, curves plotted from the data with crossings labeled and the band shaded, a price inside the band with a crossing-tied reason; 80 to 89, mostly right with a minor plotting or labeling slip; 70 to 79, curves attempted but the band not read from the crossings, or a crossing misplaced, or one or two questions worded as direct price asks; 60 to 69, questions mostly wrong, or the band asserted with no curves, or the cumulative math muddled; below 60, no real data or no band.

The single best signal of a strong submission: **the four crossings on the hand-drawn graph match the response table.** If "too cheap" and "too expensive" cross where the student labeled the OPP, the survey was run and read for real.

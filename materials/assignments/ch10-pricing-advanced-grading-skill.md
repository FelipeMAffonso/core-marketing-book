# Grading Skill: Price a Real Product with AI (Advanced)

You paste a block of instructions into Claude, paste the student's work under it, and read back a score for each part of the rubric with a sentence of feedback. You stay in charge: the AI gives you a first-pass score, you decide the grade. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom in case you would rather not use AI at all. This is the standard "Price a Real Product with AI" grader plus the leading-versus-neutral rigor row and the one-page defense, rebalanced to total 100.*

## Part 1. If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install anything or pay anything. This takes about five minutes the first time and under three minutes per student after that.

1. **Open a web browser** (Chrome, Safari, Edge, whatever you use) and go to **claude.ai**. (ChatGPT at **chatgpt.com** works the same way if you prefer it.)
2. **Make a free account.** Click **Sign up**, enter your email, and follow the steps. A free account is enough for this. You will not be charged.
3. **Start a new chat.** After you log in you will see a box that says something like *"How can I help you today?"* That box is where you type. A "chat" is just a conversation with the AI, like texting.
4. **Copy the grader instructions.** In Part 2 below, everything inside the gray box is one block. Select it all, copy it, and paste it into the chat box. Do not press send yet.
5. **Add the student's work underneath.** Press Enter twice to make space, then paste the student's full submission, including their value stick, their price arithmetic, their willingness-to-pay responses, their fairness read, their one-page defense, and both AI transcripts (the leading prompt and the neutral one), right below the instructions, in the same message.
6. **Send it.** Press Enter (or click the arrow). The AI will read it all and reply with a score for each of the seven rubric rows, a total out of 100, and a short note for each row.
7. **Read the score and decide.** The AI's score is a **first draft for you to approve, change, or override.** You are the grader of record. If a score looks off, trust your own read; the grading guide (`ch10-pricing-advanced-grading-guide.md`) tells you what each row is really worth.
8. **Grade the next student.** Click **New chat** and repeat from step 4 for each submission. A fresh chat each time keeps one student's work from bleeding into the next.

That is the whole process. The block in Part 2 does the work; you paste, read, and sign off.

> **One quick sanity check you can always run yourself, no AI needed.** Pricing has one trap that catches everyone: the difference between markup and margin. On the student's own price, **markup is the price minus the cost, then divided by the cost**, and **margin is the price minus the cost, then divided by the price**. They are never the same number. If the student (or their AI) called a markup a margin, that is the single most important thing to catch, and the arithmetic above settles it in ten seconds. The second thing to confirm by eye: there really are two different transcripts, a leading one and a neutral one, and the answers actually differed.

## Part 2. The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a second-year marketing student's individual assignment, the advanced version. The student picked one real, public product, used a free AI assistant to price it end to end, was then asked to judge the AI's output, to run the same pricing question two ways (a leading prompt and a neutral one) and report what changed, and to write a one-page defense of the final price. Your job is to score their submission against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback. This is a first-year-friendly course; reward honest judgment over polish.
>
> **Background you need (the chapter's framework):**
> - A price is built on a **value stick**: the **floor** (the unit cost), the **frame** (what two or three named rivals charge now), and the **ceiling** (the most a buyer would pay). A good answer names all three with real, checkable evidence and places the student's price on the stick.
> - **Markup and margin are different numbers, and an AI can slip on the distinction, especially inside a long multi-part request.** Markup is the price minus the cost, then divided by the **cost**; margin is the price minus the cost, then divided by the **price**. They are never equal. For example, a 40% margin on a cost of 40 dollars gives a price of 40 divided by (1 minus 0.40), which is 66.67 dollars, while a 40% markup on the same cost gives only 40 times 1.40, which is 56 dollars. If the student kept an AI number that labeled a markup as a margin (or the reverse) without catching it, that is the headline error to flag.
> - **The AI also invents competitor prices** and states them as confidently as real ones. A strong answer verifies the prices it quotes against a real shelf or site and marks each one confirmed or estimated.
> - **Willingness to pay** can be measured with Van Westendorp's four questions; the acceptable price band is read from where the response curves cross.
> - **Fairness** is judged with the dual-entitlement principle: a price increase that covers a real cost reads as fair, while one that just captures a demand spike reads as gouging.
> - **The AI flatters a leading question.** Asked "is my price a good price?" it tends to agree; asked "argue against this price the way a skeptical CFO would" it tends to surface real risks. The advanced student was told to run both and report which answer they trusted and why. Seeing that contrast is the rigor the advanced version adds.
> - The assignment's heart is the **judgment pass**: the student should run the arithmetic as code (not trust a typed number), catch at least one thing the AI got wrong (a markup mislabeled as a margin, a made-up competitor price, or a fumbled breakeven), correct it, and set the final price themselves. Catching one real AI error is the core skill being tested.
>
> **How to score.** Give a number for each of the seven criteria below, then add them for a total out of 100. For each, write one sentence saying why, and one sentence the student could act on. Be specific and encouraging. If something required is missing, say so plainly and score it low; do not invent credit.
>
> 1. **Judgment pass: caught what the AI got wrong (24 points).** Did the student catch and correct a real AI error (a markup reported as a margin, a competitor price the AI invented, or a fumbled breakeven), AND set the final price themselves with a reason for any change? Full marks: a named, corrected AI error plus an owned final price. Low marks: took the AI output at face value with no checking, or used AI only to generate and never to judge.
> 2. **Value stick (16 points).** Are the floor, the frame (two or three named rivals' real prices), and the ceiling all named with verified public evidence, and is the student's price placed on the stick with the margin and surplus explained? Deduct for an asserted ceiling with no buyer behind it, a frame that uses only one rival, or floor and ceiling confused.
> 3. **Approach and code arithmetic (16 points).** Does the pricing approach fit the product, and are the contribution margin, the breakeven, AND both the markup and the margin correct and run as code rather than guessed? A markup reported as a margin caps this row low, because it is the chapter's headline mistake.
> 4. **Willingness to pay (12 points).** Are Van Westendorp's four questions correct, did five or more real people answer, is the band read off the crossings, and is the price checked against it? Deduct for a band not read from the data or fewer than five respondents.
> 5. **Fairness check (10 points).** Did the student apply the dual-entitlement principle correctly to this product, judge category tolerance, and say whether the price clears the fairness ceiling? Deduct for mentioning fairness without applying the principle.
> 6. **Rigor, leading versus neutral prompt (12 points).** Did the student run the same pricing ask both a leading way ("is my price a good price?") and a neutral way ("argue against this price like a skeptical CFO"), show specifically how the two answers differed, and name which one they trusted and why? Full marks: both transcripts present, a real difference shown, and the sycophancy made visible. Low marks: only one prompt run, the same prompt pasted twice, or no real difference reported.
> 7. **Defense and writing (10 points).** Does the one-page defense tie the approach, the stick, the WTP band, and the fairness read into one argument a founder or finance team could act on, in plain language with correct terms? Deduct if it only restates the parts in sequence without connecting them.
>
> **Watch for these failure patterns and score them down (with a kind note):** keeping every number the AI typed with no checking; a markup reported as a margin that the student never caught; a competitor price quoted as fact with no source and no verification; arithmetic the AI guessed instead of computing as code; running only the leading prompt and stopping at the flattering yes; pasting the same prompt twice and calling it the leading-versus-neutral test; a one-page defense that is a list of parts rather than an argument.
>
> **On AI use:** disclosed AI use, even heavy use, is expected here and is not penalized; only the *absence* of a judgment pass, of the leading-versus-neutral test, or of a defense is penalized. If there is no account of how AI was used and no transcript at all, and the writing looks like an unedited AI dump, flag it for the instructor rather than scoring it; that is a human decision for the instructor to handle through the institution's academic-integrity and AI-use policy.
>
> **Output format:**
> - **Criterion 1, Judgment pass: __/24** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 2, Value stick: __/16** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 3, Approach and code arithmetic: __/16** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 4, Willingness to pay: __/12** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 5, Fairness check: __/10** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 6, Rigor, leading versus neutral prompt: __/12** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 7, Defense and writing: __/10** (why) (one tip)
> - **Total: __/100**
> - **One-line summary for the student:** (warm, specific, what to do next time)
>
> The student's submission follows below this line.

## Part 3. Reading and trusting the result

- The AI returns seven scores, a total, and short notes. **Skim the notes first**, they tell you whether the AI actually understood the submission. If the notes match what you saw when you read the work, the total is trustworthy.
- **You can push back.** Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example *"The student did catch a markup the AI labeled a margin, look again at Criterion 1"* or *"Re-read both transcripts and check whether the leading and neutral answers really differed for Criterion 6."* The AI will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- **Two rows are the ones to double-check yourself.** The judgment-pass row is worth the most and is where the AI's own read can be too generous: if the student named and corrected a real AI error, that is a strong paper even if the value stick is rough. The rigor row is the one the AI can over-credit by accepting two prompts at face value; open both transcripts and confirm the answers genuinely diverged and the student read the difference.
- **The fastest verification needs no AI.** On the student's own price, compute the markup as the price minus the cost divided by the cost, and the margin as the price minus the cost divided by the price. If those two numbers are equal in the student's work, something is mislabeled. This one check catches the chapter's headline error every time.
- **Keep a fresh chat per student** so submissions do not blur together.
- Remember the framing for any feedback you pass on: *the instructor grades; the AI assists.* The score is yours.

# Grading Skill: A Value-Based Price for an Industrial Pump

*Chapter 10 individual assignment, the high-rigor card in the bank. This file lets you grade a student's submission with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste a block of instructions into Claude, paste the student's work under it, and read back a score for each part of the rubric with a sentence of feedback. You stay in charge: the AI gives you a first-pass score, you decide the grade. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom in case you would rather not use AI at all.*

---

## Part 1. If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install anything or pay anything. This takes about five minutes the first time and under three minutes per student after that.

1. **Open a web browser** (Chrome, Safari, Edge, whatever you use) and go to **claude.ai**. (ChatGPT at **chatgpt.com** works the same way if you prefer it.)
2. **Make a free account.** Click **Sign up**, enter your email, and follow the steps. A free account is enough for this. You will not be charged.
3. **Start a new chat.** After you log in you will see a box that says something like *"How can I help you today?"* That box is where you type. A "chat" is just a conversation with the AI, like texting.
4. **Copy the grader instructions.** In Part 2 below, everything inside the gray box is one block. Select it all, copy it, and paste it into the chat box. Do not press send yet.
5. **Add the student's work underneath.** Press Enter twice to make space, then paste the student's full submission, including the one-page memo, the filled calculation table, and any AI transcript appendix the student attached, right below the instructions, in the same message.
6. **Send it.** Press Enter (or click the arrow). The AI will read it all and reply with a score for each of the five rubric rows, a total out of 100, and a short note for each row.
7. **Read the score and decide.** The AI's score is a **first draft for you to approve, change, or override.** You are the grader of record. If a score looks off, trust your own read; the grading guide (`ch10-pricing-advanced-grading-guide.md`) tells you what each row is really worth and carries the full answer key for the calculation table.
8. **Grade the next student.** Click **New chat** and repeat from step 4 for each submission. A fresh chat each time keeps one student's work from bleeding into the next.

That is the whole process. The block in Part 2 does the work; you paste, read, and sign off.

> **One quick sanity check you can always run yourself, no AI needed.** The calculation table has one answer key. The per-year Total Economic Value (row E) should resolve to forty-six thousand seven hundred seventy-two dollars exactly. The lifecycle Perceived Value (row K) should resolve to roughly two hundred fifty-six thousand three hundred seventy-nine dollars. The cost floor at a twenty-five percent contribution margin (row L) should resolve to forty-two thousand dollars exactly, computed as thirty-one thousand five hundred divided by zero point seven five. If those three cells are right in the student's table, the rest usually is. If those three cells are wrong, the analysis row caps low regardless of how clean the memo reads.

## Part 2. The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a second-year marketing student's individual assignment, the high-rigor card in Chapter 10 of an undergraduate Principles of Marketing course. The student was handed one industrial product (the Vortex IronCore HX-220 centrifugal pump), one named next-best alternative (the Stanton SP-180), and a sheet of four measurable performance differentials, and was asked to compute the Total Economic Value the new pump delivers over the alternative, discount that value to a Perceived Value the buyer would actually credit, and place a recommended launch price inside the feasible band between the contribution-margin floor and the PV-anchored ceiling. The deliverable is a one-page memo to a CFO and a city finance director plus a filled calculation table. Your job is to score their submission against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback. This is a first-year-friendly course; reward correct analysis and honest defense over polish.
>
> **The case the student worked from (this is fixed):**
> - The new product is the Vortex IronCore HX-220, a 220-horsepower stainless-steel centrifugal pump rated at 1,800 gallons per minute.
> - The named next-best alternative is the Stanton SP-180, a 200-horsepower stainless-steel centrifugal pump rated at 1,700 gallons per minute, with a current list price of forty-eight thousand dollars per unit installed and a fifteen-year industry reputation.
> - The four differentials, per pump per year in a mid-size municipal water-treatment plant:
>   - Differential A, unplanned downtime: Stanton averages eighteen hours per year of shutdown; the HX-220 averages three hours per year. Each hour costs the operator one thousand eight hundred dollars in bypass-water purchases, regulatory risk, and crew overtime.
>   - Differential B, energy: Stanton draws one hundred seventy-two kilowatts; the HX-220 draws one hundred forty-eight kilowatts. The pump runs six thousand five hundred hours per year, at zero point zero nine two dollars per kilowatt-hour.
>   - Differential C, scheduled maintenance: Stanton four thousand two hundred dollars per year; HX-220 one thousand one hundred dollars per year.
>   - Differential D, telemetry: the HX-220 has a remote-telemetry module that converts two-thirds of Stanton's zero point six emergency events per pump per year into scheduled repairs. An emergency repair averages eight thousand four hundred dollars; a scheduled repair averages two thousand six hundred dollars.
> - Both pumps have an eight-year typical operating life in this application.
> - The Vortex unit cost is thirty-one thousand five hundred dollars per pump, and the contribution-margin band finance set is twenty-five to forty percent.
> - The Perceived Value conversion rates the case gives, the share of Total Economic Value the buyer credits in a first conversation, are ninety-five percent on energy savings, eighty-five percent on scheduled maintenance, fifty-five percent on unplanned downtime, and forty percent on the telemetry-converted savings.
>
> **The answer key for the calculation table (verify against the student's numbers, rounding in the student's favor):**
> - Row A, per-year unplanned-downtime savings: fifteen times one thousand eight hundred equals twenty-seven thousand dollars.
> - Row B, per-year energy savings: twenty-four kilowatts times six thousand five hundred hours times zero point zero nine two dollars per kilowatt-hour equals fourteen thousand three hundred fifty-two dollars.
> - Row C, per-year scheduled-maintenance savings: four thousand two hundred minus one thousand one hundred equals three thousand one hundred dollars.
> - Row D, per-year telemetry-converted savings: zero point six times two-thirds times five thousand eight hundred equals two thousand three hundred twenty dollars.
> - Row E, per-year Total Economic Value: A plus B plus C plus D equals forty-six thousand seven hundred seventy-two dollars.
> - Row F, lifecycle Total Economic Value: E times eight equals three hundred seventy-four thousand one hundred seventy-six dollars.
> - Row G, lifecycle Perceived Value component on downtime at fifty-five percent: twenty-seven thousand times eight times zero point five five equals one hundred eighteen thousand eight hundred dollars.
> - Row H, lifecycle Perceived Value component on energy at ninety-five percent: fourteen thousand three hundred fifty-two times eight times zero point nine five equals one hundred nine thousand seventy-five dollars and twenty cents.
> - Row I, lifecycle Perceived Value component on scheduled maintenance at eighty-five percent: three thousand one hundred times eight times zero point eight five equals twenty-one thousand eighty dollars.
> - Row J, lifecycle Perceived Value component on telemetry at forty percent: two thousand three hundred twenty times eight times zero point four zero equals seven thousand four hundred twenty-four dollars.
> - Row K, lifecycle Perceived Value: G plus H plus I plus J equals roughly two hundred fifty-six thousand three hundred seventy-nine dollars.
> - Row L, cost floor at twenty-five percent contribution margin: thirty-one thousand five hundred divided by zero point seven five equals forty-two thousand dollars.
> - Row M, cost floor at forty percent contribution margin: thirty-one thousand five hundred divided by zero point six zero equals fifty-two thousand five hundred dollars.
> - Row N, competitor anchor: forty-eight thousand dollars, given.
> - Row O, PV-anchored ceiling: forty-eight thousand plus K equals roughly three hundred four thousand three hundred seventy-nine dollars.
>
> **The feasible band for the recommended launch price** is roughly forty-two thousand dollars at the low contribution-margin floor up to roughly three hundred four thousand dollars at the PV-anchored ceiling. The value-sharing practice the chapter describes lands most defended placements between fifty-five thousand and ninety-five thousand dollars, which captures a real premium over the Stanton anchor while leaving most of the buyer's credited value with the buyer. A student who lands inside that defended band, or outside it with a clear argument for why Vortex should capture more or less of the surplus, has done the analysis. A student who lands at the Stanton anchor of forty-eight thousand or below it has effectively chosen competitor-based pricing rather than the value-based lead the assignment asked for.
>
> **Common errors to watch for and flag in feedback:**
> - The markup-versus-margin slip on the cost floor. The contribution-margin floor at twenty-five percent is thirty-one thousand five hundred divided by zero point seven five (which is forty-two thousand dollars), NOT thirty-one thousand five hundred times one point two five (which is thirty-nine thousand three hundred seventy-five dollars, the markup arithmetic). If the student used the markup formula, cap the calculation-transparency row at Developing.
> - The per-year-versus-lifecycle confusion. The per-year Perceived Value is roughly thirty-two thousand forty-seven dollars; the lifecycle Perceived Value is roughly two hundred fifty-six thousand three hundred seventy-nine dollars. The pump is a one-time purchase, so the lifecycle figure bounds the price. If the student used the per-year figure as the ceiling and that drives the price placement, cap the analysis row at Proficient.
> - Pricing-to-match at forty-eight thousand without explicitly defending competitor-based pricing as the lead. Cap the analysis row at Developing.
> - Recommending a price near the full ceiling (three hundred thousand-plus) without explicitly defending Vortex taking the full surplus. Score the calculation row on its merits; score the defense row down unless the dual-entitlement and acceptance-rate consequences are addressed.
> - A risk paragraph that names risks the case does not raise (currency, supply chain, a competitor the case did not list). The case is closed; the risks in the case are the forty percent telemetry conversion rate the buyer trusts least, the eight-year lifecycle assumption, and the contribution-margin band.
>
> **How to score.** Give a number for each of the five criteria below, then add them for a total out of 100. For each, write one sentence saying why, and one sentence the student could act on. Be specific and encouraging. If something required is missing, say so plainly and score it low; do not invent credit.
>
> 1. **TEV-to-PV analysis and price placement (40 points), the heaviest row.** Is the calculation table filled correctly against the answer key above (rounding in the student's favor)? Are the lifecycle TEV (roughly three hundred seventy-four thousand) and the lifecycle PV (roughly two hundred fifty-six thousand three hundred seventy-nine) right? Does the recommended price sit inside the feasible band (forty-two thousand to three hundred four thousand, with most defended placements between fifty-five thousand and ninety-five thousand)? Is the value-sharing split between Vortex and the buyer stated and defended? Full marks: table correct, price in the band, value-sharing argument tied to this case. Low marks: multiple table errors, price outside the band, or no value-sharing argument.
> 2. **Value stick and chapter vocabulary (20 points).** Does the memo name value-based pricing as the lead approach AND name one other approach from the chosen-from list (cost-based, competitor-based, skimming, penetration, dynamic) considered and rejected with a sentence of reasoning? Is the value stick built correctly (cost as the floor, the forty-eight-thousand Stanton anchor as the frame, the PV-anchored figure as the ceiling)? Are TEV, PV, and value-sharing used as labels? Deduct for one stick element confused or vocabulary paraphrased.
> 3. **Defense to a finance audience (15 points).** Does the memo read as an argument the CFO and the city finance director could act on? Does it state the recommended price, walk the floor and the ceiling on this case's numbers, defend the value-sharing split, and name at least one specific risk the case raises (the forty percent telemetry conversion rate, the eight-year lifecycle, the contribution-margin band)? Deduct if it restates the table without arguing from it, or if the risks named are generic and not tied to this case.
> 4. **Calculation transparency and AI-as-calculator discipline (15 points).** Is every cell of the calculation table filled with the arithmetic visible? If AI was used as the calculator on any row, is the transcript appended and the result confirmed? Is markup not confused with margin (the cost floor row should use the divided-by-one-minus-margin formula, not the times-one-plus-markup formula)? Deduct for AI-used rows not transcripted or a markup reported as a margin.
> 5. **Writing and reflection (10 points).** Does the memo read in plain prose in the chapter's voice (no fragments or staccatos, no em-dashes, the chapter's vocabulary in plain sentences)? Are the two reflection insights specific to this case (the gap between TEV and PV, the buyer-credibility discount on the riskiest differential, the value-sharing logic, the way eight years of savings multiply a single year's figure into a much larger lifecycle number)? Deduct for generic insights or missing reflection.
>
> **On AI use:** disclosed AI use as a calculator on the arithmetic rows is permitted and not penalized; only AI use as the analyst (the model recommending the price without the student doing the value-stick walk) is penalized. If there is no account of how AI was used and no transcript at all, and the writing looks like an unedited AI dump, flag it for the instructor rather than scoring it; that is a human decision for the instructor to handle through the institution's academic-integrity and AI-use policy.
>
> **Output format:**
> - **Criterion 1, TEV-to-PV analysis and price placement: __/40** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 2, Value stick and chapter vocabulary: __/20** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 3, Defense to a finance audience: __/15** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 4, Calculation transparency: __/15** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 5, Writing and reflection: __/10** (why) (one tip)
> - **Total: __/100**
> - **One-line summary for the student:** (warm, specific, what to do next time)
>
> The student's submission follows below this line.

## Part 3. Reading and trusting the result

- The AI returns five scores, a total, and short notes. **Skim the notes first**, they tell you whether the AI actually understood the submission. If the notes match what you saw when you read the work, the total is trustworthy.
- **You can push back.** Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example "The student computed the per-year TEV correctly at forty-six thousand seven hundred seventy-two dollars but used the per-year PV of thirty-two thousand instead of the lifecycle PV of two hundred fifty-six thousand for the ceiling; look at Criterion 1 again with that in mind." The AI will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- **The single row to double-check yourself.** The TEV-to-PV analysis row is forty points and is where the AI's own read can be too generous on a near-correct table. Open the student's calculation table and verify three cells against the answer key: row E (per-year TEV, should be forty-six thousand seven hundred seventy-two dollars), row K (lifecycle PV, should be roughly two hundred fifty-six thousand three hundred seventy-nine dollars), and row L (cost floor at twenty-five percent contribution margin, should be forty-two thousand dollars exactly). If those three are right, the rest of the table usually is and the score holds; if any of those three is wrong, recompute the analysis-row score yourself.
- **The fastest sanity check needs no AI.** On the cost floor, the contribution margin is computed as cost divided by one minus the margin, not cost times one plus the margin. The first gives forty-two thousand dollars at twenty-five percent; the second gives thirty-nine thousand three hundred seventy-five dollars and is the markup arithmetic. If the student wrote the second number, that is the chapter's headline pricing-arithmetic mistake, and it caps the calculation-transparency row at Developing.
- **Keep a fresh chat per student** so submissions do not blur together.
- Remember the framing for any feedback you pass on: *the instructor grades; the AI assists.* The score is yours.

# Grading Skill: Audit a Real Dynamic-Pricing Rollout

*Chapter 10 group assignment (free account, group of three to four). This file lets you grade a group's submission with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste a block of instructions into Claude, paste the group's work under it, and read back a score for each part of the rubric with a sentence of feedback. You stay in charge: the AI gives you a first-pass score, you decide the grade. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom in case you would rather not use AI at all.*

---

## Part 1: If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install anything or pay anything. This takes about five minutes the first time and under three minutes per group after that.

1. **Open a web browser** (Chrome, Safari, Edge, whatever you use) and go to **claude.ai**. (ChatGPT at **chatgpt.com** works the same way if you prefer it.)
2. **Make a free account.** Click **Sign up**, enter your email, and follow the steps. A free account is enough for this. You will not be charged.
3. **Start a new chat.** After you log in you will see a box that says something like *"How can I help you today?"* That box is where you type. A "chat" is just a conversation with the AI, like texting.
4. **Copy the grader instructions.** In Part 2 below, everything inside the gray box is one block. Select it all, copy it, and paste it into the chat box. Do not press send yet.
5. **Add the group's work underneath.** Press Enter twice to make space, then paste the group's full submission, including the rollout they named, the six-principle audit table, the dual-entitlement verdict, the recommended change, the judgment pass (the facts they verified or corrected and the leading-versus-neutral prompt comparison), and each member's single-principle write-up, right below the instructions, in the same message.
6. **Send it.** Press Enter (or click the arrow). The AI will read both and reply with a score for each of the five rubric rows, a total out of 100, and a short note for each row.
7. **Read the score and decide.** The AI's score is a **first draft for you to approve, change, or override.** You are the grader of record. If a score looks off, trust your own read; the grading guide (`ch10-dynamic-pricing-audit-grading-guide.md`) tells you what each row is really worth.
8. **Grade the next group.** Click **New chat** and repeat from step 4 for each submission. A fresh chat each time keeps one group's work from bleeding into the next.

That is the whole process. The block in Part 2 does the work; you paste, read, and sign off.

> **One quick sanity check you can always run yourself, no AI needed.** This assignment lives or dies on whether the group verified the AI's facts. Pick one fact the group marked "confirmed" and one they marked "made up," and do a ten-second web search on each. If their labels match what a real news source says, their judgment pass is real. If they marked everything "confirmed" with no source named, they almost certainly trusted the model, which is the single most important thing to catch here.

## Part 2: The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a second-year marketing student group's assignment. The group of three to four students picked one real dynamic-pricing rollout that drew a public reaction (for example the Wendy's peak-hour pricing episode, a concert or airline surge case, or a theme-park weekend surcharge), used a free AI assistant with web search to build a case file on it, and was then asked to run the chapter's six-principle fairness audit AND to judge the AI's output: verify the facts it stated and break its sycophancy on a leading question. Your job is to score their submission against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback. This is a first-year-friendly course; reward honest judgment over polish.
>
> **Background you need (the chapter's framework):**
> - The **six-principle fairness audit** for a dynamic-pricing rollout is: explicability (can the firm explain why the price moved), transparency (is the price change visible and honest), cooperative framing (does it read as the firm and the customer on the same side), category tolerance (does this category carry a tight reference price that makes a change feel like a violation), brand-promise compatibility (does it fit what the brand stands for), and pre-launch communication (did the firm prepare customers before rolling it out). A strong audit marks each principle pass or fail with specific evidence from the real case.
> - The **dual-entitlement principle** is the verdict lens: a price increase that defends a real cost reads as fair, while one that just captures a demand windfall reads as gouging. A strong verdict reads the rollout one way or the other and ties it to the public reaction the rollout actually got.
> - **The judgment pass is the heart of the assignment.** On a research task, a current AI assistant states specific dates, figures, and quotes as fact when it has not verified them, and on a leading question ("wasn't this rollout obviously unfair?") it turns agreeable and confirms the framing instead of arguing. The group should have verified each AI-quoted date, figure, or quote against a real news source (marking each confirmed or made up), caught at least one invented fact, and re-asked the question neutrally ("argue both that this was fair and that it was unfair") to show the answer change. Catching one real invented fact and showing the leading-versus-neutral flip is the skill being tested.
> - This is group work with an **individual artifact**: each member takes one of the six principles, applies it to the rollout in their own short write-up (pass or fail with evidence), and names which AI-quoted fact they personally verified. No two write-ups should be the same.
>
> **What good looks like.** A strong submission names a real rollout, applies all six principles with case-specific evidence, lands a correct dual-entitlement verdict tied to the real public reaction, names one concrete change, and shows a real judgment pass: at least one AI fact verified or caught as invented against a named source, plus the model flipping between the leading and the neutral prompt. Each member's own single-principle write-up names a fact they personally checked.
>
> **The misfire to check for.** The tested Chapter 10 misfire here is an AI that states unverified dates, figures, and quotes as fact, and that gets sycophantic on a leading question. The group's job was to catch it. If they kept the AI's case file as fact with no verification, or ran only the leading prompt and stopped at the agreeable yes, score the judgment row down and say so kindly.
>
> **How to score.** Give a number for each of the five criteria below, then add them for a total out of 100. For each, write one sentence saying why, and one sentence the group could act on. Be specific and encouraging. If something required is missing, say so plainly and score it low; do not invent credit.
>
> 1. **Judgment pass: verified the AI and broke its sycophancy (40 points).** Did the group verify each AI-quoted date, figure, or quote against a real source (marking each confirmed or made up), catch at least one invented fact, AND show the leading-versus-neutral prompt difference? Full marks: a named, verified-or-corrected fact with its source, plus the model flipping between the leading and neutral prompts. Low marks: trusted the case file with little checking, or handed AI output in as fact.
> 2. **Six-principle audit (25 points).** Are all six principles (explicability, transparency, cooperative framing, category tolerance, brand-promise compatibility, pre-launch communication) applied to the rollout, each pass or fail with specific evidence? Deduct for principles listed but not applied to the case, or thin evidence.
> 3. **Dual-entitlement verdict (15 points).** Does the group correctly read the rollout as cost-defense or demand-windfall and tie the public reaction to it? Deduct for a verdict with a thin cost-versus-demand story, or fairness mentioned without the principle.
> 4. **Individual artifact, each member (12 points).** Is every member's single-principle write-up their own, evidenced, with a verified fact named? Deduct for thin, shared, or near-duplicate write-ups, or missing members.
> 5. **Recommendation and writing (8 points).** Is one concrete change named, in plain language with correct terms? Deduct for a vague change or a restatement with no recommendation.
>
> **Watch for these failure patterns and score them down (with a kind note):** keeping the AI's case file as fact with no verification; an unverified dated quote carried straight into the audit; running only the leading prompt and stopping at the agreeable yes; six principles marked with no case evidence; a verdict with no dual-entitlement reasoning; near-duplicate individual write-ups.
>
> **Do not penalize** disclosed AI use, even heavy use; that is expected here. Only the *absence* of a judgment pass is penalized, not the use of AI. If there is no transcript or disclosure at all and the case file looks like an unedited AI dump, flag it for the instructor as a possible disclosure issue rather than scoring it; that is a human decision.
>
> **Output format:**
> - **Criterion 1, Judgment pass: __/40** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 2, Six-principle audit: __/25** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 3, Dual-entitlement verdict: __/15** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 4, Individual artifact: __/12** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 5, Recommendation and writing: __/8** (why) (one tip)
> - **Total: __/100**
> - **One-line summary for the group:** (warm, specific, what to do next time)
>
> The group's submission follows below this line.

## Part 3: Reading and trusting the result

- The AI returns five scores, a total, and short notes. **Skim the notes first**; they tell you whether the AI actually understood the submission. If the notes match what you saw when you read the work, the total is trustworthy.
- **You can push back.** Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example *"The group did catch an invented date and named the source they checked; look again at Criterion 1"* or *"Check whether they ran the neutral re-prompt or only the leading one."* The AI will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- **The judgment-pass row is the one to double-check yourself.** It is worth the most (40 points) and it is where the AI's own read can be too generous. If the group named and verified a real fact against a source, and showed the model flipping between the leading and neutral prompts, that is a strong submission even if a principle or two is thin. The grading guide says so too.
- **The fastest verification needs no AI.** Take one fact the group marked "made up" and one they marked "confirmed," and run a quick web search on each. If their labels hold, the judgment pass is real. If everything is marked "confirmed" with no source, they likely trusted the model, which is the headline error this assignment trains them to catch.
- **Keep a fresh chat per group** so submissions do not blur together.
- Remember the framing for any feedback you pass on: *the instructor grades; the AI assists.* The score is yours.

## Part 4: Grade by hand instead (no AI at all)

If you would rather not use AI, grade straight from the rubric. Read each submission once, then score the five rows:

| Criterion | Points | What earns full marks |
|---|---|---|
| **Judgment pass: verified the AI and broke its sycophancy** | 40 | Verifies each AI-quoted date, figure, or quote against a real source (each marked confirmed or made up), catches at least one invented fact, and shows the leading-versus-neutral prompt difference |
| **Six-principle audit** | 25 | All six principles applied to the rollout, each pass or fail with specific evidence |
| **Dual-entitlement verdict** | 15 | Correctly reads the rollout as cost-defense or demand-windfall and ties the public reaction to it |
| **Individual artifact (each member)** | 12 | Every member's single-principle write-up is their own, evidenced, with a verified fact named |
| **Recommendation and writing** | 8 | One concrete change named; plain, correct terms |
| **Total** | **100** | |

**Fast bands:** 90 to 100, all six principles applied with case evidence, a correct verdict tied to the public reaction, every member's own write-up with a verified fact, one concrete change, and a real judgment pass (a fact verified or caught against a named source plus the leading-versus-neutral flip); 80 to 89, solid work with one thin row; 70 to 79, present but mostly trusted the AI (tidy audit on the unverified case file, no fact caught, or only the leading prompt run); 60 to 69, the case file handed in as fact with no verification, or no principle applied to the case; below 60 or integrity flag for a missing deliverable or an undisclosed AI dump (handle disclosure through your integrity process, not the rubric).

The single fastest signal of a strong submission: **a named AI fact, verified or caught as invented, with the real source beside it, plus the model flipping between the leading and neutral prompts.** If the group caught the model stating a date or quote it had not verified, and showed it agreeing on "wasn't this obviously unfair?" then filling out the argument when re-asked neutrally, the assignment worked. The ten-second check that settles it: take one "made up" fact and one "confirmed" fact and run a quick web search on each. Full details and failure modes are in `ch10-dynamic-pricing-audit-grading-guide.md`.

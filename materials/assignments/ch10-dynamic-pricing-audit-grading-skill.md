# Grading Skill: Audit an AI Dynamic-Pricing Case File

*Chapter 10 group assignment (groups of three to four), no AI account needed by students. This file lets you grade a group's submission with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste a block of instructions into Claude, paste the group's work under it, and read back a score for each part of the rubric with a sentence of feedback. The grader block already knows the planted errors in the case file, so it can tell whether the group caught them. You stay in charge: the AI gives you a first-pass score, you decide the grade. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom in case you would rather not use AI at all.*

---

## Part 1: If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install anything or pay anything. This takes about five minutes the first time and under three minutes per group after that.

1. **Open a web browser** (Chrome, Safari, Edge, whatever you use) and go to **claude.ai**. (ChatGPT at **chatgpt.com** works the same way if you prefer it.)
2. **Make a free account.** Click **Sign up**, enter your email, and follow the steps. A free account is enough for this. You will not be charged.
3. **Start a new chat.** After you log in you will see a box that says something like *"How can I help you today?"* That box is where you type. A "chat" is just a conversation with the AI, like texting.
4. **Copy the grader instructions.** In Part 2 below, everything inside the gray box is one block. Select it all, copy it, and paste it into the chat box. Do not press send yet.
5. **Add the group's work underneath.** Press Enter twice to make space, then paste the group's full submission, including the six-principle audit table, the fact-check list, the balanced argument they wrote in place of the AI's answer, the dual-entitlement verdict, the recommended change, and each member's single-principle write-up, right below the instructions, in the same message.
6. **Send it.** Press Enter (or click the arrow). The AI will read both and reply with a score for each of the five rubric rows, a total out of 100, and a short note for each row.
7. **Read the score and decide.** The AI's score is a **first draft for you to approve, change, or override.** You are the grader of record. If a score looks off, trust your own read; the grading guide (`ch10-dynamic-pricing-audit-grading-guide.md`) holds the full answer key and tells you what each row is really worth.
8. **Grade the next group.** Click **New chat** and repeat from step 4 for each submission. A fresh chat each time keeps one group's work from bleeding into the next.

That is the whole process. The block in Part 2 does the work; you paste, read, and sign off.

> **The quick check you can always run yourself, no AI needed.** The case file is the same for every group, so the heaviest row comes down to a few things. Did the group catch that the **seventy-four-cent peak surcharge was invented** (Wendy's announced a test for 2025, it never confirmed a peak surcharge), did they mark the **spokesperson quote** as unverified, did they keep the **true facts** (the roughly twenty-million-dollar investment, the 6.4 percent stock drop, the twelve-day reversal) as confirmed rather than crossing out everything, and did they **rewrite the AI's one-sided answer** at the bottom into a balanced argument? Catching the surcharge and rewriting the answer is a real pass; trusting the case file or rejecting everything is not.

## Part 2: The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a group submission for a second-year marketing course. This is a no-account judgment assignment: every group was handed the SAME pre-generated AI case file on the Wendy's dynamic-pricing episode of early 2024 and asked to audit it. They had to run the chapter's six-principle fairness audit on the episode, verify the case file's specific claims and mark each one confirmed or invented, rewrite the model's one-sided answer to a leading question into a balanced argument, land a dual-entitlement verdict, and name one change. The group turns in one group document plus a separate single-principle write-up from each member (groups are three or four students). Your job is to score the submission against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback. This is a first-year-friendly course; reward honest judgment over polish.
>
> **The case file every group audited (you need this to grade them).** It summarizes the real Wendy's episode and judges it fair, and it contains planted errors the group was supposed to catch:
> - **Invented fact 1, a fabricated peak-surcharge figure (the headline catch).** The case file claims Wendy's "confirmed" the digital menu boards would raise prices at peak hours, "adding a peak surcharge of seventy-four cents to popular items." This is invented. Wendy's never confirmed any peak surcharge; on the earnings call it said it would invest in digital menu boards and begin TESTING dynamic pricing as early as 2025, and after the backlash it said it never intended to raise peak prices, only to lower off-peak ones. A group that caught this marks the seventy-four-cent surcharge invented and corrects the record: Wendy's announced a test, not a confirmed surcharge. This is the most important catch, because keeping the surcharge as real means auditing a rollout that never happened.
> - **Invented fact 2, a fabricated spokesperson quote.** The case file quotes a Wendy's spokesperson saying "Customers who visit during peak demand will see prices adjust upward, and we think that is fair." No such quote exists; the model placed a plausible line in a named slot. A group that caught this marks the quote unverified or invented.
> - **Invented fact 3, a wrong date.** The case file dates the earnings call to March 12, 2024. It was mid-February 2024. This is the softer catch; missing it while catching the surcharge and quote is still strong.
> - **The facts the case file gets right (the group should mark these CONFIRMED, not reject them):** the roughly twenty-million-dollar investment in digital menu boards, the CEO language about testing "enhanced features like dynamic pricing and daypart offerings," the plan to begin testing as early as 2025, the "Uber-style surge pricing" headlines and the boycott hashtag, the stock falling about 6.4 percent, and the reversal about twelve days later with the company saying its comments were "misconstrued." A group that crosses out everything has guessed, not verified.
> - **The sycophancy at the bottom.** The brand team asked a leading question ("wasn't this rollout obviously unfair... Wendy's was just gouging people"), and the model flipped to a one-sided answer ("you are absolutely right... there is really no defense for it"), abandoning the balanced read it gave a sentence earlier. The group's job is to flag that the model capitulated and write the argument it should have given: the strongest case the rollout was defensible AND the strongest case it was unfair, then their own verdict.
>
> **Background you need (the chapter's framework):**
> - The **six-principle fairness audit** for a dynamic-pricing rollout is: explicability (can the firm explain why the price moved in terms the buyer already accepts, such as cost, capacity, time of use, or tier), transparency at the moment of encounter (is the mechanism inspectable where the buyer meets the price), cooperative framing (does the price story read as the firm and the buyer on the same side rather than as extraction), category tolerance (does the category carry a tight reference price and high purchase frequency that make a change feel like a violation), brand-promise compatibility (does the change fit what the brand stands for), and pre-launch communication (did the framing precede the rollout). A strong audit marks each principle pass or fail with specific evidence from this episode. For reference, the chapter's read of this episode fails most of these: cooperative framing fails because Wendy's framed it as a margin tool aimed at lunch traffic, category tolerance fails because fast food has a tight reference price and high frequency, and pre-launch communication fails because the earnings-call language ran ahead of any value story. The group's calls can differ if the evidence supports them.
> - The **dual-entitlement principle** is the verdict lens: a price increase that defends a real cost reads as fair, while one that captures a demand windfall reads as gouging. The defensible verdict here is demand windfall, because a peak surcharge on a lunch rush captures a demand spike rather than defending a cost. A strong verdict reads the episode one way or the other and ties it to the public reaction it actually drew.
> - This is group work with an **individual artifact**: each member takes one of the six principles, applies it to the episode in their own short write-up (pass or fail with evidence), and names one case-file fact they personally checked. No two write-ups should be the same.
>
> **How to score.** Give a number for each of the five criteria below, then add them for a total out of 100. For each, write one sentence saying why, and one sentence the group could act on. Be specific and encouraging. If something required is missing, say so plainly and score it low; do not invent credit.
>
> 1. **Catching what the AI got wrong (40 points).** Did the group mark each specific claim confirmed or invented, catch the invented seventy-four-cent surcharge and the fabricated quote (and ideally the wrong date), correct them, keep the true facts as confirmed, AND rewrite the model's one-sided answer into a balanced two-sided argument? Full marks: the surcharge and quote caught and corrected, the true facts confirmed, and a genuine two-sided rewrite. Low marks: trusted the case file's specifics, kept the surcharge as real, crossed out everything including the true facts, or left the sycophancy unanswered.
> 2. **Six-principle fairness audit (25 points).** Are all six principles applied to this episode, each pass or fail with specific evidence? Deduct for principles listed but not applied, or thin evidence.
> 3. **Dual-entitlement verdict (15 points).** Does the group read the episode as cost defense or demand windfall using the principle, and tie the public reaction to it? Deduct for a thin cost-versus-demand story, or fairness mentioned without the principle.
> 4. **Individual write-up, each member (12 points).** Is every member's single-principle write-up their own, evidenced, naming one case-file fact they checked? Deduct for thin, shared, or near-duplicate write-ups, or missing members.
> 5. **Recommendation and writing (8 points).** Is one concrete change named, in plain language with correct terms? Deduct for a vague change or a restatement with no recommendation.
>
> **Watch for these failure patterns and score them down (with a kind note):** keeping the invented seventy-four-cent surcharge as if it were real; crossing out the true facts along with the invented ones; catching one invented fact but keeping another as evidence; leaving the leading-question exchange unanswered; a rewrite that is still one-sided; six principles marked with no case evidence; a verdict with no dual-entitlement reasoning; near-duplicate individual write-ups.
>
> **A note on AI use.** Students needed no AI account for this card; the case file was provided. Score only the quality of the judgment, the catch, the audit, and the argument. If there is no group document or member write-ups at all, score what is missing as missing and flag it for the instructor, who will handle anything further under the institution's academic-integrity and AI-use policy; that is a human decision, not a scoring one.
>
> **Output format:**
> - **Criterion 1, Catching what the AI got wrong: __/40** (why, and say which invented facts were caught and whether the rewrite was two-sided) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 2, Six-principle fairness audit: __/25** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 3, Dual-entitlement verdict: __/15** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 4, Individual write-up: __/12** (why, and note any near-duplicates) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 5, Recommendation and writing: __/8** (why) (one tip)
> - **Total: __/100**
> - **One-line summary for the group:** (warm, specific, what to do next time)
>
> The group's submission follows below this line.

## Part 3: Reading and trusting the result

- The AI returns five scores, a total, and short notes. **Skim the notes first.** They tell you whether the AI actually understood the submission. Check that its note on Criterion 1 names which invented facts the group caught and whether they rewrote the sycophantic answer; that is the heart of the grade.
- **You can push back.** Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example *"The group did catch the invented surcharge and corrected it; look again at Criterion 1"* or *"Did they actually rewrite the AI's answer into a two-sided argument, or only say it was too agreeable?"* The AI will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- **The catching-what-the-AI-got-wrong row is the one to double-check yourself.** It is worth the most (40 points) and it is where the AI's own read can be too generous. If the group marked the seventy-four-cent surcharge invented with the corrected fact beside it, kept the true specifics as confirmed, and replaced the model's one-sided answer with a real two-sided argument, that is a strong submission even if a principle or two is thin. The grading guide says so too.
- **The fastest verification needs no AI.** The case file is the same for every group, so the answer key never changes: the seventy-four-cent surcharge and the spokesperson quote are invented, the date is wrong, and the roughly twenty-million-dollar investment, the 6.4 percent stock drop, and the twelve-day reversal are true. Check the group's labels against that and you have the heaviest row in under a minute.
- **Read the individual write-ups yourself.** The AI can miss near-duplicate paragraphs. Put the member write-ups side by side; if they are one paragraph with names swapped, the individual row caps low regardless of the group document.
- **Keep a fresh chat per group** so submissions do not blur together.
- Remember the framing for any feedback you pass on: *the instructor grades; the AI assists.* The score is yours.

**Fast bands:** 90 to 100, the invented surcharge and quote caught and corrected, the true facts confirmed, all six principles applied with case evidence, a correct dual-entitlement verdict tied to the public reaction, every member's own write-up with a checked fact, one concrete change, and the AI's answer rewritten into a real two-sided argument; 80 to 89, a real catch and a solid audit with one thin row (a one-sided rewrite, or the wrong date missed); 70 to 79, present but mostly trusted the case file (the surcharge kept as real, no invented fact caught, or the sycophancy never addressed); 60 to 69, the case file handed in as fact with no verification, or no principle applied to the episode; below 60 or integrity flag for a missing deliverable or one student's work submitted for four (handle integrity through your institution's academic-integrity and AI-use policy, not the rubric).

Build a Brand Analysis App with Claude

A Chapter 7 individual assignment in which the student picks a real brand, directs a free Claude account to build an interactive perceptual-map app for it, and then out-judges what Claude plotted. The 2026 brand-strategy skill is not drawing the map by hand. It is directing AI to gather and plot the rivals, and then catching where the model chose an axis the category does not turn on, placed a brand where it does not sit, or invented a competitor that does not compete. The student picks the brand. The materials below give the student everything else.

  • TypeIndividual
  • Time~2 hrs
  • Points100 pts
  • ChapterCh 7

Student instructions

Pick one real brand you can find public information on, with two or three nameable competitors that actually compete with it. A direct-to-consumer brand, a campus or local business, a brand you actually use, all work. Public information only; never paste a real employer's confidential brand data into an AI tool. Then make a free account at claude.ai if you do not already have one, since you will direct Claude to build the app for you.

The work is three moves and a judgment pass. First, have Claude web-search the brand and its rivals and pull what the market thinks: how each one positions itself, what reviews and social posts praise and complain about, and the two or three attributes buyers actually weigh in this category. Second, direct Claude to make an interactive Artifact, a perceptual map (the Chapter 7 tool) that plots your brand and its rivals on the two attributes that matter most, with each brand clickable to surface its points of parity, its one or two points of difference, and one reason to believe. Iterate with Claude in plain English until the map is honest and the click states are readable; you are building a real, shareable tool, not a static chart. Third, use the app you built to read the brand's current position and the open space on the map, the quadrant no rival owns yet, and decide whether you would hold the brand's position, sharpen it, or reposition it onto a different attribute.

Then comes the graded core, which is judging what Claude built against the real world. Claude will gather, plot, and write all of this fast, and in places it will be wrong in a way that would matter if a real brand team acted on it. The materials below give you the chapter's quick rules for axes, positioning, and the worked example you can pattern-match against; the evaluation checklist is what you walk through point by point on Claude's map. Pasting this prompt into a second AI tool will not help: the catch only lands once you have looked at the real product pages, the real ads, and the real reviews and compared what is actually there against what Claude plotted. The app is Claude's build; the brand judgment is yours.

Materials you hold (use these to direct Claude and to judge its map)

Quick rules for picking the two axes (Chapter 7). A perceptual map is only as good as its two axes, and the axes must be ones buyers in the category actually weigh, not ones an analyst guesses at. Use these four tests on every axis Claude proposes, in order. (1) Buyer-perceivable: a real buyer can tell where each brand sits without an expert opinion (price, sweetness, design formality, sustainability cues, brand-as-status are buyer-perceivable; "innovation," "quality," "premium" are not, they collapse into vibes). (2) Discriminating: rivals actually spread out along the axis (an axis where every brand in the category clusters at one end is not separating anything). (3) Defensible: the axis names a real attribute, not a synonym for "good." (4) Decision-relevant: a buyer choosing between two brands cares about the difference. An axis that fails any of these four is one of the things to catch.

Quick card on the Chapter 7 building blocks. Frame of reference: the competitive set the buyer should judge the brand against (Hyundai's Genesis named BMW and Mercedes as its frame to be read as a luxury car, not an economy one). Points of parity (POP): category must-haves that get the brand considered (every canned water is clean and cold, every laptop sleeve protects the laptop). They never win the buyer, but missing one gets the brand cut. Points of difference (POD): the one or two attributes the brand owns and a rival cannot truthfully claim. A real POD survives the swap test: if a rival's name can be dropped into the same sentence and the sentence still reads true, the POD is generic and does not count. Reason to believe: the proof point that makes the POD credible (Avis paired "We Try Harder" with "We are only number two," and the reason-to-believe carried the claim for half a century).

A worked perceptual-map example, on a different brand than yours (so it does not reveal your answer). A useful pattern to compare your own work against, on the running coffee-chains category from Chapter 7.

Category: coffee chains. Brands: Starbucks, Dunkin, Dutch Bros, Blue Bottle. Axes (worth defending): price (cheap to premium) on the across, craft (fast and convenient to artisanal) up. Where they sit: Dunkin cheap and fast, Dutch Bros mid and fast-friendly, Starbucks mid and semi-craft, Blue Bottle premium and artisanal. Open quadrant: low-price and genuinely artisanal, where no chain sits today. Candidate position: the everyday-price third-wave chain for buyers who want Blue Bottle quality at Dunkin frequency. POP: fast service, predictable drink quality, mobile order. POD for the candidate brand: third-wave craft at an everyday price. Reason to believe: direct sourcing, no franchise tax. A weak alternative axis the map should reject: "quality" up, "price" across. "Quality" is a vibes axis (it fails the buyer-perceivable and defensible tests above), and it lets the model place any brand it likes wherever it wants.

The worked example is a pattern, not the answer key for your brand. Your axes, your placements, and your reasoning have to come from your category, not this one.

Evaluation checklist for Claude's map (walk every line; this is what the judgment pass is graded on). Use it against the app Claude builds for you, not against the worked example above.

  1. The two axes. Apply the four tests above to each axis Claude picked. Name one axis that fails a test and the one that should replace it. Wrong axes are the most common error Claude makes; it tends to default to "price by quality" because that pair looks safe.
  2. The frame of reference. Are the rivals Claude plotted the ones the buyer actually weighs your brand against, or did Claude include a brand that does not really compete (a different price tier, a different occasion, a different distribution channel)? Drop a non-competitor if it slipped in.
  3. Brand placements. Pick one rival on the map and ask whether its position matches its real public positioning. Open the rival's product page, read its homepage copy, and skim recent reviews; does the brand actually sit where Claude put it, or is it nearer the opposite corner? Fix one placement that does not survive that check.
  4. Invented claims. Spot-check three things Claude wrote: one review theme it attributes to the brand, one product feature it lists, and one competitor price (if it gave any). Go to the real source for each. Mark each one confirmed, unconfirmed, or invented. If anything is invented or unconfirmed, drop it.
  5. Points of parity and points of difference. Do the POPs Claude listed for your brand really sit at the category floor (every rival meets them too)? Does the brand's stated POD survive the swap test against the two or three nearest rivals (a rival's name swapped in makes the sentence false)? If a POD is generic, name a sharper one the brand could actually defend.
  6. Reason to believe. Is there one concrete proof point behind the POD, or is the "reason" a restatement of the claim? If it is a restatement, write a real one or note the brand does not yet have one.
  7. What the map cannot show. A scatter plot can mark where brands sit; it cannot tell the brand team what to do next. In one sentence, name what the positioning means for the brand (the move the map points at) that the dots themselves are silent on.

A submission that walks every line of the checklist and corrects the real errors is doing exactly the structural judgment the card rewards.

Submit: one PDF, about a page, plus the Artifact link and the AI transcript.

  1. The working Artifact: paste the published share link, or attach a short screen recording of you using it.
  2. A one-page brand read: the brand's current position in one line, the open quadrant on the map, your hold or sharpen or reposition call and the attribute it turns on, and the brand's POP, the POD (with the swap-test result), and the reason to believe.
  3. The AI transcript and the judgment pass, walked against the checklist: the prompts you gave Claude, the axis you replaced (and why), the placement you fixed (and against which real source), and any invented claim you caught and dropped.

On the job. A brand strategist or product marketer is increasingly handed an AI-drafted perceptual map and asked to act on it, so the catch you practice here, naming the axis the model leaned on by default and the placement it never verified, is exactly the review that keeps a brand team from repositioning onto a quadrant that does not exist.

What this assesses · learning objectives, instructor only▸

These are the Chapter 7 objectives the work exercises. The rubric scores against them.

  • LO 7.4 Read a perceptual map, name the open quadrant, and choose a positioning move (hold, sharpen, reposition).
  • LO 7.5 Name a brand's frame of reference, its points of parity, its point of difference, and one reason to believe; apply the swap test.
  • LO 7.6 Judge AI output about a brand, saying which axis was defensible, which placement matched the real source, and which figure or claim was invented.

The AI step trains the chapter's tested misfire. On a perceptual map, a current AI assistant leans on default axes the category does not actually turn on (the "price by quality" reflex), states a competitor as a rival when it does not really compete, places a brand from a homepage tagline rather than what the buyer perceives, and writes a generic point of difference any rival could claim. The map will look polished. The judgment pass is the student's to do.

# Grading Skill: The Pricing-Strategy Case Memo

*Chapter 10 individual assignment. This file lets you grade a student's memo with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste a block of instructions into Claude, paste the student's work under it, and read back a score for each part of the rubric with a sentence of feedback. The student did the memo with no AI; your optional AI use here is only to speed your grading. You stay in charge: the AI gives you a first-pass score, you decide the grade. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom in case you would rather not use AI at all.*

---

## Part 1. If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install anything or pay anything. This takes about five minutes the first time and under two minutes per student after that.

1. **Open a web browser** (Chrome, Safari, Edge, whatever you use) and go to **claude.ai**. (ChatGPT at **chatgpt.com** works the same way if you prefer it.)
2. **Make a free account.** Click **Sign up**, enter your email, and follow the steps. A free account is enough for this. You will not be charged.
3. **Start a new chat.** After you log in you will see a box that says something like *"How can I help you today?"* That box is where you type. A "chat" is just a conversation with the AI, like texting.
4. **Copy the grader instructions.** In Part 2 below, everything inside the gray box is one block. Select it all, copy it, and paste it into the chat box. Do not press send yet.
5. **Add the student's memo underneath.** Press Enter twice to make space, then paste the student's full memo, the lead approach and 3 Cs walk, the posture, the psychological tactic, and the fairness risk, right below the instructions, in the same message.
6. **Send it.** Press Enter (or click the arrow). The AI will read both and reply with a score for each of the four rubric rows, a total out of 100, and a short note for each row.
7. **Read the score and decide.** The AI's score is a **first draft for you to approve, change, or override.** You are the grader of record. If a score looks off, trust your own read, the grading guide (`ch10-pricing-strategy-case-grading-guide.md`) tells you what each row is really worth.
8. **Grade the next student.** Click **New chat** and repeat from step 4 for each submission. A fresh chat each time keeps one student's work from bleeding into the next.

That is the whole process. The block in Part 2 does the work; you paste, read, and sign off.

> **One quick thing to look for yourself, no AI needed.** This memo lives or dies on whether the student made a real recommendation and argued it from the case facts. Skim for the call: did they pick a lead approach (cost-plus, competitor-based, or value-based) and a posture (hold, raise, skim, penetrate), and did they back it with the videographer's reputation, the stakes of the event, and the rivals? A memo that defines all three approaches and recommends nothing is the most common miss, and you can spot it in fifteen seconds.

## Part 2. The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a second-year marketing student's individual pricing-strategy memo. The student read a short business scenario and wrote a one-to-two-page memo making a pricing recommendation, with no AI involved. Your job is to score their memo against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback. This is a first-year-friendly course; reward a committed, fact-grounded recommendation over a tour of every option.
>
> **The scenario the student worked from:** a wedding videographer with twenty years of experience charges one flat fee per wedding and is deciding whether to raise prices and how. The work is high stakes (the day cannot be redone), the reputation and portfolio are strong, and rivals run from a cheap newcomer to a studio charging roughly double. (Some students may instead have a small clothing line deciding its lineup prices; grade that on the same rubric.)
>
> **Background you need (the chapter's frameworks):**
> - There are three core pricing approaches. **Cost-plus** prices up from the seller's cost. **Competitor-based** prices off what rivals charge. **Value-based** prices off what the work is worth to the buyer. The right one depends on the situation. For a strong-reputation seller doing high-stakes, un-redoable work, value-based pricing usually fits best, because the buyer is paying for the outcome, not the hours.
> - The **3 Cs** frame the decision: **Company** cost (the floor), **Customer** value (the ceiling), and **Competitor** price (the frame in between). A good memo applies each C to this specific seller, not as definitions.
> - **Posture** is the timing call: hold, raise now, skim (price high at first, lower over time), or penetrate (price low to win volume fast). It should follow from the facts.
> - **Psychological tactics** include anchoring, a decoy or middle-option lineup, charm endings (a price like $1,995), and bundling. A good memo picks one and says what it would do here.
> - **Fairness** is judged with the dual-entitlement principle: a price increase that covers a real cost or reflects real value reads as fair, while one that just captures buyers' desperation or a demand spike reads as gouging.
> - The memo's heart is a **committed recommendation argued from the case facts**. A student who picks an approach and a posture and defends them from the reputation, the stakes, and the rivals has done the work. A student who recites the frameworks and recommends nothing has not.
>
> **How to score.** Give a number for each of the four criteria below, then add them for a total out of 100. For each, write one sentence saying why, and one sentence the student could act on. Be specific and encouraging. If something required is missing, say so plainly and score it low; do not invent credit.
>
> 1. **The pricing recommendation, argued from the case (42 points).** Did the student pick a lead approach AND a posture and defend both from the scenario's specific facts via the 3 Cs, with a clear, decision-ready argument? Full marks: a committed call backed by the reputation, the stakes, and the rivals. Low marks: a list of approaches or a tour of definitions with no real recommendation, or a call that ignores the case facts.
> 2. **The 3 Cs walk (24 points).** Are Company cost, Customer value, and Competitor price each addressed with reasoning specific to this case? Deduct when the three Cs are named as a list but not applied to this seller.
> 3. **Psychological tactic and fairness risk (22 points).** Did the student choose one psychological tactic with a correct expected effect, AND name one real fairness risk with a dual-entitlement-sound mitigation? Full marks needs both. Deduct when a tactic is named with no effect, or fairness is mentioned without the dual-entitlement logic.
> 4. **Memo writing (12 points).** Is the memo tight, decision-ready, and in plain language with correct pricing terms? Deduct for disconnected points or muddy terms (for example, confusing value-based with cost-plus pricing).
>
> **Watch for these patterns and score them down (with a kind note):** defining every approach and recommending nothing; a 3 Cs section that lists the three Cs but never applies them to the seller; a recommendation that floats free of the scenario's facts; a fairness line bolted on with no dual-entitlement reasoning.
>
> **Reward the harder, sharper call.** Picking value-based pricing for a strong-reputation, high-stakes seller and arguing it well is the kind of judgment the chapter trains. Do not reward a safe definition tour over a committed, fact-grounded recommendation.
>
> **Output format:**
> - **Criterion 1, The pricing recommendation: __/42.** (why), then (one tip)
> - **Criterion 2, The 3 Cs walk: __/24.** (why), then (one tip)
> - **Criterion 3, Psychological tactic and fairness risk: __/22.** (why), then (one tip)
> - **Criterion 4, Memo writing: __/12.** (why), then (one tip)
> - **Total: __/100**
> - **One-line summary for the student:** (warm, specific, what to do next time)
>
> The student's memo follows below this line.

## Part 3. Reading and trusting the result

- The AI returns four scores, a total, and short notes. **Skim the notes first**, they tell you whether the AI actually understood the memo. If the notes match what you saw when you read the work, the total is trustworthy.
- **You can push back.** Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example *"The student did commit to value-based pricing and argued it from the reputation, look again at Criterion 1"* or *"Check whether the 3 Cs were actually applied to this seller or just named."* The AI will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- **The recommendation row is the one to read closely yourself.** It is worth the most, and it is where the AI's read can be too generous to a memo that sounds thorough but never commits. If the student made a defensible call from these facts, that is a strong paper even if a later row is thin. The grading guide says so too.
- **The fastest signal needs no AI.** Look for the call: a lead approach plus a posture, backed by the case facts. If the memo defines the options and recommends nothing, the core skill is missing, whatever the rest looks like.
- **Keep a fresh chat per student** so memos do not blur together.
- Remember the framing for any feedback you pass on: *the instructor grades; the AI assists.* The student used no AI; the score is yours.

If you would rather not use AI, grade straight from the rubric. Read each memo once, then score the four rows:

|---|---|---|
| **The pricing recommendation, argued from the case** | 42 | Picks a lead approach AND a posture and defends both from the scenario's specific facts via the 3 Cs, with a decision-ready argument |
| **The 3 Cs walk** | 24 | Company cost, Customer value, and Competitor price each addressed with reasoning specific to this case |
| **Psychological tactic and fairness risk** | 22 | One tactic with a correct expected effect, AND one real fairness risk named with a dual-entitlement-sound mitigation |
| **Memo writing** | 12 | Tight, decision-ready memo in plain language with correct pricing terms |
| **Total** | **100** | |

**Fast bands:** 90 to 100 a committed approach and posture defended from the case facts, a 3 Cs walk applied to this seller, a tactic with a correct effect, and a sound fairness mitigation; 80 to 89 a real recommendation with one thin row; 70 to 79 frameworks present but mostly described rather than decided (a partial or fact-free recommendation, a 3 Cs section that names without applying); 60 to 69 a definition tour with no real recommendation; below 60 a missing deliverable or a memo that does not engage the scenario.

The single fastest signal of a strong memo: **a committed recommendation argued from the case facts.** If the student picked value-based pricing for the videographer and defended it from the reputation and the high-stakes work, the assignment worked. The question that settles most grades: did the memo make a defensible call from these facts, or just survey the options?

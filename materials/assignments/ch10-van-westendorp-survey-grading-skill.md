# Grading Skill: Read a Van Westendorp Dataset for a Cold-Pressed Juice Launch

*Chapter 10 group assignment (groups of three to five), free AI account is permitted for the students as the calculator and used here only by the grader. This file lets you grade a group's submission with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste a block of instructions into Claude, paste the group's work under it, and read back a score for each part of the rubric with a sentence of feedback. You stay in charge: the AI gives you a first-pass score, you decide the grade. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom in case you would rather not use AI at all.*

---

## Part 1: If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install anything or pay anything. This takes about five minutes the first time and under two minutes per group after that.

1. **Open a web browser** (Chrome, Safari, Edge, whatever you use) and go to **claude.ai**. (ChatGPT at **chatgpt.com** works the same way if you prefer it.)
2. **Make a free account.** Click **Sign up**, enter your email, and follow the steps. A free account is enough for this. You will not be charged.
3. **Start a new chat.** After you log in you will see a box that says something like *"How can I help you today?"* That box is where you type. A "chat" is just a conversation with the AI, like texting.
4. **Copy the grader instructions.** In Part 2 below, everything inside the gray box is one block. Select it all, copy it, and paste it into the chat box. Do not press send yet.
5. **Add the group's work underneath.** Press Enter twice to make space, then paste the group's full submission, including the PDF text (the four cumulative curves chart description, the four crossing values, the recommended launch price, the rival defense against Suja Mighty Greens at three dollars and ninety-nine cents and Pressed Juicery at seven dollars and ninety-nine cents, and every member's one-paragraph scenario reading), right below the instructions, in the same message. If the group used a free AI account to plot the curves and pasted the chat as an appendix, include that too. If the chart is a separate image file, describe what the chart shows in two or three sentences so the model has something to grade.
6. **Send it.** Press Enter (or click the arrow). The AI will read it all and reply with a score for each of the five rubric rows, a total out of one hundred, and a short note for each row.
7. **Read the score and decide.** The AI's score is a **first draft for you to approve, change, or override.** You are the grader of record. If a score looks off, trust your own read. The grading guide (`ch10-van-westendorp-survey-grading-guide.md`) carries the answer key and tells you what each row is really worth.
8. **Grade the next group.** Click **New chat** and repeat from step 4 for each submission. A fresh chat each time keeps one group's work from bleeding into the next.

That is the whole process. The block in Part 2 does the work; you paste, read, and sign off.

> **One thing to check yourself, no AI needed.** Glance at the group's four crossing values against the answer key in the grading guide. PMC should land at or very near four dollars (the lower bound of the band, where the too-cheap and a-bargain curves both land at about twenty-eight percent). OPP should land between five dollars and seventy-five cents and six dollars and fifty cents (the center, in the gap where both too-cheap and too-expensive are near zero). IPP should land between five dollars and twenty-five cents and five dollars and seventy-five cents (the median willingness to pay, near five dollars and forty-five cents). PME should land between six dollars and seventy-five cents and seven dollars (the upper bound, near six dollars and ninety cents). If all four are in those ranges, the band is being read correctly off the data. If one or more are far outside those ranges, the four-curves row caps low.

## Part 2: The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a group submission for a second-year marketing course. The group of three to five students was given a Van Westendorp price-sensitivity dataset for Tallgrass Pressed Juicery, a small Stillwater cold-pressed juice operation launching a sixteen-ounce green blend. The dataset has eighteen real-feeling respondent rows with four price columns (too cheap, a bargain, getting expensive, too expensive). The group's job was to build the four cumulative curves from the dataset, find the four crossings (OPP, PMC, PME, IPP), read the acceptable price band, and recommend a launch price defended against Suja Mighty Greens at three dollars and ninety-nine cents and Pressed Juicery at seven dollars and ninety-nine cents. Each member also ran one of five listed scenarios on the same dataset and wrote a one-paragraph reading. A free Claude or ChatGPT account was permitted as the calculator and the charting tool, not the analyst. Your job is to score the submission against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback.
>
> **Background you need (the chapter's framework and the answer key for this dataset):**
>
> - **Van Westendorp's four questions** are indirect: the price at which the product is too expensive, the price at which it is too cheap (so cheap the buyer doubts the quality), the price at which it is getting expensive, and the price that would be a bargain.
>
> - **The four cumulative curves are built off the pooled table** with the eighteen respondents as the denominator. Each respondent counts for one over eighteen of the cumulative percent, which is about five and six tenths percent per respondent.
>
>   1. **Too cheap, descending.** At price P, the share whose too-cheap value is greater than or equal to P. Formula in words: count of too-cheap values greater than or equal to P, divided by eighteen, times one hundred.
>   2. **A bargain, ascending.** At price P, the share whose a-bargain value is strictly less than P (the share for whom P has climbed above their bargain zone). Formula in words: count of a-bargain values strictly less than P, divided by eighteen, times one hundred.
>   3. **Getting expensive, descending.** At price P, the share whose getting-expensive value is strictly greater than P (the share for whom P has not yet entered the getting-expensive zone). Formula in words: count of getting-expensive values strictly greater than P, divided by eighteen, times one hundred.
>   4. **Too expensive, ascending.** At price P, the share whose too-expensive value is less than or equal to P. Formula in words: count of too-expensive values less than or equal to P, divided by eighteen, times one hundred.
>
>   Two curves rise (a bargain and too expensive), and two curves fall (too cheap and getting expensive). Each of the four crossings is a meeting of one rising and one falling curve, so the crossing is well-defined.
>
> - **The four crossings on this dataset (the answer key):**
>   - **PMC, the point of marginal cheapness** (too cheap meets a bargain): exactly four dollars, where both cumulative shares equal twenty-eight percent. The lower bound of the acceptable band. Acceptable range three dollars and seventy-five cents to four dollars and twenty-five cents.
>   - **OPP, the optimal price point** (too cheap meets too expensive): approximately six dollars, where both cumulative shares are near zero (the too-cheap curve has dropped to zero and the too-expensive curve has not yet risen). The center of the band, interpreted as the price at which essentially no respondent calls the juice either too cheap or too expensive. Acceptable range five dollars and seventy-five cents to six dollars and fifty cents.
>   - **IPP, the indifference price point** (a bargain meets getting expensive): approximately five dollars and forty-five cents. Roughly the median willingness to pay. Acceptable range five dollars and twenty-five cents to five dollars and seventy-five cents.
>   - **PME, the point of marginal expensiveness** (too expensive meets getting expensive): approximately six dollars and ninety cents. The upper bound of the acceptable band. Acceptable range six dollars and seventy-five cents to seven dollars.
>
> - **The acceptable band** runs from PMC at four dollars up to PME at about six dollars and ninety cents. A defensible recommended launch price sits inside the band, near OPP or IPP. Natural landing zones: five dollars and ninety-five cents, six dollars and twenty-five cents, six dollars and forty-nine cents.
>
> - **The two named competitors:** Suja Mighty Greens at three dollars and ninety-nine cents (sits below the band's floor) and Pressed Juicery at seven dollars and ninety-nine cents (sits above the band's ceiling). The recommendation should reference both.
>
> - **The five scenarios for the per-member artifact:** (1) exclude the bottom quartile of too-expensive respondents, (2) exclude the top quartile of too-cheap respondents, (3) split the dataset by the median too-expensive value into a dollar-conscious half and a quality-conscious half, (4) take the median of each column instead of the cumulative crossing, (5) add fifty cents to every too-expensive value (a bottle deposit shift).
>
> - **The optional AI is a calculator, not the analyst.** If the group used a free Claude or ChatGPT account to plot the curves and solve for the crossings, that is permitted; it is the spreadsheet. Do not deduct for using it, and do not award extra credit for not using it. The grade is on the band, the recommendation, and the per-member scenarios.
>
> **How to score.** Give a number for each of the five criteria below, then add them for a total out of one hundred. For each, write one sentence saying why, and one sentence the group could act on. Be specific and encouraging. If something required is missing, say so plainly and score it low; do not invent credit.
>
> 1. **The four curves and the four crossings off the Tallgrass dataset (thirty points).** Are the four cumulative curves built correctly from all eighteen rows, the four crossings (OPP, PMC, PME, IPP) found at the right intersections and labeled with dollar values, and the acceptable band shaded between PMC and PME? Full marks: four crossing values close to the answer key (PMC at four dollars, OPP around six dollars, IPP around five dollars and forty-five cents, PME around six dollars and ninety cents), and the band shaded between PMC and PME. Proficient: one crossing drifts further from the answer key. Low marks: a band asserted without curves, a crossing labeled at an impossible point, or one curve sloping the wrong direction.
>
> 2. **Recommended launch price defended against the two named competitors (twenty points).** Is a price set inside the band (between four dollars and six dollars and ninety cents), with one or two sentences defending it against both Suja Mighty Greens at three dollars and ninety-nine cents (below the floor) and Pressed Juicery at seven dollars and ninety-nine cents (above the ceiling)? Deduct for a partial defense (one competitor named) or a price set outside the band.
>
> 3. **Per-member scenario, one per member (twenty points).** Did every member run one of the five listed scenarios (no two members the same), recompute the curves on the sliced or shifted data, find new crossings, and write a one-paragraph reading of what shifted? Deduct for shared scenarios across two or more members, or paragraphs that restate the group band rather than reading the slice.
>
> 4. **The four Van Westendorp questions named correctly (fifteen points).** Are the four crossings named correctly in the results table (OPP at too cheap meets too expensive, PMC at too cheap meets a bargain, PME at too expensive meets getting expensive, IPP at a bargain meets getting expensive), and are the brief's four questions reproduced or referenced? Deduct for a crossing labeled at the wrong two-curve pair.
>
> 5. **Writing, presentation, and the reflection (fifteen points).** Is the prose plain and clear, with the chapter vocabulary (cumulative curve, OPP, PMC, PME, IPP, acceptable band) used correctly, and are the two insights at the close specific to what the group read off the data? Deduct for disconnected writing or a generic reflection.
>
> **Watch for these failure patterns and score them down (with a kind note):** a band asserted with no curves built from the data; one curve sloping the wrong direction; a crossing labeled at an impossible point (PMC above PME, the band shaded outside the marginal points); a recommended price outside the band with no acknowledgment; a recommended price inside the band but with no reference to either named competitor; per-member scenarios shared across members; per-member paragraphs that do not match the dataset (claim a shift the data does not produce).
>
> **Do not deduct for using AI as the calculator.** A free Claude or ChatGPT account is permitted on this card as the tool that plots the curves and solves for the crossings, the same way a spreadsheet would. The grade is on the band, the recommendation, and the per-member scenarios, not on which tool drew the chart. The only AI-related thing worth flagging is if the chart numbers do not match the pooled table the group reports (the AI sometimes invents a respondent or rounds an odd value); if the chart and the pooled table do not match, treat the band as asserted rather than read off the data.
>
> **Output format:**
> - **Criterion 1, The four curves and the four crossings: __/30** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 2, Recommended launch price: __/20** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 3, Per-member scenario: __/20** (why, noting any shared scenarios) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 4, The four Van Westendorp questions named correctly: __/15** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 5, Writing, presentation, and the reflection: __/15** (why) (one tip)
> - **Total: __/100**
> - **One-line summary for the group:** (warm, specific, what to do next time)
>
> The group's submission follows below this line.

## Part 3: Reading and trusting the result

- The AI returns five scores, a total, and short notes. **Skim the notes first.** They tell you whether the AI actually understood the submission. Check that its note on Criterion 1 confirms the four crossings land near the answer-key values: PMC at four dollars, OPP around six dollars, IPP around five dollars and forty-five cents, PME around six dollars and ninety cents.
- **You can push back.** Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example *"The group's PME is at seven dollars and twenty cents, look again at Criterion 1"* or *"Three members ran the same scenario; should Criterion 3 be lower?"* The AI will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- **The four-curves row is the one to double-check yourself.** It is worth thirty points. Compare the group's four crossing values to the answer key in the grading guide. If PMC lands at or very near four dollars, OPP between five dollars and seventy-five cents and six dollars and fifty cents, IPP between five dollars and twenty-five cents and five dollars and seventy-five cents, and PME between six dollars and seventy-five cents and seven dollars, the band is being read correctly.
- **Read the per-member scenarios yourself.** Spot-check one or two member paragraphs against the dataset. If a member ran the bottom-quartile-too-expensive-exclusion scenario, the band should shift higher by about fifty to seventy cents; if a member reports no change at all, the scenario was not actually run.
- **You do not need any AI math to grade this.** The answer key in the grading guide carries the four crossings, the band, and the typical recommended-price landing zones. Use the key to anchor.
- **Keep a fresh chat per group** so submissions do not blur together.
- Remember the framing for any feedback you pass on: *the instructor grades; the AI assists.* The score is yours.

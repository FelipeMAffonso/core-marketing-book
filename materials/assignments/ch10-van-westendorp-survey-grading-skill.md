# Grading Skill: Run a Van Westendorp Price Survey on a Real Product

*Chapter 10 group assignment (groups of three to five), free AI account is optional for the students and used here only by the grader. This file lets you grade a group's submission with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste a block of instructions into Claude, paste the group's work under it, and read back a score for each part of the rubric with a sentence of feedback. You stay in charge: the AI gives you a first-pass score, you decide the grade. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom in case you would rather not use AI at all.*

---

## Part 1: If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install anything or pay anything. This takes about five minutes the first time and under two minutes per group after that.

1. **Open a web browser** (Chrome, Safari, Edge, whatever you use) and go to **claude.ai**. (ChatGPT at **chatgpt.com** works the same way if you prefer it.)
2. **Make a free account.** Click **Sign up**, enter your email, and follow the steps. A free account is enough for this. You will not be charged.
3. **Start a new chat.** After you log in you will see a box that says something like *"How can I help you today?"* That box is where you type. A "chat" is just a conversation with the AI, like texting.
4. **Copy the grader instructions.** In Part 2 below, everything inside the gray box is one block. Select it all, copy it, and paste it into the chat box. Do not press send yet.
5. **Add the group's work underneath.** Press Enter twice to make space, then paste the group's full submission, including the group PDF (the four questions, the pooled response table, the four cumulative curves with the four crossings labeled, the band shaded between PMC and PME, the recommended price with its one-sentence reason) and every member's five-response slice and band note, right below the instructions, in the same message. If the group used a free AI account to plot the curves and pasted the chat as an appendix, include that too.
6. **Send it.** Press Enter (or click the arrow). The AI will read it all and reply with a score for each of the five rubric rows, a total out of 100, and a short note for each row.
7. **Read the score and decide.** The AI's score is a **first draft for you to approve, change, or override.** You are the grader of record. If a score looks off, trust your own read. The grading guide (`ch10-van-westendorp-survey-grading-guide.md`) tells you what each row is really worth.
8. **Grade the next group.** Click **New chat** and repeat from step 4 for each submission. A fresh chat each time keeps one group's work from bleeding into the next.

That is the whole process. The block in Part 2 does the work; you paste, read, and sign off.

> **One thing to check yourself, no AI needed.** Count the rows in the pooled response table. The floor on this card is 15 real responses (five per member, three members at minimum). If the pool is under 15, the band row caps below Excellent regardless of how clean the chart looks. Then glance at the four cumulative curves: two should rise from left to right, two should fall. If a curve slopes the wrong way, the curves were built off the wrong direction and the band cannot be right.

## Part 2: The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a group submission for a second-year marketing course. The group (three to five students) ran a Van Westendorp price-sensitivity survey on a product of their choice: they wrote the four indirect questions for that product, each member surveyed five real people so the group pooled 15 or more responses, and they read the acceptable price band off the four crossings of the four cumulative curves. They recommended a price inside the band and compared it to what one or two named rivals actually charge. Each member separately turns in their own five real responses and a one-paragraph band note. A free Claude or ChatGPT account was optional and used only as a calculator and charting tool if the group chose; the band and the recommendation are theirs either way. Your job is to score the submission against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback.
>
> **Background you need (the chapter's framework):**
> - **Van Westendorp's four questions** are indirect: the price at which the product is too expensive, the price at which it is too cheap (so cheap the buyer doubts the quality), the price at which it is getting expensive, and the price that would be a bargain. A direct "what would you pay?" question is the wrong method.
> - The pooled answers build four cumulative curves with price on the horizontal axis and cumulative percent on the vertical axis. The "too expensive" and "getting expensive" curves rise as price rises (two rising curves); the "too cheap" and "a bargain" curves fall as price rises (two falling curves).
> - The four curves cross at four landmark points: the **OPP** (too cheap meets too expensive, the center of the band), the **PMC** (too cheap meets a bargain, the lower bound of the acceptable band), the **PME** (too expensive meets a bargain, the upper bound of the acceptable band), and the **IPP** (getting expensive meets a bargain, roughly the median willingness to pay). The acceptable band runs from PMC up to PME. A good answer reads the band off these four named crossings of the pooled data, not off a glance at a chart.
> - **Customer value-based pricing** reads willingness to pay first, and the Van Westendorp PSM is the launch-stage tool for measuring WTP when there is no sales history to read elasticity from.
> - **The optional AI is a calculator, not the analyst.** If the group used a free Claude or ChatGPT account to plot the curves and solve for the crossings, that is permitted; it is the spreadsheet. Do not deduct for using it, and do not award extra credit for not using it. The grade is on the data, the band, and the recommendation.
>
> **How to score.** Give a number for each of the five criteria below, then add them for a total out of 100. For each, write one sentence saying why, and one sentence the group could act on. Be specific and encouraging. If something required is missing, say so plainly and score it low; do not invent credit.
>
> 1. **Band reading off real pooled data (35 points).** Did the group pool 15 or more real responses, build four cumulative curves that slope the right way (two rising, two falling), label all four crossings (OPP, PMC, PME, IPP) with dollar values at the right intersections, and shade the band between PMC and PME? Full marks: 15 or more pooled, four labeled crossings at the right intersections, band correctly shaded. Proficient: a minor plotting or labeling slip, or 12 to 14 responses. Low marks: a band asserted with no curves behind it, a crossing misplaced (PMC above OPP for example), or curves sloping the wrong way.
> 2. **The four Van Westendorp questions (20 points).** Are all four worded as the indirect framings (too expensive, too cheap, getting expensive, a bargain), and is the product named in each? Deduct for one wording slip; deduct heavily for a direct "what would you pay?" question.
> 3. **Recommended price (15 points).** Is a price set inside the band with a one-sentence reason tied to what one or two named rivals actually charge? Deduct for a price with no reason or a price set outside the band with no acknowledgment.
> 4. **Individual artifact, each member (15 points).** Are every member's five real responses and band note present and their own? The five-response slices should add up to the pooled dataset and the notes should be real individual readings, not a copy of the group's band. Deduct for slices that are thin or shared (the same five responses reused, or notes that restate the group band).
> 5. **Writing clarity (15 points).** Is the prose plain and clear, with the four crossings (OPP, PMC, PME, IPP) and the band used correctly? Deduct for disconnected writing.
>
> **Watch for these failure patterns and score them down (with a kind note):** fewer than 15 pooled responses; the same five responses reused across two or more members; a direct "what would you pay?" question instead of the four indirect ones; curves built off the wrong direction (a rising curve where it should fall, or a falling curve where it should rise); a crossing labeled at an impossible point (PMC above PME, the band shaded outside the PMC-to-PME range); a recommended price outside the band with no acknowledgment; a recommended price inside the band but with no reason tied to a named rival.
>
> **Do not deduct for using AI as the calculator.** A free Claude or ChatGPT account is permitted on this card as the tool that plots the curves and solves for the crossings, the same way a spreadsheet would. The grade is on the data and the band, not on which tool drew the chart. The only AI-related thing worth flagging is if the chart numbers do not match the pooled table the group reports (the AI sometimes invents a respondent); if the chart and the pooled table do not match, treat the band as asserted rather than read off the data.
>
> **Output format:**
> - **Criterion 1, Band reading: __/35** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 2, The four questions: __/20** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 3, Recommended price: __/15** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 4, Individual artifact: __/15** (why, and note any shared slices) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 5, Writing clarity: __/15** (why) (one tip)
> - **Total: __/100**
> - **One-line summary for the group:** (warm, specific, what to do next time)
>
> The group's submission follows below this line.

## Part 3: Reading and trusting the result

- The AI returns five scores, a total, and short notes. **Skim the notes first.** They tell you whether the AI actually understood the submission. Check that its note on Criterion 1 confirms the pool was at least 15, the curves slope the right way (two rising, two falling), and the four crossings (OPP, PMC, PME, IPP) sit at the right intersections.
- **You can push back.** Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example *"The pooled table shows 18 responses, not 12; look again at Criterion 1"* or *"Three members show the same five responses; should Criterion 4 be lower?"* The AI will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- **The band row is the one to double-check yourself.** It is worth the most. Count the pooled responses (15 is the floor) and glance at the chart: two curves rising, two falling, OPP between the diagonals, the band shaded between PMC and PME, IPP between "getting expensive" and "a bargain."
- **Read the member slices yourself.** The five-response slices should add up to the pooled dataset. If they overlap or do not sum, the slices were shared and the individual-artifact row caps low.
- **You do not need any AI math to grade this.** The crossings are visual on the chart and the pool size is a count. If you ever want to spot-check a cumulative percent yourself: for the "too expensive" curve at price P, count how many respondents said the product is too expensive at or below P, then divide by the pool size and multiply by 100. The "too cheap" curve at price P counts how many said it is too cheap at or above P, divided by the pool size and multiplied by 100. Two rising curves and two falling curves.
- **Keep a fresh chat per group** so submissions do not blur together.
- Remember the framing for any feedback you pass on: *the instructor grades; the AI assists.* The score is yours.

## Part 4: Grade by hand instead (no AI at all)

If you would rather not use AI, grade straight from the rubric. Read the group PDF and the member slices once, then score the five rows.

| Criterion | Points | What earns full marks |
|---|---|---|
| **Band reading off real pooled data** | 35 | 15 or more pooled real responses; four cumulative curves that slope the right way; the four crossings (OPP, PMC, PME, IPP) labeled at the right intersections; the band shaded between PMC and PME |
| **The four Van Westendorp questions** | 20 | All four worded as the indirect framings (too expensive, too cheap, getting expensive, a bargain) and adapted to the chosen product |
| **Recommended price** | 15 | A price set inside the band with a one-sentence reason tied to what one or two named rivals actually charge |
| **Individual artifact (each member)** | 15 | Every member's five real responses and band note present and their own; slices add up to the pooled data |
| **Writing clarity** | 15 | Plain, clear prose with the four crossings and the band used correctly |
| **Total** | **100** | |

**Fast bands:** 90 to 100, four correct questions, 15 or more pooled responses, a band read off four labeled crossings on a chart built from the data, a price inside the band tied to a named rival, distinct member slices and notes, plain writing; 80 to 89, a solid band on 15 or more responses with one thin row (a labeling slip, a partial recommendation reason, or one shared slice); 70 to 79, present but the data is thin or the band is loosely read (fewer than 15 responses, a crossing misplaced, or two members sharing slices); 60 to 69, a band asserted with no curves behind it, the four questions written as a direct price ask, or shared slices throughout; below 60 or integrity flag for a fabricated pooled table, a missing deliverable, or no real survey data (handle any academic-integrity question through your institution's policy, not the rubric).

The single fastest signal of a strong submission on this card: **a pooled table you can count to 15 or more, four cumulative curves that slope the right way, four labeled crossings at the right intersections, and a recommended price inside the band tied to a named rival.** Full details and failure modes are in `ch10-van-westendorp-survey-grading-guide.md`.

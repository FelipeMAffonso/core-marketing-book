# Grading Skill: Read a Brand's Five Decisions with AI

*Chapter 2 individual assignment. This file lets you grade a student's submission with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste a block of instructions into Claude, paste the student's work under it, and read back a score for each part of the rubric with a sentence of feedback. You stay in charge: the AI gives you a first-pass score, you decide the grade. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom in case you would rather not use AI at all.*

---

## Part 1. If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install anything or pay anything. This takes about five minutes the first time and under two minutes per student after that.

1. Open a web browser (Chrome, Safari, Edge, whatever you use) and go to claude.ai. (ChatGPT at chatgpt.com works the same way if you prefer it.)
2. Make a free account. Click Sign up, enter your email, and follow the steps. A free account is enough for this. You will not be charged.
3. Start a new chat. After you log in you will see a box that says something like "How can I help you today?" That box is where you type. A "chat" is just a conversation with the AI, like texting.
4. Copy the grader instructions. In Part 2 below, everything inside the gray box is one block. Select it all, copy it, and paste it into the chat box. Do not press send yet.
5. Add the student's work underneath. Press Enter twice to make space, then paste the student's full submission, including their five-decision map, the lines they verified against the brand's website with the sources they cited, the AI line they corrected with the brand's page as evidence, the decision they named as the one that breaks the rest, the positioning statement, the one-line takeaway, and their AI transcript. Paste all of it in the same message, right below the instructions.
6. Send it. Press Enter (or click the arrow). The AI will read it all and reply with a score for each of the five rubric rows, a total out of 100, and a short note for each row.
7. Read the score and decide. The AI's score is a first draft for you to approve, change, or override. You are the grader of record. If a score looks off, trust your own read; the grading guide (ch02-five-decisions-grading-guide.md) tells you what each row is really worth.
8. Grade the next student. Click New chat and repeat from step 4 for each submission. A fresh chat each time keeps one student's work from bleeding into the next.

That is the whole process. The block in Part 2 does the work; you paste, read, and sign off.

> The ten-second sanity check you can always run yourself, no AI needed. Open the brand's website in a browser tab. Find the price the student wrote in the map and confirm it on the live shop page; find the channel the student wrote and confirm it on the brand's store locator. If the student says they verified two or more lines and the lines hold up on the brand's own pages, the catching row is real.

## Part 2. The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a second-year marketing student's individual assignment. The student picked one real, public brand, used an AI assistant to map the brand's five marketing decisions from public information, and was then asked to verify the AI's map against the brand's own website, product or shop page, and current ads, catching at least one line the AI got wrong and naming the decision that would sink the other four. Your job is to score the submission against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback. This is a first-year-friendly course; reward verification against the brand's real material over polished writing.
>
> Background you need (the chapter's framework):
> - Marketing is five decisions that all have to agree: one, what to make; two, who to serve; three, what to charge; four, where to sell it; five, how to talk about it.
> - The decisions multiply, they do not add. One weak decision sinks the other four. So naming the single decision that would break the rest is the key insight, not grading the brand "four out of five."
> - A good positioning statement names a real buyer and one benefit in this shape: for a buyer who wants something, the brand is the kind of product that delivers one benefit. "For everyone" or "high quality" is filler, not a real choice.
> - The assignment's heart is the verification pass against the brand's real material. The student should open the brand's website, its product or shop page, and a current ad, check at least three of the AI's five lines against what the brand itself shows, and catch at least one line the AI guessed or invented (a wrong price, a guessed channel or retailer, a feature the brand does not claim). A current AI on a brand question states guesses as confidently as facts, so catching one against the brand's own page is the skill being tested.
> - The student should briefly quote or paraphrase what the brand's real page says where the AI got it wrong, so the correction is anchored to a real source rather than another guess.
>
> How to score. Give a number for each of the five criteria below, then add them for a total out of 100. For each, write one sentence saying why, and one sentence the student could act on. Be specific and encouraging. If something required is missing, say so plainly and score it low; do not invent credit.
>
> 1. Catching what the AI got wrong (30 points). Did the student verify three or more of the AI's lines against the brand's own website, product page, or current ad, with the source named for each, AND catch and correct at least one line the AI guessed or invented, with the brand's real page quoted briefly as evidence? Full marks: clear verification against the brand's pages plus a named, corrected AI error anchored to the real source. Low marks: took the AI output at face value with no checking against the brand's own material, or used AI only to generate and never to judge.
> 2. The five-decision map (22 points). Are all five decisions named and filled with specific, verified detail (a real price from the shop page, a real channel from the store locator, a real buyer the brand itself describes), not generic filler? Deduct for merged, missing, or vague slots ("good product, fair price").
> 3. The decision that breaks the rest (20 points). Did the student name the single decision that, if it changed, would break the other four (or two decisions that already contradict each other), and explain the knock-on effect in the multiply-not-add logic? Low marks if they note a weak slot without explaining what it drags down, or list "improvements" instead of finding the conflict.
> 4. Positioning and exchange (16 points). Does the one-line positioning name a real buyer and one benefit (not "everyone," not "high quality")? Does the student show why the trade is worth it to that buyer in the chapter's exchange logic? Deduct for filler any rival could copy.
> 5. Takeaway and writing (12 points). Is there one clear sentence in the student's own voice on what holds the brand together or what will break it, in plain language with correct use of the chapter's terms?
>
> Watch for these failure patterns and score them down (with a kind note): taking AI output at face value with no checking against the brand's own pages; verifying a price by typing the AI's rounded number again rather than the live shop price; picking a giant brand and writing a vague history instead of a five-slot read; handing in a list of campaign tactics instead of finding the decision that breaks the rest (the exact mistake the chapter warns about); a buyer defined as "everyone."
>
> Defer any concern about disclosure or undisclosed AI use to the instructor under the institution's academic-integrity and AI-use policy, not the rubric.
>
> Output format:
> - Criterion 1, Catching what the AI got wrong: __ / 30 (one sentence on why) (one tip)
> - Criterion 2, Five-decision map: __ / 22 (one sentence on why) (one tip)
> - Criterion 3, Decision that breaks the rest: __ / 20 (one sentence on why) (one tip)
> - Criterion 4, Positioning and exchange: __ / 16 (one sentence on why) (one tip)
> - Criterion 5, Takeaway and writing: __ / 12 (one sentence on why) (one tip)
> - Total: __ / 100
> - One-line summary for the student: warm, specific, what to do next time
>
> The student's submission follows below this line.

## Part 3. Reading and trusting the result

- The AI returns five scores, a total, and short notes. Skim the notes first; they tell you whether the AI actually understood the submission. If the notes match what you saw when you read the work, the total is trustworthy.
- You can push back. Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example "the student did catch a wrong price in their transcript and quoted the brand's shop page, look again at Criterion 1" or "be stricter on the map row, three slots are generic." The AI will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- The catching row is the one to double-check yourself. It is worth the most and it is where the AI's own read can be too generous. Open the brand's website in a tab while reading the submission; if the student says the price is one number and the live shop page says another, the verification did not actually verify.
- Keep a fresh chat per student so submissions do not blur together.
- Remember the framing for any feedback you pass on: the instructor grades, the AI assists. The score is yours.

## Part 4. Grade by hand instead (no AI at all)

If you would rather not use AI, grade straight from the rubric. Read each submission once, then score the five rows.

| Criterion | Points | What earns full marks |
|---|---|---|
| **Catching what the AI got wrong** | 30 | Verifies three or more AI lines against the brand's own website, product page, or current ad with the source named; catches and corrects at least one line the AI guessed or invented, with the brand's real page quoted briefly as evidence |
| **The five-decision map** | 22 | All five decisions named and filled with specific, verified detail, not generic filler |
| **The decision that breaks the rest** | 20 | Names the one decision that would sink the other four (or two that contradict) and explains the knock-on in the multiply-not-add logic |
| **Positioning and exchange** | 16 | One-line positioning names a real buyer and one benefit; shows why the trade is worth it to that buyer |
| **Takeaway and writing** | 12 | One clear sentence on what holds the brand together or breaks it; plain, correct terms |
| **Total** | **100** | |

**Fast bands:** 90 to 100, specific map plus a verification pass against the brand's own pages with at least one corrected AI error anchored to a quoted source, and the breaking decision named with its knock-on; 80 to 89, solid work with one thin row; 70 to 79, present but mostly trusted the AI (tidy map, little checking against the brand's real material, weak conflict, or an "everyone" buyer); 60 to 69, face-value AI with no catch, or a book report; below 60 for a missing deliverable or a brand with no real public information to verify against (handle disclosure questions through your institution's academic-integrity and AI-use policy, not the rubric).

The single fastest signal of a strong paper: a named, corrected AI error with the brand's real page quoted briefly as evidence. If the student caught the model in a wrong price or a guessed channel and fixed it against the live shop page or store locator, the assignment worked.

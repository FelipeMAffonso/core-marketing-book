# Grading Skill: Critique an AI Pricing Answer

*Chapter 10 individual assignment, no AI account needed by students. This file lets you grade a submission with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste the block below into Claude or ChatGPT, paste the student's write-up under it, and read back a score for each rubric row with a sentence of feedback. The block already knows the errors planted in the memo, so it can tell whether the student caught them. You stay the grader of record; the AI gives a first pass. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom.*

---

## Part 1: If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install or pay for anything. This takes about five minutes the first time, and under two minutes per student after that.

1. Open a web browser and go to **claude.ai** (or **chatgpt.com**, which works the same way).
2. Make a free account: click Sign up, enter your email, follow the steps. A free account is enough.
3. Start a new chat. The box that asks how it can help is where you type.
4. Copy the grader instructions in Part 2 below (everything in the gray box) and paste them into the chat. Do not send yet.
5. Press Enter twice for space, then paste the student's full write-up underneath, in the same message.
6. Send it. The AI replies with a score for each of the three rubric rows, a total out of 100, and a short note per row.
7. Read the score and decide. It is a first draft for you to approve or override; you are the grader of record.
8. Click New chat for the next student, so submissions do not blur together.

The ten-second check you can always run yourself, no AI needed: did the student write **$66.67** as the corrected price (with a markup-versus-margin explanation), and did they mark the **Cuyana $78** competitor price as unverified? Both found is an Excellent judgment pass; one is Proficient; neither is Developing or Not yet.

## Part 2: The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a second-year marketing student's critique of an AI-written pricing memo. The student was handed the memo (they used no AI of their own) and asked to find its errors and reset the price. Score the student's write-up against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback. Reward catching the memo's errors over polished writing.
>
> The memo the student critiqued priced a leather laptop sleeve at a $40 unit cost. It contains two planted errors and one shaky claim:
>
> 1. A markup labeled a margin. The memo gave $56 for what it called a "40% margin" on the $40 cost. That is a 40% markup, not a margin. A 40% markup on $40 is $40 times 1.40 = $56, and the margin on a $56 price is only ($56 minus $40) divided by $56 = 28.6%. A true 40% margin needs $40 divided by (1 minus 0.40) = $66.67. A student who caught this writes the corrected price $66.67 and explains the markup-versus-margin difference.
> 2. An invented competitor price. The memo cited "Cuyana at $78" as a verified fact without checking it. A student who caught this marks each competitor price confirmed or invented and drops the unverified Cuyana figure.
> 3. A shaky fairness claim (bonus, not required for full marks). The memo called the price fair because it undercuts competitors. Real fairness here is the dual-entitlement principle (a cost-based price reads as fair, a demand windfall reads as gouging), and the verdict rested on the broken $56 price.
>
> Score these three rows and add them for a total out of 100. For each, give a number, one sentence on why, and one sentence the student could act on.
>
> 1. Judgment pass, caught what the AI got wrong (55 points). Full marks: catches the markup-as-margin error and corrects to $66.67, marks the competitor prices confirmed or invented and drops the Cuyana figure, and notices the fairness verdict rests on the broken price. Proficient (about 38): catches one of the two errors and corrects it. Developing (about 30): calls the memo "mostly good" without catching either. Not yet (about 15): keeps the $56 and the 40%-margin label.
> 2. Arithmetic check (25 points). Full marks: shows the true 40% margin price ($66.67) and the real margin the $56 earns (28.6%), with markup and margin as distinct numbers. Low if the student repeats the memo's "markup = margin = 40%."
> 3. Final price and reasoning (20 points). Full marks: a clear corrected price with a plain reason, a two-sentence note on the evidence that would confirm the price sits in a range buyers accept, and a fairness read that uses dual entitlement rather than "cheaper than rivals."
>
> Output format:
> - Judgment pass: __ /55 (why) (one tip)
> - Arithmetic check: __ /25 (why) (one tip)
> - Final price and reasoning: __ /20 (why) (one tip)
> - Total: __ /100
> - One-line summary for the student
>
> The student's write-up follows below this line.

## Part 3: Reading and trusting the result

- Skim the notes first; they tell you whether the AI understood the submission. The note on the judgment row is the heart of the grade, so check that it names whether the student caught the markup-as-margin error and the invented price.
- You can push back. Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example "the student did mark the Cuyana price unverified, look again at the judgment row," and it will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- The judgment row is easy to double-check yourself, because the answer never changes: look for $66.67 with a markup-versus-margin correction, and for the Cuyana price marked unverified.
- Keep a fresh chat per student. The grade is yours; the AI only assists.

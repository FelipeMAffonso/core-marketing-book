# Grading Skill: Read the Value Stick on a Product You Know

*Chapter 10 individual assignment, the gentle on-ramp card. This file lets you grade a student's submission with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste a block of instructions into Claude, paste the student's work under it, and read back a score for each part of the rubric with a sentence of feedback. You stay in charge: the AI gives you a first-pass score, you decide the grade. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom in case you would rather not use AI at all.*

---

## Part 1: If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install anything or pay anything. This takes about five minutes the first time and under two minutes per student after that.

1. **Open a web browser** (Chrome, Safari, Edge, whatever you use) and go to **claude.ai**. (ChatGPT at **chatgpt.com** works the same way if you prefer it.)
2. **Make a free account.** Click **Sign up**, enter your email, and follow the steps. A free account is enough for this. You will not be charged.
3. **Start a new chat.** After you log in you will see a box that says something like *"How can I help you today?"* That box is where you type. A "chat" is just a conversation with the AI, like texting.
4. **Copy the grader instructions.** In Part 2 below, everything inside the gray box is one block. Select it all, copy it, and paste it into the chat box. Do not press send yet.
5. **Add the student's work underneath.** Press Enter twice to make space, then paste the student's full submission, including their value stick (the floor, frame, and ceiling with sources), the real price they placed on it, the approach they named, and their AI price-check table, right below the instructions, in the same message.
6. **Send it.** Press Enter (or click the arrow). The AI will read both and reply with a score for each of the four rubric rows, a total out of 100, and a short note for each row.
7. **Read the score and decide.** The AI's score is a **first draft for you to approve, change, or override.** You are the grader of record. If a score looks off, trust your own read. The grading guide (`ch10-value-stick-memo-grading-guide.md`) tells you what each row is really worth.
8. **Grade the next student.** Click **New chat** and repeat from step 4 for each submission. A fresh chat each time keeps one student's work from bleeding into the next.

That is the whole process. The block in Part 2 does the work; you paste, read, and sign off.

> **One quick check you can always run yourself, no AI needed.** The heart of this card is the price check. Open the student's table and click one link they say confirms a competitor price. If the real page shows that price, the confirmation is real. If a price they marked "made up" is nowhere on the real page, they did the work. A table with no clickable links, or links that do not show the prices, is not a real price check no matter how many rows it has.

## Part 2: The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a second-year marketing student's individual assignment. This is the gentle, first-week-friendly version of the pricing assignment, so reward the verification habit and clear reasoning over polish. The student picked one real, familiar product, built its value stick, named the pricing approach, then used a free AI account for one small step: asking it for competitor prices and a rough unit cost, and checking every number against a real store page before keeping it. Your job is to score their submission against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback.
>
> **Background you need (the chapter's framework):**
> - A price sits on a **value stick** with three parts. The **floor** is a rough unit cost (a public guess is fine on this card). The **frame** is what one or two named rivals charge right now. The **ceiling** is the most the student personally would pay before walking away. A good answer names all three with a number and a source, and places the product's real shelf price between them.
> - The four **pricing approaches** are cost-based, competitor-based, value-based, and customer-determined. A good answer names the one the seller looks like it is using and gives a one-sentence reason tied to the stick.
> - **The AI invents competitor prices.** It states a price it has made up in the same confident voice it uses for a real one. This is the single failure the card trains. A strong answer checks every AI-quoted price against a real store or product page, marks each one confirmed (with the link checked) or made up, and names at least one price the AI invented that did not hold up.
> - The heart of this assignment is the **price check**: treating every price the AI quotes as a claim to verify, not a fact to keep. A student who kept the AI's numbers without checking has missed the point, even if the value stick is tidy.
>
> **How to score.** Give a number for each of the four criteria below, then add them for a total out of 100. For each, write one sentence saying why, and one sentence the student could act on. Be specific and encouraging. If something required is missing, say so plainly and score it low; do not invent credit.
>
> 1. **AI price-check: caught what the AI made up (45 points).** Did the student check every AI-quoted competitor price against a real page, mark each one confirmed (with a link) or made up, and name at least one price the AI invented? Full marks: every price checked, marked, and sourced, with at least one named, verified miss. Low marks: quoted the AI prices with little or no checking, or handed them in as verified fact.
> 2. **Value stick built (30 points).** Are the floor (rough unit cost), the frame (one or two named rivals' real prices), and the ceiling (the most the student would pay) all named with a number and a source, and is the real price placed correctly between them? Deduct for a part that is thin or asserted, and deduct more if floor and ceiling are confused (for example, a ceiling set below the competitor frame).
> 3. **Approach named (15 points).** Did the student name the pricing approach that fits and give a real one-sentence reason tied to the stick? Deduct for an approach named by reflex with no reason.
> 4. **Writing (10 points).** Is the prose plain and clear, with the pricing terms (floor, frame, ceiling, the four approaches) used correctly? Deduct for restating the parts without connecting them.
>
> **Watch for these failure patterns and score them down (with a kind note):** keeping every AI-quoted price without checking; a price-check table with no clickable links or links that do not show the prices; an invented price kept as fact; floor and ceiling confused; an approach named with no reason.
>
> **Do not penalize** disclosed AI use; the AI step is required here. Only the *absence* of a real price check is penalized, not the use of AI. If there is no price-check table and no sign the AI step happened, yet the writing looks like an unedited AI dump, flag it for the instructor as a possible disclosure issue rather than scoring it. That is a human decision.
>
> **Output format:**
> - **Criterion 1, AI price-check: __/45** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 2, Value stick built: __/30** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 3, Approach named: __/15** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 4, Writing: __/10** (why) (one tip)
> - **Total: __/100**
> - **One-line summary for the student:** (warm, specific, what to do next time)
>
> The student's submission follows below this line.

## Part 3: Reading and trusting the result

- The AI returns four scores, a total, and short notes. **Skim the notes first.** They tell you whether the AI actually understood the submission. If the notes match what you saw when you read the work, the total is trustworthy.
- **You can push back.** Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example *"The student did catch a made-up competitor price; look again at Criterion 1"* or *"The ceiling is below the frame here; should the value-stick row be lower?"* The AI will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- **The price-check row is the one to double-check yourself.** It is worth the most, and it is where the AI's own read can be too generous. Click one link in the student's table. If the page matches a confirmed price, or a price they marked made up is nowhere on the real page, the row earns its marks. The grading guide says so too.
- **The fastest verification needs no AI.** Click one of the student's links. A real, clickable product page that shows the price is the whole bar for this card. A table of prices with no sources is not a price check.
- **Keep a fresh chat per student** so submissions do not blur together.
- Remember the framing for any feedback you pass on: *the instructor grades; the AI assists.* The score is yours.

## Part 4: Grade by hand instead (no AI at all)

If you would rather not use AI, grade straight from the rubric. Read each submission once, then score the four rows.

| Criterion | Points | What earns full marks |
|---|---|---|
| **AI price-check: caught what the AI made up** | 45 | Checks every AI-quoted competitor price against a real page, marks each confirmed (with the link) or made up, names at least one price the AI invented |
| **Value stick built** | 30 | Floor, frame (1 to 2 real rivals), and ceiling all named with a number and a source; the real price placed correctly between them |
| **Approach named** | 15 | Names the approach that fits and gives a real one-sentence reason tied to the stick |
| **Writing** | 10 | Plain, clear, correct pricing terms |
| **Total** | **100** | |

**Fast bands:** 90 to 100, a complete sourced stick, the fitting approach with a real reason, and a full price check with at least one named, verified miss; 80 to 89, solid work with one thin row; 70 to 79, present but mostly trusted the AI (tidy stick, little checking, no real catch); 60 to 69, face-value AI prices kept as fact, or floor and ceiling confused; below 60 or integrity flag for a missing deliverable or an undisclosed AI dump (handle disclosure through your integrity process, not the rubric).

The single fastest signal of a strong paper on this card: **a named, verified miss.** If the student caught the AI quoting a competitor price that the real page does not show, and marked it made up, the assignment worked. The ten-second check that settles it: click one link in their table and see whether the real page matches. Full details and failure modes are in `ch10-value-stick-memo-grading-guide.md`.

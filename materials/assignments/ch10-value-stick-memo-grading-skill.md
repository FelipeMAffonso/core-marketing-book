# Grading Skill: Three Small Businesses, Three Pricing Calls

*Chapter 10 individual assignment, the gentle on-ramp to pricing. This file lets you grade a student's submission with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste the block below into Claude, paste the student's memo under it, and read back a score for each part of the rubric with a sentence of feedback. The block already knows the three scenarios the student was given and the strong-fit strategies for each one, so it can tell whether the student picked the right strategies, tied each pick to the scenario's specific numbers, and closed with feasible recommendations. You stay in charge: the AI gives you a first-pass score, you decide the grade. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom in case you would rather not use AI at all.*

---

## Part 1: If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install anything or pay anything. This takes about five minutes the first time and under three minutes per student after that.

1. **Open a web browser** (Chrome, Safari, Edge, whatever you use) and go to **claude.ai**. (ChatGPT at **chatgpt.com** works the same way if you prefer it.)
2. **Make a free account.** Click **Sign up**, enter your email, and follow the steps. A free account is enough for this. You will not be charged.
3. **Start a new chat.** After you log in you will see a box that says something like *"How can I help you today?"* That box is where you type. A chat is just a conversation with the AI, like texting.
4. **Copy the grader instructions.** In Part 2 below, everything inside the gray box is one block. Select it all, copy it, and paste it into the chat box. Do not press send yet.
5. **Add the student's work underneath.** Press Enter twice to make space, then paste the student's full memo, including all three scenario sections (Halloran Films, Ember and Oak, Stillwater Smash), the named strategies they picked, the justifications, the recommendations, and the closing reflection, right below the instructions, in the same message.
6. **Send it.** Press Enter (or click the arrow). The AI will read both and reply with a score for each of the five rubric rows, a total out of one hundred, and a short note for each row.
7. **Read the score and decide.** The AI's score is a first draft for you to approve, change, or override. You are the grader of record. If a score looks off, trust your own read. The grading guide (`ch10-value-stick-memo-grading-guide.md`) holds the full answer key and tells you what each row is really worth.
8. **Grade the next student.** Click **New chat** and repeat from step 4 for each submission. A fresh chat each time keeps one student's work from bleeding into the next.

That is the whole process. The block in Part 2 does the work; you paste, read, and sign off.

> **The thirty-second check you can always run yourself, no AI needed.** The three scenarios are the same for every student, so the chapter labels that fit each one never change. Did the student use the chapter's exact labels from the chosen-from list (cost-based, value-based, competitor-based, dynamic, odd-even, reference, decoy, extremity aversion, prestige, bundle, multiple-unit, loss leader, skimming, penetration), and did they pick at least four different strategies across the three scenarios? Is each justification tied to a specific number or a specific competitor from the scenario in front of them (Tulsa Reel Co. at three thousand two hundred dollars, the Atelier cost of forty-two dollars, the campus dining hall plate at eleven dollars and ninety-nine cents)? Did they close with two real insights drawn across the three scenarios? A memo that hits those three checks is a strong paper.

## Part 2: The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a second-year marketing student's individual assignment. This is the gentle, first-week-friendly version of the pricing assignment, so reward defensible strategy picks and clear scenario-specific justifications over polished writing. Every student was handed the SAME three small-business scenarios and asked to pick two pricing strategies from a chosen-from list for each one, justify each pick in two to three sentences tied to the scenario's specific numbers, close each section with one further recommendation, and end with a two-insight reflection across the three scenarios. Your job is to score their memo against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback.
>
> **The three scenarios every student was given (you need this to grade them).**
>
> **Scenario 1: Halloran Films.** Maya Halloran runs a one-person wedding-videography studio in Tulsa, Oklahoma. Her single package is a flat two thousand four hundred dollars for an eight-hour shoot day. Her target buyers are couples planning weddings in the twenty-two thousand to thirty-five thousand dollar total-budget band. Tulsa Reel Co. charges about three thousand two hundred dollars and is booked further out. Heartland Wedding Films, a newer one-person shop, undercuts at one thousand six hundred fifty dollars. She wants to know what to do for the 2026 season.
>
> **Scenario 2: Ember and Oak.** Daniel Ortiz runs a small soy-candle company. His Home line is twenty-four dollars per candle and his Signature line is fifty-eight dollars per candle. He is launching a new Atelier line at one of three candidate prices, one hundred forty dollars, one hundred eighty-five dollars, or two hundred twenty dollars, against a variable cost of forty-two dollars per candle. Mass-market rivals like Yankee Candle at about eighteen dollars and Mrs. Meyer's at about fourteen dollars are pressing the Home line. He needs to price the Atelier launch and figure out how to keep the Home line from getting buried.
>
> **Scenario 3: Stillwater Smash.** Lena Park runs a food truck on the OSU campus in Stillwater, Oklahoma. Her menu is a smash burger and fries at nine dollars, a double smash and fries at eleven dollars and fifty cents, and a canned drink at two dollars. Her average ticket is about eleven dollars and seventy-five cents. She is launching a crispy chicken sandwich with fries (variable cost three dollars and eighty cents) and needs an opening price. Her rivals are the campus dining hall at eleven dollars and ninety-nine cents, Chick-fil-A at ten dollars and seventy-nine cents, and Hideaway Pizza at thirteen dollars and fifty cents. She has a hunch that lunches under ten dollars move faster than lunches over twelve dollars.
>
> **The chosen-from list of strategies the student picked from:** cost-based, value-based, competitor-based, dynamic, odd-even, reference, decoy, extremity aversion, prestige, bundle, multiple-unit, loss leader, skimming, penetration. Each scenario requires two picks, and the student must use at least four different strategies across the three scenarios.
>
> **The strong-fit strategies (the answer key, not the only right answers):**
>
> - **Scenario 1 (Halloran):** competitor-based pricing (raise to about two thousand eight hundred or two thousand nine hundred dollars, between Tulsa Reel Co. and Heartland), value-based pricing (read the photographer-referral funnel and the twenty-two-thousand-to-thirty-five-thousand-dollar buyer band), and extremity aversion if the student proposes three real tiers. A strong recommendation: raise to about two thousand nine hundred dollars, hold premium dates, decline to chase Heartland on price.
> - **Scenario 2 (Ember and Oak):** prestige pricing for the Atelier launch at one hundred eighty-five or two hundred twenty dollars (the price-as-quality signal), the decoy effect or extremity aversion to read the three-tier lineup (Home at twenty-four, Signature at fifty-eight, Atelier at one hundred eighty-five) as good-better-best, and reference pricing as a tactic for the Home line getting buried (a per-ounce comparison against Yankee Candle). Skimming is also defensible for the Atelier launch. A strong recommendation: launch Atelier at one hundred eighty-five dollars, reposition Home with a per-ounce reference or a two-candle bundle pulling Home buyers toward Signature.
> - **Scenario 3 (Stillwater):** odd-even pricing (the chicken sandwich at nine dollars and ninety-nine cents, holding the under-ten-dollar anchor the line responds to), bundle pricing (sandwich plus drink for about ten dollars and ninety-nine cents), competitor-based pricing (between Chick-fil-A at ten seventy-nine and the campus dining hall at eleven ninety-nine). Loss leader is defensible if the student names what it pulls into. A strong recommendation: launch at nine dollars and ninety-nine cents with an optional drink bundle, hold the under-ten anchor.
>
> **The common slips that should cost points:**
>
> - **Skimming and penetration are launch moves**, not re-pricing moves. A student who calls Halloran's situation skimming or penetration has misread (she is repricing an existing package). A student who calls the Stillwater chicken sandwich a skimming play has also misread (a campus food truck launching a chicken sandwich is not a skim-then-lower play). Skimming fits the Atelier launch in Scenario 2 if argued correctly.
> - **Prestige pricing on a food truck.** A fourteen-dollar chicken sandwich on a campus truck does not signal status, especially when the case data says lunches over twelve dollars move slower. Score Scenario 3 down if prestige pricing is the pick.
> - **Loss leader on a website with no foot traffic.** Loss-leader is a store-funnel move; Ember and Oak's own website does not have the foot-traffic logic to make it work for the Home line. Score Scenario 2 down if loss leader is the headline strategy.
> - **The recycled strategy.** A student who picks cost-based pricing for all three scenarios, or recycles the same two strategies across all three, fails the at-least-four-different-strategies criterion.
> - **The label by reflex.** Calling penetration "low pricing" or calling prestige "high pricing" is a paraphrase, not the chapter's vocabulary. Dock the vocabulary row.
> - **The generic justification.** A justification that could be lifted onto any scenario, with no tie to the actual numbers (the two thousand four hundred dollar Halloran package, the forty-two dollar Atelier cost, the eleven dollar ninety-nine cent campus plate) or the named competitors. Dock the strategy-fit row.
>
> **How to score.** Give a number for each of the five criteria below, then add them for a total out of one hundred. For each, write one sentence saying why, and one sentence the student could act on. Be specific and encouraging. If something required is missing, say so plainly and score it low; do not invent credit.
>
> 1. **Strategy fit and justification across the three scenarios (50 points), scored per-scenario at roughly seventeen, seventeen, and sixteen points.** For each of the three scenarios, are two strategies from the chosen-from list named, each justified in two to three sentences tied to the scenario's specific numbers and named competitors, with one further recommendation that follows from the two strategies? Deduct per-scenario for generic justifications, for recycled language across scenarios, for misread strategies (skimming as a re-pricing move, prestige on a food truck), and for thin recommendations.
> 2. **Use of the chapter's named vocabulary (15 points).** Did the student name each strategy by the chapter's exact label from the chosen-from list (cost-based, value-based, competitor-based, dynamic, odd-even, reference, decoy, extremity aversion, prestige, bundle, multiple-unit, loss leader, skimming, penetration), used correctly, with at least four different strategies across the three scenarios? Deduct for paraphrased labels ("low pricing" for penetration, "high pricing" for prestige) and for reuse of the same one or two strategies across all three scenarios.
> 3. **Feasibility of the recommendation (15 points).** Is each of the three recommendations a move the owner could plausibly run next week, tied to the scenario's numbers and buyers, with no internal contradictions (a prestige call paired with a deep discount, a penetration call paired with a two-hundred-dollar price)? Deduct for vague recommendations (raise prices, lower prices) and for contradictions inside one scenario.
> 4. **Writing clarity (10 points).** Do the three sections read as a memo to a small-business owner, in connected sentences, with the chapter's vocabulary used in plain prose and no fragments or staccatos? Deduct for hard-to-follow reasoning.
> 5. **Reflection close (10 points).** Does the closing reflection list two real insights drawn across the three scenarios (not a paraphrase of one scenario), each a sentence the student could carry into a future pricing question? Deduct for a single thin insight, a reflection that restates the prompt, or one-insight-per-scenario rather than two cross-scenario insights.
>
> **Watch for these failure patterns and score them down with a kind note:** the recycled strategy across all three scenarios; the label by reflex (low pricing, high pricing); the generic justification with no tie to the scenario's specific numbers; the contradictory recommendation; a missing or paraphrased reflection.
>
> **On academic integrity:** the instructor follows their own institution's academic-integrity and AI-use policy. If the memo reads as an unedited AI dump that flattens the differences between the three scenarios, with no scenario-specific numbers and a generic structure across all three, do not try to rule on it; flag it for the instructor as something to review under that policy, and score only what is on the page.
>
> **Output format:**
> - **Criterion 1, Strategy fit (Scenario 1, Halloran): __/17** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 1, Strategy fit (Scenario 2, Ember and Oak): __/17** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 1, Strategy fit (Scenario 3, Stillwater): __/16** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 2, Use of the chapter's named vocabulary: __/15** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 3, Feasibility of the recommendation: __/15** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 4, Writing clarity: __/10** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 5, Reflection close: __/10** (why) (one tip)
> - **Total: __/100**
> - **One-line summary for the student:** (warm, specific, what to do next time)
>
> The student's memo follows below this line.

## Part 3: Reading and trusting the result

- The AI returns scores for each rubric row (with the strategy-fit row broken into three per-scenario scores), a total, and short notes. **Skim the notes first.** They tell you whether the AI actually understood the memo. Check the per-scenario notes on Criterion 1, because that is where most of the points sit and where a careless read shows up first.
- **You can push back.** Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example *"The student named competitor-based pricing for Halloran and tied it to Tulsa Reel Co.'s three thousand two hundred dollars; look again at Scenario 1"* or *"The reflection here only restates one scenario; should Criterion 5 be lower?"* The AI will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- **The vocabulary row is the one to double-check yourself, and it is easy.** Read the three sections of the memo and tally the distinct chapter labels the student used (cost-based, value-based, decoy, prestige, bundle, multiple-unit, loss leader, skimming, penetration, and the rest). Four or more distinct strategies across the three scenarios is the bar for full marks. Three or fewer drops the row.
- **The fastest sanity check on the strategy-fit row needs no AI.** For each scenario, look at the two strategies the student named and ask whether the justification names at least one number or one competitor from that scenario specifically. A justification for Halloran that does not mention Tulsa Reel Co. or Heartland by name is generic; a justification for Stillwater that does not mention the campus dining hall, Chick-fil-A, or the under-ten-dollar demand signal is generic. The grading guide spells this out.
- **Keep a fresh chat per student** so submissions do not blur together.
- Remember the framing for any feedback you pass on: *the instructor grades; the AI assists.* The score is yours.

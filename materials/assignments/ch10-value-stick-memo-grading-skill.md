# Grading Skill: Build the Value Stick for a New Bottle

*Chapter 10 individual assignment, the gentle on-ramp to pricing. This file lets you grade a student's submission with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste the block below into Claude, paste the student's memo under it, and read back a score for each part of the rubric with a sentence of feedback. The block already knows the brief the student was given and the corrected numbers, so it can tell whether the student built the stick and got the margin right. You stay in charge: the AI gives you a first-pass score, you decide the grade. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom in case you would rather not use AI at all.*

---

## Part 1: If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install anything or pay anything. This takes about five minutes the first time and under two minutes per student after that.

1. **Open a web browser** (Chrome, Safari, Edge, whatever you use) and go to **claude.ai**. (ChatGPT at **chatgpt.com** works the same way if you prefer it.)
2. **Make a free account.** Click **Sign up**, enter your email, and follow the steps. A free account is enough for this. You will not be charged.
3. **Start a new chat.** After you log in you will see a box that says something like *"How can I help you today?"* That box is where you type. A "chat" is just a conversation with the AI, like texting.
4. **Copy the grader instructions.** In Part 2 below, everything inside the gray box is one block. Select it all, copy it, and paste it into the chat box. Do not press send yet.
5. **Add the student's work underneath.** Press Enter twice to make space, then paste the student's full memo, including the value stick (the floor, the competitor frame, and the ceiling), the margin arithmetic, the opening price they chose, and the approach they named, right below the instructions, in the same message.
6. **Send it.** Press Enter (or click the arrow). The AI will read both and reply with a score for each of the four rubric rows, a total out of 100, and a short note for each row.
7. **Read the score and decide.** The AI's score is a **first draft for you to approve, change, or override.** You are the grader of record. If a score looks off, trust your own read. The grading guide (`ch10-value-stick-memo-grading-guide.md`) holds the full answer key and tells you what each row is really worth.
8. **Grade the next student.** Click **New chat** and repeat from step 4 for each submission. A fresh chat each time keeps one student's work from bleeding into the next.

That is the whole process. The block in Part 2 does the work; you paste, read, and sign off.

> **The ten-second check you can always run yourself, no AI needed.** The brief is the same for every student, so the heaviest figures never change. Did the student write the price that earns a true 45 percent margin, which is **$32.73**, with the arithmetic shown? And did they avoid the trap of adding 45 percent to the eighteen-dollar cost and landing on $26.10, which is a markup, not a margin? A correct $32.73 margin price and a value stick that places one defended opening price between the floor and the ceiling is the heart of a strong paper.

## Part 2: The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a second-year marketing student's individual assignment. This is the gentle, first-week-friendly version of the pricing assignment, so reward a defensible value stick and a clear recommendation over polished writing. Every student was handed the SAME launch brief and asked to step into the brand manager's chair, build a value stick, set an opening price they can defend, and name the pricing approach it reflects. Your job is to score their memo against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback.
>
> **The brief every student was given (you need this to grade them).** The product is the Wend bottle, a 24-ounce stainless-steel insulated water bottle, the first product of a small direct-to-consumer brand. The variable cost is eighteen dollars per bottle, all in. The founder wants a 45 percent margin. A teammate gathered three competitor prices without saving the links: Hydro Flask at about $44.95, Owala FreeSip at about $31.99, and an unnamed direct-to-consumer brand at about $19.00. Early fans who already own a premium bottle and want a second say they start to hesitate around $35 to $40.
>
> **The corrected numbers (the answer key):**
> - **The margin price.** The founder asked for a 45 percent MARGIN, which is measured against price. The price that earns a true 45 percent margin is the cost divided by one minus the margin, that is eighteen dollars divided by (one minus 0.45), which is **$32.73**. The contribution margin at that price is thirty-two dollars and seventy-three cents minus eighteen dollars, which is **$14.73** per bottle.
> - **The planted trap.** A student may add 45 percent to the cost: eighteen dollars times 1.45 is $26.10. That is a 45 percent MARKUP, not a margin. The margin it actually earns is twenty-six dollars and ten cents minus eighteen dollars, divided by twenty-six dollars and ten cents, which is about **31 percent**, well short of the 45 percent the founder asked for. A student who lands on $26.10 and calls it a 45 percent margin made the exact mistake the chapter warns about; mark the margin row down and explain the difference.
> - **The floor** is the eighteen-dollar variable cost; a price below it loses money on every bottle.
> - **The competitor frame** runs from roughly nineteen dollars to about forty-five dollars. The teammate sent the prices without sources on purpose, so each is a claim to confirm. The named brands (Hydro Flask, Owala) are checkable on their real product pages; the unnamed brand at $19.00 cannot be verified and should be marked unconfirmed and not used as an anchor.
> - **The ceiling** sits in the thirty-five-to-forty-dollar willingness-to-pay range. There is no single right number; reward a ceiling read from that signal, defended in the student's own words, and kept above the frame and the floor.
> - **A reasonable opening price** is anywhere from about thirty-three to forty dollars: at or just above the $32.73 margin price, inside the willingness-to-pay band, and below the Hydro Flask high end. Credit the placement and the reason, not a specific number.
>
> **How to score.** Give a number for each of the four criteria below, then add them for a total out of 100. For each, write one sentence saying why, and one sentence the student could act on. Be specific and encouraging. If something required is missing, say so plainly and score it low; do not invent credit.
>
> 1. **Value stick built and the price defended (40 points).** Are the floor (the eighteen-dollar cost), the competitor frame (the band from the three prices), and the ceiling (read from the thirty-five-to-forty-dollar signal) all set with a number and a reason, is the ceiling defended in the student's own words, and is one opening price placed between the floor and the ceiling with a clear reason for where it sits? Deduct for a band that is thin or asserted or a shallow ceiling defense, and deduct more if the floor and ceiling are confused (the cost treated as the ceiling, or the ceiling set below the frame).
> 2. **Margin arithmetic (25 points).** Did the student set the true 45 percent margin price, which is $32.73 (eighteen dollars divided by (one minus 0.45)), with the arithmetic shown, and name the contribution margin of $14.73? Full marks for $32.73 with the work and the contribution named. Mark it down hard if the student added 45 percent to the cost, landed on $26.10, and called it a 45 percent margin.
> 3. **Competitor frame confirmed (20 points).** Did the student describe the band from the three prices and confirm at least one against a real product page with the link, marking each confirmed or unconfirmed and naming the unnamed $19.00 brand as the one not to anchor on until checked? Deduct for stating the prices as settled facts with no checking.
> 4. **Approach named and memo written (15 points).** Did the student name the pricing approach the price reflects (cost-based, competitor-based, value-based, or customer-determined) with a one-sentence reason tied to the stick, in a memo that reads as a recommendation to the founder rather than a bare list of numbers? Deduct for an approach named by reflex with no reason, or numbers with no recommendation.
>
> **Watch for these failure patterns and score them down (with a kind note):** adding 45 percent to the cost and calling the resulting $26.10 a margin; a page of numbers with no recommendation or no defended ceiling; keeping all three competitor prices as fact with no checking; floor and ceiling confused; an approach named with no reason.
>
> **On academic integrity:** the instructor follows their own institution's academic-integrity and AI-use policy. If the memo reads as an unedited AI dump that ignores the specific brief, with no shown arithmetic and a generic recommendation, do not try to rule on it; flag it for the instructor as something to review under that policy, and score only what is on the page.
>
> **Output format:**
> - **Criterion 1, Value stick built and the price defended: __/40** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 2, Margin arithmetic: __/25** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 3, Competitor frame confirmed: __/20** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 4, Approach named and memo written: __/15** (why) (one tip)
> - **Total: __/100**
> - **One-line summary for the student:** (warm, specific, what to do next time)
>
> The student's memo follows below this line.

## Part 3: Reading and trusting the result

- The AI returns four scores, a total, and short notes. **Skim the notes first.** They tell you whether the AI actually understood the memo. Check the note on Criterion 2, the margin arithmetic, because that is where the answer never changes and the AI's read is easiest to confirm.
- **You can push back.** Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example *"The student did reach $32.73 with the work shown; look again at Criterion 2"* or *"The ceiling here is below the competitor frame; should Criterion 1 be lower?"* The AI will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- **The margin row is the one to double-check yourself, and it is easy.** The brief is the same for every student, so the answer never moves: look for **$32.73** as the margin price with the arithmetic shown, and make sure the student did not add 45 percent to cost and land on $26.10. The grading guide spells this out.
- **The fastest sanity check on the competitor frame needs no AI.** Click one of the student's confirmation links. A real product page that shows the bottle near the price they quoted is the bar; a price marked confirmed with no link is not a confirmation.
- **Keep a fresh chat per student** so submissions do not blur together.
- Remember the framing for any feedback you pass on: *the instructor grades; the AI assists.* The score is yours.

**Fast bands:** 90 to 100, a complete sourced stick with a defended ceiling, the correct $32.73 margin price, one competitor price confirmed with a link, and a placed opening price with the approach named in a real memo; 80 to 89, solid work with one thin row; 70 to 79, present but underdone (bands listed with no defended price, or the markup-as-margin slip, or prices taken on faith); 60 to 69, floor and ceiling confused or a page of numbers with no recommendation; below 60 or an integrity question for a missing memo or work that does not engage the brief (handle any integrity question through your institution's academic-integrity and AI-use policy, not the rubric).

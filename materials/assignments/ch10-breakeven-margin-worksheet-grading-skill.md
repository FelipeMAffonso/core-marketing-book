# Grading Skill: The Breakeven and Margin Worksheet (by hand)

*Chapter 10 individual assignment. This file lets you grade a student's hand-worked pricing math with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste a block of instructions into Claude, paste the student's worksheet under it, and read back a score for each rubric row with a sentence of feedback. The student did this worksheet with no AI; the only AI here is yours, and only to speed your grading. You stay in charge: the AI gives a first-pass score, you decide the grade. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom in case you would rather not use AI at all.*

---

## Part 1: If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install anything or pay anything. This takes about five minutes the first time and under two minutes per student after that.

1. **Open a web browser** (Chrome, Safari, Edge, whatever you use) and go to **claude.ai**. (ChatGPT at **chatgpt.com** works the same way if you prefer it.)
2. **Make a free account.** Click **Sign up**, enter your email, and follow the steps. A free account is enough. You will not be charged.
3. **Start a new chat.** After you log in you will see a box that says something like *"How can I help you today?"* That box is where you type. A "chat" is just a conversation with the AI, like texting.
4. **Copy the grader instructions.** In Part 2 below, everything inside the gray box is one block. Select it all, copy it, and paste it into the chat box. Do not press send yet.
5. **Add the student's worksheet underneath.** Press Enter twice to make space, then paste (or describe, if it is a photo) the student's full worksheet right below the instructions, in the same message. If the work is a photo, most AI tools let you attach the image; otherwise type out the student's numbers and short reasons.
6. **Send it.** Press Enter (or click the arrow). The AI will read both and reply with a score for each of the four rubric rows, a total out of 100, and a short note for each row.
7. **Read the score and decide.** The AI's score is a **first draft for you to approve, change, or override.** You are the grader of record. If a score looks off, trust your own read; the grading guide (`ch10-breakeven-margin-worksheet-grading-guide.md`) tells you what each row is really worth and gives the full answer key.
8. **Grade the next student.** Click **New chat** and repeat from step 4. A fresh chat each time keeps one student's work from bleeding into the next.

That is the whole process. The block in Part 2 does the work; you paste, read, and sign off.

> **One quick sanity check you can always run yourself, no AI needed.** This worksheet has one trap that catches everyone: the difference between markup and margin. On the student's price, **markup is (price minus cost) divided by cost**, and **margin is (price minus cost) divided by price**. They are never the same number. If the student called a markup a margin, that is the single most important thing to catch, and the math above settles it in ten seconds.

## Part 2: The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a second-year marketing student's hand-worked pricing math. The student worked a short worksheet for a campus coffee cart by hand, with a calculator and no AI tool. Your job is to score their worksheet against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback. This is a first-year-friendly course; reward correct method and a clear catch of the planted error over neat presentation.
>
> **The exact correct numbers for this worksheet.** The cart's price is $4.00 per cup, variable cost $1.50 per cup, fixed cost $1,800 per month, current sales 850 cups per month. The right answers are:
> - **Contribution margin:** price minus variable cost = $4.00 minus $1.50 = $2.50 per cup, which is 62.5 percent of the $4.00 price.
> - **Breakeven volume:** fixed cost divided by contribution margin per cup = $1,800 divided by $2.50 = 720 cups per month.
> - **After the price cut to $3.50:** contribution margin = $3.50 minus $1.50 = $2.00 per cup; new breakeven = $1,800 divided by $2.00 = 900 cups per month. The breakeven rose because the lower price leaves less contribution per cup.
> - **Markup and margin on the original $4.00 price:** markup = (price minus cost) divided by cost = $2.50 divided by $1.50 = about 166.7 percent; margin = (price minus cost) divided by price = $2.50 divided by $4.00 = 62.5 percent. They differ because markup divides by cost and margin divides by price.
> - **The read:** at 850 cups the cart is above the original 720 breakeven by 130 cups, and below the new 900 breakeven (after the cut) by 50 cups.
> - **The planted error:** the worksheet shows a seller with a $40 cost who wants a 40 percent margin and writes "$40 times 1.40 = $56." That is a markup, not a margin. The correct margin price is $40 divided by (1 minus 0.40) = $66.67. A strong answer marks this wrong, names the mistake, and shows the $66.67.
>
> **How to score.** Give a number for each of the four criteria below, then add them for a total out of 100. Check the student's arithmetic against the correct numbers above. The markup-versus-margin row plus catching the planted error is the heaviest. Flag clearly any place the student labels a markup as a margin (or the reverse), because that is the chapter's headline mistake and the whole point of the worksheet. For each criterion, write one sentence saying why and one sentence the student could act on. Be specific and encouraging. If a required step is missing or shown with no work, say so plainly and score it low; do not invent credit.
>
> 1. **Markup vs margin read, and the planted error caught (40 points).** Are both the markup percent (about 167 percent) and the margin percent (62.5 percent) correct on the same cost, is the one-sentence reason they differ right (markup divides by cost, margin divides by price), and is the planted $56 error marked wrong with the correct $66.67 margin price shown? Full marks: both percents right, a sound reason, and the error caught and corrected. Low marks: markup and margin treated as the same number, or the planted error missed.
> 2. **Contribution margin and breakeven (28 points).** Are the contribution margin ($2.50, and 62.5 percent of price) and the monthly breakeven (720 cups) both correct with the steps shown? Deduct for the breakeven formula inverted (dividing fixed cost by price instead of by contribution margin) or a contribution margin computed wrong.
> 3. **The price-cut breakeven and the read (22 points).** Did the student recompute the breakeven after the $3.50 price cut correctly (contribution margin $2.00, breakeven 900 cups) and state correctly that 850 cups is above the original breakeven by 130 and below the new one by 50? Deduct for a vague read or a missing recompute.
> 4. **Showing the work and correct terms (10 points).** Is every step shown, and are the terms (variable cost, contribution margin, breakeven, markup, margin) used correctly? Deduct for right answers with no work shown, since partial credit cannot be awarded without steps.
>
> **Do not penalize** the student for not using AI; this worksheet is meant to be done by hand. Penalize only wrong or missing math and a markup labeled a margin.
>
> **Output format:**
> - **Criterion 1, Markup vs margin and the planted error: __/40**, (why), (one tip)
> - **Criterion 2, Contribution margin and breakeven: __/28**, (why), (one tip)
> - **Criterion 3, Price-cut breakeven and the read: __/22**, (why), (one tip)
> - **Criterion 4, Showing the work and correct terms: __/10**, (why), (one tip)
> - **Total: __/100**
> - **One-line summary for the student:** (warm, specific, what to do next time)
>
> The student's worksheet follows below this line.

## Part 3: Reading and trusting the result

- The AI returns four scores, a total, and short notes. **Skim the notes first**; they tell you whether the AI actually read the student's steps. If the notes match what you saw on the worksheet, the total is trustworthy.
- **You can push back.** Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example *"Re-check the breakeven; the student divided $1,800 by the price, not the contribution margin"* or *"The student did catch the $56 error and wrote $66.67, look again at Criterion 1."* The AI will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- **The markup-versus-margin row is the one to double-check yourself.** It is worth the most, and an AI grading it can be too generous. If the student kept markup and margin distinct and corrected the planted error to $66.67, that is a strong sheet even if the presentation is rough.
- **The fastest verification needs no AI.** On the student's price, compute markup = (price minus cost) divided by cost and margin = (price minus cost) divided by price. If those two come out equal in the student's work, something is mislabeled. On this cart they should be about 167 percent versus 62.5 percent.
- **Keep a fresh chat per student** so worksheets do not blur together.
- Remember the framing: *the instructor grades; the AI assists.* The student used no AI at all; your optional AI use is only to speed the grading. The score is yours.

# Grading Skill: Three-Store Price Audit

*Chapter 10 individual assignment. This file lets you grade a student's submission with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste a block of instructions into Claude, paste the student's work under it, and read back a score for each part of the rubric with a sentence of feedback. You stay in charge: the AI gives you a first-pass score, you decide the grade. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom in case you would rather not use AI at all.*

---

## Part 1. If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install anything or pay anything. This takes about five minutes the first time and under two minutes per student after that.

1. **Open a web browser** (Chrome, Safari, Edge, whatever you use) and go to **claude.ai**. (ChatGPT at **chatgpt.com** works the same way if you prefer it.)
2. **Make a free account.** Click **Sign up**, enter your email, and follow the steps. A free account is enough for this. You will not be charged.
3. **Start a new chat.** After you log in you will see a box that says something like *"How can I help you today?"* That box is where you type. A "chat" is just a conversation with the AI, like texting.
4. **Verify the three photographs yourself first** (this part the AI cannot do for you). Open the student's PDF and check four things, each takes about five seconds: (a) there are three photographs, one each from a grocery store, a mass merchandiser, and a convenience store; (b) the price tag is readable in each photo; (c) each photo carries a date and all three dates fall within one seven-day window; (d) the store names in the captions match the table. If any of these four fails, write "Photo verification failed: [what is missing]" at the top of your grade notes, because the AI will be scoring the analysis without being able to confirm the audit, and the audit row caps the rest. The audit row is the entry condition.
5. **Copy the grader instructions.** In Part 2 below, everything inside the gray box is one block. Select it all, copy it, and paste it into the chat box. Do not press send yet.
6. **Add the student's work underneath.** Press Enter twice to make space, then paste the student's full submission, including their comparison table, the photo captions (in plain text, since you cannot paste images into a chat the same way), Response 1, Response 2, Response 3, and the two-insight reflection, right below the instructions in the same message. Tell the AI in one short line at the end whether your photo verification (step 4) passed or failed.
7. **Send it.** Press Enter (or click the arrow). The AI will read both and reply with a score for each of the five rubric rows, a total out of 100, and a short note for each row.
8. **Read the score and decide.** The AI's score is a **first draft for you to approve, change, or override.** You are the grader of record. If a score looks off, trust your own read; the grading guide (`ch10-pricing-grading-guide.md`) tells you what each row is really worth.
9. **Grade the next student.** Click **New chat** and repeat from step 4 for each submission. A fresh chat each time keeps one student's work from bleeding into the next.

That is the whole process. The block in Part 2 does the work; you paste, read, and sign off.

> **The one check that an AI cannot do for you, no matter how clever the prompt.** Verify the three photos by eye yourself in step 4. Three photos, three distinct store formats, dates visible, dates within one week. About thirty seconds. The whole assignment is AI-resistant because the photos exist in the real world, on the real shelf, at the student's real local store this real week. If the photos are not there, the audit row caps the analysis rows at Weak; if you skip the photo check, you have skipped the only step that distinguishes a real audit from a fluently written essay.

## Part 2. The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a second-year marketing student's individual assignment. The student picked one specific SKU, visited three different store formats (a grocery store, a mass merchandiser, and a convenience store) in person within a single seven-day window, photographed the dated price tag at each store, filled a comparison table, and wrote three short responses plus a two-insight reflection. The instructor has already verified the photographs themselves and will tell you whether the photo verification passed or failed. Your job is to score the submission against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback. This is a first-year-friendly course; reward evidence-based reasoning over polish.
>
> **Background you need (the chapter's framework):**
> - The chapter names twelve pricing strategies; the student picks from this closed list: **cost-based, value-based, competitor-based, dynamic, odd-even, reference, decoy, extremity aversion, prestige, bundle, multiple-unit, loss leader.** Strategies the student invents that are not on this list (such as "smart pricing" or "strategic pricing") do not earn credit. The strategies most commonly observable in this kind of audit are odd-even (a "$2.49" or "$1.99" tag), multiple-unit (a "2 for $5.00" or "3 for $10.00" sign), reference (a "Was $3.49, Now $2.49" sign or a "Compare at" tag), and loss leader (a staple priced unusually low, often on an endcap, to draw the customer into the store).
> - The **3 Cs framework** explains a price spread: **Company cost** (the retailer's own cost structure, plus overhead, plus markup), **Competitor frame** (what other retailers in the same format charge nearby), and **Customer value** (what the buyer is actually paying for at that store, which includes convenience, ambiance, basket-trip context). A good 3 Cs analysis names which C explains the spread most and defends the pick. For a same-SKU cross-format audit (grocery vs mass vs convenience), Customer value usually explains more of the spread than Company cost, because a single bottled drink or candy bar does not vary much in wholesale cost across formats but does vary enormously in what the buyer is paying for (a weekly-basket trip vs a between-classes grab).
> - The **buyer-choice defense** asks the student to recommend a store for two real buyers: a **price-sensitive shopper** (a student stretching a grocery budget, or a parent buying a week of supplies on one income) and a **convenience-oriented shopper** (someone five minutes from class, on a road trip, or valuing a one-stop trip with no second errand). The correct posture is that the same product can be the right buy for different shoppers at different stores. Convenience is part of value, not a mistake the convenience buyer is making.
> - **The audit (the three dated photos and the filled table) is the entry condition for the analytical rows.** If the instructor tells you the photo verification failed, you must cap the four analytical rows (Strategy identification, 3 Cs spread explanation, Buyer-choice defense, Writing and presentation) at the Weak band, because the responses have no evidence to rest on.
>
> **How to score.** Give a number for each of the five criteria below, then add them for a total out of 100. For each, write one sentence saying why, and one sentence the student could act on. Be specific and encouraging. If something required is missing, say so plainly and score it low, do not invent credit.
>
> 1. **Audit completeness, the entry condition (25 points).** Based on what the instructor told you about the photo verification AND what you can see in the comparison table: are there three dated photographs from three distinct store formats, all within seven days, all showing the price tag clearly? Is the table filled with regular price, any sale or promo, any multi-unit deal, and shelf position for each store? Full marks require all of the above. If the instructor told you the photo verification failed, score this row in the Weak band (0 to 6 of 25) and cap criteria 2 through 5 at the Weak band too.
> 2. **Strategy identification (25 points).** For each of the three stores, did the student name one or two pricing strategies from the chapter's closed list (cost-based, value-based, competitor-based, dynamic, odd-even, reference, decoy, extremity aversion, prestige, bundle, multiple-unit, loss leader) and quote the evidence from the photo or table? Deduct for invented strategies that are not in the chapter, or for strategies misapplied (calling "$2.49" a bundle instead of odd-even, for instance).
> 3. **3 Cs spread explanation (20 points).** Did the student apply Company cost, Competitor frame, AND Customer value to this specific SKU at these three stores, and say which C explains the spread most with reasoning tied to the audit evidence? Deduct for one or two Cs only, for a definition rather than an analysis, or for "the C-store just charges more" without the framework.
> 4. **Buyer-choice defense (15 points).** Did the student recommend a specific store for the price-sensitive shopper AND a specific store for the convenience-oriented shopper, tying each recommendation to the audit evidence and at least one chapter concept, and treating convenience as part of value rather than as a mistake? Deduct for giving both buyers the same answer, or for telling the convenience buyer they should go to the cheaper store anyway.
> 5. **Writing and presentation (15 points).** Does the two-page brief read cleanly, use the chapter vocabulary correctly, present the table and the captioned photos clearly, and close with two insights specific to what the student saw (not generic)? Deduct for sloppy presentation, terms used incorrectly, or generic insights that could have been written without the audit.
>
> **Watch for these failure patterns and score them down (with a kind note):** an audit missing photos or with photos that fail the freshness gate; strategies invented rather than picked from the chapter's list; the 3 Cs listed as definitions rather than applied to the spread; both buyers given the same store recommendation; insights at the close that are generic and could have been written without visiting any store.
>
> **Do not penalize** disclosed AI use for help polishing the prose of the three responses, that is normal academic work. The audit itself is what cannot be AI-generated, and the rubric handles that through the audit row. If the writing reads like an unedited generative dump and the photos look pulled from a stock-image search rather than a phone camera, flag it for the instructor as a possible disclosure issue rather than scoring it, that is a human decision.
>
> **Output format:**
> - **Criterion 1, Audit completeness: __/25** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 2, Strategy identification: __/25** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 3, 3 Cs spread explanation: __/20** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 4, Buyer-choice defense: __/15** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 5, Writing and presentation: __/15** (why) (one tip)
> - **Total: __/100**
> - **One-line summary for the student:** (warm, specific, what to do next time)
>
> The instructor's photo-verification result and the student's submission follow below this line.

## Part 3. Reading and trusting the result

- The AI returns five scores, a total, and short notes. **Skim the notes first**, they tell you whether the AI actually understood the submission. If the notes match what you saw when you read the work, the total is trustworthy.
- **You can push back.** Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example "The student correctly named odd-even pricing at the grocery store and quoted the $2.49 tag; look again at Criterion 2" or "The student treated the convenience-oriented buyer as making a mistake; look again at Criterion 4." The AI will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- **The audit row is the one to double-check yourself.** It is worth the most, it is the entry condition for the rest, and it is the one row only you can verify (the AI does not see the photos). If your photo verification in Part 1 step 4 found a problem, write that finding directly into the chat before you trust the AI's analytical scores.
- **The fastest verification needs no AI.** Look at the three captioned photos. Three distinct formats? Dates visible? Dates within one week? Price tag readable? About thirty seconds. That is the irreplaceable check.
- **Keep a fresh chat per student** so submissions do not blur together.
- Remember the framing for any feedback you pass on: *the instructor grades; the AI assists.* The score is yours.

## Part 4. Grade by hand instead (no AI at all)

If you would rather not use AI, grade straight from the rubric. Verify the three photographs first (about thirty seconds), then read each submission once and score the five rows:

| Criterion | Points | What earns full marks |
|---|---|---|
| **Audit completeness (entry condition)** | 25 | Three dated photographs from three distinct store formats (grocery, mass, convenience) within seven days, with the price tag readable in each; comparison table filled correctly with regular price, sale or promo, multi-unit deal, and shelf position for each store |
| **Strategy identification** | 25 | One or two strategies named per store, picked from the chapter's closed list (cost-based, value-based, competitor-based, dynamic, odd-even, reference, decoy, extremity aversion, prestige, bundle, multiple-unit, loss leader), with evidence quoted from the photo or table |
| **3 Cs spread explanation** | 20 | Company cost, Competitor frame, Customer value all applied to this SKU at these three stores; names which C explains the spread most and defends the pick with audit evidence |
| **Buyer-choice defense** | 15 | Both a price-sensitive shopper and a convenience-oriented shopper get a store recommendation tied to evidence and at least one chapter concept; convenience treated as value |
| **Writing and presentation** | 15 | Two-page brief reads cleanly; chapter vocabulary used correctly; captioned photos and table easy to read; two specific insights at the close |
| **Total** | **100** | |

**Fast bands:** 90 to 100 audit complete and accurate with strategies correctly named, the 3 Cs framework applied, both buyers given a defensible store recommendation, and specific insights at the close; 80 to 89 strong work with one row thin; 70 to 79 audit complete but analysis is generic; 60 to 69 audit incomplete or photos missing, which caps the analysis rows here as the entry condition specifies; below 60 or re-submit no audit evidence at all (handle any academic-integrity question through your institution's policy, not the rubric).

The single fastest signal of a strong paper: **the student quoted evidence from their own photo** to support a strategy claim. "Odd-even pricing at the grocery store, the tag in my photo reads $2.49" or "Multiple-unit at the C-store, the sign in my photo says 2 for $5.00" is the assignment working.

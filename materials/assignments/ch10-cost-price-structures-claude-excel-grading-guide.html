Grading Guide — Analyzing Cost and Price Structures with Claude in Excel

Chapter 10 advanced (Tier 3) individual assignment. This guide is for whoever grades the work: you by hand, a TA, or an AI you direct. It says what the assignment is really testing, gives the answer key for the Trailhead Supply Co. dataset so you can check any number in seconds, lays out what good and weak work look like row by row, names the failure modes, and turns the rubric into a fair score fast. You do not need to open Excel yourself; the key below has every figure that matters.

What this assignment is really testing

Not whether the student can run a contribution-margin formula, Claude for Excel will do that in seconds. This assignment tests whether a student can direct the add-in through a real cost analysis and then out-judge it: spot the SKU that loses money on every unit, recognize the item that does not belong in a product breakeven, catch a formula or framing error, and make the call the tool will not make. That second half is the whole point. A workbook full of correct formulas with no judgment pass has missed the assignment. A workbook with rougher charts but a sharp memo that caught the loss-maker has hit it.

Keep the chapter's core idea in front of you while grading: a healthy-looking markup can still hide a margin too thin to cover fixed cost, and a confident analysis can be confidently wrong. The single most important thing a student must find in this dataset is the one product that is underwater on every unit. The add-in will happily fold it into a line total and never flag it.

The answer key for this dataset (Trailhead Supply Co.)

The fixed cost is $128,000 per quarter. Contribution margin per unit is unit price minus unit variable cost; breakeven volume is $128,000 divided by that contribution margin.

The line as a whole: total contribution last quarter was about $507,630 against the $128,000 fixed cost, so the line cleared roughly $379,630 in contribution above fixed cost. The line is healthy overall; the problems are inside it.

The two things every strong submission finds:

  1. TS-150 Trail Snack Box 12pk loses money on every unit. Its $21.00 price is below its $22.40 variable cost, so the contribution margin is −$1.40 per unit. It has no breakeven (you cannot cover fixed cost with a negative contribution). Worse, it sold the most units of any SKU (6,100), so it drained about $8,540 of contribution last quarter. The fix is to raise the price above $22.40 (at minimum), cut the cost, or discontinue it.
  2. TS-160 Gift Card $50 does not belong in a product breakeven. A gift card is deferred revenue, not a product with a real variable cost or shelf inventory (note its $0 returns and zero units in stock). Treating its near-zero "cost" as a 97% margin inflates the line's apparent health and distorts any "most profitable SKU" ranking. It should be set aside before the product analysis, not ranked among the sleeves and packs.

A subtler point worth credit: the contribution margins above ignore returns, and two SKUs have heavy ones (TS-120 Merino Sock at 140 returns, TS-121 Base Layer Top at 88). A student who notes that a returns-adjusted contribution margin would pull those two down is doing real analysis the add-in skipped.

The three ways to grade (your choice)

You own the grade. Pick the path that fits your section and your time.

  1. By hand. Read the memo and skim the workbook against the five rubric rows, checking figures against the key above. Budget about ten minutes each. You do not need Excel open; the key has every number. This is the most reliable path and the one to use for any borderline or flagged paper.
  2. Hand it to TAs. Give your TAs this guide (with the key) and the rubric. Calibrate first: grade three papers together so everyone anchors the bands the same way. The most common TA drift is rewarding a workbook full of tidy formulas while missing that the student never caught TS-150, the opposite of what this assignment values. Calibrate on that specifically.
  3. Use the grading skill (optional). The Ch10 pricing grading skill (ch10-pricing-grading-skill.md) is built for the prose assignment, but its walkthrough and its grader block adapt cleanly to this rubric: paste the block, swap in these five criteria and the key above, then paste the student's memo and transcript. Treat the AI's score as a draft you sign off on, never the final grade.

All three use the same rubric, so a student gets the same standard whichever path you pick.

What to look for, row by row

Judgment pass (30 pts) — the heaviest row, grade it first.

  • Strong: the student caught TS-150 with the arithmetic ($21 price below $22.40 cost, −$1.40 contribution), flagged TS-160 as not belonging in a product breakeven, fixed a real formula or framing error, and named the single price to change first with a target number.
  • Look for the catch. The single best signal of a strong paper is the loss-maker named with its negative contribution. "TS-150 sells for $21 but costs $22.40, so it loses $1.40 every time and there is no breakeven; at 6,100 units that is roughly $8,540 gone, I would raise it to at least $26 or drop it" is the assignment working.
  • Weak: "Claude analyzed all twelve SKUs and they look healthy." No catch of TS-150. That is the Developing/Not-yet band no matter how clean the formulas are.

Contribution margin and breakeven (24 pts).

  • Strong: both correct for every SKU and for the line, written as cell formulas against the $128,000 fixed cost (not typed-in constants), with above-versus-below-breakeven read correctly. Check a couple against the key.
  • Weak: a clear arithmetic error, breakeven not computed against the fixed cost, or numbers pasted as values with no formula. A wrong line breakeven caps this row at Developing.

Markup versus margin (14 pts).

  • Strong: both shown per SKU and clearly distinct, with markup (on cost) never reported as margin (on price). This is the chapter's headline trap; the add-in can slip on it just as a chat model does.
  • Weak: only one shown, or the two conflated. Absent is Not-yet.

Pricing scenario (14 pts).

  • Strong: the student modeled a real price change on one SKU, showed the new contribution margin and the new breakeven volume, and judged whether the change is worth it. (Raising TS-150 above $22.40 is the obvious, and best, choice.)
  • Weak: a number changed with no breakeven effect shown. No scenario is Not-yet.

Memo and disclosure (18 pts).

  • Strong: a one-page memo giving three defensible calls a founder could act on (the loss-maker and the fix, the highest-priority price change with its new breakeven, and the gift-card or returns point), plus a clear disclosure of the prompts and what was corrected.
  • Weak: a memo that restates numbers without a recommendation, or a thin disclosure. Unclear or missing memo is Not-yet.

Common failure modes (and how to score them)

  • The clean-formulas, no-judgment paper. Every formula is right, the charts are tidy, and the student never noticed TS-150 is underwater. The most common miss. The judgment row lands in Developing or Not-yet; the arithmetic row can still earn its points. Net: usually mid-range, because the graded skill is missing.
  • Missed the loss-maker. The student treats TS-150 as just another low-margin SKU, or worse, as healthy because it sold the most units. Volume hides the bleed. Score the judgment row down and note that high volume on a negative contribution makes the loss bigger, not smaller.
  • Kept the gift card in the ranking. The student ranks TS-160 as the "most profitable" product on its 97% margin. Flag it: a gift card is deferred revenue, not a product, and including it distorts the line. Partial credit if they at least noticed it looked odd.
  • The markup-margin slip. A markup reported as a margin somewhere in the sheet, uncaught. This is the chapter's headline mistake; score the markup-margin row down and note it.
  • Formulas pasted as values. The numbers are right but there are no live formulas, so nothing traces to a cell and the analysis cannot be audited or re-run. Cap the arithmetic row at Proficient and note the assignment asked for formulas in the sheet.
  • The hidden AI use. No transcript and no disclosure, yet the memo reads like an unedited model dump. This is an integrity matter, not a grading deduction; follow your institution's process. Disclosed use, even heavy use, is never the violation; hiding it is.

Score bands (for a fast, fair landing)

Use these to convert the five rows into a defensible total.

  • 90–100 (Excellent). Correct contribution and breakeven for every SKU and the line as live formulas, markup and margin distinct, a real price scenario with its new breakeven, and a judgment pass that caught TS-150 with the arithmetic, flagged TS-160, and named the price to change. The memo gives a founder three calls to act on.
  • 80–89 (Strong/Proficient). Solid formulas and a genuine judgment pass that caught the loss-maker, but one row is thin: the scenario read is shallow, the gift card slipped, or the memo is light on recommendations.
  • 70–79 (Developing). The work is present but the student mostly trusted the add-in: tidy formulas, no real catch of TS-150, or a breakeven not run against fixed cost. The judgment half is underdone.
  • 60–69 (Weak). Output repeated with little checking, the loss-maker missed, or a markup reported as a margin and never noticed. The assignment's core skill is missing.
  • Below 60 / integrity flag. No memo, no workbook, or an undisclosed AI dump. Handle disclosure issues through your integrity process, not the rubric.

A note for graders on the AI itself

If you run the dataset through Claude for Excel (or a free chat model with the CSV) yourself, expect it to compute the per-SKU contribution margins correctly and then say nothing about the fact that one of them is negative. It will rank TS-160's gift card as a star on its 97% margin. It may report a markup as a margin. None of that is a reason to distrust the student; it is the exact thing the assignment trains them to catch. Reward the catch. The fastest verification is the key above: scan for TS-150's negative contribution and check whether the student found it. A student who caught the loss-making SKU and owned the price call has done more pricing thinking than one whose formulas were all correct and who flagged nothing.

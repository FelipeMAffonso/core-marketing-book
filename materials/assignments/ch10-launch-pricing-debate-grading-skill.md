# Grading Skill: Skim or Penetrate, Judge an AI Launch Call

*Chapter 10 group assignment (groups of three or four), no AI account needed by students. This file lets you grade a group's submission with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste a block of instructions into Claude, paste the group's work under it, and read back a score for each part of the rubric with a sentence of feedback. The grader block already knows the two planted errors in the memo, so it can tell whether the group caught them. You stay in charge: the AI gives you a first-pass score, you decide the grade. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom in case you would rather not use AI at all.*

---

## Part 1: If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install anything or pay anything. This takes about five minutes the first time and under two minutes per group after that.

1. **Open a web browser** (Chrome, Safari, Edge, whatever you use) and go to **claude.ai**. (ChatGPT at **chatgpt.com** works the same way if you prefer it.)
2. **Make a free account.** Click **Sign up**, enter your email, and follow the steps. A free account is enough for this. You will not be charged.
3. **Start a new chat.** After you log in you will see a box that says something like *"How can I help you today?"* That box is where you type. A "chat" is just a conversation with the AI, like texting.
4. **Copy the grader instructions.** In Part 2 below, everything inside the gray box is one block. Select it all, copy it, and paste it into the chat box. Do not press send yet.
5. **Add the group's work underneath.** Press Enter twice to make space, then paste the group's full submission, including the group decision sheet (the posture chosen, the q/p reasoning, the two errors they caught and corrected, and the fairness read) and every member's one-paragraph verdict, right below the instructions, in the same message.
6. **Send it.** Press Enter (or click the arrow). The AI will read it all and reply with a score for each of the four rubric rows, a total out of 100, and a short note for each row.
7. **Read the score and decide.** The AI's score is a **first draft for you to approve, change, or override.** You are the grader of record. If a score looks off, trust your own read. The grading guide (`ch10-launch-pricing-debate-grading-guide.md`) holds the full answer key and tells you what each row is really worth.
8. **Grade the next group.** Click **New chat** and repeat from step 4 for each submission. A fresh chat each time keeps one group's work from bleeding into the next.

That is the whole process. The block in Part 2 does the work; you paste, read, and sign off.

> **The two-second check you can always run yourself, no AI needed.** The memo is the same for every group, so the heaviest row comes down to two things. Did the group write **$66.67** as the corrected launch price (with a correct markup-versus-margin explanation), and did they mark **both** competitor prices ($59 at Stanley, $52 for the bookstore kit) as **unconfirmed**? Both found is an Excellent judgment pass; one found is Proficient; neither found is Developing or Not yet.

## Part 2: The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a group submission for a second-year marketing course. This is a no-account judgment assignment: every group was handed the SAME pre-generated AI memo and asked to judge it, debate a launch posture, and turn in one group decision sheet plus a separate one-paragraph verdict from each member (groups are three or four students). Your job is to score the submission against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback. Reward catching the AI's errors over polished writing.
>
> **The memo every group judged (you need this to grade them).** The product is a campus co-branded cold-brew launch kit at a stated $40 unit cost. The memo recommends a skimming posture, quotes two competitor prices, and gives a launch price at a claimed 40% margin. The memo contains two planted errors the group was supposed to catch:
> - **Planted error 1, a markup labeled a margin (the headline error).** The memo says a 40% margin on a $40 cost prices at $56 ($40 times 1.40) and calls $56 a 40% margin. That is wrong: $40 times 1.40 is a 40% MARKUP, not a margin. A true 40% MARGIN needs a price of $40 divided by (1 minus 0.40), which is $66.67. The $56 price is actually only a 28.6% margin. A group that caught this writes the corrected price **$66.67** and explains the markup-versus-margin difference.
> - **Planted error 2, invented competitor prices stated as fact.** The memo states "Stanley sells a co-branded kit at $59" and "a university-bookstore cold-brew kit runs about $52" as verified facts, but it verified neither. A group that caught this marks both prices **unconfirmed** and refuses to use them as anchors.
> - **Two softer flags that earn credit but are not required:** the memo picks skimming, but a campus drink is arguably HIGH q/p (party photos, group chats, and tumblers carried across campus are imitation channels that spread adoption fast), and a high q/p favors PENETRATION; a sharp group ties its posture to that q/p logic. And the memo's "fair because it matches competitors" is shallow; real fairness is the dual-entitlement principle (a cost story reads as fair, a demand windfall reads as gouging), and the memo's fairness claim rests on the broken $56 price and the unverified competitor numbers.
>
> **How to score.** Give a number for each of the four criteria below, then add them for a total out of 100. For each, write one sentence saying why, and one sentence the group could act on. Be specific and encouraging. If something required is missing, say so plainly and score it low; do not invent credit.
>
> 1. **Judgment pass: caught what the AI got wrong (45 points).** Did the group catch BOTH planted errors and correct the margin arithmetic to $66.67? Full marks: both caught (the markup labeled a margin, corrected to $66.67, and the two competitor prices marked unconfirmed). Proficient: one planted error caught and corrected. Developing: notes the memo is "mostly fine" without catching either. Not yet: takes the memo at face value and keeps the $56 price and the 40% margin label.
> 2. **Skim vs penetrate call (25 points).** Did the group pick a posture and tie it to the q/p logic for THIS product (explaining why the imitation channel is strong or weak), rather than picking by reflex? A defensible case for either posture earns credit; repeating the memo's "skimming" with no q/p reasoning does not. Deduct for a posture stated with no reasoning.
> 3. **Individual verdict, each member (20 points).** Is every member's one-paragraph verdict their own, naming a real piece of evidence that moved them and flagging one thing the memo got wrong? Read the paragraphs against each other. Near-duplicate paragraphs (the same sentences with names swapped) cap this row low even if the group sheet is strong. Deduct for thin or missing verdicts.
> 4. **Fairness read (10 points).** Is there a one-line dual-entitlement read on whether the opening price reads as fair (a cost story reads as fair, a demand windfall reads as gouging)? Full credit, better still, if the group notices the memo's fairness claim rested on the broken $56 price and the unverified competitor numbers. Deduct for fairness mentioned without the principle.
>
> **Watch for these failure patterns and score them down (with a kind note):** taking the memo at face value and keeping the $56 price; catching only one of the two planted errors; "correcting" the margin but landing on the wrong number (the right answer is $66.67); repeating the memo's skimming with no q/p reasoning; near-duplicate individual verdicts; a fairness line that just says "it matches competitors."
>
> **Do not penalize** the group for using no AI account; this is a no-account card and the memo was provided. Penalize only the absence of judgment, not the absence of AI use.
>
> **Output format:**
> - **Criterion 1, Judgment pass: __/45** (why, and say which planted errors were caught) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 2, Skim vs penetrate call: __/25** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 3, Individual verdict: __/20** (why, and note any near-duplicates) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 4, Fairness read: __/10** (why) (one tip)
> - **Total: __/100**
> - **One-line summary for the group:** (warm, specific, what to do next time)
>
> The group's submission follows below this line.

## Part 3: Reading and trusting the result

- The AI returns four scores, a total, and short notes. **Skim the notes first.** They tell you whether the AI actually understood the submission. Check that its note on Criterion 1 names which planted errors the group caught; that is the heart of the grade.
- **You can push back.** Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example *"The group did mark the Stanley price unconfirmed; look again at Criterion 1"* or *"Two of these verdicts are nearly identical; should Criterion 3 be lower?"* The AI will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- **The judgment-pass row is the one to double-check yourself, and it is easy.** The memo is the same for every group, so the answer key never changes: look for **$66.67** with a correct markup-versus-margin explanation, and look for **both** competitor prices marked **unconfirmed**. Both found is Excellent; one found is Proficient; neither is Developing or Not yet. The grading guide spells this out.
- **Read the individual verdicts yourself.** The AI can miss near-duplicate paragraphs. Put the member verdicts side by side; if they are one paragraph with names swapped, the individual-verdict row caps low regardless of the group sheet.
- **Keep a fresh chat per group** so submissions do not blur together.
- Remember the framing for any feedback you pass on: *the instructor grades; the AI assists.* The score is yours.

# Grading Skill: Three Decoy Lineups, Three Diagnoses

*Chapter 10 individual assignment, the decoy-effect read across three industries. This file lets you grade a student's submission with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste the block below into Claude, paste the student's memo under it, and read back a score for each part of the rubric with a sentence of feedback. The block already knows the three three-tier lineups the student was given and the strong reads for each one, so it can tell whether the student picked the right decoy and target in each scenario, tied the read to the lineup's specific prices and split data, and closed with feasible moves. You stay in charge: the AI gives you a first-pass score, you decide the grade. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom in case you would rather not use AI at all.*

---

## Part 1: If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install anything or pay anything. This takes about five minutes the first time and under three minutes per student after that.

1. **Open a web browser** (Chrome, Safari, Edge, whatever you use) and go to **claude.ai**. (ChatGPT at **chatgpt.com** works the same way if you prefer it.)
2. **Make a free account.** Click **Sign up**, enter your email, and follow the steps. A free account is enough for this. You will not be charged.
3. **Start a new chat.** After you log in you will see a box that says something like *"How can I help you today?"* That box is where you type. A chat is just a conversation with the AI, like texting.
4. **Copy the grader instructions.** In Part 2 below, everything inside the gray box is one block. Select it all, copy it, and paste it into the chat box. Do not press send yet.
5. **Add the student's work underneath.** Press Enter twice to make space, then paste the student's full memo, including all three scenario sections (Brewline Coffee Roasters, Mercator Analytics, Skybridge Atlantic Airlines), the named decoys and targets, the named mechanisms, the justifications, the further moves, and the closing reflection, right below the instructions, in the same message.
6. **Send it.** Press Enter (or click the arrow). The AI will read both and reply with a score for each of the five rubric rows, a total out of one hundred, and a short note for each row.
7. **Read the score and decide.** The AI's score is a first draft for you to approve, change, or override. You are the grader of record. If a score looks off, trust your own read. The grading guide (`ch10-decoy-pricing-page-grading-guide.md`) holds the full answer key and tells you what each row is really worth.
8. **Grade the next student.** Click **New chat** and repeat from step 4 for each submission. A fresh chat each time keeps one student's work from bleeding into the next.

That is the whole process. The block in Part 2 does the work; you paste, read, and sign off.

> **The thirty-second check you can always run yourself, no AI needed.** The three lineups are the same for every student, so the decoy and the target in each scenario never change. Brewline's decoy is the middle Tall cup and the target is the largest Grande, because the Grande beats the Tall on per-ounce price for only fifty cents more. Mercator's decoy is the middle Pro plan and the target is the Enterprise plan, because the Enterprise plan beats Pro on seats, connectors, and support for only thirty dollars more per month. Skybridge's Premium Economy is the target middle option, not a decoy, because it is not dominated by any other cabin and it sells thirty-three percent of seats, which is a compromise share, not a decoy share. Did the student get all three identifications right, including the judgment call on Skybridge that there is no decoy in the lineup? Did they name at least four different mechanisms from the chosen-from list across the three scenarios? Did they close with two real insights drawn across the three? A memo that hits those three checks is a strong paper.

## Part 2: The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a second-year marketing student's individual assignment. This is the second-instance three-scenario short-memo card in Chapter 10, the one foregrounding the decoy cluster (decoy effect, extremity aversion, good-better-best, anchoring, reference). Reward defensible decoy identifications and clear scenario-specific justifications over polished writing. Every student was handed the SAME three three-tier pricing lineups from three different industries and asked to identify the decoy and the target in each, pick at least two mechanisms from a chosen-from list, justify each read in one hundred to one hundred fifty words tied to the scenario's specific prices and split data, close each section with one further move (or a defended hold), and end with a two-insight reflection across the three scenarios. Your job is to score their memo against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback.
>
> **The three lineups every student was given (you need this to grade them).**
>
> **Scenario 1: Brewline Coffee Roasters.** A Midwest coffee chain selling drip coffee in three cup sizes at every store: Short at two dollars and ninety-five cents for eight ounces (about thirty-seven cents per ounce), Tall at three dollars and forty-five cents for twelve ounces (about twenty-nine cents per ounce), Grande at three dollars and ninety-five cents for sixteen ounces (about twenty-five cents per ounce). The head of retail designed the middle Tall to make the largest Grande look like the obvious value: fifty cents more buys four more ounces and the best per-ounce price. Point-of-sale data shows seventy-one percent of buyers picking Grande, twenty-three percent picking Tall, six percent picking Short. Average revenue per drip-coffee transaction is three dollars and seventy-eight cents, up from three dollars and twenty-one cents a year ago when the chain ran only a single twelve-ounce size at three dollars and twenty-five cents.
>
> **Scenario 2: Mercator Analytics.** A B2B marketing-analytics SaaS sold to mid-market and enterprise companies. Three plans on the pricing page: Starter at seventy-nine dollars a month (three seats, five connectors, ten dashboards, forty-eight-hour email support), Pro at two hundred forty-nine dollars a month (eight seats, twenty-five connectors, unlimited dashboards, twenty-four-hour email support), Enterprise at two hundred seventy-nine dollars a month (twenty-five seats, unlimited connectors, unlimited dashboards, four-hour email and chat support, dedicated success manager). The VP of growth designed the Pro plan to be the decoy, priced just thirty dollars below Enterprise to make Enterprise read as the obvious value. Last six months of sign-up data show forty-one percent picking Starter, fourteen percent picking Pro, forty-five percent picking Enterprise. The head of customer success has flagged that Pro customers churn at roughly twice the rate of Starter and Enterprise customers.
>
> **Scenario 3: Skybridge Atlantic Airlines.** A US-to-Europe long-haul airline. Three cabins on the JFK-to-Heathrow round trip: Economy at seven hundred forty-nine dollars (thirty-one-inch seat pitch, no lie-flat, no lounge), Premium Economy at one thousand six hundred forty-nine dollars (thirty-eight-inch pitch, no lie-flat, no lounge), Business at four thousand four hundred ninety-nine dollars (seventy-eight-inch pitch, lie-flat, lounge access). The premium between Economy and Premium Economy is about nine hundred dollars; the premium between Premium Economy and Business is about two thousand eight hundred fifty dollars. Bookings split is fifty-eight percent Economy, thirty-three percent Premium Economy, nine percent Business; the nine percent Business is almost entirely corporate-account bookings.
>
> **The chosen-from list of mechanisms the student picked from:** the decoy effect (asymmetric dominance), reference pricing, extremity aversion (the compromise effect), prestige, charm, anchoring, good-better-best, bundle, multiple-unit. Each scenario requires at least two picks, and the student must use at least four different mechanisms across the three scenarios.
>
> **The strong reads (the answer key, not the only right answers):**
>
> - **Scenario 1 (Brewline):** the Tall is the decoy and the Grande is the target. The Grande dominates the Tall on per-ounce price (about twenty-five cents per ounce versus about twenty-nine cents per ounce) for only fifty cents more total, which makes the Grande the obvious value to any buyer reading the per-ounce math. The lineup is doing what Brewline designed, supported by the seventy-one percent Grande share and average revenue per transaction rising from three dollars twenty-one cents to three dollars seventy-eight cents. Strong-fit mechanisms: the decoy effect (the per-ounce dominance lever); extremity aversion (some buyers pulled away from Short to Tall by the avoid-the-cheapest reflex, then lost to Grande on the per-ounce read); anchoring (the Grande price anchoring the Tall as poor value); good-better-best (the staircase across willingness-to-pay bands). A strong recommendation is to hold the lineup, defended with the seventy-one percent Grande share and the rise in average revenue per transaction.
> - **Scenario 2 (Mercator):** the Pro is the decoy and the Enterprise is the target. Enterprise dominates Pro on seats (twenty-five versus eight), connectors (unlimited versus twenty-five), and support (four-hour with a dedicated success manager versus twenty-four-hour email) for only thirty more dollars per month, so Enterprise reads as the obvious value. The structure is doing some of what the VP designed (forty-five percent Enterprise), but the fourteen percent Pro share and the doubled Pro churn say the Pro plan is partly functioning as a real option for buyers who do not need Enterprise, who then leave quickly because they bought the wrong plan. Strong-fit mechanisms: the decoy effect (the thirty-dollar gap that lets Enterprise dominate Pro); anchoring or reference pricing (the Pro price anchoring the Enterprise step up); good-better-best for the overall lineup; extremity aversion for the forty-one percent who picked the cheapest Starter. A strong recommendation: tighten the Pro decoy by moving its price closer to Enterprise (say two hundred sixty-nine or two hundred seventy-nine dollars) and stripping seats or support so Pro loses on more attributes, with the aim of dropping Pro share toward zero and raising Enterprise share. A defensible alternative is to relabel Pro as a real mid-tier and add a fourth, true decoy between Pro and Enterprise.
> - **Scenario 3 (Skybridge):** Premium Economy is the **target middle option**, not a decoy. The dominance test rules it out: Premium Economy is not dominated by Economy (Premium Economy beats Economy on seat pitch and softer seat) and it is not dominated by Business (Premium Economy is priced well below Business). The thirty-three percent share is the compromise share, not a decoy share (a real decoy approximates zero share). The Business cabin is the anchor that makes Premium Economy read as a moderate upgrade, and the Economy floor makes the nine-hundred-dollar premium read as affordable. Strong-fit mechanisms: extremity aversion (the compromise effect) for the thirty-three percent landing on Premium Economy; anchoring or reference pricing (the four thousand four hundred ninety-nine dollar Business anchoring the read of Premium Economy as moderate); good-better-best for the three-cabin staircase; prestige pricing for the Business cabin's nine percent share of corporate buyers reading the high fare as quality signal. A strong recommendation is to hold the lineup, defended with the compromise structure and the corporate-account read on Business. A defensible alternative is to narrow the Premium-Economy-to-Business gap (test three thousand four hundred ninety-nine dollars) to pull a few leisure buyers up.
>
> **The common slips that should cost points:**
>
> - **Calling Premium Economy a decoy.** This is the central failure mode and the main thing this card screens for. Premium Economy is not dominated by any other cabin, and a thirty-three percent share is not a decoy share; calling it a decoy fails the dominance test the chapter defines. Score Scenario 3 down and feedback should name the dominance test.
> - **Calling Brewline's Short the decoy.** The Short is not dominated by anything in the lineup; it serves a real eight-ounce buyer and its six percent share is real demand. The Tall is the decoy.
> - **Ignoring the Mercator Pro churn signal.** The doubled Pro churn is the tell that the Pro plan is selling to real buyers who should not be there; a student who never names the churn has missed the read.
> - **Ignoring the Skybridge corporate-account skew.** The nine percent Business share is almost entirely corporate-account bookings reading the price as prestige; a student who never names that has missed the read.
> - **The recycled mechanism.** A student who picks the decoy effect for all three scenarios with the same justification, including Scenario 3, has missed the chapter's central concept. The vocabulary row also drops on the at-least-four-different-mechanisms criterion.
> - **The label by reflex.** Calling the decoy "the trick option", calling extremity aversion "the middle-pick effect", calling anchoring "the reference number". The chapter labels are the chapter labels.
> - **The generic justification.** A justification that could be lifted onto any scenario, with no tie to the actual prices (about twenty-nine cents per ounce, the thirty-dollar Mercator gap, the two thousand eight hundred fifty dollar cabin gap). Dock the strategy-fit row.
> - **The contradictory recommendation.** Calling Mercator's Pro a decoy and then recommending the Enterprise price be cut to two hundred forty-nine dollars (which collapses the dominance), or calling Brewline's Tall a decoy and then recommending it be repriced at three dollars and eighty-five cents (which erases the per-ounce gap). Dock the feasibility row.
>
> **How to score.** Give a number for each of the five criteria below, then add them for a total out of one hundred. For each, write one sentence saying why, and one sentence the student could act on. Be specific and encouraging. If something required is missing, say so plainly and score it low; do not invent credit.
>
> 1. **Decoy identification and reasoning across the three scenarios (50 points), scored per-scenario at roughly seventeen, seventeen, and sixteen points.** For each of the three scenarios, are the decoy and the target correctly identified (including the judgment call on Scenario 3 that Premium Economy is the target not the decoy), are at least two mechanisms from the chosen-from list named, is the justification tied to the scenario's specific prices and split data, and is there one further move (or defended hold) that follows from the read? Deduct per-scenario for misidentified decoys or targets (especially the Premium Economy decoy mistake), for generic justifications, for recycled language across scenarios, and for thin further moves.
> 2. **Use of the chapter's named mechanisms (15 points).** Did the student name each mechanism by the chapter's exact label from the chosen-from list (decoy effect, reference pricing, extremity aversion, prestige, charm, anchoring, good-better-best, bundle, multiple-unit), used correctly, with at least four different mechanisms across the three scenarios? Deduct for paraphrased labels ("the trick option", "the middle-pick effect", "the reference number") and for reuse of the same one or two mechanisms across all three scenarios.
> 3. **Feasibility of the recommended move (15 points).** Is each of the three recommended moves (or defended holds) a step the firm could plausibly run next quarter, tied to the lineup's specific numbers and buyers, with no internal contradictions (a decoy-strengthening move paired with a price cut that breaks the dominance, a prestige call paired with a bundle discount)? Deduct for vague recommendations (change the prices, change the tiers) and for contradictions inside one scenario.
> 4. **Writing clarity (10 points).** Do the three sections read as a paragraph to a colleague, in connected sentences, with the chapter's vocabulary used in plain prose and no fragments or staccatos? Deduct for hard-to-follow reasoning.
> 5. **Reflection close (10 points).** Does the closing reflection list two real insights drawn across the three scenarios (not a paraphrase of one scenario), each a sentence the student could carry into a future pricing-page read? Deduct for a single thin insight, a reflection that restates the prompt, or one-insight-per-scenario rather than two cross-scenario insights.
>
> **Watch for these failure patterns and score them down with a kind note:** Premium Economy called a decoy without the judgment call that it is the target; the Brewline Short called the decoy; the Mercator Pro churn signal ignored; the Skybridge corporate-account skew ignored; the decoy effect recycled across all three scenarios; the label by reflex; the generic justification with no tie to the scenario's specific prices; the contradictory recommendation; a missing or paraphrased reflection.
>
> **On academic integrity:** the instructor follows their own institution's academic-integrity and AI-use policy. If the memo reads as an unedited AI dump that flattens the differences between the three scenarios, with no scenario-specific numbers and a generic structure across all three, do not try to rule on it; flag it for the instructor as something to review under that policy, and score only what is on the page.
>
> **Output format:**
> - **Criterion 1, Decoy identification (Scenario 1, Brewline): __/17** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 1, Decoy identification (Scenario 2, Mercator): __/17** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 1, Decoy identification (Scenario 3, Skybridge): __/16** (why, and say whether the student got the judgment call right that Premium Economy is the target not the decoy) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 2, Use of the chapter's named mechanisms: __/15** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 3, Feasibility of the recommended move: __/15** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 4, Writing clarity: __/10** (why) (one tip)
> - **Criterion 5, Reflection close: __/10** (why) (one tip)
> - **Total: __/100**
> - **One-line summary for the student:** (warm, specific, what to do next time)
>

## Part 3: Reading and trusting the result

- The AI returns scores for each rubric row (with the decoy-identification row broken into three per-scenario scores), a total, and short notes. **Skim the notes first.** They tell you whether the AI actually understood the memo. Check the per-scenario notes on Criterion 1, because that is where most of the points sit and where a careless read shows up first.
- **You can push back.** Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example *"The student called Premium Economy a decoy in Scenario 3 but Premium Economy is not dominated by any other cabin; should Criterion 1 for Scenario 3 be lower?"* or *"The student tied the Brewline read to the per-ounce math and the seventy-one percent Grande share; look again at Scenario 1."* The AI will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- **The Scenario 3 read is the one to double-check yourself, and it is fast.** Look at how the student handled Premium Economy. If they called it a decoy without ever applying the dominance test, the Scenario 3 score drops. If they correctly identified it as the target middle option and named extremity aversion (or anchoring) as the mechanism, the score holds.
- **The vocabulary row is the next one to double-check.** Read the three sections and tally the distinct chapter labels the student used (decoy effect, reference pricing, extremity aversion, prestige, charm, anchoring, good-better-best, bundle, multiple-unit). Four or more distinct mechanisms across the three scenarios is the bar for full marks. Three or fewer drops the row.
- **The fastest sanity check on the strategy-fit row needs no AI.** For each scenario, look at the decoy and target the student named and ask whether the justification names at least one number or one piece of split data from that scenario specifically. A justification for Brewline that does not mention the per-ounce math or the seventy-one percent Grande share is generic; a justification for Mercator that does not mention the thirty-dollar gap or the fourteen percent Pro share is generic; a justification for Skybridge that does not mention the two thousand eight hundred fifty dollar cabin gap or the corporate-account skew on Business is generic. The grading guide spells this out.
- **Keep a fresh chat per student** so submissions do not blur together.
- Remember the framing for any feedback you pass on: *the instructor grades; the AI assists.* The score is yours.

Graded individual assignment for Chapter 2 (What Marketing Is), mapped to the chapter's learning objectives and AACSB outcomes.

Read a Brand's Five Decisions with AI

Chapter 2 individual assignment. Pick a real brand you can look up, use an AI assistant to map its five marketing decisions from public information, and then do the part that earns the grade: judge where the AI actually knew something and where it guessed, and name the one decision that fights the other four. You direct the AI; you out-judge it. Done alone, in about two hours.

Chapter: 2 — What Marketing Is &middot; Type: Individual (graded, solo) &middot; Time: about 2 hours &middot; Length: about one and a half pages plus your AI transcript.

What you will do (the short version)

Every product you have ever bought came from a company making five decisions that all have to agree: what to make, who to serve, what to charge, where to sell it, and how to talk about it. You will pick one real brand, use an AI assistant to fill in those five from public information, check what it told you, and then point at the single decision that, if it changed, would break the other four. You hand in the five-decision map, a short judgment pass, and your AI transcript.

Learning objectives this assignment assesses

These are the Chapter 2 objectives the work exercises. The rubric scores against them.

  • LO 2.1 Define marketing as an exchange, the voluntary trade where each side gives up something and gets something it values more.
  • LO 2.2 Name the five marketing decisions and explain why they multiply rather than add, so one weak decision sinks the other four.
  • LO 2.6 Use the four-part positioning statement to say who a brand is for and the one benefit it leads with.
  • LO 2.9 Evaluate AI output about a brand's marketing, say which decision the AI knew versus guessed, and flag what it left out.

Pick the brand

One real, public brand you can look up in a few minutes: something you actually use, a local or campus business, or a brand with a clear story online. Two rules keep this honest and finishable:

  • Pick one specific brand or product line, not a giant company. "Crumbl Cookies" or "the Owala water bottle" works. "Amazon" or "Nike" turns into a book report. If you love a big brand, scope down to one product line, not "PepsiCo" but "Gatorade," not "Disney" but "Disney+."
  • Use public information only. Everything you need is on the brand's website, its About page, its social posts, and a couple of news stories. Do not use private sales data or anything you would not want public.

The chapter's brands are your worked models, not your answer. Chapter 2 reads the five decisions on Patagonia (premium gear, a buyer who hates waste, few stores, field-story ads, all pointing the same way for forty years), Costco (where a $65 membership fee is the engine that funds near-cost prices), Liquid Death (a heavy-metal water can aimed at concertgoers who want to skip alcohol without looking like it), and Bud Light (twenty years of coherent decisions, then one promotion aimed at a buyer the other four were not serving, and the whole thing cracked). Use these as the shape of good work. Pick your own brand.

Map the five decisions with AI

Open an AI assistant (Claude or ChatGPT) and run the move Chapter 2 teaches: ask the sharp way, not the vague way. The chapter's §2.2 widget shows the difference on the same model for the same cost. A vague ask ("How should this brand market itself?") returns mush. A sharp ask names the five decisions and the one that breaks the rest.

  1. Fill the five slots. Paste the brand's name (and its website or About page if you can) and ask: "Read this brand's five marketing decisions, one line each: what it makes, who it serves, what it charges, where it sells, and how it talks about itself. Use only public information and tell me when you are unsure." You direct it; it drafts. Every line it returns is a claim to check, not a fact to keep.
  2. Write the positioning slot in plain form. Have it state who the brand is for and the one benefit it leads with, in the chapter's four-part shape: for [a target buyer] who [wants something], [brand] is the [kind of product] that [delivers one benefit]. If the AI fills the buyer slot with "everyone," that is a flag, not a finish.
  3. Ask it to find the conflict, not pile on tactics. Run the chapter's AI-Misfire question: "Of these five decisions, which one would break the other four if it changed, and which two (if any) already contradict each other?" Make it find the weak slot instead of suggesting a louder campaign.

The graded core: where you out-judged the AI

The mapping takes the model a minute. Most of your grade is the judgment pass: read back through what it gave you and write, in your own words,

  • Where it knew versus where it guessed. Mark at least two lines you could verify against the real website or a news story (a real price, a real channel, a real buyer the brand actually names), and at least one line the AI guessed or made up, an invented "fact," a competitor that does not compete, a buyer the brand does not serve. Models state guesses in the same confident voice as facts, so the checking is the skill. Correct what was wrong.
  • The one decision that fights the others. Name the single decision that, if it changed, would break the other four, and say in two or three sentences why. This is the chapter's whole point: the five decisions multiply, so one wrong slot sinks the rest. If two slots already contradict each other, name that instead, that is the brand's risk.
  • What the AI left out. Say one thing the map does not capture that matters for this brand: the relationship the brand has with its buyer, the value it really leads with (does it sell money saved, performance, an experience, or belonging?), or a decision the AI flattened into a cliche.

Then write a one-line takeaway in your own voice: in one sentence, what holds this brand together, or what is about to break it. That call is yours, not the model's.

Hand in

One document, about a page and a half, plus the transcript:

  1. The five-decision map. Five labeled lines (what / who / charge / where / talk), each with your one verified detail, plus the one-sentence positioning statement.
  2. The judgment pass. The two-plus things you verified, the one thing the AI guessed and how you corrected it, the one decision that breaks the other four, and the one thing the map leaves out.
  3. Your one-line takeaway. What holds the brand together, or what is about to break it.
  4. The AI transcript and your AI-Use Note. The prompts you used and the model's answers, with your corrections marked. This is your disclosure, and it is graded.

Difficulty tiers (same task, three on-ramps)

Pick the row that matches what you have. The deliverable and the rubric are the same for all three; only how you get the AI output changes.

  • Tier 1 — No AI account needed (paper-proof). You do not need to sign up for anything. We hand you a pre-generated AI five-decision map for a sample brand (one with a real error baked in) attached to this assignment. Your whole job is the judgment pass on it: find the line the AI guessed, check it against the real brand's website, correct it, name the decision that breaks the other four, and write your takeaway. This is the most important half of the assignment anyway, and it is fully gradable with no AI.
  • Tier 2 — Free Claude or ChatGPT (the default). Make a free account at claude.ai or chatgpt.com, run the three prompts above on a brand you pick, then do the judgment pass on what it gives you. Paste the chat as your transcript. This is what most of you will do.
  • Tier 3 — Advanced. If you have a paid account or the live in-chapter widget, set up a saved Project named after the brand, load its homepage and About page once, and rerun the five-decision read with live web search so the prices and channels are current. Note in your AI-Use Note which tool you used.

The AI-integration requirement

This is an AI-integrated course, so using AI here is expected, and so is judging it. Chapter 2 advances rung 1 of the AI ladder, "ask well": the same model gives you mush or a diagnosis depending only on how sharply you ask.

What AI use is expected. Use the assistant to draft the five-decision map, the positioning line, and the conflict-finder (the three steps above). The chapter's §2.2 "Read a brand's five decisions" widget is the model for the vague-versus-sharp move; any free assistant runs the same play.

Use AI as a critic, not an oracle. The course rewards the student who makes the model find the weak decision rather than suggest a louder campaign, and who then checks every line. The chapter names the exact failure to watch for: ask an AI to "make my brand more successful" and it piles on tactics (a new product, a new channel, a louder ad) instead of spotting the one decision that is fighting the others. It will also state a guess about a price or a competitor in the same confident voice it uses for a fact. Both are yours to catch. The final call about what holds the brand together is yours.

What you must disclose. Paste your prompts and the model's output into your submission; that paste doubles as your AI-Use Note. The disclosure is graded. Public information only; never paste personal, confidential, or proprietary data into a public AI tool.

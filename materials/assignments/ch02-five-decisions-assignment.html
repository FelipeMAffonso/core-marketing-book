Read a Brand's Five Decisions with AI

Chapter 2 individual assignment. The student steps into the role of a brand's new marketing analyst: pick a real brand, use an AI assistant to map its five marketing decisions from public information, then judge where the AI knew something and where it guessed, and name the one decision that fights the other four. The student directs the AI and out-judges it. Done alone, about two hours.

Build the student handout

Set the two levers for your class. The panel below is the only student-facing part; copy it into your LMS.

Student access
Difficulty Level

Pick one real, public brand you can look up in a few minutes: one you use, a local or campus business, or a brand with a clear story online. Pick one specific brand or product line, not a giant company. "Crumbl Cookies" or "the Owala water bottle" works; "Amazon" or "Nike" turns into a book report. Public information only.

Map its five marketing decisions, one line each: what it makes, who it serves, what it charges, where it sells, and how it talks about itself. Write its positioning in the four-part shape: for a target buyer who wants something, the brand is the kind of product that delivers one benefit. Then name the single decision that, if it changed, would break the other four, and say why in two or three sentences. The five decisions multiply rather than add, so one wrong slot sinks the rest. If two slots already contradict each other, name that instead.

The grade is mostly the judgment pass. Mark at least two lines you verified against the real brand and at least one line that was guessed or made up, and correct it. Then write a one-line takeaway in your own voice: what holds this brand together, or what is about to break it. That call is yours.

one document, about a page and a half, plus your AI transcript: the five-decision map with one verified detail per line, the one-sentence positioning statement, the judgment pass (what you verified, the line the AI guessed and your correction, the decision that breaks the other four), and your one-line takeaway.

You do not need to sign up for anything. We hand you a pre-generated five-decision map for a sample brand, attached to this assignment, with a real error baked in. Your whole job is the judgment pass on it: find the line that was guessed, check it against the real brand's website, correct it, name the decision that would break the other four, and write your one-line takeaway.

Make a free account at claude.ai or chatgpt.com. Paste the brand's name and its About page, then ask it to read the five decisions one line each, write the four-part positioning line, and name the one decision that would break the other four. Every line it returns is a claim to check, not a fact to keep. Do the judgment pass on what it gives you and paste the chat as your transcript.

Use a paid account or the live in-chapter widget. Set up a saved project named after the brand, load its homepage and About page once, and rerun the five-decision read with live web search so the prices and channels are current. Note which tool you used.

Work from the pre-generated map we provide rather than running the tool yourself. Do the judgment pass on it: find the guessed line, check it against the real brand, correct it, and name the decision that would break the other four. You do not have to write the positioning line from scratch; instead, say in one sentence whether the map's buyer is a real, specific person or a vague "everyone."

Map your own brand end to end. Run the three prompts, then hand in the five-decision map with one verified detail per line, the four-part positioning statement, the one decision that breaks the other four, the one line the AI guessed and your correction, and your one-line takeaway.

Do the Medium task, then add rigor. Verify each of the five lines against a real source you name (the website, a news story, a real price page), not just two of them. Add a short written defense: in one paragraph, argue why your named weak decision is the one that would break the brand, the way a skeptical strategist would, and answer the strongest objection to your call.

What this assesses — learning objectives, instructor only▸

These are the Chapter 2 objectives the work exercises. The rubric scores against them.

  • LO 2.1 Define marketing as an exchange, the voluntary trade where each side gives up something and gets something it values more.
  • LO 2.2 Name the five marketing decisions and explain why they multiply rather than add, so one weak decision sinks the other four.
  • LO 2.6 Use the four-part positioning statement to say who a brand is for and the one benefit it leads with.
  • LO 2.9 Evaluate AI output about a brand's marketing, say which decision the AI knew versus guessed, and flag what it left out.

The AI step trains the chapter's tested misfire. On a brand question, a current assistant states a guess about a brand fact, a price, or a competitor in the same confident voice it uses for something it actually knows, and it caves to a leading question, agreeing with whatever framing the student implies rather than pushing back. Both are the student's to catch; the chapter's running brands (Patagonia, Costco, Liquid Death, Bud Light) are the worked models, not the answer.

The judgment-pass row is the heaviest on purpose: the AI maps the brand in seconds, so the graded skill is catching the line it got wrong and naming the decision it could not see. A clear, honest account of how the student used AI and what they caught scores well. A hidden use that is discovered is an integrity violation, not a deduction.

Critique an AI Pricing Answer

Chapter 10 individual assignment. The student needs no AI account. We hand over a pre-generated pricing answer for a sample product with a real error baked in, and the whole job is the judgment pass: run the arithmetic, catch the markup the model labeled a margin, check the competitor prices it quoted, and set the price you would actually charge. The 2026 pricing skill is not doing the math by hand, it is out-judging the analysis a tool hands you. Done alone.

Student instructions

You do not need to sign up for anything. We hand you a pre-generated pricing answer for a sample product, the Foundry Goods leather laptop sleeve, attached to this assignment as the Tier-1 sample. It reads like a confident analyst wrote it, and it has a real error baked in. Your whole job is the judgment pass on it.

Read the answer, then check it three ways. Run the arithmetic yourself and find the line that reports a markup as if it were a margin: the model was asked for a 40% margin on a $40 cost, and you should be able to show what a true 40% margin price actually is and what margin the price it gave really earns. Check every competitor price it quotes against a real shelf or store page, and mark each one confirmed or made up. Then look at the fairness verdict and decide whether it still holds once the arithmetic is corrected.

Set the price you would actually charge and explain what you changed. You do not have to run a willingness-to-pay survey. Instead, in two sentences, say what evidence would tell you whether your price sits in a band buyers would accept.

Submit: one short document, about a page, plus the corrections marked on the sample: the markup-versus-margin line you caught with the corrected arithmetic, each competitor price marked confirmed or made up, your read on whether the fairness claim still holds, your final price with a one-line reason, and the two-sentence note on what evidence would confirm the band.

    Grading Guide — Price a Real Product with AI

    Chapter 10 individual assignment. This guide is for whoever grades the work: you by hand, a TA, or an AI you direct. It says what the assignment is really testing, what good and weak work look like, the failure modes to watch for, and how to turn the rubric into a fair score fast. The companion ch10-pricing-grading-skill.md turns this same rubric into something you can paste into a free Claude account if you want a first-pass score; this guide stands on its own without it.

    What this assignment is really testing

    Not whether the student can compute a breakeven, the test bank already checks that. This assignment tests whether a student can direct an AI to price a real product and then out-judge it: catch the figure the model guessed, catch the markup it reported as a margin, and set a final price they can defend. That second half is the whole point. A submission with a beautiful value stick that takes every AI number at face value has missed the assignment. A submission with a rougher stick but a sharp judgment pass that caught a real error has hit it.

    Keep the chapter's core idea in front of you while grading: price is the only mix element that creates revenue, and a small price move swings profit hard. So the arithmetic has to be right, and the student has to know which number is which. The single most common AI failure here is the one the chapter names: the model does the arithmetic by guessing and reports a markup as if it were a margin, and it states a competitor price it invented in the same confident voice it uses for a real one. Catching that is the graded skill.

    The three ways to grade (your choice)

    You own the grade. Pick the path that fits your section and your time.

    1. By hand. Read the submission against the six rubric rows. Budget about ten minutes each. The score bands below let you land a number quickly. This is the most reliable path and the one to use for any borderline or flagged paper. Keep a calculator handy for one move: re-run the markup and the margin on the student's own price and confirm the two numbers differ and are labeled correctly.
    2. Hand it to TAs. Give your TAs this guide and the rubric. Calibrate first: grade three papers together (a strong, a middling, a weak one) so everyone anchors the bands the same way. The most common TA drift is over-rewarding a tidy value stick and under-rewarding a messy-but-real judgment pass, the opposite of what this assignment values, so calibrate on that specifically. The second drift is letting a confident-sounding number slide without checking the markup-versus-margin label.
    3. Use the grading skill (optional). If you want a fast first pass, open ch10-pricing-grading-skill.md, follow its click-by-click walkthrough, and let an AI return per-criterion scores and short feedback. Treat that as a draft a human signs off on, never the final grade. It is a time-saver, not a grader of record.

    All three use the same rubric, so a student gets the same standard whichever path you pick.

    What to look for, row by row

    Judgment pass: caught what the AI got wrong (28 pts) — the heaviest row, grade it first.

    • Strong: the student caught a real AI error and corrected it. The three errors to watch for, in order of how reliably the model makes them: (1) a competitor price the model invented and stated as fact (it does this on almost every run), (2) a markup reported as a margin (the model returns cost × 1.40 and calls it a 40% margin; an intermittent slip that surfaces inside a long multi-part prompt), (3) a breakeven or contribution-margin figure the model fumbled. The student names the line, shows the right arithmetic or the real price, and sets a final price.
    • Look for the catch. The single best signal of a strong paper is a named, corrected AI error. "The AI said a 40% margin on my $40 cost meant a $56 price, but that is the markup. The real margin price is $40 / (1 − 0.40) = $66.67, so I raised it" is the assignment working.
    • Weak: "The AI did all the math and it looked right, so I used its price." No verification, no catch. That is the Developing/Not-yet band no matter how polished the rest is.

    Value stick (18 pts).

    • Strong: floor (rough unit cost), frame (two or three named rivals' real current prices), and ceiling (the most a rival's buyer would pay) all named with verified public evidence, and the student's price placed on the stick with the margin and the surplus explained.
    • Weak: the ceiling is asserted with no buyer behind it, or the frame leans on one rival, or floor and ceiling are confused. A stick with no real ceiling caps this row at Developing.

    Approach and code arithmetic (18 pts).

    • Strong: the pricing approach fits the product, and the contribution margin, the breakeven, and both the markup and the margin are correct and were run as code, not typed from the model's head. Markup and margin are clearly different numbers.
    • Weak: an approach chosen by reflex, or a clear arithmetic error, especially a markup reported as a margin. That specific error caps this row at Developing even if everything else is clean, because it is the chapter's headline mistake.

    Willingness to pay (14 pts).

    • Strong: Van Westendorp's four questions are correct, five or more real people answered, the acceptable band is read off the crossings, and the student says whether the price sits inside it with a next step.
    • Weak: questions asked but the band is not read from the data, or fewer than five respondents. No WTP evidence is Not-yet.

    Fairness check (12 pts).

    • Strong: the dual-entitlement principle applied correctly to this product (a cost story reads as fair, a demand windfall reads as gouging), a judgment about category tolerance, and a clear statement of whether the price clears the fairness ceiling.
    • Weak: fairness mentioned without the dual-entitlement logic applied. Cap at Developing.

    Defense and writing (10 pts).

    • Strong: one paragraph that ties the approach, the stick, the WTP band, and the fairness read into an argument a founder or a finance team could act on, in plain language with correct terms.
    • Weak: the parts restated without being connected, or muddy terms (calling a markup a margin in the prose too).

    Common failure modes (and how to score them)

    • The face-value paper. Prices the product fluently, no checking, no catch, kept every number the AI typed. The most common miss. Judgment-pass row lands in Developing or Not-yet; the value-stick and arithmetic rows can still earn points if the work is specific, but re-run the arithmetic yourself before you trust it. Net: usually low-to-mid range.
    • The markup-margin slip the student did not catch. The submission reports a markup as a margin and never notices. This is the exact AI Misfire the chapter trains. Score the arithmetic row down and, more importantly, score the judgment row down, because catching this was the point. If the student caught it in the AI's output and fixed it, that is the opposite, a strong paper.
    • The invented competitor price. A confident rival price with no source, presented as fact. If the student labeled it an estimate or marked it unconfirmed, fine. If they kept an AI-quoted price they never checked and it is made up, that belongs in the judgment-pass deduction, it is precisely what they were supposed to catch.
    • The guessed arithmetic. The student let the model type the breakeven and the margin instead of running them as code, and a number is wrong. Score the arithmetic row on whether the numbers are right, and note in feedback that the chapter's rule is to make the model compute, not guess.
    • The sycophancy trap. The student asked "is my $X a good price?" got a yes, and stopped. The assignment asked them to also run the neutral "argue against this price like a skeptical CFO" and say which they trusted. A paper that only ran the leading ask misses the judgment row's full credit.
    • The hidden AI use. No AI-Use Note and no transcript, yet the prose reads like an unedited model dump. This is an integrity matter, not a grading deduction, follow your institution's process. Disclosed AI use, even heavy use, is never the violation; hiding it is.

    Score bands (for a fast, fair landing)

    Use these to convert the six rows into a defensible total.

    • 90–100 (Excellent). A complete value stick with a real ceiling, correct code-run arithmetic with markup and margin clearly distinct, a WTP band read off real responses, a sound fairness read, and a real caught-and-corrected AI error. The student clearly out-judged the AI and owns the final price.
    • 80–89 (Strong/Proficient). Solid stick and arithmetic and a genuine judgment pass with at least one correction, but one row is thin, the WTP band is read loosely, or the fairness point is shallow.
    • 70–79 (Developing). The work is present but the student mostly trusted the AI: a tidy stick with little checking, arithmetic that is mostly right but with a markup-margin wobble, or no real catch. The judgment half is underdone.
    • 60–69 (Weak). Face-value AI output with no catch, a markup reported as a margin and never noticed, or a value stick with no real ceiling. The assignment's core skill is missing.
    • Below 60 / integrity flag. No disclosure and an apparent unedited AI dump, a missing deliverable, or a product with no real public cost or competitors to reason about. Handle disclosure issues through your integrity process, not the rubric.

    A note for graders on the AI itself

    If you run any student's transcript yourself, expect the model to be confidently wrong about specific numbers: a markup it calls a margin, a competitor price that does not exist, a breakeven that ignored a cost the student gave it. That is not a reason to distrust the student; it is the exact thing the assignment trains them to catch. Reward the catch. The fastest way to verify a paper is to re-run the markup and the margin on the student's own price: markup is (price − cost) / cost, margin is (price − cost) / price, and they are never the same number. A student who noticed the AI mixed them up and fixed it has done more pricing thinking than one whose AI happened to be right and who checked nothing.

    Tier 1 Pre-Generated AI Sample — Price a Real Product

    Assignment: Price a Real Product with AI

    This is the pre-generated AI output for Tier 1 students. The student is pricing a real product they would launch: a full-grain leather laptop sleeve sold direct-to-consumer under the made-up campus brand "Foundry Goods." The model was given this exact prompt:

    "I am launching a full-grain leather 14-inch laptop sleeve under my brand Foundry Goods, sold direct-to-consumer on my own website. My unit cost is $40 (leather, hardware, cut-and-sew labor, packaging). Web-search the floor, the frame (what two or three named competitors charge now), and the ceiling (the most a competitor's buyer would pay). Then I want a 40% margin on my $40 cost, so tell me the price. Give me the markup and the margin on that price, the contribution margin, and the breakeven against $9,000 of fixed cost for the launch. Finally, tell me whether my price is fair."

    The AI's Response

    The value stick for your leather sleeve:

    • Floor (your cost): $40 per unit, as you gave me. That is your hard floor; selling below it loses money on every sleeve.
    • Frame (what competitors charge): Full-grain leather laptop sleeves in the direct-to-consumer space cluster between roughly $65 and $120. Three named comparisons: Bellroy lists its leather laptop sleeve at $89, Harber London sells a comparable full-grain sleeve at $99, and Cuyana offers a leather laptop case at $78. Your product sits in premium-handmade territory, above mass-market neoprene but below luxury fashion houses.
    • Ceiling (the most a buyer would pay): A buyer who already shops Bellroy or Harber London for a full-grain sleeve will pay up to about $110–$120 for a comparable handmade product with a strong brand story, especially if the leather and stitching read as higher quality in photos.

    Your price at a 40% margin: You asked for a 40% margin on your $40 cost. That gives you a price of $56.00 ($40 cost plus 40% = $40 × 1.40 = $56). This sits comfortably below your competitors, which gives you room to win on price while still earning a healthy 40% margin.

    Markup and margin on the $56 price:

    • Margin: 40%. You are earning a 40% margin on every sleeve.
    • Markup: 40%. The markup is also 40%, since you added 40% to your cost.

    Contribution margin: Price minus variable cost = $56 − $40 = $16 per sleeve.

    Breakeven against $9,000 of fixed cost: $9,000 / $16 = 562.5, so 563 sleeves to cover your launch costs.

    Is your price fair? Yes. At $56 you are well below the $78–$99 competitors charge, so buyers are getting premium full-grain leather at a noticeably lower price. A cost-based 40% margin is a transparent, defensible way to price, and undercutting the frame this much makes the value obvious. This is a fair and competitive price.

    Instructor Key — Planted Errors and Grading Guide

    This section is for instructor use only. Do not share with students before they submit.

    Planted error 1: A markup labeled as a margin (the headline error)

    The claim: "You asked for a 40% margin on your $40 cost. That gives you a price of $56.00 ($40 cost plus 40% = $40 × 1.40 = $56)... You are earning a 40% margin on every sleeve."

    Why it is wrong: This is the exact AI Misfire Chapter 10 trains, and it is the most important thing a Tier 1 student must catch. The student asked for a 40% margin, but the model computed a 40% markup and then labeled it a margin. The two are not the same:

    • Markup is measured against cost: a 40% markup on a $40 cost is $40 × 1.40 = $56. The margin on a $56 price is only ($56 − $40) / $56 = 28.6%, not 40%.
    • Margin is measured against price: a true 40% margin on a $40 cost requires a price of $40 / (1 − 0.40) = $66.67. At $66.67 the markup is ($66.67 − $40) / $40 = 66.7%.

    So the model's own claim that "the markup is also 40%, the margin is also 40%" is impossible. Markup and margin are never equal (except at a zero markup). The $56 price the AI produced earns a 28.6% margin, well short of the 40% the student wanted. To actually hit a 40% margin, the price must be $66.67.

    The type of error: Arithmetic-by-guessing plus a definitional mix-up, stated with total confidence. The model added a percentage to cost (a markup) and called the result a margin, the single most common pricing mistake the chapter names.

    What a student should write: Something like "The AI gave me $56 and called it a 40% margin, but that is a 40% markup. The margin on $56 is only 28.6%. To get a real 40% margin on my $40 cost I need $40 / (1 − 0.40) = $66.67. I re-ran both numbers as a quick calculation: at $66.67 the markup is 66.7% and the margin is 40%. I am charging $66.67, not $56."

    Planted error 2: An invented competitor price stated as fact (Where it sells / the frame)

    The claim: "Cuyana offers a leather laptop case at $78."

    Why it is wrong: This is the second AI Misfire the chapter documents: the model states a competitor price it has made up in the same confident voice it uses for a real one. The specific dollar figure attached to a named brand is presented as a verified fact, but it is not anchored to anything the student checked, and a quick look at the named brand's site will not confirm that exact product at that exact price. (Brand line-ups and prices change; the model is filling a plausible-looking number into a named slot.) The lesson is not which brand is "right", it is that every competitor price the AI quotes is a claim to verify against the real site before it goes in the value-stick frame. A student who keeps "$78 at Cuyana" without checking has done exactly what the assignment warns against.

    The type of error: Fabricated competitor price stated as verified fact, indistinguishable in the model's voice from a real one.

    What a student should write: Something like "I checked each competitor price the AI quoted against the brand's actual website. Two I could roughly confirm in the right range; the Cuyana price the AI stated did not match what I found, so I marked it unconfirmed and dropped it from my frame. I only kept the prices I verified, and I labeled the rest as estimates."

    Note on the fairness claim (a softer flag, worth credit)

    The AI's "this price is fair because it undercuts competitors" is shallow and a sharp student may push on it. Fairness in the chapter is the dual-entitlement principle (a cost-based price reads as fair; a demand windfall reads as gouging), not "cheaper than rivals." More to the point, the fairness verdict rests on the wrong price: once the student corrects the margin to $66.67, the product no longer sits far below the frame, so the entire "obvious value by undercutting" story collapses. A student who notices that the fairness claim depends on the broken arithmetic is doing exactly the structural judgment the assignment rewards.

    What Tier 1 students must demonstrate to earn full marks

    For the judgment-pass row (28 points, the heaviest):

    • Excellent: The student catches planted error 1 (the markup labeled a margin), shows the correct arithmetic (true 40% margin price = $66.67, and the $56 price is really a 28.6% margin), and ideally also flags planted error 2 (the unverified competitor price) by marking prices confirmed versus estimated. Setting a corrected final price seals it.
    • Proficient: The student senses something is off with the margin or one competitor price but does not fully run the corrected arithmetic or fully verify the price.
    • Developing: The student notes the analysis is "mostly good" without catching either planted error.
    • Not yet: No judgment pass, or the AI output is handed in as the student's own verified work (kept the $56 price and the 40%-margin label).

    For the approach-and-arithmetic row (18 points): full marks require the student to compute markup and margin correctly and as distinct numbers. A student who repeats the AI's "markup = margin = 40%" without catching it caps this row low; the chapter's headline mistake is exactly this conflation.

    For the value-stick row (18 points): the frame should keep only the competitor prices the student verified. Keeping the fabricated $78 unchecked is the row's main failure mode here.

    For the fairness row (12 points): credit a student who applies the dual-entitlement principle correctly and, better still, notices that the AI's fairness verdict was built on the broken $56 price.

    How the AI transcript for Tier 1 works

    Tier 1 students do not generate their own transcript. This document is their transcript. Their AI-Use Note should read something like: "I used the pre-generated AI output provided for Tier 1. I found that the AI computed a 40% markup ($56) and labeled it a 40% margin; the real 40% margin price is $66.67, so I corrected it and re-ran the math. I also checked the competitor prices, marked which I could confirm, and dropped the one I could not verify. The AI's fairness claim depended on the wrong price, so I rewrote it." A note that just says "I used the Tier 1 sample" with no judgment earns a Developing or Not Yet on disclosure.

    Grading Guide — Price a Real Product with AI

    Chapter 10 individual assignment. This guide is for whoever grades the work: you by hand, a TA, or an AI you direct. It says what the assignment is really testing, what good and weak work look like, the failure modes to watch for, and how to turn the rubric into a fair score fast. The companion ch10-pricing-grading-skill.md turns this same rubric into something you can paste into a free Claude account if you want a first-pass score; this guide stands on its own without it.

    What this assignment is really testing

    Not whether the student can compute a breakeven, the test bank already checks that. This assignment tests whether a student can direct an AI to price a real product and then out-judge it: catch the figure the model guessed, catch the markup it reported as a margin, and set a final price they can defend. That second half is the whole point. A submission with a beautiful value stick that takes every AI number at face value has missed the assignment. A submission with a rougher stick but a sharp judgment pass that caught a real error has hit it.

    Keep the chapter's core idea in front of you while grading: price is the only mix element that creates revenue, and a small price move swings profit hard. So the arithmetic has to be right, and the student has to know which number is which. The single most common AI failure here is the one the chapter names: the model does the arithmetic by guessing and reports a markup as if it were a margin, and it states a competitor price it invented in the same confident voice it uses for a real one. Catching that is the graded skill.

    The three ways to grade (your choice)

    You own the grade. Pick the path that fits your section and your time.

    1. By hand. Read the submission against the six rubric rows. Budget about ten minutes each. The score bands below let you land a number quickly. This is the most reliable path and the one to use for any borderline or flagged paper. Keep a calculator handy for one move: re-run the markup and the margin on the student's own price and confirm the two numbers differ and are labeled correctly.
    2. Hand it to TAs. Give your TAs this guide and the rubric. Calibrate first: grade three papers together (a strong, a middling, a weak one) so everyone anchors the bands the same way. The most common TA drift is over-rewarding a tidy value stick and under-rewarding a messy-but-real judgment pass, the opposite of what this assignment values, so calibrate on that specifically. The second drift is letting a confident-sounding number slide without checking the markup-versus-margin label.
    3. Use the grading skill (optional). If you want a fast first pass, open ch10-pricing-grading-skill.md, follow its click-by-click walkthrough, and let an AI return per-criterion scores and short feedback. Treat that as a draft a human signs off on, never the final grade. It is a time-saver, not a grader of record.

    All three use the same rubric, so a student gets the same standard whichever path you pick.

    What to look for, row by row

    Judgment pass: caught what the AI got wrong (28 pts) — the heaviest row, grade it first.

    • Strong: the student caught a real AI error and corrected it. The three errors to watch for, in order of how reliably the model makes them: (1) a competitor price the model invented and stated as fact (it does this on almost every run), (2) a markup reported as a margin (the model returns cost × 1.40 and calls it a 40% margin; an intermittent slip that surfaces inside a long multi-part prompt), (3) a breakeven or contribution-margin figure the model fumbled. The student names the line, shows the right arithmetic or the real price, and sets a final price.
    • Look for the catch. The single best signal of a strong paper is a named, corrected AI error. "The AI said a 40% margin on my $40 cost meant a $56 price, but that is the markup. The real margin price is $40 / (1 − 0.40) = $66.67, so I raised it" is the assignment working.
    • Weak: "The AI did all the math and it looked right, so I used its price." No verification, no catch. That is the Developing/Not-yet band no matter how polished the rest is.

    Value stick (18 pts).

    • Strong: floor (rough unit cost), frame (two or three named rivals' real current prices), and ceiling (the most a rival's buyer would pay) all named with verified public evidence, and the student's price placed on the stick with the margin and the surplus explained.
    • Weak: the ceiling is asserted with no buyer behind it, or the frame leans on one rival, or floor and ceiling are confused. A stick with no real ceiling caps this row at Developing.

    Approach and code arithmetic (18 pts).

    • Strong: the pricing approach fits the product, and the contribution margin, the breakeven, and both the markup and the margin are correct and were run as code, not typed from the model's head. Markup and margin are clearly different numbers.
    • Weak: an approach chosen by reflex, or a clear arithmetic error, especially a markup reported as a margin. That specific error caps this row at Developing even if everything else is clean, because it is the chapter's headline mistake.

    Willingness to pay (14 pts).

    • Strong: Van Westendorp's four questions are correct, five or more real people answered, the acceptable band is read off the crossings, and the student says whether the price sits inside it with a next step.
    • Weak: questions asked but the band is not read from the data, or fewer than five respondents. No WTP evidence is Not-yet.

    Fairness check (12 pts).

    • Strong: the dual-entitlement principle applied correctly to this product (a cost story reads as fair, a demand windfall reads as gouging), a judgment about category tolerance, and a clear statement of whether the price clears the fairness ceiling.
    • Weak: fairness mentioned without the dual-entitlement logic applied. Cap at Developing.

    Defense and writing (10 pts).

    • Strong: one paragraph that ties the approach, the stick, the WTP band, and the fairness read into an argument a founder or a finance team could act on, in plain language with correct terms.
    • Weak: the parts restated without being connected, or muddy terms (calling a markup a margin in the prose too).

    Common failure modes (and how to score them)

    • The face-value paper. Prices the product fluently, no checking, no catch, kept every number the AI typed. The most common miss. Judgment-pass row lands in Developing or Not-yet; the value-stick and arithmetic rows can still earn points if the work is specific, but re-run the arithmetic yourself before you trust it. Net: usually low-to-mid range.
    • The markup-margin slip the student did not catch. The submission reports a markup as a margin and never notices. This is the exact AI Misfire the chapter trains. Score the arithmetic row down and, more importantly, score the judgment row down, because catching this was the point. If the student caught it in the AI's output and fixed it, that is the opposite, a strong paper.
    • The invented competitor price. A confident rival price with no source, presented as fact. If the student labeled it an estimate or marked it unconfirmed, fine. If they kept an AI-quoted price they never checked and it is made up, that belongs in the judgment-pass deduction, it is precisely what they were supposed to catch.
    • The guessed arithmetic. The student let the model type the breakeven and the margin instead of running them as code, and a number is wrong. Score the arithmetic row on whether the numbers are right, and note in feedback that the chapter's rule is to make the model compute, not guess.
    • The sycophancy trap. The student asked "is my $X a good price?" got a yes, and stopped. The assignment asked them to also run the neutral "argue against this price like a skeptical CFO" and say which they trusted. A paper that only ran the leading ask misses the judgment row's full credit.
    • The hidden AI use. No AI-Use Note and no transcript, yet the prose reads like an unedited model dump. This is an integrity matter, not a grading deduction, follow your institution's process. Disclosed AI use, even heavy use, is never the violation; hiding it is.

    Score bands (for a fast, fair landing)

    Use these to convert the six rows into a defensible total.

    • 90–100 (Excellent). A complete value stick with a real ceiling, correct code-run arithmetic with markup and margin clearly distinct, a WTP band read off real responses, a sound fairness read, and a real caught-and-corrected AI error. The student clearly out-judged the AI and owns the final price.
    • 80–89 (Strong/Proficient). Solid stick and arithmetic and a genuine judgment pass with at least one correction, but one row is thin, the WTP band is read loosely, or the fairness point is shallow.
    • 70–79 (Developing). The work is present but the student mostly trusted the AI: a tidy stick with little checking, arithmetic that is mostly right but with a markup-margin wobble, or no real catch. The judgment half is underdone.
    • 60–69 (Weak). Face-value AI output with no catch, a markup reported as a margin and never noticed, or a value stick with no real ceiling. The assignment's core skill is missing.
    • Below 60 / integrity flag. No disclosure and an apparent unedited AI dump, a missing deliverable, or a product with no real public cost or competitors to reason about. Handle disclosure issues through your integrity process, not the rubric.

    A note for graders on the AI itself

    If you run any student's transcript yourself, expect the model to be confidently wrong about specific numbers: a markup it calls a margin, a competitor price that does not exist, a breakeven that ignored a cost the student gave it. That is not a reason to distrust the student; it is the exact thing the assignment trains them to catch. Reward the catch. The fastest way to verify a paper is to re-run the markup and the margin on the student's own price: markup is (price − cost) / cost, margin is (price − cost) / price, and they are never the same number. A student who noticed the AI mixed them up and fixed it has done more pricing thinking than one whose AI happened to be right and who checked nothing.

    Tier 1 Pre-Generated AI Sample — Price a Real Product

    Assignment: Price a Real Product with AI

    This is the pre-generated AI output for Tier 1 students. The student is pricing a real product they would launch: a full-grain leather laptop sleeve sold direct-to-consumer under the made-up campus brand "Foundry Goods." The model was given this exact prompt:

    "I am launching a full-grain leather 14-inch laptop sleeve under my brand Foundry Goods, sold direct-to-consumer on my own website. My unit cost is $40 (leather, hardware, cut-and-sew labor, packaging). Web-search the floor, the frame (what two or three named competitors charge now), and the ceiling (the most a competitor's buyer would pay). Then I want a 40% margin on my $40 cost, so tell me the price. Give me the markup and the margin on that price, the contribution margin, and the breakeven against $9,000 of fixed cost for the launch. Finally, tell me whether my price is fair."

    The AI's Response

    The value stick for your leather sleeve:

    • Floor (your cost): $40 per unit, as you gave me. That is your hard floor; selling below it loses money on every sleeve.
    • Frame (what competitors charge): Full-grain leather laptop sleeves in the direct-to-consumer space cluster between roughly $65 and $120. Three named comparisons: Bellroy lists its leather laptop sleeve at $89, Harber London sells a comparable full-grain sleeve at $99, and Cuyana offers a leather laptop case at $78. Your product sits in premium-handmade territory, above mass-market neoprene but below luxury fashion houses.
    • Ceiling (the most a buyer would pay): A buyer who already shops Bellroy or Harber London for a full-grain sleeve will pay up to about $110–$120 for a comparable handmade product with a strong brand story, especially if the leather and stitching read as higher quality in photos.

    Your price at a 40% margin: You asked for a 40% margin on your $40 cost. That gives you a price of $56.00 ($40 cost plus 40% = $40 × 1.40 = $56). This sits comfortably below your competitors, which gives you room to win on price while still earning a healthy 40% margin.

    Markup and margin on the $56 price:

    • Margin: 40%. You are earning a 40% margin on every sleeve.
    • Markup: 40%. The markup is also 40%, since you added 40% to your cost.

    Contribution margin: Price minus variable cost = $56 − $40 = $16 per sleeve.

    Breakeven against $9,000 of fixed cost: $9,000 / $16 = 562.5, so 563 sleeves to cover your launch costs.

    Is your price fair? Yes. At $56 you are well below the $78–$99 competitors charge, so buyers are getting premium full-grain leather at a noticeably lower price. A cost-based 40% margin is a transparent, defensible way to price, and undercutting the frame this much makes the value obvious. This is a fair and competitive price.

    Instructor Key — Planted Errors and Grading Guide

    This section is for instructor use only. Do not share with students before they submit.

    Planted error 1: A markup labeled as a margin (the headline error)

    The claim: "You asked for a 40% margin on your $40 cost. That gives you a price of $56.00 ($40 cost plus 40% = $40 × 1.40 = $56)... You are earning a 40% margin on every sleeve."

    Why it is wrong: This is the exact AI Misfire Chapter 10 trains, and it is the most important thing a Tier 1 student must catch. The student asked for a 40% margin, but the model computed a 40% markup and then labeled it a margin. The two are not the same:

    • Markup is measured against cost: a 40% markup on a $40 cost is $40 × 1.40 = $56. The margin on a $56 price is only ($56 − $40) / $56 = 28.6%, not 40%.
    • Margin is measured against price: a true 40% margin on a $40 cost requires a price of $40 / (1 − 0.40) = $66.67. At $66.67 the markup is ($66.67 − $40) / $40 = 66.7%.

    So the model's own claim that "the markup is also 40%, the margin is also 40%" is impossible. Markup and margin are never equal (except at a zero markup). The $56 price the AI produced earns a 28.6% margin, well short of the 40% the student wanted. To actually hit a 40% margin, the price must be $66.67.

    The type of error: Arithmetic-by-guessing plus a definitional mix-up, stated with total confidence. The model added a percentage to cost (a markup) and called the result a margin, the single most common pricing mistake the chapter names.

    What a student should write: Something like "The AI gave me $56 and called it a 40% margin, but that is a 40% markup. The margin on $56 is only 28.6%. To get a real 40% margin on my $40 cost I need $40 / (1 − 0.40) = $66.67. I re-ran both numbers as a quick calculation: at $66.67 the markup is 66.7% and the margin is 40%. I am charging $66.67, not $56."

    Planted error 2: An invented competitor price stated as fact (Where it sells / the frame)

    The claim: "Cuyana offers a leather laptop case at $78."

    Why it is wrong: This is the second AI Misfire the chapter documents: the model states a competitor price it has made up in the same confident voice it uses for a real one. The specific dollar figure attached to a named brand is presented as a verified fact, but it is not anchored to anything the student checked, and a quick look at the named brand's site will not confirm that exact product at that exact price. (Brand line-ups and prices change; the model is filling a plausible-looking number into a named slot.) The lesson is not which brand is "right", it is that every competitor price the AI quotes is a claim to verify against the real site before it goes in the value-stick frame. A student who keeps "$78 at Cuyana" without checking has done exactly what the assignment warns against.

    The type of error: Fabricated competitor price stated as verified fact, indistinguishable in the model's voice from a real one.

    What a student should write: Something like "I checked each competitor price the AI quoted against the brand's actual website. Two I could roughly confirm in the right range; the Cuyana price the AI stated did not match what I found, so I marked it unconfirmed and dropped it from my frame. I only kept the prices I verified, and I labeled the rest as estimates."

    Note on the fairness claim (a softer flag, worth credit)

    The AI's "this price is fair because it undercuts competitors" is shallow and a sharp student may push on it. Fairness in the chapter is the dual-entitlement principle (a cost-based price reads as fair; a demand windfall reads as gouging), not "cheaper than rivals." More to the point, the fairness verdict rests on the wrong price: once the student corrects the margin to $66.67, the product no longer sits far below the frame, so the entire "obvious value by undercutting" story collapses. A student who notices that the fairness claim depends on the broken arithmetic is doing exactly the structural judgment the assignment rewards.

    What Tier 1 students must demonstrate to earn full marks

    For the judgment-pass row (28 points, the heaviest):

    • Excellent: The student catches planted error 1 (the markup labeled a margin), shows the correct arithmetic (true 40% margin price = $66.67, and the $56 price is really a 28.6% margin), and ideally also flags planted error 2 (the unverified competitor price) by marking prices confirmed versus estimated. Setting a corrected final price seals it.
    • Proficient: The student senses something is off with the margin or one competitor price but does not fully run the corrected arithmetic or fully verify the price.
    • Developing: The student notes the analysis is "mostly good" without catching either planted error.
    • Not yet: No judgment pass, or the AI output is handed in as the student's own verified work (kept the $56 price and the 40%-margin label).

    For the approach-and-arithmetic row (18 points): full marks require the student to compute markup and margin correctly and as distinct numbers. A student who repeats the AI's "markup = margin = 40%" without catching it caps this row low; the chapter's headline mistake is exactly this conflation.

    For the value-stick row (18 points): the frame should keep only the competitor prices the student verified. Keeping the fabricated $78 unchecked is the row's main failure mode here.

    For the fairness row (12 points): credit a student who applies the dual-entitlement principle correctly and, better still, notices that the AI's fairness verdict was built on the broken $56 price.

    How the AI transcript for Tier 1 works

    Tier 1 students do not generate their own transcript. This document is their transcript. Their AI-Use Note should read something like: "I used the pre-generated AI output provided for Tier 1. I found that the AI computed a 40% markup ($56) and labeled it a 40% margin; the real 40% margin price is $66.67, so I corrected it and re-ran the math. I also checked the competitor prices, marked which I could confirm, and dropped the one I could not verify. The AI's fairness claim depended on the wrong price, so I rewrote it." A note that just says "I used the Tier 1 sample" with no judgment earns a Developing or Not Yet on disclosure.

    What to look for, row by row

    Judgment pass: caught what the AI got wrong (28 pts) — the heaviest row, grade it first.

    • Strong: the student caught a real AI error and corrected it. The three errors to watch for, in order of how reliably the model makes them: (1) a competitor price the model invented and stated as fact (it does this on almost every run), (2) a markup reported as a margin (the model returns cost × 1.40 and calls it a 40% margin; an intermittent slip that surfaces inside a long multi-part prompt), (3) a breakeven or contribution-margin figure the model fumbled. The student names the line, shows the right arithmetic or the real price, and sets a final price.
    • Look for the catch. The single best signal of a strong paper is a named, corrected AI error. "The AI said a 40% margin on my $40 cost meant a $56 price, but that is the markup. The real margin price is $40 / (1 − 0.40) = $66.67, so I raised it" is the assignment working.
    • Weak: "The AI did all the math and it looked right, so I used its price." No verification, no catch. That is the Developing/Not-yet band no matter how polished the rest is.

    Value stick (18 pts).

    • Strong: floor (rough unit cost), frame (two or three named rivals' real current prices), and ceiling (the most a rival's buyer would pay) all named with verified public evidence, and the student's price placed on the stick with the margin and the surplus explained.
    • Weak: the ceiling is asserted with no buyer behind it, or the frame leans on one rival, or floor and ceiling are confused. A stick with no real ceiling caps this row at Developing.

    Approach and code arithmetic (18 pts).

    • Strong: the pricing approach fits the product, and the contribution margin, the breakeven, and both the markup and the margin are correct and were run as code, not typed from the model's head. Markup and margin are clearly different numbers.
    • Weak: an approach chosen by reflex, or a clear arithmetic error, especially a markup reported as a margin. That specific error caps this row at Developing even if everything else is clean, because it is the chapter's headline mistake.

    Willingness to pay (14 pts).

    • Strong: Van Westendorp's four questions are correct, five or more real people answered, the acceptable band is read off the crossings, and the student says whether the price sits inside it with a next step.
    • Weak: questions asked but the band is not read from the data, or fewer than five respondents. No WTP evidence is Not-yet.

    Fairness check (12 pts).

    • Strong: the dual-entitlement principle applied correctly to this product (a cost story reads as fair, a demand windfall reads as gouging), a judgment about category tolerance, and a clear statement of whether the price clears the fairness ceiling.
    • Weak: fairness mentioned without the dual-entitlement logic applied. Cap at Developing.

    Defense and writing (10 pts).

    • Strong: one paragraph that ties the approach, the stick, the WTP band, and the fairness read into an argument a founder or a finance team could act on, in plain language with correct terms.
    • Weak: the parts restated without being connected, or muddy terms (calling a markup a margin in the prose too).

    Common failure modes (and how to score them)

    • The face-value paper. Prices the product fluently, no checking, no catch, kept every number the AI typed. The most common miss. Judgment-pass row lands in Developing or Not-yet; the value-stick and arithmetic rows can still earn points if the work is specific, but re-run the arithmetic yourself before you trust it. Net: usually low-to-mid range.
    • The markup-margin slip the student did not catch. The submission reports a markup as a margin and never notices. This is the exact AI Misfire the chapter trains. Score the arithmetic row down and, more importantly, score the judgment row down, because catching this was the point. If the student caught it in the AI's output and fixed it, that is the opposite, a strong paper.
    • The invented competitor price. A confident rival price with no source, presented as fact. If the student labeled it an estimate or marked it unconfirmed, fine. If they kept an AI-quoted price they never checked and it is made up, that belongs in the judgment-pass deduction, it is precisely what they were supposed to catch.
    • The guessed arithmetic. The student let the model type the breakeven and the margin instead of running them as code, and a number is wrong. Score the arithmetic row on whether the numbers are right, and note in feedback that the chapter's rule is to make the model compute, not guess.
    • The sycophancy trap. The student asked "is my $X a good price?" got a yes, and stopped. The assignment asked them to also run the neutral "argue against this price like a skeptical CFO" and say which they trusted. A paper that only ran the leading ask misses the judgment row's full credit.
    • The hidden AI use. No AI-Use Note and no transcript, yet the prose reads like an unedited model dump. This is an integrity matter, not a grading deduction, follow your institution's process. Disclosed AI use, even heavy use, is never the violation; hiding it is.

    Score bands (for a fast, fair landing)

    Use these to convert the six rows into a defensible total.

    • 90–100 (Excellent).     • 80–89 (Strong/Proficient). Solid stick and arithmetic and a genuine judgment pass with at least one correction, but one row is thin, the WTP band is read loosely, or the fairness point is shallow.
    • 70–79 (Developing). The work is present but the student mostly trusted the AI: a tidy stick with little checking, arithmetic that is mostly right but with a markup-margin wobble, or no real catch. The judgment half is underdone.
    • 60–69 (Weak). Face-value AI output with no catch, a markup reported as a margin and never noticed, or a value stick with no real ceiling. The assignment's core skill is missing.
    • Below 60 / integrity flag. No disclosure and an apparent unedited AI dump, a missing deliverable, or a product with no real public cost or competitors to reason about. Handle disclosure issues through your integrity process, not the rubric.

    A note for graders on the AI itself

    If you run any student's transcript yourself, expect the model to be confidently wrong about specific numbers: a markup it calls a margin, a competitor price that does not exist, a breakeven that ignored a cost the student gave it. That is not a reason to distrust the student; it is the exact thing the assignment trains them to catch. Reward the catch. The fastest way to verify a paper is to re-run the markup and the margin on the student's own price: markup is (price − cost) / cost, margin is (price − cost) / price, and they are never the same number.     Assignment: Price a Real Product with AI

    This is the pre-generated AI output for Tier 1 students. The student is pricing a real product they would launch: a full-grain leather laptop sleeve sold direct-to-consumer under the made-up campus brand "Foundry Goods." The model was given this exact prompt:

    "I am launching a full-grain leather 14-inch laptop sleeve under my brand Foundry Goods, sold direct-to-consumer on my own website.     The AI's Response

    The value stick for your leather sleeve:

    • Floor (your cost): $40 per unit, as you gave me. That is your hard floor; selling below it loses money on every sleeve.
    • Frame (what competitors charge): Full-grain leather laptop sleeves in the direct-to-consumer space cluster between roughly $65 and $120. Three named comparisons: Bellroy lists its leather laptop sleeve at $89, Harber London sells a comparable full-grain sleeve at $99, and Cuyana offers a leather laptop case at $78. Your product sits in premium-handmade territory, above mass-market neoprene but below luxury fashion houses.
    • Ceiling (the most a buyer would pay): A buyer who already shops Bellroy or Harber London for a full-grain sleeve will pay up to about $110–$120 for a comparable handmade product with a strong brand story, especially if the leather and stitching read as higher quality in photos.

    Your price at a 40% margin: You asked for a 40% margin on your $40 cost. That gives you a price of $56.00 ($40 cost plus 40% = $40 × 1.40 = $56). This sits comfortably below your competitors, which gives you room to win on price while still earning a healthy 40% margin.

    Markup and margin on the $56 price:

    • Margin: 40%. You are earning a 40% margin on every sleeve.
    • Markup: 40%. The markup is also 40%, since you added 40% to your cost.

    Contribution margin: Price minus variable cost = $56 − $40 = $16 per sleeve.

    Breakeven against $9,000 of fixed cost: $9,000 / $16 = 562.5, so 563 sleeves to cover your launch costs.

    Is your price fair? Yes. At $56 you are well below the $78–$99 competitors charge, so buyers are getting premium full-grain leather at a noticeably lower price. A cost-based 40% margin is a transparent, defensible way to price, and undercutting the frame this much makes the value obvious. This is a fair and competitive price.

    Instructor Key — Planted Errors and Grading Guide

    This section is for instructor use only. Do not share with students before they submit.

    Planted error 1: A markup labeled as a margin (the headline error)

    The claim: "You asked for a 40% margin on your $40 cost. That gives you a price of $56.00 ($40 cost plus 40% = $40 × 1.40 = $56)... You are earning a 40% margin on every sleeve."

    Why it is wrong: This is the exact AI Misfire Chapter 10 trains, and it is the most important thing a Tier 1 student must catch. The student asked for a 40% margin, but the model computed a 40% markup and then labeled it a margin. The two are not the same:

    • Markup is measured against cost: a 40% markup on a $40 cost is $40 × 1.40 = $56. The margin on a $56 price is only ($56 − $40) / $56 = 28.6%, not 40%.
    • Margin is measured against price: a true 40% margin on a $40 cost requires a price of $40 / (1 − 0.40) = $66.67. At $66.67 the markup is ($66.67 − $40) / $40 = 66.7%.

    So the model's own claim that "the markup is also 40%, the margin is also 40%" is impossible. Markup and margin are never equal (except at a zero markup). The $56 price the AI produced earns a 28.6% margin, well short of the 40% the student wanted. To actually hit a 40% margin, the price must be $66.67.

    The type of error: Arithmetic-by-guessing plus a definitional mix-up, stated with total confidence. The model added a percentage to cost (a markup) and called the result a margin, the single most common pricing mistake the chapter names.

    What a student should write: Something like "The AI gave me $56 and called it a 40% margin, but that is a 40% markup. The margin on $56 is only 28.6%. To get a real 40% margin on my $40 cost I need $40 / (1 − 0.40) = $66.67. I re-ran both numbers as a quick calculation: at $66.67 the markup is 66.7% and the margin is 40%. I am charging $66.67, not $56."

    Planted error 2: An invented competitor price stated as fact (Where it sells / the frame)

    The claim: "Cuyana offers a leather laptop case at $78."

    Why it is wrong: This is the second AI Misfire the chapter documents: the model states a competitor price it has made up in the same confident voice it uses for a real one. The specific dollar figure attached to a named brand is presented as a verified fact, but it is not anchored to anything the student checked, and a quick look at the named brand's site will not confirm that exact product at that exact price. (Brand line-ups and prices change; the model is filling a plausible-looking number into a named slot.) The lesson is not which brand is "right", it is that every competitor price the AI quotes is a claim to verify against the real site before it goes in the value-stick frame. A student who keeps "$78 at Cuyana" without checking has done exactly what the assignment warns against.

    The type of error: Fabricated competitor price stated as verified fact, indistinguishable in the model's voice from a real one.

    What a student should write: Something like "I checked each competitor price the AI quoted against the brand's actual website. Two I could roughly confirm in the right range; the Cuyana price the AI stated did not match what I found, so I marked it unconfirmed and dropped it from my frame. I only kept the prices I verified, and I labeled the rest as estimates."

    Note on the fairness claim (a softer flag, worth credit)

    The AI's "this price is fair because it undercuts competitors" is shallow and a sharp student may push on it. Fairness in the chapter is the dual-entitlement principle (a cost-based price reads as fair; a demand windfall reads as gouging), not "cheaper than rivals." More to the point, the fairness verdict rests on the wrong price: once the student corrects the margin to $66.67, the product no longer sits far below the frame, so the entire "obvious value by undercutting" story collapses. A student who notices that the fairness claim depends on the broken arithmetic is doing exactly the structural judgment the assignment rewards.

    What Tier 1 students must demonstrate to earn full marks

    For the judgment-pass row (28 points, the heaviest):

    • Excellent: The student catches planted error 1 (the markup labeled a margin), shows the correct arithmetic (true 40% margin price = $66.67, and the $56 price is really a 28.6% margin), and ideally also flags planted error 2 (the unverified competitor price) by marking prices confirmed versus estimated. Setting a corrected final price seals it.
    • Proficient: The student senses something is off with the margin or one competitor price but does not fully run the corrected arithmetic or fully verify the price.
    • Developing: The student notes the analysis is "mostly good" without catching either planted error.
    • Not yet: No judgment pass, or the AI output is handed in as the student's own verified work (kept the $56 price and the 40%-margin label).

    For the approach-and-arithmetic row (18 points): full marks require the student to compute markup and margin correctly and as distinct numbers. A student who repeats the AI's "markup = margin = 40%" without catching it caps this row low; the chapter's headline mistake is exactly this conflation.

    For the value-stick row (18 points): the frame should keep only the competitor prices the student verified. Keeping the fabricated $78 unchecked is the row's main failure mode here.

    For the fairness row (12 points): credit a student who applies the dual-entitlement principle correctly and, better still, notices that the AI's fairness verdict was built on the broken $56 price.

    Assignment: Skim or Penetrate: Judge an AI Launch Call

    The AI's Response

    Skim or penetrate?

    For a campus co-branded launch like this, I recommend a skimming posture: open at a higher price and bring it down later. Here is the reasoning. A signature drink kit is a premium, identity-driven product, and the students who most want to show campus pride will pay a premium to be first with the new tumbler. Launching high lets you capture that willingness to pay before the novelty fades, and you can step the price down for the spring re-order once the early adopters are served.     What competitors charge for a comparable kit:

    Co-branded drink kits in the campus and lifestyle space cluster between roughly $45 and $75.     Margin check:

    • Margin: 40%. You are earning a 40% margin on every kit at $56.
    • Markup: 40%. The markup is also 40%, since you added 40% to your cost.

At $56 you are priced in line with the $52 to $59 comparable kits, and a 40% margin on a real, disclosed cost is a transparent and defensible way to price a launch.     Instructor Key — Planted Errors and Grading Guide

    This section is for instructor use only. Do not share with students before they submit.

    Planted error 1: A markup labeled as a margin (the headline error)

    The claim: "You asked for a 40% margin on your $40 unit cost. That gives you a launch price of $56.00 ($40 cost plus 40% = $40 × 1.40 = $56)... You are earning a 40% margin on every kit at $56."

    Why it is wrong: This is the exact AI Misfire Chapter 10 trains, and it is the most important thing the group must catch. The memo was asked for a 40% margin, but the model computed a 40% markup and then labeled it a margin. The two are not the same:

    • Markup is measured against cost: a 40% markup on a $40 cost is $40 × 1.40 = $56. The margin on a $56 price is only ($56 − $40) / $56 = 28.6%, not 40%.
    • Margin is measured against price: a true 40% margin on a $40 cost requires a price of $40 / (1 − 0.40) = $66.67. At $66.67 the markup is ($66.67 − $40) / $40 = 66.7%.

    So the memo's own claim that "the markup is also 40%, the margin is also 40%" is impossible. Markup and margin are never equal (except at a zero markup). The $56 price the AI produced earns a 28.6% margin, well short of the 40% the union asked for. To actually hit a 40% margin on the $40 kit cost, the launch price must be $66.67.

    The type of error: Arithmetic-by-guessing plus a definitional mix-up, stated with total confidence. The model added a percentage to cost (a markup) and called the result a margin, the single most common pricing mistake the chapter names.

    What a group should write: Something like "The memo gave us $56 and called it a 40% margin, but that is a 40% markup. The margin on $56 is only 28.6%. To get a real 40% margin on the $40 kit cost the union needs $40 / (1 − 0.40) = $66.67. We corrected it: at $66.67 the markup is 66.7% and the margin is 40%. The launch price for a true 40% margin is $66.67, not $56."

    Planted error 2: An invented competitor price stated as fact

    The claim: "Stanley sells a co-branded drink-and-tumbler bundle at $59, and a comparable university-bookstore cold-brew launch kit runs about $52."

    Why it is wrong: This is the second AI Misfire the chapter documents: the model states a competitor price it has made up in the same confident voice it uses for a real one. The specific dollar figures attached to named comparisons are presented as verified facts, but the memo never checked them against anything, and a quick look will not confirm that exact product at that exact price. (Co-branded launch line-ups and prices are one-off and change constantly; the model is filling a plausible-looking number into a named slot.) The lesson is not which number is "right," it is that every competitor price the AI quotes is a claim to verify against a real page before it anchors a launch decision. A group that keeps "$59 at Stanley" without checking has done exactly what the assignment warns against.

    The type of error: Fabricated competitor price stated as verified fact, indistinguishable in the model's voice from a real one.

    What a group should write: Something like "The memo states two competitor prices as fact but verified neither. We could not confirm a Stanley co-branded kit at $59 or a bookstore kit at $52 on a real page, so we marked both as unconfirmed and treated them as estimates, not anchors. Any launch price has to rest on prices we can actually verify."

    Note on the skim-versus-penetrate call (the live debate, worth credit)

    The memo picks skimming, and a sharp group should push on whether that matches the q/p read for this product. A campus co-branded drink is arguably a high-q/p product: every party photo, every group chat, and every tumbler carried across the quad is an imitation channel, so adoption spreads fast through social proof. The chapter's rule is that a high q/p favors penetration (price low to ride the imitation wave and get the tumbler into as many hands as possible), while a low q/p favors skimming. So there is a real, defensible argument that the memo picked the wrong posture, or at least that it asserted skimming without doing the q/p read at all. The group does not have to land on penetration; a group that argues skimming can be right too if it makes the case that the early buyers are a small premium segment. The credit is for tying the posture to the q/p logic rather than picking one by reflex, which is exactly what the memo failed to do.

    Note on the fairness claim (a softer flag, worth credit)

    The memo's "this opening price is fair because it is in line with competitors" is shallow, and a sharp group may push on it. Fairness in the chapter is the dual-entitlement principle (a cost-based price reads as fair; a demand windfall reads as gouging), not "in line with rivals." More to the point, the fairness verdict rests on the wrong price and on unverified competitor numbers: once the group corrects the margin to $66.67 and marks the competitor prices unconfirmed, the "priced right in the band" story no longer stands on anything checked. A group that notices the fairness claim depends on the broken arithmetic and the made-up comparisons is doing exactly the structural judgment the assignment rewards.

Design a Pricing Page That Uses the Decoy

Chapter 10 group assignment. Your team designs a three-tier pricing page where one tier is a deliberate decoy that pushes buyers toward the target, then predicts the buyer split with and without it. The hard part is building a structure that truly satisfies asymmetric dominance and knowing the boundary where the decoy stops working. The AI drafts tiers and prices and estimates the split; the work is checking the benchmarks it invents and resisting the flattering yes it gives a leading question. Done in a group of three to four.

Student instructions

Work in a group of three to four. Pick a product or service that could sell in three tiers: a streaming plan, a campus gym membership, a software tool, or another your team knows well. Public information only; never paste a real employer's confidential pricing data into an AI tool.

As a group, design a three-tier pricing page where the middle or top tier is the target you want buyers to pick, and one tier is a deliberate decoy. The decoy has to be a real decoy: one other tier beats it on every attribute, so the target reads as the obvious win. That is the asymmetric-dominance structure, and a fuzzy version where the decoy is only sometimes worse does not count. Predict the buyer split with the decoy on the page and without it, and tie your prediction to the Economist subscription replication, where a useless print-only middle option moved the split from 68% / 32% to 16% / 0% / 84%. Then name the boundary where the decoy stops working: a target priced too cheap to need a push, or real differentiation between tiers that breaks the dominance.

Here is the AI step. Use a free Claude or ChatGPT account to draft tier names, feature lists, and candidate prices, and to estimate the buyer split. This is where the chapter's tested misfire shows up. First, the model invents competitor tier prices and presents them as real market benchmarks, so verify each tier against a real pricing page and mark it confirmed or made up. Catch at least one price it invented. Second, the model flatters a leading prompt: ask it "this decoy works, right?" and watch it agree, then re-ask it to argue the decoy will fail and report the stronger critique. Your final design has to survive the chapter's dominance-breaks boundary, not the AI's flattery. Name at least one figure your group verified or corrected. That judgment pass is most of the grade.

Each member turns in individually: each member designs their own three-tier pricing page for the same product, with their own three prices, their own feature lists, and their own decoy placement, plus a one-paragraph prediction of the buyer split and why. The group then picks or merges the strongest design into one final page with a written rationale. No two members' pages should be the same.

Submit: one group document plus the AI transcript: the final three-tier page (prices, features, the labeled decoy), the predicted buyer split with and without the decoy and the reasoning, the dominance-breaks boundary named, the verified competitor benchmarks, and the judgment pass (the figure corrected, the leading-versus-neutral prompt difference). Plus each member's own page and split prediction.

Build a Freemium Model in Excel and Stress-Test It

Chapter 10 group assignment. Your team is advising the founder of a freemium product. We hand you one product's monthly cohort inputs, and you use the Claude for Excel add-in to build a 24-month cohort model that writes real formulas into cells, then stress-test it: each member runs one lever, and the group finds which single change does the most for the unit economics. The payoff is the chapter's trap, that a conversion lift paired with one extra point of churn can make a bigger paying cohort worth less per user. The model is the tool's; the judgment and the call are yours. Done in a group of three to four.

Instructor assignment · Ch 10
  • TypeGroup of 3-4
  • Time~4 hrs
  • Points100 pts

Student instructions

Work in a group of three to four. This assignment needs a paid Claude plan and Microsoft Excel: the Claude for Excel add-in (Pro, Max, Team, or Enterprise) reads your open workbook and writes real formulas into cells. There is no free-account path for this one; if your class does not have paid plans, run the free-account group cards, "Run a Van Westendorp Price Survey with AI" or "Audit a Real Dynamic-Pricing Rollout," instead.

Install the Claude for Excel add-in: in Excel, open Home, then Add-ins, then More Add-ins, search "Claude by Anthropic," click Add, and sign in. The sidebar reads your open workbook, writes real formulas into cells, and cites the cells it used (Ctrl+Alt+C on Windows, Ctrl+Option+C on Mac).

Download ch10-freemium-cohort.csv: 24 months of cohort inputs for one freemium product. Each row is a month with its free signups, free-to-trial rate, trial-to-paid rate, monthly churn, price, and customer acquisition cost (CAC). The data is fictional and public; use it freely.

Using the add-in, build a 24-month cohort model that writes real formulas into cells so every number traces back to one. For each month compute the paid base (carry last month's paid users forward, subtract churn, add the new paid users the funnel produced), the lifetime value (LTV) per paid user, the LTV/CAC ratio, CAC payback in months, and monthly recurring revenue (MRR). Add a chart of the paid base over the 24 months so the trajectory is visible.

Then stress-test it. Each member runs one lever in their own copy of the workbook and reads what it does to the LTV/CAC ratio and the month-24 paid base: one member raises conversion, one raises price, one cuts churn, one raises CAC. As a group, compare which single change does the most for the unit economics. The group must also demonstrate the chapter's trap: a 10-point conversion lift paired with one extra point of churn can make the larger paying cohort worth less per user, not more, because LTV per user falls faster than the cohort grows. Show it with the numbers.

Here is where the chapter's tested misfire shows up. Working a multi-step model like this, the Claude for Excel add-in produces it confidently and some of it is wrong. It can report a markup as a margin on the price line, compute LTV without dividing by churn correctly, or treat a conversion lift as a pure win while ignoring the churn that came with it. Your group's job is to catch what it got wrong in the cells, fix the formula, and own the recommendation. Catch and fix at least one formula error and show the corrected cell. That judgment pass is most of the grade.

Each member turns in individually: each member runs one scenario (conversion, price, churn, or CAC) on the shared model in their own copy of the workbook and submits a one-paragraph reading of what their single change did to the LTV/CAC ratio and the month-24 paid base, plus the one formula or framing error they personally caught. The group merges the four scenarios into one recommendation. No two members run the same lever.

Submit: one group PDF plus the workbook (or a shared link): the finished model with formulas visible, a chart of the paid base over 24 months, the four-scenario comparison (which lever wins for LTV/CAC), a demonstration of the conversion-versus-churn trap, a one-page recommendation to the founder, and the AI transcript with the formula error caught and corrected. Plus each member's scenario tab and note.

# Grading Skill: The Online-vs-Store Price Audit

*Chapter 10 individual assignment. This file lets you grade a submission with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste the block below into Claude or ChatGPT, paste the student's report under it, and read back a score for each rubric row with a sentence of feedback. The block already knows what good price captures look like, what the AI usually slips on with this kind of question, and what a defensible price defense reads like. You stay the grader of record; the AI gives a first pass. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom.*

---

## Part 1: If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install or pay for anything. This takes about five minutes the first time, and under two minutes per student after that.

1. Open a web browser and go to **claude.ai** (or **chatgpt.com**, which works the same way).
2. Make a free account: click Sign up, enter your email, follow the steps. A free account is enough.
3. Start a new chat. The box that asks how it can help is where you type.
4. Copy the grader instructions in Part 2 below (everything in the gray box) and paste them into the chat. Do not send yet.
5. Press Enter twice for space, then paste the student's full report underneath, in the same message: the audit table, the AI transcript summary, the four one-paragraph judgments, and the price defense.
6. Send it. The AI replies with a score for each of the four rubric rows, a total out of 100, and a short note per row.
7. Read the score and decide. It is a first draft for you to approve or override; you are the grader of record.
8. Click New chat for the next student, so submissions do not blur together.

The ten-second check you can always run yourself, no AI needed: are the **four prices for the same exact model** (the rubric explicitly tests this), and did the student **catch at least one specific slip on the AI's read** with a quoted sentence (an invented retailer cost, a sale read as a strategy, a wrong approach named by reflex)? Both yes is Excellent on rows 1 and 2.

## Part 2: The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a second-year marketing student's submission for a Chapter 10 price-audit assignment. The student picked one real product, captured four prices for the same exact model across three online retailers (one of them the manufacturer's site, one a major marketplace, one a category specialist) and one brick-and-mortar store they visited in person, asked a free AI assistant to explain the spread and name the pricing approach each seller is using, and judged the AI's reads. Score the student's report against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback. Reward specific catches on the AI's reads and a real price defense over polished writing.
>
> The three AI failures this assignment is built to catch:
>
> 1. Inventing a retailer cost. The assistant will write "this retailer's lower price reflects its lower cost structure" without any source for the retailer's wholesale cost. The strong student catches this and treats the cost claim as an unverified guess.
> 2. Mistaking a promotion for a strategy. If one of the four prices is on a sale and the prompt did not flag the sale clearly, the assistant will read the sale price as the seller's regular strategy. The strong student flags this.
> 3. Naming a wrong pricing approach by reflex. The assistant often calls the cheapest seller "cost-based" without evidence and the most expensive seller "value-based" without evidence. The strong student catches the slip and proposes the actual approach (often competitor-based with a brand-protection wrap, not value-based).
>
> What a good price defense looks like: a specific price set on the spread (above the highest, at the highest, in the middle, at the lowest, below the lowest), one chapter-named approach (cost-based, competitor-based, value-based, or customer-determined), and a dual-entitlement-sound reason that ties the price to the store's positioning rather than a margin grab. A defense that just says "I would charge less than everyone else" without naming the approach earns Developing.
>
> Score these four rows and add them for a total out of 100. For each, give a number, one sentence on why, and one sentence the student could act on.
>
> 1. Four prices captured honestly (28 points). Full marks: all four prices for the same exact model, with screenshots of the three online prices and a photo of the brick-and-mortar shelf tag, dated, regular price separated from any running promotion. Lower if a photo is missing or a promotion is not flagged.
> 2. The AI's read judged on each seller (34 points). Full marks: one-paragraph judgment for each of the four sellers, with at least one specific catch on the AI's read citing the sentence from the assistant being responded to. Lower if judgments are "mostly right" without specifics.
> 3. A price defended on the spread (22 points). Full marks: a specific price on the spread with one chapter-named approach and a dual-entitlement-sound reason that ties to the store's positioning. Lower if the defense names "cheaper is better" without a fairness read.
> 4. Pricing-approach reads on each seller (16 points). Full marks: the student's own one-line read of which approach each of the four sellers is using, tied to a specific signal in the audit. Lower if the reads are stated by reflex.
>
> Output format:
> - Four prices captured: __ /28 (why) (one tip)
> - AI's reads judged: __ /34 (why) (one tip)
> - Price defended: __ /22 (why) (one tip)
> - Approach reads: __ /16 (why) (one tip)
> - Total: __ /100
> - One-line summary for the student
>
> The student's report follows below this line.

## Part 3: Reading and trusting the result

- Skim the notes first; they tell you whether the AI understood the report. The note on the AI-judged row is the heart of the grade, so check that it names whether the student caught at least one specific slip (an invented retailer cost, a sale read as strategy, a wrong approach named by reflex).
- You can push back. Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example "the student did catch the AI calling Amazon's price 'cost-based' without evidence, look again at the AI-judged row," and it will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- The four-prices row is the easiest to double-check yourself: open the audit table and scan whether all four prices are for the same brand, model, and size. If not, that row drops at least one band.
- Keep a fresh chat per student. The grade is yours; the AI only assists.

## Part 4: Grade by hand instead (no AI at all)

Read the report against the rubric below; the full answer key is in `ch10-online-vs-store-price-audit-grading-guide.md`.

| Row | Points | What earns full marks |
|---|---|---|
| Four prices captured | 28 | All four prices for the same exact model with screenshots and an in-store photo, dated, regular price separated from any promotion |
| AI's reads judged | 34 | One-paragraph judgment for each of the four sellers with at least one specific catch citing the assistant's sentence |
| Price defended | 22 | A specific price on the spread, one chapter-named approach, and a dual-entitlement-sound reason tied to positioning |
| Approach reads | 16 | The student's own approach reads on each seller, tied to a specific signal in the audit |
| **Total** | **100** | |

Fast bands: 90 to 100, four prices captured cleanly with at least one specific catch on the AI's reads and a real price defense; 80 to 89, audit strong with one thin spot on the catches or the defense; 70 to 79, four prices captured but the AI's reads accepted on most sellers; 60 to 69 or below, four prices captured for different models, the in-store photo missing, or no catches at all. The single fastest signal of a strong submission is one specific catch on the AI's reads with the assistant's sentence quoted back.

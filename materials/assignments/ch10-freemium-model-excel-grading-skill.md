# Grading Skill: Design the Pricing-Analytics Workflow for a Freemium Product

*Chapter 10 advanced (paid plan) group assignment, group of three to four with a required individual diagram from each member. This file lets you grade a team's submission with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste a block of instructions into Claude, paste the team's work under it, and read back a score for each part of the rubric with a sentence of feedback. You stay in charge: the AI gives you a first-pass score, and you decide the grade. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom in case you would rather not use AI at all. The ten-minute walkthrough or recorded oral defense is the layer you grade yourself, since the integrity decisions only land when the team explains them out loud. You do not need Excel or a paid plan to grade; the grader block carries the formulas and the answer key.*

## Part 1: If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install anything or pay anything to grade this. (The team needed a paid Claude plan and Excel to build the cohort model; you only need a free account to score the written submission.) This takes about five minutes the first time and about three minutes per team after that.

1. **Open a web browser** (Chrome, Safari, Edge, whichever you use) and go to **claude.ai**. ChatGPT at **chatgpt.com** works the same way if you prefer it.
2. **Make a free account.** Click Sign up, enter your email, and follow the steps. A free account is enough for grading. You will not be charged.
3. **Start a new chat.** After you log in you will see a box that says something like "How can I help you today?" That box is where you type. A "chat" is just a conversation with the AI, like texting.
4. **Copy the grader instructions.** In Part 2 below, everything inside the gray box is one block. Select it all, copy it, and paste it into the chat box. Do not press send yet.
5. **Add the team's work underneath.** Press Enter twice to make space, then paste the team's full written submission below the instructions in the same message. That includes the one-page workflow diagram description (or a screenshot of the diagram if it was hand-drawn), the 500-word workflow specification, the LLM-as-judge prompt the team wrote out, the one-page recommendation memo, the four individual member diagrams, the disclosure of which AI tools the team used, and any screenshots of the Excel workbook the team provided. If the team submitted the workbook as a separate file, paste the visible cells of the cohort tab and the conversion-versus-churn scenario tab as text.
6. **Send it.** Press Enter (or click the arrow). The AI will read both and reply with a score for each of the four rubric rows, a total out of 100, and a short note for each row.
7. **Read the score and decide.** The AI's score is a first draft for you to approve, change, or override. You are the grader of record. If a score looks off, trust your own read; the grading guide (`ch10-freemium-model-excel-grading-guide.md`) has the full answer key and the per-row standards.
8. **Grade the walkthrough yourself.** Whichever path you pick for the written submission, the ten-minute walkthrough (or the team's recorded oral defense, if your section is asynchronous) is graded by you, in the room or watching the recording, against the four questions in Part 2 below. The AI cannot grade what it did not see.
9. **Grade the next team.** Click New chat and repeat from step 4. A fresh chat for each team keeps one team's work from bleeding into the next.

That is the whole process. The block in Part 2 does the work; you paste, read, and sign off.

> **One quick sanity check you can always run yourself, no AI needed.** The cohort model has one trap that catches everyone. LTV per paid user equals price divided by monthly churn, with churn written as a decimal. At month 24 that is 32 divided by 0.068, which is about 471 dollars. If the team's LTV cell is price times some rate, or a flat number of months with no churn in it, the formula is wrong, and a workflow with no human-review gate on the LTV cell is the failure the assignment trains the team to design against. The second trap is the conversion-versus-churn principle: a workflow that lets a conversion-lift recommendation pass without ever pairing the lift with churn has shipped the trap. If the scenario tab shows LTV per user dropping when conversion and churn both rise (from about 471 to about 410 at the month-24 inputs, or from about 644 to about 527 at the month-1 inputs), the team showed the principle. If it does not show that drop with real cells, the workflow's defense against the trap is not real.

## Part 2: The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a second-year marketing student team's advanced assignment. The team of three to four students was briefed as the pricing-analytics function of a freemium subscription product company. The team designed an end-to-end pricing-recommendation workflow that combines AI tools (the Claude for Excel add-in writing the cohort model's live formulas plus a separate prompted call for a competitor scan), human review gates (decisions the workflow never auto-approves), evaluation steps (an LLM-as-judge prompt the team wrote plus a human spot-check checklist), and logging plus disclosure. The team built the working version of the workflow on a real 24-month freemium cohort dataset and defended it. The graded deliverable is the workflow, which means its integrity, not whether the price recommendation matches any single right answer. Your job is to score the team's written submission against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback. This is a first-year-friendly course; reward honest design work over polish.
>
> **Background you need (the chapter's framework and the correct formulas):**
> - The dataset is 24 months of cohort inputs: free signups, free-to-trial rate, trial-to-paid rate, monthly churn (a percent), price, and CAC. Price steps from 29 dollars (months 1 to 11) to 32 dollars (months 12 to 24). Monthly churn climbs from 4.5 percent to 6.8 percent. CAC rises from 38 dollars to 74 dollars.
> - The correct formulas: new paid users in a month equals free signups times the free-to-trial rate times the trial-to-paid rate. Paid base equals last month's paid base times (1 minus this month's churn), plus this month's new paid users. LTV per paid user equals price divided by monthly churn (as a decimal). LTV divided by CAC equals the LTV/CAC ratio. CAC payback in months equals CAC divided by price. MRR equals paid base times price.
> - Checkpoints to verify a few cells: month 12 should show a paid base near 1,041, LTV per user near 571 dollars, LTV/CAC near 11.0, MRR near 33,320 dollars. Month 24 should show a paid base near 3,532, LTV per user near 471 dollars, LTV/CAC near 6.4, MRR near 113,028 dollars. The paid base grows the whole way while LTV per user falls, because churn keeps rising.
> - The conversion-versus-churn principle (the demonstration the workbook must show with real cells, not assert): at the month-24 inputs (price 32 dollars, churn 6.8 percent), LTV per user is 32 divided by 0.068, about 470.59 dollars. A 10-point conversion lift with churn unchanged leaves LTV per user the same because LTV per user does not depend on conversion; the cohort is just bigger, so the lift looks like a pure win. A 10-point conversion lift paired with one extra point of churn (6.8 percent to 7.8 percent) drops LTV per user to 32 divided by 0.078, about 410.26 dollars, a fall of about 60 dollars per user (down about 13 percent), and LTV/CAC falls from about 6.4 to about 5.5. The bigger paying cohort is now worth less per user. That reversal is the trap, and a workflow with no human-review gate that catches it has shipped the failure mode.
> - Markup versus margin on the price line: markup is on cost and can pass 100 percent. Margin is on price and never can. The classic slip is that a 40 percent margin on a 40-dollar cost gives a price of 40 divided by (1 minus 0.40) equals 66.67 dollars, not 40 times 1.40 equals 56 dollars (which is the markup). If the price line in the workflow reports a markup as a margin and the team's human-review gates never catch it, that is a workflow integrity failure.
> - The chapter's chosen-from list for the recommended pricing strategy (the team must name one from this list, not invent): cost-based, value-based, competitor-based, dynamic, odd-even, reference, decoy, extremity aversion, prestige, bundle, multiple-unit, loss-leader, skimming, or penetration. For a freemium subscription, the workflow will most often land on value-based or penetration pricing for the paid tier, sometimes paired with a bundle (annual plan or team-seat) or a price-fence dynamic component, although the choice depends on the cohort signal and the competitor scan.
> - The six required workflow elements the team's spec must address: (1) inputs (cohort CSV, competitor scan, cost sheet, customer signals); (2) the two AI calls (Claude for Excel writing live formulas, a separate prompted call for the competitor scan), each with the prompt and the output specified; (3) at least three human-review gates with a named role owning each gate, including a gate that catches the LTV formula against the price-divided-by-monthly-churn definition and a gate that applies the dual-entitlement fairness read before a price recommendation reaches a customer; (4) evaluation steps, including the LLM-as-judge prompt written out in full as a paste-ready block and a human spot-check checklist with five or six concrete items; (5) what gets logged internally and what gets disclosed to the customer, with the disclosure sentence written out in the team's own words; (6) at least three failure modes the workflow defends against, each tied to a specific gate or evaluation step that catches it.
>
> **What good looks like.** A strong submission has all six required workflow elements specified with named owners on each gate, the LLM-as-judge prompt written out in full, the cohort model correct as live cell formulas over 24 months (matching the checkpoints), the conversion-versus-churn scenario tab demonstrating with real cells that LTV per user falls when conversion and churn both rise, four distinct member diagrams that cover four different human-review gates between them, a recommended pricing strategy from the chapter's list tied to the cohort signal, and a disclosure sentence in the team's own writing.
>
> **The integrity decisions to check fast.** Does the workflow have a named human-review gate that catches the conversion-versus-churn principle before a recommendation reaches a customer. Does the workflow have a named gate that applies the dual-entitlement fairness read before the recommendation goes out. Does the workflow have a named gate that reads the LTV cell against the price-divided-by-monthly-churn definition. If any of those three gates is missing, the workflow integrity row caps at Developing no matter how clean the rest of the spec looks.
>
> **The failure modes to watch for and score down (with a kind note):** a flow chart of AI calls with no human-review gates anywhere; an LLM-as-judge prompt described in two sentences but not written out as a paste-ready block; the disclosure sentence missing; the conversion-versus-churn principle mentioned but not assigned to a gate; the recommended pricing strategy not from the chapter's list; shared or identical individual diagrams, or two members on the same gate; the cohort model on numbers pasted as values with no live formulas; a scenario tab that shows a conversion lift as a pure win because the team never paired the lift with churn.
>
> **Do not penalize disclosed AI use.** The whole workflow is built around AI tools, so disclosed use is the expected pattern. The rubric grades the integrity of the workflow the team designed. If there is no transcript or disclosure at all and the submission reads like an unedited tool output, flag it for the instructor as a possible disclosure issue rather than scoring it; that is a human decision.
>
> **How to score.** Give a number for each of the four criteria below, then add them for a total out of 100. For each, write one sentence saying why and one sentence the team could act on. Be specific and encouraging. If something required is missing, say so plainly and score it low; do not invent credit. The walkthrough row is graded by the instructor (not by you), so for that row give a placeholder of "graded by instructor" and leave the score blank; the instructor will fill it in after the walkthrough.
>
> 1. Workflow integrity (40 points). Are all six required elements specified, with named owners on the human-review gates, the LLM-as-judge prompt written out in full as a paste-ready block, a named gate that catches the conversion-versus-churn principle, a named gate that applies the dual-entitlement fairness read, a disclosure sentence in the team's own words, and a recommended pricing strategy from the chapter's list (cost-based, value-based, competitor-based, dynamic, odd-even, reference, decoy, extremity aversion, prestige, bundle, multiple-unit, loss-leader, skimming, or penetration) tied to the cohort signal? Deduct for thin or missing elements, for gates without named owners, for a sketched LLM-as-judge prompt, for a missing disclosure sentence, or for a strategy outside the chapter list.
> 2. Funnel economics built on the cohort (25 points). Are the paid base, LTV per paid user, LTV/CAC, CAC payback, and MRR all correct as live cell formulas over 24 months, matching the checkpoints? Does the scenario tab show with real cells that a 10-point conversion lift paired with one extra point of churn drops LTV per user (about 471 dollars to about 410 dollars at the month-24 inputs, or about 644 dollars to about 527 dollars at the month-1 inputs)? Deduct for one metric or formula off, for a thin scenario tab, or for numbers pasted as values with no live formula.
> 3. Individual diagram and gate defense, per member (20 points). Is every member's own one-page diagram present and distinct, with each member highlighting a different human-review gate (no two on the same gate), and each member's paragraph defending why that specific gate matters? Deduct for thin, shared, or duplicate diagrams, or for two or more members on the same gate.
> 4. In-class walkthrough or recorded oral defense (15 points). Write "graded by instructor" and leave the score blank; the instructor grades the walkthrough live or by watching the recording.
>
> Output format:
> - Criterion 1, Workflow integrity: __ out of 40 (why) (one tip)
> - Criterion 2, Funnel economics built on the cohort: __ out of 25 (why) (one tip)
> - Criterion 3, Individual diagram and gate defense, per member: __ out of 20 (why) (one tip)
> - Criterion 4, In-class walkthrough or recorded oral defense: graded by instructor (leave blank)
> - Total (written portion): __ out of 85
> - One-line summary for the team: (warm, specific, what to do next time)

## Part 3: Reading and trusting the result

- The AI returns four scores (with the walkthrough row blank for you), a written-portion total out of 85, and short notes. **Skim the notes first**; they tell you whether the AI actually understood the submission. If the notes match what you saw when you read the work, the total is trustworthy.
- **You can push back.** Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example: "Check whether the LLM-as-judge prompt is written out as a paste-ready block in the team's spec; if it is only described, the workflow integrity row should cap at Developing." or "Look again at Criterion 3 and confirm that no two members picked the same human-review gate." The AI will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- **The workflow integrity row is the one to double-check yourself.** It is worth the most (40 points) and it is where the AI's own read can be too generous because a polished-looking spec can read as strong even when the gates have no named owners. The grading guide tells you how to read each of the six required elements.
- **The fastest verification needs no Excel.** Read the team's three (or more) human-review gates against the question of which one catches each named failure mode. If the workflow has no gate that catches the conversion-versus-churn principle, or no gate that applies the dual-entitlement fairness read, the workflow integrity row caps at Developing no matter what else is on the page.
- **Keep a fresh chat per team** so submissions do not blur together.
- **You grade the walkthrough yourself**, since the AI cannot grade what it did not see. Use the four questions in the assignment card: which decision never auto-approves and who owns the sign-off, where the conversion-versus-churn principle gets caught and what would happen if it slipped through, what the LLM-as-judge prompt actually checks for, and which named strategy from the chapter list the team recommended and why. A team whose members answer in the room without reading from the spec earns the full 15; a team that reads the spec aloud caps at Developing.
- Remember the framing for any feedback you pass on: the instructor grades, the AI assists. The score is yours.

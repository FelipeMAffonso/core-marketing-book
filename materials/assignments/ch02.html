Read a Brand's Five Decisions with AI

Chapter 2 individual assignment. The student steps into the role of a brand's new marketing analyst: pick a real brand, use an AI assistant to map its five marketing decisions from public information, then judge where the AI knew something and where it guessed, and name the one decision that fights the other four. The student directs the AI and out-judges it. Done alone, about two hours.

Instructor assignment · Ch 2
  • TypeIndividual
  • Time~2 hrs
  • Points100 pts
  • ChapterCh 2

Student instructions

Pick one real, public brand you can look up in a few minutes: one you use, a local or campus business, or a brand with a clear story online. Pick one specific brand or product line, not a giant company. "Crumbl Cookies" or "the Owala water bottle" works; "Amazon" or "Nike" turns into a book report. Public information only.

Make a free account at claude.ai or chatgpt.com. Paste the brand's name and its About page, then ask it to map the five marketing decisions, one line each: what it makes, who it serves, what it charges, where it sells, and how it talks about itself. Ask it to write the positioning in the four-part shape (for a target buyer who wants something, the brand is the kind of product that delivers one benefit) and to name the single decision that, if it changed, would break the other four. The five decisions multiply rather than add, so one wrong slot sinks the rest. Every line it returns is a claim to check, not a fact to keep.

Then map your own brand end to end. Hand in the five-decision map with one verified detail per line, the four-part positioning statement, and the one decision that breaks the other four (or, if two slots already contradict each other, name that instead) with a two-or-three-sentence reason.

The grade is mostly the judgment pass. Mark at least two lines you verified against the real brand and at least one line the AI guessed or made up, and correct it. Then write a one-line takeaway in your own voice: what holds this brand together, or what is about to break it. Paste the chat as your transcript. That call is yours.

Submit: one document, about a page and a half, plus your AI transcript: the five-decision map with one verified detail per line, the one-sentence positioning statement, the judgment pass (what you verified, the line the AI guessed and your correction, the decision that breaks the other four), and your one-line takeaway.

On the job. Reading a brand as five decisions that have to line up is how a product marketer spots the one that is off, and checking what an AI got right or guessed is fast becoming part of that same daily work.

What this assesses · learning objectives, instructor only▸

These are the Chapter 2 objectives the work exercises. The rubric scores against them.

  • LO 2.1 Define marketing as an exchange, the voluntary trade where each side gives up something and gets something it values more.
  • LO 2.2 Name the five marketing decisions and explain why they multiply rather than add, so one weak decision sinks the other four.
  • LO 2.6 Use the four-part positioning statement to say who a brand is for and the one benefit it leads with.
  • LO 2.9 Evaluate AI output about a brand's marketing, say which decision the AI knew versus guessed, and flag what it left out.

The AI step trains the chapter's tested misfire. On a brand question, a current assistant states a guess about a brand fact, a price, or a competitor in the same confident voice it uses for something it actually knows, and it caves to a leading question, agreeing with whatever framing the student implies rather than pushing back. Both are the student's to catch; the chapter's running brands (Patagonia, Costco, Liquid Death, Bud Light) are the worked models, not the answer.

    Grading Guide: Read a Brand's Five Decisions with AI

    Chapter 2 individual assignment. This guide is for whoever grades the work: you by hand, a TA, or an AI you direct. It says what the assignment is really testing, what good and weak work look like, the failure modes to watch for, and how to turn the rubric into a fair score fast. The companion ch02-five-decisions-grading-skill.md turns this same rubric into something you can paste into a free Claude account if you want a first-pass score; this guide stands on its own without it.

    What this assignment is really testing

    Not whether the student can list the five decisions, the test bank already checks that. This assignment tests whether a student can direct an AI to map a real brand and then out-judge it: catch the line the model guessed, name the one decision that would sink the other four, and say what the map cannot see. That second half is the whole point. A submission that maps the brand beautifully but takes every AI line at face value has missed the assignment. A submission with a rougher map but a sharp judgment pass has hit it.

    Keep the chapter's core idea in front of you while grading: the five decisions multiply, they do not add. The strongest submissions show that a student feels this, they can point at one slot and explain how it drags the other four down, rather than grading the brand "four out of five."

    The three ways to grade (your choice)

    You own the grade. Pick the path that fits your section and your time.

    1. By hand. Read the submission against the five rubric rows. Budget about eight to ten minutes each. The score bands below let you land a number quickly. This is the most reliable path and the one to use for any borderline or flagged paper.
    2. Hand it to TAs. Give your TAs this guide and the rubric. Calibrate first: grade three papers together (a strong, a middling, a weak one) so everyone anchors the bands the same way. The most common TA drift is over-rewarding a tidy map and under-rewarding a messy-but-real judgment pass, the opposite of what this assignment values, so calibrate on that specifically.
    3. Use the grading skill (optional). If you want a fast first pass, open ch02-five-decisions-grading-skill.md, follow its click-by-click walkthrough, and let an AI return per-criterion scores and short feedback. Treat that as a draft a human signs off on, never the final grade. It is a time-saver, not a grader of record.

    All three use the same rubric, so a student gets the same standard whichever path you pick.

    What to look for, row by row

    Judgment pass: knew vs. guessed (30 pts), the heaviest row, grade it first.

    • Strong: the student verified specific lines against the real brand (a price they checked on the site, a channel they confirmed, a buyer the brand actually names) and caught at least one line the AI got wrong, an invented competitor, a stale price, a feature the brand does not have, and corrected it against the source.
    • Look for the catch. The single best signal of a strong paper is a named, corrected AI error. If the student writes "the AI said it sells in Target, but the brand's store locator only lists its own site, so I fixed that," that is the assignment working.
    • Weak: "The AI was very accurate and gave me a great overview." No verification, no catch. That is the Developing/Not-yet band no matter how polished the rest is.

    The five-decision map (22 pts).

    • Strong: all five slots named with a verified, specific detail. Not "good product," but "ships only four core styles and repairs them for free."
    • Weak: slots merged or generic ("great quality, fair price, sold online"). A merged or missing slot caps this row at Developing.

    The decision that breaks the rest (20 pts).

    • Strong: names one decision and traces the knock-on, "if Patagonia dropped to Walmart prices, the supply chain and the repair program and the trust all collapse, because the price funds them." That is the multiply-not-add logic in action.
    • Acceptable alternative: naming two slots that already contradict each other (the Bud Light pattern) and explaining the risk.
    • Weak: "the weakest decision is their advertising" with no account of what it drags down. Noticing a weak slot without the knock-on is Developing.

    Positioning and exchange (16 pts).

    • Strong: a one-line positioning that names a real buyer and one benefit, and a sentence on why the trade is worth it to that buyer (what each side gives and gets).
    • Weak: the buyer is "everyone," or the benefit is "high quality" / "great value", filler any rival could claim. Cap at Developing.

    Takeaway and writing (12 pts).

    • Strong: one clear sentence in the student's own voice on what holds the brand together or what will break it, plus plain, correct use of the chapter's terms.
    • Weak: a summary of the map with no call, or muddy terms (calling the message "the marketing").

    Common failure modes (and how to score them)

    • The face-value paper. Maps the brand fluently, no checking, no catch. The most common miss. Judgment-pass row lands in Developing or Not-yet; the map row can still earn its points if the slots are specific. Net: usually low-to-mid range.
    • The book report. Student picked a giant brand (Amazon, Nike) and wrote three pages of history. The map turns vague because the brand is too big to read on five slots. Reward whatever real five-decision reading survives; the vagueness self-penalizes through the map and conflict rows.
    • The tactics dump. The student let the AI "suggest improvements" and handed in a list of campaign ideas. This is the exact AI Misfire the chapter names. There is no judgment pass and no conflict found, score both rows low and note that the assignment asked for the decision that breaks the rest, not a to-do list.
    • The unverifiable claim. A confident price or competitor with no source. If the student labeled it an estimate, fine. If they presented an AI guess as fact and it is wrong, that belongs in the judgment-pass deduction, it is precisely what they were supposed to catch.
    • The hidden AI use. No AI-Use Note and no transcript, yet the prose reads like an unedited model dump. This is an integrity matter, not a grading deduction, follow your institution's process. Disclosed AI use, even heavy use, is never the violation; hiding it is.
    • The "everyone" buyer. The positioning slot says the brand is "for everyone." Treat as the chapter does: a brand that serves everyone serves no one with depth. Cap the positioning row at Developing.

    Score bands (for a fast, fair landing)

    Use these to convert the five rows into a defensible total.

    • 90 to 100 (Excellent). Specific five-decision map, a real verified-versus-guessed catch with a correction, the breaking decision named with its knock-on, a sharp positioning line, and a clean takeaway. The student clearly out-judged the AI.
    • 80 to 89 (Strong/Proficient). Solid map and a genuine judgment pass with at least one correction, but one row is thin, the conflict is named without full knock-on logic, or the positioning is a little loose.
    • 70 to 79 (Developing). The work is present but the student mostly trusted the AI: a tidy map with little checking, a weak-slot mention without the multiply logic, or an "everyone" buyer. The judgment half is underdone.
    • 60 to 69 (Weak). Face-value AI output with no catch, or a book report with a vague map. The assignment's core skill is missing.
    • Below 60 / integrity flag. No disclosure and an apparent unedited AI dump, a missing deliverable, or a brand with no real public information to read. Handle disclosure issues through your integrity process, not the rubric.

    A note for graders on the AI itself

    If you run any student's transcript yourself, expect the model to be confidently wrong about specific facts, prices that are stale, competitors that do not compete, features a brand does not have. That is not a reason to distrust the student; it is the exact thing the assignment trains them to catch. Reward the catch. A student who notices the AI invented a price and corrects it has done more marketing thinking than one whose AI happened to be right and who checked nothing.

    Tier 1 Pre-Generated AI Sample: Five Decisions

    Assignment: Read a Brand's Five Decisions with AI

    This is the pre-generated AI output for Tier 1 students. The model was given this exact prompt:

    "Read Olipop's five marketing decisions, one line each: what it makes, who it serves, what it charges, where it sells, and how it talks about itself. Use only public information and tell me when you are unsure. Then state who the brand is for and the one benefit it leads with, in this form: for [a target buyer] who [wants something], Olipop is the [kind of product] that [delivers one benefit]. Finally, of these five decisions, which one would break the other four if it changed, and which two (if any) already contradict each other?"

    The AI's Response

    What it makes: Prebiotic sparkling sodas in flavors like Vintage Cola, Strawberry Vanilla, and Classic Grape, formulated with plant fiber, botanicals, and prebiotics to support digestive health, positioned as a better-for-you alternative to traditional soda with 35 to 45 calories and 2 to 5 grams of sugar per can.

    Who it serves: Health-conscious millennials and Gen Z consumers, primarily women aged 25 to 38 with household incomes above $85,000, who miss the taste and nostalgia of soda but have given it up because of sugar and artificial ingredients.

    What it charges: $2.49 to $2.99 per can at retail, or roughly $35 to $39 for a 12-pack on its website, a significant premium over conventional soda (Coca-Cola runs under $0.50 per can in bulk) that signals the product's better-for-you positioning.

    Where it sells: Primarily through grocery and natural-food retail, with Whole Foods, Target, Kroger, and Sprouts among its biggest doors, plus a growing direct-to-consumer channel on its website. Grocery is its biggest channel by revenue, accounting for the majority of sales.

    How it talks about itself: A warm, nostalgic voice that leads with flavor and emotional memory ("the soda you loved, made better") rather than clinical health language. Heavy on social media, especially Instagram and TikTok, featuring the brand's retro-illustrated cans and creator partnerships, plus a loyal ambassador community of wellness influencers.

    Positioning statement: For health-conscious millennials and Gen Z soda-lovers who want the taste and nostalgia of soda without the sugar and artificial ingredients, Olipop is the prebiotic sparkling soda that delivers the full soda experience without the guilt.

    The decision that would break the other four: Pricing. Olipop's entire identity rests on the idea that a better-for-you soda is worth paying a premium for. Drop the price to conventional soda levels and the signal collapses: the buyer it is serving (health-conscious, higher-income, quality-seeking) no longer has a reason to believe the product is different, and the distribution strategy (natural food and premium grocery) loses its logic. Every other decision points toward a buyer who pays more for something that performs better; price is the hinge.

    Any two decisions that already contradict each other: The targeting (women 25 to 38, higher-income, health-focused) and the TikTok-heavy social strategy are not a contradiction exactly, that buyer is present on TikTok, but there is a slight tension: TikTok skews younger and more impulsive than the deliberate wellness-shopper Olipop's pricing and channel presuppose. Not a fatal contradiction, but worth watching as the brand decides how far down in age to push its audience.

    Instructor Key: Planted Errors and Grading Guide

    This section is for instructor use only. Do not share with students before they submit.

    Planted error 1: The household income figure (Who it serves)

    The claim: "primarily women aged 25 to 38 with household incomes above $85,000"

    Why it is wrong: Olipop's public marketing materials, press coverage, and founder interviews describe the target buyer in psychographic terms, soda-lovers who want a healthier option, nostalgia-seekers, wellness-adjacent consumers, not in demographic income brackets. No public source pins Olipop's target to a specific household income figure. The $85,000 threshold is a confident-sounding fabrication of the kind Ch2's verified misfire documents: the model filled in a plausible demographic detail (health-food brands do index toward higher income) with a specific number it invented. A student checking Olipop's website, its About page, or any press profile will find no such figure.

    The type of error: Invented specific statistic stated as fact, indistinguishable in the model's voice from verified data.

    What a student should write: Something like "I couldn't find any public source naming $85,000 as Olipop's income target. Their marketing materials describe their buyer in terms of values, wanting real ingredients, nostalgia for soda, not income brackets. The AI invented a plausible-sounding number."

    Planted error 2: The "biggest channel" claim (Where it sells)

    The claim: "Grocery is its biggest channel by revenue, accounting for the majority of sales."

    Why it is wrong: Olipop does not publish a channel-revenue breakdown. The statement may well be directionally true, Olipop is widely distributed in grocery, but the confident assertion "biggest channel by revenue, accounting for the majority of sales" is presented as a specific, verified fact when it is the model's inference. This is the exact pattern from the Ch2 tested misfire: the AI asserts an unranked channel as ranked fact without a source. No public earnings report, SEC filing, or press release confirms this split. A student checking Olipop's website or a news search will find no channel-revenue figure.

    The type of error: Unsupported "biggest" / "majority" claim stated as verified fact.

    What a student should write: Something like "Olipop is definitely in grocery, but I couldn't find any public data confirming that grocery 'accounts for the majority of sales.' The AI stated it as a fact, but there's no revenue breakdown publicly available. This is a guess dressed as a statistic."

    What Tier 1 students must demonstrate to earn full marks

    For the judgment-pass row (30 points, the heaviest):

    • Excellent: The student catches at least one of the two planted errors above, names the exact line, checks it against Olipop's real website or a news story, and corrects it (or notes it cannot be confirmed). Catching both earns full marks for thoroughness.
    • Proficient: The student flags some concern about one of the two lines but does not fully verify or correct it.
    • Developing: The student notes the map is "mostly good" without identifying either planted error.
    • Not yet: No judgment pass, or the AI output is handed in as the student's own verified work.

    For the "decision that breaks the others" row (20 points): The AI's answer (pricing) is well-reasoned and defensible. A student who agrees with it and explains the multiply-not-add logic in their own words scores well. A student who picks a different decision and argues it coherently also scores well. There is no single right answer here, only a reasoned argument.

    For the "what the map leaves out" row (part of the judgment pass): The AI does not mention Olipop's retail velocity challenges (getting and keeping shelf space against established soda brands), the brand's reliance on emotional/nostalgia storytelling as distinct from clinical health claims (a deliberate choice with legal and brand-strategy logic), or the founder's origin story and the trust it builds with the wellness community. Any of these earns credit.

    How the AI transcript for Tier 1 works

    Tier 1 students do not generate their own transcript. This document is their transcript. Their AI-Use Note should read something like: "I used the pre-generated AI output provided for Tier 1. I found [the line they flagged] and checked it against Olipop's website, where [what they found]. The AI's pricing analysis seemed well-supported, but [the income/channel claim] could not be verified." A note that just says "I used the Tier 1 sample" with no judgment earns a Developing or Not Yet on disclosure.

Rebrand a Boring Product

Chapter 2 individual assignment. The student steps into the role of a brand strategist: hand an AI a plain, boring product, make it invent three different brands for three different kinds of people, then judge which one is a real marketing idea and which is just a louder way of saying "buy this." The five decisions are the ruler. The judging is the grade. Done alone, about two hours.

Instructor assignment · Ch 2
  • TypeIndividual
  • Time~2 hrs
  • Points100 pts
  • ChapterCh 2

Student instructions

Start with something deliberately plain, a product almost nobody gets excited about, so the branding has to do real work: bottled water, a spiral notebook, a white t-shirt, a bar of soap, a phone charger, table salt, a pack of socks. The blander the better. The brands are invented, so you do not need a real company; use public information only.

Make a free account at claude.ai or chatgpt.com. Ask it to invent three different brands for three different buyers from your plain product. For each, have it write a name, a one-line pitch, the buyer it is for, and the five marketing decisions, one line each: what to make it feel like, who exactly it serves, what to charge, where to sell it, and how to talk about it. Have it name the value each brand leads with: money saved, performance, an experience, or belonging. You direct it; it invents.

The grade is the judgment pass on what it gives you. Decide which one is a real marketing idea: a real idea picks a specific buyer and lines all five decisions up behind that person, so the product almost sells itself (the marketing concept). Decide which one is just a louder "buy this": the same plain product with a hyped-up voice and no real buyer behind it (the selling concept). Name the tell that gives the hype away. You do not have to find a contradiction across all five decisions or write a full build defense; instead, in one sentence, name the buyer the real idea serves, and say which one brand you would actually build. Paste the chat as your transcript. That choice is yours.

Submit: one document, about a page, plus your AI transcript: the three brands (name, buyer, pitch, five-decision grid), the judgment pass (which is the real marketing idea and which is the louder "buy this," with the tell), the one-sentence buyer for the real idea, and your build call.

On the job. Telling a real brand idea from a louder sales pitch is the call a brand manager makes on every pitch and tagline, and it is what separates positioning that holds from hype a rival can copy.

What this assesses · learning objectives, instructor only▸

These are the Chapter 2 objectives the work exercises. The rubric scores against them.

  • LO 2.2 Name the five marketing decisions (what to make, who to serve, what to charge, where to sell it, how to talk about it) and use them as a coherence check on a brand idea.
  • LO 2.3 Tell the marketing concept (understand the buyer so well the product almost sells itself) from the selling concept (push harder on a product nobody asked for).
  • LO 2.6 Use the four value types (money saved, performance, experience, belonging) and write a one-line positioning statement that names a buyer and a benefit.
  • LO 2.9 Evaluate AI-generated brand ideas and flag which is a real choice versus buzzword filler a competitor could copy.

The AI step trains the chapter's tested misfire. Asked to improve a brand, a current assistant adds things (a louder campaign, a discount, a new tagline) instead of choosing one buyer and committing the five decisions to that person, and it will call a hyped-up plain product a "brand" when it is just the selling concept in nicer words. It also states invented details (market sizes, competitors, claims) in the same confident voice it uses for a fact. The student's job is to judge which brand actually works.

    What this assignment is really testing

    Not whether the student can get an AI to spit out three brand names, that takes one prompt. This assignment tests one judgment: can the student tell a real marketing idea from a louder sales pitch? The chapter calls these the marketing concept (understand the buyer so well the product almost sells itself) and the selling concept (push harder on a product nobody specifically asked for). A real brand picks one buyer and lines all five decisions up behind her. A louder "buy this" is the same plain product with hype bolted on and no buyer behind it.

    The student uses the five decisions as the ruler and uses the four value types and positioning to back the call. The grade lives in the judgment, not in the brands themselves, the AI made those. Reward the student who can explain why one idea is real and another is just volume.

    Keep the chapter's core rule in front of you: the five decisions multiply, they do not add. The contradiction the student is asked to find (a premium price with a dollar-store channel, a rebel brand for "busy professionals") is that rule in action, one wrong slot would break the others.

    The three ways to grade (your choice)

    You own the grade. Pick the path that fits your section and your time.

    1. By hand. Read the submission against the five rubric rows. Budget about eight to ten minutes each. The score bands below let you land a number quickly. Use this for any borderline or flagged paper.
    2. Hand it to TAs. Give your TAs this guide and the rubric. Calibrate first: grade three papers together (a strong, a middling, a weak one) so everyone anchors the bands the same way. The drift to watch is TAs rewarding clever brand names, the names are the AI's, not the student's, so a great name with no judgment behind it is a weak paper. Calibrate on that.
    3. Use the grading skill (optional). For a fast first pass, open ch02-rebrand-grading-skill.md, follow its click-by-click walkthrough, and let an AI return per-criterion scores and short feedback. Treat that as a draft a human signs off on, never the final grade.

    All three use the same rubric, so a student gets the same standard whichever path you pick.

    What to look for, row by row

    Real idea vs. louder "buy this" (30 pts), the heaviest row, grade it first.

    • Strong: the student correctly identifies which of the three brands is the real marketing idea and which is just hype, and names the tell using the chapter's language, "this one names a specific buyer and the five decisions all serve her, so it is the marketing concept; that one is the same notebook with a hype tagline and a buyer of 'everyone,' which is the selling concept, just a louder buy-this."
    • The single best signal: the student can articulate the difference between value (a reason this buyer chooses it) and volume (a louder pitch at no one in particular).
    • Weak: "Brand 2 is the best because it sounds the coolest." No marketing-vs-selling reasoning. That is Developing or Not-yet regardless of how lively the writing is.

    Five-decision coherence + the contradiction (24 pts).

    • Strong: walks the five decisions for the chosen brand and shows they agree, and finds one slot in one of the three that fights another, with what it would break.
    • Weak: lists the five decisions back without checking whether they line up, or finds no contradiction at all. Cap at Developing.

    Value type and positioning (18 pts).

    • Strong: names what each brand leads with, money saved, performance, experience, or belonging, and writes a one-line positioning that names a real buyer and one benefit.
    • Weak: confuses the value types (calls a status purchase "functional"), or the positioning is "high quality" filler any rival could claim. Cap at Developing.

    The build call (16 pts).

    • Strong: commits to one brand, names its buyer and lead benefit, and gives two sound sentences. Rejecting all three and fixing the best one is also strong, it shows real judgment.
    • Weak: describes a brand without committing to a buyer, or hedges across all three.

    Judgment write-up and disclosure (12 pts).

    • Strong: a clear, plain write-up a reader could follow, plus a transcript and an AI-Use Note.
    • Weak: restates the three brands with no judgment, or no disclosure.

    Common failure modes (and how to score them)

    • The beauty-contest paper. Student picks the brand with the best-sounding name and stops. No marketing-vs-selling reasoning, no five-decision check. The names came from the AI, so this rewards the model, not the student. Row 1 and Row 2 land low.
    • The "all three are great" paper. Student praises every brand and makes no real call. The whole assignment is the discrimination, telling real from loud, so refusing to discriminate is the miss. Row 1 and the build-call row score low.
    • Starting too exciting. Student rebrands something already glamorous (a sports car, designer sneakers). The branding does no work and there is nothing to judge, every option looks "real." Note it; the thin judgment will self-penalize through Row 1.
    • The tactics dump. Student let the AI "improve" the brands and handed in a pile of campaign ideas. This is the AI Misfire the chapter names, adding volume instead of choosing a buyer. There is no judgment pass; score Rows 1 and 2 low and note that the assignment asked which idea is real, not for more tactics.
    • No contradiction found. Every brand is declared coherent. Usually means the student did not actually walk the five decisions. Cap Row 2 at Developing.
    • Repeating AI "facts" as truth. The student cites a market size or a competitor the AI invented. The brands are fiction by design, so invented market data is exactly what they should not present as real, note it under disclosure.
    • The hidden AI use. No AI-Use Note and no transcript, yet the work reads like a model dump. Integrity matter, not a grading deduction, follow your institution's process. Disclosed AI use, even heavy use, is never the violation; hiding it is.

    Score bands (for a fast, fair landing)

    • 90 to 100 (Excellent). Correctly separates the real idea from the louder "buy this" with the marketing-vs-selling reasoning, walks the five decisions and finds a real contradiction, names value types and a sharp positioning, and commits to a build call. The judgment is clearly the student's.
    • 80 to 89 (Strong/Proficient). A genuine real-vs-loud call and a five-decision read, but one row is thin, the contradiction's effect is loose, or the positioning is a little generic.
    • 70 to 79 (Developing). The work is present but the student leaned on the AI: praised the brands without the marketing-vs-selling distinction, listed decisions without checking coherence, or hedged the build call.
    • 60 to 69 (Weak). A beauty contest or an "all three are great" paper, no real discrimination, the core skill missing.
    • Below 60 / integrity flag. Missing deliverable, an undisclosed AI dump, or a product so exciting there was nothing to judge. Handle disclosure through your integrity process, not the rubric.

    A note for graders on the AI itself

    If you run the prompts yourself, you will see the model cheerfully present a hyped-up plain product as a "brand" and pad it with invented market sizes. That is the raw material the student is meant to judge, not trust. Reward the student who looked at three confident AI brands and said, in effect, "two of these are real and one is just shouting, and here is how I can tell." That instinct, value versus volume, is the entire point of the chapter, and of this assignment.

    Tier 1 Pre-Generated AI Sample: Rebrand a Boring Product

    Assignment: Rebrand a Boring Product

    This is the pre-generated AI output for Tier 1 students. The model was given these exact prompts:

    Prompt 1: "Here is a plain product: a canned water (sparkling or still, 12 oz, produced for about $0.08 to $0.12 per can). Act as a marketer and invent three completely different brands I could build from it, each for a different kind of person. For each, give me a name, a one-line pitch, and the buyer it is for."

    Prompt 2: "For each of these three brands, give me one line each on what to make it feel like, who exactly it serves, what to charge, where to sell it, and how to talk about it. Then name which of the four value types each one leads with: money saved, performance, an experience, or belonging."

    Prompt 3: "Write a one-line positioning statement for each brand in this form: for [buyer] who [wants something], [brand] is the [product] that [delivers one benefit]."

    The AI's Three Brands

    Plain product: A 12 oz can of water, either sparkling or still, costing roughly $0.10 to produce.

    Brand 1: Fieldwork

    Pitch: Clean water in a can built for people who are actually outside.

    Buyer: Outdoor recreationalists, hikers, trail runners, climbers, aged 22 to 40 who distrust the plastic taste and environmental footprint of single-use plastic bottles and want their gear to feel intentional.

    Five decisions:

    • What to make it feel like: A matte-finish aluminum can with a simple topographic-line illustration, available in still and lightly sparkling. No flavoring, no sweeteners, no electrolyte marketing, just clean water in packaging that looks at home in a pack or a gear shop.
    • Who exactly it serves: Outdoor-active adults who already invest in gear and personal sustainability; they have paid $30 for a stuff sack and they have opinions about trail ethics.
    • What to charge: $2.49 per can, or $28 for a 12-pack online. More expensive than bottled water at a gas station, less than a specialty sports drink, so the price says "intentional product," not "impulse buy."
    • Where to sell it: REI, Backcountry, independent outdoor shops, national park visitor centers, and a direct website. Not grocery, not convenience stores, because the distribution is part of the signal.
    • How to talk about it: Dry, understated, specific ("12 oz. Still. Made for the approach."). No wellness claims, no athletes-on-peaks imagery. The brand talks the way a gear review talks: straightforward, knowledgeable, no hype.

    Value type: Belonging. The can is a signal that says "I am the kind of person who thinks about this stuff."

    Positioning statement: For trail-active adults who want water that fits their gear-forward, low-waste ethic, Fieldwork is the canned water that makes the right choice feel like a natural part of their kit.

    Brand 2: Source

    Pitch: The cheapest water you can buy by the case, honest about what it is.

    Buyer: Budget-conscious households, families, college students, anyone stocking a fridge, who buy water in bulk and care mainly about cost and convenience.

    Five decisions:

    • What to make it feel like: A plain white can with the word SOURCE in bold black. No story, no lifestyle, no origin claim. The design signals that you are paying for water, not a brand.
    • Who exactly it serves: Price-first buyers who already buy the store-brand everything else at the grocery store; they are not embarrassed to hand someone a plain can of water.
    • What to charge: $0.49 per can, or $16 for a 36-pack at warehouse stores. The cheapest canned water on the shelf, always.
    • Where to sell it: Costco, Sam's Club, grocery store bulk sections, Amazon Subscribe & Save. Maximum availability at maximum volume.
    • How to talk about it: Functionally, directly. "It is water. It is in a can. It costs less." The brand's only claim is that it never pretends to be more than it is.

    Value type: Money saved. The entire brand is a promise that you did not overpay for water.

    Positioning statement: For budget-conscious households who buy water by the case and want the lowest honest price, Source is the canned water that strips out everything you are not paying for.

    Brand 3: Elev8

    Pitch: Premium hydration for the ambitious go-getter who refuses to settle.

    Buyer: Everyone who wants to perform at their best and live their most elevated life, whether at the gym, in the boardroom, or on the go.

    Five decisions:

    • What to make it feel like: A sleek, modern can with a dynamic gradient design and the Elev8 logo prominently featured. Bold, energetic, premium-looking, a can that announces itself.
    • Who exactly it serves: Go-getters, high performers, hustlers, gym-goers, professionals, students, athletes, and anyone who wants to live better. Essentially anyone with ambition.
    • What to charge: $3.99 per can, a premium price that signals quality and exclusivity.
    • Where to sell it: Everywhere: gyms, grocery stores, convenience stores, Amazon, the brand's own website, corporate wellness programs, hotel minibars, airports. Maximum reach for maximum impact.
    • How to talk about it: High-energy, aspirational, motivational. "Hydrate to elevate." "Your water should work as hard as you do." Heavy digital advertising across all platforms: Instagram, TikTok, YouTube, LinkedIn, podcasts.

    Value type: Performance. Water that works as hard as you do.

    Positioning statement: For ambitious people who want to perform at their best in every area of life, Elev8 is the premium hydration that fuels the unstoppable version of you.

    Instructor Key: Which Brand Is Weak and Why

    This section is for instructor use only. Do not share with students before they submit.

    Brand 3 (Elev8) is the "louder buy this", the selling concept, not the marketing concept

    What gives it away:

    No real buyer. The "who exactly it serves" slot reads: "go-getters, high performers, hustlers, gym-goers, professionals, students, athletes, and anyone who wants to live better." That is not a target, it is everyone with a pulse. The chapter's specific test: if you asked "who is NOT this brand's buyer?", Elev8 has no answer. Fieldwork excludes people who do not care about gear and trail ethics. Source excludes people willing to pay a premium. Elev8 excludes no one, which means it is targeting no one in particular.

    The five decisions point in different directions. The brand claims premium ($3.99, top of market) while simultaneously saying "sell everywhere: gyms, grocery, convenience stores, Amazon, hotel minibars, airports." A premium product sold in convenience store impulse bins next to $1.29 single-serve bottles undermines its own premium signal. Fieldwork's distribution (REI only, no grocery) is part of the identity. Source's distribution (Costco, bulk) reinforces the cost claim. Elev8's "everywhere" strategy fights its own $3.99 price.

    The value type claim does not hold. The AI labeled Elev8 "performance," but the product is water. There is no performance mechanism, no electrolytes, no minerals, nothing in the can that outperforms a glass of tap water on any measurable axis. "Performance" as a value type requires a real delivery mechanism; naming water "performance" is a hype label on an identical product. Fieldwork leads on belonging (a real social-identity mechanism), Source leads on money saved (a real price mechanism), and both are honest about what they are actually delivering. Elev8 claims performance by vocabulary alone.

    The positioning statement is filler. "For ambitious people who want to perform at their best in every area of life", any product could claim this. A protein bar, a laptop, a car. The chapter's test: could a rival copy this word for word? Every brand targeting anyone ambitious could take this statement. A real positioning statement names a buyer so specifically that the wrong buyer self-selects out.

    What students should find in Brands 1 and 2

    Fieldwork is a real marketing idea because it names a specific buyer (outdoor-active adults, gear-forward, sustainability-minded) and all five decisions cohere: the design fits the buyer, the price fits the buyer, the distribution fits the buyer, and the voice fits the buyer. A price-first buyer walks past Fieldwork at REI and it does not matter, because that buyer is not Fieldwork's. That is the marketing concept.

    Source is also a real marketing idea. It names a different specific buyer (price-first household shoppers) and all five decisions cohere in the opposite direction: plain design, lowest price, bulk distribution, functional voice. No hype, no story, no premium, and that is the whole point. The buyer Source is after does not want to pay for a story.

    The one contradiction to find (in any of the three brands)

    The assignment asks students to find one decision in any of the three brands that contradicts another. Elev8 provides the clearest contradiction: $3.99 premium pricing + maximum convenience distribution (convenience stores, gas stations, impulse buys). Premium positioning requires distribution that protects the premium signal. If you can buy Elev8 next to the $1.49 generic at a gas station, the $3.99 price no longer signals anything. Students who find this are applying the multiply-not-add principle correctly.

    A secondary option: Elev8's "everyone" buyer + a premium price is also a contradiction. Premium positioning depends on being for someone specific, and specificity is what earns the higher price. A product for "everyone with ambition" has no premium story to tell.

    Grading notes for the judgment pass (30 points, the heaviest row)

    Excellent: The student correctly identifies Elev8 as the selling-concept brand and names at least one specific tell: the "everyone" buyer, the distribution contradiction, the hollow performance claim, or the copyable positioning statement. Explains, in their own words, why Fieldwork or Source works (buyer-led, five decisions agree).

    Proficient: The student calls Elev8 the weak one with a partial reason (e.g., "the target is too broad" without connecting it to why that matters for the five decisions).

    Developing: The student picks Elev8 as "worse" without explaining the marketing-vs-selling difference, or calls it weak because it "sounds generic" without unpacking why.

    Not yet: No judgment, or the student repeats the AI's three brands as the answer.

    Note on the value-type labels

    The AI labeled Elev8 "performance," which is technically one of the chapter's four value types. This is exactly the kind of move students should catch: using the right vocabulary to mask a hollow claim. A real performance brand (Gatorade, Liquid IV) has a delivery mechanism. Students who push back on Elev8's performance label, asking "what exactly performs better?", are applying the chapter's framework precisely as intended.

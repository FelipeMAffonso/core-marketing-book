# Grading Skill: Value-Stick Store Field Study

*Chapter 10 individual assignment. This file lets you grade a student's field study with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste a block of instructions into Claude, paste the student's report under it, and read back a score for each rubric row with a sentence of feedback. The student did this study with no AI; the only AI here is yours, and only to speed your grading. You stay in charge: the AI gives a first-pass score, you decide the grade. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom in case you would rather not use AI at all.*

---

## Part 1: If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install anything or pay anything. This takes about five minutes the first time and under two minutes per student after that.

1. **Open a web browser** (Chrome, Safari, Edge, whatever you use) and go to **claude.ai**. (ChatGPT at **chatgpt.com** works the same way if you prefer it.)
2. **Make a free account.** Click **Sign up**, enter your email, and follow the steps. A free account is enough. You will not be charged.
3. **Start a new chat.** After you log in you will see a box that says something like *"How can I help you today?"* That box is where you type. A "chat" is just a conversation with the AI, like texting.
4. **Copy the grader instructions.** In Part 2 below, everything inside the gray box is one block. Select it all, copy it, and paste it into the chat box. Do not press send yet.
5. **Add the student's report underneath.** Press Enter twice to make space, then paste the student's full field study right below the instructions, in the same message, including the price table, the value stick, the approach calls, and the psychological tactic. If the value stick is a photo or sketch, describe it to the AI or attach the image if your tool allows.
6. **Send it.** Press Enter (or click the arrow). The AI will read both and reply with a score for each of the four rubric rows, a total out of 100, and a short note for each row.
7. **Read the score and decide.** The AI's score is a **first draft for you to approve, change, or override.** You are the grader of record. If a score looks off, trust your own read; the grading guide (`ch10-value-stick-store-study-grading-guide.md`) tells you what each row is really worth.
8. **Grade the next student.** Click **New chat** and repeat from step 4. A fresh chat each time keeps one student's work from bleeding into the next.

That is the whole process. The block in Part 2 does the work; you paste, read, and sign off.

> **One thing to sanity-check yourself, no AI needed.** This is a field study, so the data should look real: a named store, a date, prices that carry charm endings rather than all-round dollars, and ideally a shelf photo. If a study has none of that, the prices may be desk research rather than firsthand observation. The AI cannot tell you whether the student really stood in the aisle; you decide that.

## Part 2: The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a second-year marketing student's field study. The student went to a real store, recorded the real shelf prices of three or four competing products in one category with their own eyes, and built a value stick by hand, with no AI and no online price lookup. Your job is to score their report against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback. This is a first-year-friendly course; reward a real reading of the prices over a tidy table.
>
> **Background you need (the chapter's framework):**
> - A price is built on a **value stick**: the **floor** (a rough unit cost, the least the price can be), the **frame** (the band of what real competitors charge, here drawn from three or four prices the student observed on the shelf), and the **ceiling** (the most a buyer would pay before walking away). A good study places all three with a number and a clear basis.
> - The **four pricing approaches**: cost-based (price set off the cost), competitor-based (price set off what rivals charge, often just under a name brand), value-based (price set off the value to the customer, often well above the field), and customer-determined. A strong study names the approach for each observed product and backs it with the shelf evidence.
> - **Psychological pricing tactics** show up on shelves: a 99-cent charm ending, a "compare at" anchor, a good-better-best three-tier lineup, a multi-buy bundle. A strong study spots one and reads what it does to the shopper.
> - The heart of the study is **reading strategy off the real prices**: explaining why the spread of shelf prices implies which approach each seller chose, and where the buyer's ceiling sits relative to the field. A study that just lists prices and assigns labels has not done this.
>
> **How to score.** Give a number for each of the four criteria below, then add them for a total out of 100. Reward specific ties between the observed prices and the approach calls (for example, "the store brand at $3.49 sits just under the $3.99 name brand, so it reads as competitor-based"). Flag a study that looks like desk research rather than firsthand observation (no store, no date, all-round prices, no photo) so the instructor can check it; do not score the honesty question yourself, just note it. For each criterion, write one sentence saying why and one sentence the student could act on. Be specific and encouraging. If something required is missing, say so plainly and score it low; do not invent credit.
>
> 1. **Reading approach and value off the real prices (42 points).** Does the student name the pricing approach for each product with specific shelf evidence, and explain where the value ceiling sits relative to the observed field and why? Full marks: each approach call tied to the spread of prices, and the ceiling reasoned against the field. Low marks: approaches stated by reflex with little tie to the observed prices.
> 2. **The value stick built from observation (28 points).** Are the floor, the frame, and the ceiling all placed with a number and a clear basis, is the frame drawn from three or four real observed prices, and are the products placed correctly on the stick? Deduct for a stick that is incomplete, a frame using too few prices, or floor and ceiling confused.
> 3. **Field data collected honestly (18 points).** Are the store and date noted, are three or four real competing prices recorded accurately (brand, size, price), and is a shelf photo or sketch included? Deduct for thin sourcing; flag for the instructor (do not score down on your own) prices that look like they were not observed firsthand.
> 4. **Psychological tactic spotted, and writing (12 points).** Did the student name one real shelf tactic (a charm ending, an anchor, a tiered lineup, a bundle) with a correct one-sentence read, in plain, clear language with correct terms? Deduct for a missing or misread tactic.
>
> **Do not penalize** the student for not using AI; this study is meant to be done by hand in a real store. Penalize only a weak reading of the prices, an incomplete value stick, thin field data, or a missing tactic.
>
> **Output format:**
> - **Criterion 1, Reading approach and value off the prices: __/42**, (why), (one tip)
> - **Criterion 2, The value stick built from observation: __/28**, (why), (one tip)
> - **Criterion 3, Field data collected honestly: __/18**, (why), (one tip)
> - **Criterion 4, Psychological tactic and writing: __/12**, (why), (one tip)
> - **Total: __/100**
> - **One-line summary for the student:** (warm, specific, what to do next time)
>
> The student's field study follows below this line.

## Part 3: Reading and trusting the result

- The AI returns four scores, a total, and short notes. **Skim the notes first**; they tell you whether the AI understood the study. If the notes match what you saw when you read the report, the total is trustworthy.
- **You can push back.** Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example *"The student did tie the store-brand call to the price gap, look again at Criterion 1"* or *"This study has no store and no date, weight the field-data row down."* The AI will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- **Sanity-check whether the field data looks real yourself.** This is the one thing the AI cannot judge: it cannot tell you whether the student actually stood in the aisle. A named store, a date, charm-ending prices, and a shelf photo say yes; their absence is worth a look before you trust the score.
- **The reading-the-prices row is the one to double-check.** It is worth the most and it is where an AI can be too generous, giving credit for a tidy table that never reasons from the spread. If the study ties each approach call to the observed prices, that is a strong study even if the formatting is plain.
- **Keep a fresh chat per student** so studies do not blur together.
- Remember the framing: *the instructor grades; the AI assists.* The student used no AI at all; your optional AI use is only to speed the grading. The score is yours.

## Part 4: Grade by hand instead (no AI at all)

If you would rather not use AI, grade straight from the rubric. Read each study once, then score the four rows.

| Criterion | Points | What earns full marks |
|---|---|---|
| **Reading approach and value off the real prices** | 42 | Names the pricing approach for each product with specific shelf evidence, and explains where the value ceiling sits relative to the observed field and why |
| **The value stick built from observation** | 28 | Floor, frame, and ceiling all placed with a number and a clear basis, the frame drawn from three or four real observed prices, the products placed correctly |
| **Field data collected honestly** | 18 | Store and date noted, three or four real competing prices recorded accurately, a shelf photo or sketch included |
| **Psychological tactic spotted, and writing** | 12 | One real shelf tactic named with a correct one-sentence read; plain, clear, correct terms |
| **Total** | **100** | |

**Fast bands:** 90 to 100, real dated documented field data, a complete value stick with a reasoned ceiling, each approach tied to the observed spread, and one tactic read correctly; 80 to 89, solid data and stick with one thin row; 70 to 79, present but mostly reflex labeling, or a ceiling with no buyer reason; 60 to 69, a price list with labels and no reasoning, or data that looks not firsthand; below 60 or an honesty flag for missing or apparently fabricated field data (handle honesty through your integrity process, not the rubric).

The one question that separates strong from weak: **does the report explain why this spread of prices implies these approaches, or just label them?** A study that reasons from the gap between the store brand and the name brand has done the chapter's field thinking.

# Grading Skill: The Fitness-Chain Pricing Consultants

*Chapter 10 group activity, no AI account needed by students. This file lets you grade a submission with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste the block below into Claude or ChatGPT, paste the team's packet under it, and read back a score for each rubric row with a sentence of feedback. The block already knows what good tier designs look like for each lever, so it can tell whether the team's prices and reasoning are within band. You stay the grader of record; the AI gives a first pass. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom.*

---

## Part 1: If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install or pay for anything. This takes about five minutes the first time, and under two minutes per team after that.

1. Open a web browser and go to **claude.ai** (or **chatgpt.com**, which works the same way).
2. Make a free account: click Sign up, enter your email, follow the steps. A free account is enough.
3. Start a new chat. The box that asks how it can help is where you type.
4. Copy the grader instructions in Part 2 below (everything in the gray box) and paste them into the chat. Do not send yet.
5. Press Enter twice for space, then paste the team's full packet underneath, in the same message: the design worksheet, the room summary, and the team's individual paragraphs.
6. Send it. The AI replies with a score for each of the four rubric rows, a total out of 100, and a short note per row.
7. Read the score and decide. It is a first draft for you to approve or override; you are the grader of record.
8. Click New chat for the next team, so submissions do not blur together.

The ten-second check you can always run yourself, no AI needed: did the team's prices sit between **$22** and **$199**, and did each price tie to a specific signal in the chain's numbers (the heavy-user top quartile, the budget rival, the corporate ask, the student ask), not a generic argument about fitness customers? Both yes is an Excellent tier-design row; only prices within band is Proficient; neither is Developing or Not yet.

## Part 2: The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a second-year marketing team's submission for a Chapter 10 in-class price-customization activity. The team was assigned one of the chapter's four price-customization levers (controlling availability, buyer characteristics, transaction characteristics, or product-line good/better/best) and designed a price tier for a small fitness chain called Pulse Studios, with seven studios, a current $89 flat membership, a $22 per-member-per-month cost floor, and a rival frame from a budget chain at $15 to a mid-tier studio at $129 to a high-end boutique at $199. The chain's value signal has a top quartile of heavy users at 18-plus visits per month, a bottom quartile of light users at two or three visits, a corporate ask for groups of 20-plus, and a student ask for a cheaper plan with limited hours. Score the team's packet against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback. Reward designs that anchor each price to a specific signal in the chain's numbers over polished writing.
>
> What good tier designs look like, by lever:
>
> 1. Controlling availability: an off-peak basic plan at about $49 to $59 per month and the original unlimited plan at about $99 to $109, with the lower tier tied to the budget rival and the higher tier tied to the heavy-user signal.
> 2. Buyer characteristics: a verified student plan at about $49 to $55, a senior plan at about $59 to $65, a healthcare or first-responder plan at about $69, and a corporate per-employee rate at about $59 to $69 for groups of 20-plus. Each price tied to a willingness-to-pay signal, and the category verified with a real signal like a current .edu email.
> 3. Transaction characteristics: month-to-month at the current $89, six-month commit at about $79 per month, annual at about $69 per month, with single-studio access for $15 to $20 less per month at each tier. The annual discount has to be at least 15% to compensate the member for losing flexibility.
> 4. Good-better-best: basic at about $59 to $69 with restricted feature set, mid-tier at about $99 to $109 with full network and classes, top tier at about $149 to $169 with sauna, guest pass, and a quarterly assessment. The basic tier has to actually take features away, not just rename the current plan.
>
> Every price has to sit between the $22 floor and the $199 high-end ceiling. A price below $22 loses money; a price above $199 prices out of the local market.
>
> Score these four rows and add them for a total out of 100. For each, give a number, one sentence on why, and one sentence the team could act on.
>
> 1. The price tier designed and defended (40 points). Full marks: two or three sub-tiers with prices in the $22 to $199 band, each tier earning its number from a specific signal in the chain's numbers, and the team held up against the room's questions. Proficient (about 28): tiers proposed with partial reasoning from the chain's data. Developing (about 22): tiers proposed by reflex with little tie to the chain's numbers. Not yet (about 12): no defense.
> 2. The lever used correctly (18 points). Full marks: the team's tier uses the assigned lever and does not slip into a different one. Lower if the team conflates two levers (a transaction team that turned into a good-better-best team mid-presentation).
> 3. Fairness risk named and mitigated (18 points). Full marks: one specific fairness risk the tier introduces with a dual-entitlement-sound mitigation. Lower if only generic "people might feel cheated" without a specific risk and a specific mitigation.
> 4. Individual verdict, each member (24 points). Full marks: every member's paragraph is their own, names the lever they would anchor on, ties the call to a specific signal in the chain's numbers, and identifies one fairness risk to manage. Lower if verdicts are near-duplicates of the team summary.
>
> Output format:
> - Tier designed and defended: __ /40 (why) (one tip)
> - Lever used correctly: __ /18 (why) (one tip)
> - Fairness risk: __ /18 (why) (one tip)
> - Individual verdict: __ /24 (why) (one tip)
> - Total: __ /100
> - One-line summary for the team
>
> The team's packet follows below this line.

## Part 3: Reading and trusting the result

- Skim the notes first; they tell you whether the AI understood the packet. The note on the tier-design row is the heart of the grade, so check that it names whether the prices fall in the $22 to $199 band and tie back to the chain's actual numbers (the heavy-user share, the budget rival, the corporate ask, the student ask).
- You can push back. Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example "the team did name the corporate ask as the reason for the $69 employee rate, look again at the tier-design row," and it will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- The fairness row is easy to double-check yourself, because the chapter's four lever-specific risks are well known: availability tiers can price out shift workers, buyer-characteristic tiers can be gamed (a student plan with no `.edu` check), transaction tiers can lock in hardship cases, and good-better-best tiers can take away features the chain claimed all along.
- Keep a fresh chat per team. The grade is yours; the AI only assists.

## Part 4: Grade by hand instead (no AI at all)

Read the packet against the rubric below; the full answer key is in `ch10-fitness-chain-consultants-grading-guide.md`.

| Row | Points | What earns full marks |
|---|---|---|
| Tier designed and defended | 40 | Sub-tiers with prices in the $22 to $199 band, each price tied to a specific signal in the chain's numbers, and the team held up against the room's questions |
| Lever used correctly | 18 | The team's tier actually uses the assigned lever and does not slip into a different one |
| Fairness risk | 18 | One specific fairness risk named with a dual-entitlement-sound mitigation |
| Individual verdict | 24 | Each member's own paragraph naming a lever, a specific reason from the chain's numbers, and a named fairness risk; not copied across the team |
| **Total** | **100** | |

Fast bands: 90 to 100, prices within band, every price tied to a specific signal, the team held up under questioning, the fairness risk real and the mitigation working; 80 to 89, tier well-designed but one price asserted rather than reasoned; 70 to 79, reasoning slid from the chain's data into general arguments; 60 to 69 or below, the team slipped its lever or set prices outside the $22-to-$199 band. The single fastest signal of a strong submission is a price tier where every number ties to one of the four named value signals in the chain's data.

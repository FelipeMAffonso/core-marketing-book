# Grading Skill: The Wendy's Dynamic Pricing Spotlight

*Chapter 10 individual assignment, no AI account needed by students. This file lets you grade a submission with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste the block below into Claude or ChatGPT, paste the student's three short essay answers under it, and read back a score for each rubric row with a sentence of feedback. The block already knows what each answer should land on, so it can tell whether the student named a specific buyer in the fairness read and whether they designed a real alternative. You stay the grader of record; the AI gives a first pass. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom.*

---

## Part 1: If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install or pay for anything. This takes about five minutes the first time, and under two minutes per student after that.

1. Open a web browser and go to **claude.ai** (or **chatgpt.com**, which works the same way).
2. Make a free account: click Sign up, enter your email, follow the steps. A free account is enough.
3. Start a new chat. The box that asks how it can help is where you type.
4. Copy the grader instructions in Part 2 below (everything in the gray box) and paste them into the chat. Do not send yet.
5. Press Enter twice for space, then paste the student's three essay answers underneath, in the same message.
6. Send it. The AI replies with a score for each of the four rubric rows, a total out of 100, and a short note per row.
7. Read the score and decide. It is a first draft for you to approve or override; you are the grader of record.
8. Click New chat for the next student, so submissions do not blur together.

The ten-second check you can always run yourself, no AI needed: did the student name a **specific Tuesday-noon buyer** in Question 2 (a construction worker, a nurse, a parent at preschool pickup), and did the student name **two specific moves and one chapter mechanism** (anchoring, bundling, or a charm ending) in Question 3? Both yes is an Excellent on rows 2 and 3; only one is Proficient; neither is Developing or Not yet.

## Part 2: The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a second-year marketing student's three short essay answers on the Wendy's dynamic-pricing case from February 2024. The student was handed the case on the assignment page (no outside research) and answered three questions, each between 150 and 300 words: (1) what pricing approach was Wendy's actually using, (2) why did the public read the move as gouging and where does the fairness ceiling sit on lunch, and (3) what would a defensible version of this strategy look like. Score the student's answers against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback. Reward answers that anchor to specific pieces of the case over polished writing.
>
> The strong answers, summarized:
>
> 1. The pricing approach is closest to **value-based pricing with a customer-determined wrap** (the chain's plan paired personalized AI-driven offers in the mobile app with two-way price changes on the boards, not one-directional surge pricing on every customer). A student who calls the move "cost-based" or "competitor-based" has misread the case; a student who calls it "surge pricing" without correcting the framing has not done the chapter work.
>
> 2. The dual-entitlement principle holds that a price change reads as fair when it rests on a real cost or value story and reads as gouging when it captures the buyer's necessity at a moment when they cannot easily go elsewhere. The strong answer names a specific Tuesday-noon buyer (a construction worker on a thirty-minute break, a hospital nurse between shifts, a parent at preschool pickup) and walks through why that buyer has no real alternatives in the next ten minutes, then contrasts with the airline case (Friday-evening travelers chose Friday, the surge is disclosed a week ahead, rivals price the same way). A student who says "people would feel cheated" without anchoring to a specific buyer earns Developing.
>
> 3. The strong answer designs the discount-only-plus-app-loyalty-plus-bundle structure: an off-peak discount window from 2 to 4 p.m. (using anchoring off the standard menu price), an app-only happy hour for loyalty members (price-customization tied to a verifiable buyer characteristic), and a bundled afternoon meal that prices below the standard combo (price bundling from §10.5). The strong answer names two specific moves, one chapter mechanism, and one residual fairness risk (an app-only price excludes buyers without smartphones) with a mitigation (post the discount on the menu boards too).
>
> Score these four rows and add them for a total out of 100. For each, give a number, one sentence on why, and one sentence the student could act on.
>
> 1. The pricing approach named and defended (32 points). Full marks: names value-based or customer-determined with a specific reason from the case (the personalized AI offers, the two-way price changes), and corrects the "surge pricing for burgers" framing. Lower if the approach is named without defense, or if "surge pricing" goes uncorrected.
> 2. The fairness read on lunch (32 points). Full marks: applies the dual-entitlement principle to a specific Tuesday-noon buyer (construction worker, nurse, parent), names the alternatives that buyer has in the next ten minutes, and explains why a surge price at that hour crosses the ceiling in a way an airline's Friday-evening fare does not. Lower if the buyer is generic.
> 3. The defensible alternative designed (24 points). Full marks: two specific moves named (an off-peak discount window, an app-only happy hour, a 2-to-4 p.m. bundled meal), one chapter psychological mechanism leaned on (anchoring, bundling, charm), and one fairness risk still to manage. Lower if only one move or no mechanism.
> 4. Essay writing and concept use (12 points). Full marks: three answers between 150 and 300 words each, in plain prose with no bullet lists, using the chapter's named concepts correctly. Lower if word counts are off or the student used bullet lists where prose was asked for.
>
> Output format:

> - Fairness read: __ /32 (why) (one tip)
> - Defensible alternative: __ /24 (why) (one tip)
> - Essay writing: __ /12 (why) (one tip)
> - Total: __ /100
> - One-line summary for the student
>

## Part 3: Reading and trusting the result

- Skim the notes first; they tell you whether the AI understood the answers. The note on the fairness row is the heart of the grade, so check that it names whether the student wrote a specific Tuesday-noon buyer or a generic "the customer."
- You can push back. Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example "the student did name the construction worker as the specific buyer, look again at the fairness row," and it will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- Question 1 is the easiest to double-check yourself: did the student call the move surge pricing without correcting the framing, or did they read the two-way-price-change and the personalized-AI-offer details and land on value-based or customer-determined? The answer is fixed.
- Keep a fresh chat per student. The grade is yours; the AI only assists.

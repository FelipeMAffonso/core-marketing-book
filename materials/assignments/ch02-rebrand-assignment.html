Rebrand a Boring Product

Chapter 2 individual assignment. The student steps into the role of a brand strategist: hand an AI a plain, boring product, make it invent three different brands for three different kinds of people, then judge which one is a real marketing idea and which is just a louder way of saying "buy this." The five decisions are the ruler. The judging is the grade. Done alone, about two hours.

Build the student handout

Set the two levers for your class. The panel below is the only student-facing part; copy it into your LMS.

Student access
Difficulty Level

Start with something deliberately plain, a product almost nobody gets excited about, so the branding has to do real work: bottled water, a spiral notebook, a white t-shirt, a bar of soap, a phone charger, table salt, a pack of socks. The blander the better. The brands are invented, so you do not need a real company; use public information only.

Invent three different brands for three different buyers. For each, write a name, a one-line pitch, the buyer it is for, and the five marketing decisions, one line each: what to make it feel like, who exactly it serves, what to charge, where to sell it, and how to talk about it. Name the value each brand leads with: money saved, performance, an experience, or belonging.

The grade is the judgment pass. Decide which one is a real marketing idea: a real idea picks a specific buyer and lines all five decisions up behind that person, so the product almost sells itself (the marketing concept). Decide which one is just a louder "buy this": the same plain product with a hyped-up voice and no real buyer behind it (the selling concept). Find one decision in any of the three that contradicts another, and say what it would break. Then pick the one brand you would actually build, name its buyer and the one benefit it leads with, and say in two sentences why. That choice is yours.

one document, about a page and a half, plus your AI transcript: the three brands (name, buyer, pitch, five-decision grid), the judgment pass (the real idea with its five decisions walked through, the louder "buy this" with its tell, and one contradiction), and your build call with two sentences on why.

You do not need to sign up for anything. We hand you a pre-generated set of three brands for a sample plain product, attached to this assignment, with one of them deliberately just a louder "buy this." Your whole job is the judgment pass on them: say which is a real marketing idea and which is hype, walk the five decisions, find the contradiction, and make your build call.

Make a free account at claude.ai or chatgpt.com. Ask it to invent three different brands for three buyers from your plain product, then to fill the five decisions for each and name the value type each leads with. You direct it; it invents. Do the judgment pass on what it gives you and paste the chat as your transcript.

Use a paid account or the live in-chapter widget. Have the AI generate the three brands, then in the same chat ask it to argue against its own weakest brand ("which of these three is just a louder version of buy-this, and why?"), and judge whether its self-critique matches yours. Note which tool you used.

Work from the pre-generated set of three brands we provide rather than running the tool yourself. Do the judgment pass on it: say which is the real marketing idea and which is the louder "buy this," and name the tell. You do not have to find the contradiction or make a full build call; instead, in one sentence, name the buyer the real idea serves.

Invent your own three brands. Run the prompts, then hand in the three brands with their five-decision grids, the judgment pass (real idea, louder "buy this," and one contradiction), and your build call with its buyer and lead benefit.

Do the Medium task, then add rigor. After your build call, run a quick reality check: ask three real people which of your three brands they would actually buy and why, and report whether their pick matches your judgment. Add a one-paragraph defense of your build call that answers the strongest objection to it.

What this assesses — learning objectives, instructor only▸

These are the Chapter 2 objectives the work exercises. The rubric scores against them.

  • LO 2.2 Name the five marketing decisions (what to make, who to serve, what to charge, where to sell it, how to talk about it) and use them as a coherence check on a brand idea.
  • LO 2.3 Tell the marketing concept (understand the buyer so well the product almost sells itself) from the selling concept (push harder on a product nobody asked for).
  • LO 2.6 Use the four value types (money saved, performance, experience, belonging) and write a one-line positioning statement that names a buyer and a benefit.
  • LO 2.9 Evaluate AI-generated brand ideas and flag which is a real choice versus buzzword filler a competitor could copy.

The AI step trains the chapter's tested misfire. Asked to improve a brand, a current assistant adds things (a louder campaign, a discount, a new tagline) instead of choosing one buyer and committing the five decisions to that person, and it will call a hyped-up plain product a "brand" when it is just the selling concept in nicer words. It also states invented details (market sizes, competitors, claims) in the same confident voice it uses for a fact. The student's job is to judge which brand actually works.

# Grading Skill: The Restaurant Pricing Game (in-class)

*Chapter 10 group activity. This file lets you grade a student's work with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste a block of instructions into Claude, paste the work under it, and read back a score for each part of the rubric with a sentence of feedback. The students played the game and wrote their debriefs with no AI; your optional AI use here is only to speed your grading. Because this is a group activity with a per-member individual debrief, you grade one member at a time. You stay in charge: the AI gives you a first-pass score, you decide the grade. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom in case you would rather not use AI at all.*

---

## Part 1. If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install anything or pay anything. This takes about five minutes the first time and under two minutes per student after that.

1. **Open a web browser** (Chrome, Safari, Edge, whatever you use) and go to **claude.ai**. (ChatGPT at **chatgpt.com** works the same way if you prefer it.)
2. **Make a free account.** Click **Sign up**, enter your email, and follow the steps. A free account is enough for this. You will not be charged.
3. **Start a new chat.** After you log in you will see a box that says something like *"How can I help you today?"* That box is where you type. A "chat" is just a conversation with the AI, like texting.
4. **Copy the grader instructions.** In Part 2 below, everything inside the gray box is one block. Select it all, copy it, and paste it into the chat box. Do not press send yet.
5. **Add the work underneath.** This is a group game, so paste the **team results sheet** and then **one member's own debrief paragraph** right below the instructions, in the same message. Grade one member per chat so their individual debrief is scored on its own and you can spot near-duplicates across the team.
6. **Send it.** Press Enter (or click the arrow). The AI will read both and reply with a score for each of the four rubric rows, a total out of 100, and a short note for each row.
7. **Read the score and decide.** The AI's score is a **first draft for you to approve, change, or override.** You are the grader of record. If a score looks off, trust your own read, the grading guide (`ch10-restaurant-pricing-game-grading-guide.md`) tells you what each row is really worth.
8. **Grade the next member.** Click **New chat**, paste the grader block again, then the same team sheet plus the next member's debrief. A fresh chat each time keeps one member's work from bleeding into the next and helps you catch copied paragraphs.

That is the whole process. The block in Part 2 does the work; you paste, read, and sign off.

> **One quick thing to look for yourself, no AI needed.** This activity lives or dies on whether the debrief *explains the price war* rather than just narrating the rounds. Skim for the mechanism: did the student say *why* the cuts kept coming (the elastic market hands the crowd to whoever undercuts), and did they name *what would have beaten it* (hold, differentiate, think ahead)? And glance across the team's paragraphs for near-duplicates, since the individual debrief is the anti-slacker rule.

## Part 2. The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a second-year marketing student's work from an in-class pricing game, played with no AI and no computer. Teams ran competing campus taco stands, set a price each round, and the most elastic market sent most customers to the cheapest stand. After five rounds, the team turned in a results sheet, and each member wrote their own one-paragraph debrief. Your job is to score ONE member's contribution (their individual debrief, read against the team results sheet) using the rubric below, and give short, kind, specific feedback. This is a first-year-friendly course; reward a real reading of why the price war happened over a play-by-play of the rounds.
>
> **How the game worked:** every taco cost the stand $2.00; each round there were 120 customers; the lowest-priced team won 60 customers (ties split that 60), the other 60 split among the rest, and a team's profit was (its price minus $2.00) times the customers it won. Teams played five rounds.
>
> **Background you need (the chapter's frameworks):**
> - **Price elasticity** is how much volume responds to price. In a *very elastic* market, a small price difference swings most of the volume to the cheapest seller, which is exactly what tempts every team to undercut.
> - **Competitor-based pricing** sets price off what rivals charge. When every team just matches the lowest price, the result is a **price war**: a race to the bottom that wins customers but destroys margin, so it benefits no team.
> - The smarter move in this market is to **not match**, to **differentiate**, or to **think two rounds ahead**, rather than chase the lowest price.
> - A strong debrief **explains the mechanism** (why the elastic market drove the cuts) and **names what would have beaten the price war**. A weak debrief just narrates the prices round by round. Note too: a team that won the most rounds at a rock-bottom price did not "win", the margin collapsed; seeing that is part of the lesson.
>
> **How to score.** Give a number for each of the four criteria below, then add them for a total out of 100. For each, write one sentence saying why, and one sentence the student could act on. Be specific and encouraging. If something required is missing, say so plainly and score it low; do not invent credit.
>
> 1. **Reading the game: why the price war happened and what beats it (42 points).** Does the debrief explain how the elastic market drove the price war, name who would have won by holding or differentiating, and draw a correct lesson about competitor-based pricing? Full marks: the mechanism plus the better move. Low marks: a play-by-play of the rounds with no explanation, or the "we won by cutting" read that misses the collapsed margin.
> 2. **Round play and results recorded (22 points).** Are the round-by-round prices, customers won, and profits recorded correctly for all teams on the results sheet, matching the demand rule (lowest price takes 60, the rest split 60, profit is price minus $2.00 times customers)? Deduct for gaps or arithmetic that does not match the rule.
> 3. **Individual debrief (22 points).** Is this member's paragraph their own, does it name their team's prices and the price-war dynamic, and does it state a real lesson? If it reads like a near-duplicate of a teammate's paragraph, say so and score it low; the individual debrief is the anti-slacker check.
> 4. **Writing and terms (14 points).** Is the writing plain and clear, using the terms correctly (elasticity, competitor-based pricing, price war)? Deduct for disconnected sentences or loose terms.
>
> **Watch for these patterns and score them down (with a kind note):** a debrief that narrates the rounds but never explains why the price war happened; a missing "what would have beaten it"; a results sheet whose profits do not match the demand rule; a paragraph that looks copied from a teammate; concluding that undercutting "worked" while ignoring that the margin collapsed.
>
> **Output format:**
> - **Criterion 1, Reading the game: __/42.** (why), then (one tip)
> - **Criterion 2, Round play and results recorded: __/22.** (why), then (one tip)
> - **Criterion 3, Individual debrief: __/22.** (why), then (one tip)
> - **Criterion 4, Writing and terms: __/14.** (why), then (one tip)
> - **Total: __/100**
> - **One-line summary for the student:** (warm, specific, what to do next time)
>
> The team results sheet and one member's debrief follow below this line.

## Part 3. Reading and trusting the result

- The AI returns four scores, a total, and short notes. **Skim the notes first**, they tell you whether the AI actually understood the debrief. If the notes match what you saw, the total is trustworthy.
- **You can push back.** Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example *"This member did explain why the elastic market drove the cuts, look again at Criterion 1"* or *"Check the results sheet profit against the demand rule."* The AI will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- **The game-reading row is the one to read closely yourself.** It is worth the most, and it is where a tidy play-by-play can fool the AI into generosity. A debrief that explains the mechanism and names the better move is strong even if the recording is plain.
- **Catch near-duplicates yourself.** Because you grade one member per chat, lay the team's debriefs side by side once. If three paragraphs are the same with a word swapped, score the copies down on the individual-debrief row, that is the anti-slacker rule working.
- **Keep a fresh chat per member** so paragraphs do not blur together.
- Remember the framing for any feedback you pass on: *the instructor grades; the AI assists.* The students used no AI; the score is yours.

## Part 4. Grade by hand instead (no AI at all)

If you would rather not use AI, grade straight from the rubric. Read the team results sheet once, then score each member's debrief on the four rows:

| Criterion | Points | What earns full marks |
|---|---|---|
| **Reading the game: why the price war happened and what beats it** | 42 | Explains how the elastic market drove the price war, names who would have won by holding or differentiating, and draws a correct lesson about competitor-based pricing |
| **Round play and results recorded** | 22 | Round-by-round prices, customers won, and profits recorded correctly for all teams, matching the demand rule |
| **Individual debrief** | 22 | The member's own paragraph, naming their team's prices and the price-war dynamic, with a real lesson (not a near-duplicate of a teammate's) |
| **Writing and terms** | 14 | Plain, clear writing with correct terms (elasticity, competitor-based, price war) |
| **Total** | **100** | |

**Fast bands:** 90 to 100 a debrief that explains the elastic-market mechanism and names what would have beaten the price war, a correct results sheet, every member's own distinct debrief, clean terms; 80 to 89 a real reading with one thin row; 70 to 79 played and recorded but the debrief mostly narrates; 60 to 69 near-duplicate debriefs or a results sheet that does not match the demand rule; below 60 a missing results sheet or missing debriefs.

The single fastest signal of a strong submission: **a debrief that names what would have beaten the price war.** Recording the prices is the easy half; reading why the cuts kept coming and what a smarter team would have done is the lesson. Because the individual debrief is the anti-slacker rule, grade each member's own paragraph and score copied ones down.

Segment, Target, and Position a Real Brand

A Chapter 6 individual assignment that runs the full STP discipline on a real brand of the student's choice, with AI used the way the chapter teaches it: as a critic that pushes back on the student's positioning statement, not as the author of it. The student writes the segment cut, the targeting filter, the positioning statement, and the perceptual map themselves, verifies any segment claim the AI offers against real public material the brand actually puts out, and defends the position in their own words.

  • TypeIndividual
  • Time~3 hrs
  • Points100 pts
  • ChapterCh 6

Student instructions

Pick one real, public brand or product line you could reason about in an afternoon, scoped tight enough that a few hours of work can land a real read on it. A challenger or regional brand in food, drink, apparel, fitness, beauty, a campus business, an app, or a direct-to-consumer product is the right size. Scope down on giants (not "Nike" but "the Nike Run Club app"; not "Coca-Cola" but "Coke Zero Sugar"). Avoid any brand whose answer would need private sales data; everything you need should be visible from the shelf, the website, and reviews.

Run the chapter's STP sequence on it. Cut its category into three or four segments using the four bases (geographic, demographic, psychographic, behavioral) and name at least one demand space, the occasion that triggers the need, because the occasion is usually the most actionable cut. Choose the one segment the brand can actually win, by running it through the five-criteria filter (measurable, accessible, substantial, differentiable, actionable) and naming the criterion that is shakiest, then argue compatibility before attractiveness, why your brand can out-serve rivals for these buyers before you argue the segment is big enough. Write your positioning statement in the template embedded below, name the points of parity the category requires and your one point of difference, run the POD through the three tests below, and name which of the three positioning moves you are making (claim the rung, find the creneau, reposition the competition). Draw a perceptual map that puts your brand and two real rivals on two axes the buyer actually uses to choose, add buyer ideal points, and answer the empty-quadrant question in one line: do buyers want to live in the empty space, or is it dead air the incumbents already priced in?

Then turn AI on your own statement as a critic. The Chapter 6 move is to ask the model the vague way first (paste the statement, say "make it sound better"), notice that what comes back is usually a slogan no rival could disprove and no buyer could check, then ask the sharp way ("audit this clause by clause; for the point of difference, tell me whether it is desirable, deliverable, and differentiating, and flag any clause a competitor could say word for word"). Judge the critique against the chapter, mark what the model got right and where it missed (a POD that fails the differentiating test because every rival could claim it, an empty quadrant it called an opportunity without asking where the buyers are, a segment claim it stated as fact without checking your brand's own page), revise your statement, and keep both versions. Verify any segment claim the AI states about your brand or its rivals against the brand's real public material before you keep it: the actual product page, the brand's own ads or social posts, real reviews. Do not paste personal, confidential, or proprietary data into a public AI tool.

Submit: one PDF, two to three pages of prose plus the perceptual map and the AI critique transcript. Hand in the four written parts (segment cut, targeting filter and compatibility call, positioning statement v1 and v2 with the three-test read on the POD and the positioning move named, the labeled map with the empty-quadrant answer), the one-paragraph defense to the founder, and the AI-use note (the vague-ask prompt, the sharp-ask prompt, the model's output, what you kept and what you cut). The final positioning statement and the judgment behind it are yours.

On the job. Running STP on a brand is the core of what a product marketer or brand strategist is hired to do, and a positioning statement that survives a sharp clause-by-clause audit is the deliverable a founder or a client actually acts on.

What this assesses · learning objectives, instructor only▸

These are the Chapter 6 objectives the work exercises. The rubric scores against them.

  • LO 6.2 Apply the four segmentation bases (geographic, demographic, psychographic, behavioral) and use the demand space as the most actionable behavioral cut.
  • LO 6.3 Evaluate a candidate segment with the five-criteria usability filter (measurable, accessible, substantial, differentiable, actionable).
  • LO 6.4 Distinguish target compatibility (can you create more value than rivals can?) from target attractiveness (is the segment big and growing?), and choose compatibility first.
  • LO 6.5 Treat positioning as work done in the buyer's mind; use the mental-ladder idea and one of the three positioning moves.
  • LO 6.6 Write a positioning statement with a clear POD and the POPs the category requires, and run the POD through the three tests (desirable, deliverable, differentiating).
  • LO 6.7 Build and read a perceptual map, and apply the empty-quadrant rule.

The AI step trains the chapter's tested misfire. On STP, a current assistant invents segment claims about a brand the student picked, calling out demographics and demand spaces it never verified against the brand's own pages; it produces positioning statements with PODs that fail the differentiating test because every rival in the category could say the same words; and it labels an empty quadrant on a map an opportunity without asking whether buyers actually want to live there. The student is the one who catches all three.

The materials you need are on this page

You do not need a textbook tab open. Everything the assignment asks you to apply is below.

The positioning statement template (use this exact frame)

For [target segment] who [the need or occasion the segment carries], [brand] is the [category] that [the one biggest difference] because [the reason to believe, evidence a buyer could check].

The three POD tests (run your point of difference through every one)

TestThe questionFailure looks like
DesirableDo buyers in the target want this attribute and name it unprompted when asked what matters about the category?A POD buyers have to be coached to care about.
DeliverableCan the brand prove it today and keep proving it?A claim the operations or product cannot stand behind for long.
DifferentiatingIf a rival in the category said the exact same words, would it ring just as true?A line every competitor could use verbatim. That is a category POP, not a POD.

The five-criteria usability filter (run your chosen segment through every one)

CriterionThe question
MeasurableCan you size the segment with real, accessible data?
AccessibleCan you reach these buyers through channels the brand can afford?
SubstantialIs the segment big and profitable enough to repay the cost of serving it?
DifferentiableDo these buyers respond differently from other segments to the marketing program you would run?
ActionableDoes the brand actually have the resources to design and execute a program for them?

The three positioning moves (name which one you are making)

  1. Claim the rung the buyer has already handed you. Avis took the second-place rung in rental cars and said "We try harder," asking buyers to value the extra effort that came with being number two. The cheapest move when the buyer's memory already has you slotted somewhere honest.
  2. Find a creneau (an empty frame the category never claimed). Liquid Death sold canned mountain water at a heavy-metal beverage party, an empty quadrant in a category every analyst read as covered. New territory, not a fight for an existing rung.
  3. Reposition the competition. Tylenol ran "for the millions who should not take aspirin," listing aspirin's side effects until the analgesic rung dissolved under Bayer, then stepped into the space. The hardest move; it changes what the buyer values so a rival's strength stops looking like one.

The perceptual map shape (and the empty-quadrant rule)

A perceptual map plots brands on two axes that are the attributes the buyer actually uses to choose, not the ones the firm wishes mattered. Plot your brand and at least two real rivals, then do the move that matters most: add the buyers. Mark roughly where buyer ideal points cluster, estimated from real public reviews and common sense, not from a survey you ran. Look for an empty quadrant, then answer the empty-quadrant question in one line: do buyer ideal points cluster in it (a real opening), or is it empty because no buyer wants to live there (dead air the incumbents already priced in)? Without the buyers on the map, dead air and real demand look identical.

A worked example, on a brand that is not your answer. Liquid Death sits on a map of bottled water along two axes a buyer uses: wellness signaling (low to high) and rebellion or attitude signaling (low to high). Aquafina, Dasani, and Fiji cluster in the high-wellness, low-attitude quadrant. The high-wellness, high-attitude quadrant was empty, and so was high-attitude, low-wellness (canned water that reads as anti-wellness). Buyer ideal points for a chunk of younger drinkers who hate "wellness marketing" cluster in that high-attitude quadrant; the move worked because buyers actually wanted to live there. An empty quadrant on its own would have been dead air; the buyer cluster turned it into a creneau.

Use that shape on your brand. Do not analyze Liquid Death; pick your own.

The chapter's running brands (the patterns to recognize, never to copy as your answer)

Chapter 6 runs the full funnel on Stanley insulated drinkware, the 2018 dashboard read the Quencher as a weak product, the real buyer (a woman drinking from her desk all day) sat in a segment the dashboard never asked about, the same 1913 product was re-pointed at the buyer it found and revenue moved from roughly $73M to past $750M. Knorr India ran a demand-space reframe (soup recast as a kids' pre-dinner snack, sales up 127% on a product never reformulated). Avis claimed the second rung. Liquid Death found a creneau. Coca-Cola against Dr Pepper is the double-jeopardy case. Use these as the shape of good work; do not analyze them as your answer.

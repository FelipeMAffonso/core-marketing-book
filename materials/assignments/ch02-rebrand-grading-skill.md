# Grading Skill: Rebrand a Boring Product

*Chapter 2 individual assignment. This file lets you grade a student's submission with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste the block below into Claude or ChatGPT, paste the student's write-up under it, and read back a score for each rubric row with a sentence of feedback. The block already knows the AI's most common failure pattern across plain products, so it can tell whether the student caught it. You stay the grader of record; the AI gives a first pass. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom.*

---

## Part 1: If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install or pay for anything. This takes about five minutes the first time and under two minutes per student after that.

1. Open a web browser (Chrome, Safari, Edge, whatever you use) and go to **claude.ai**. ChatGPT at **chatgpt.com** works the same way if you prefer it.
2. Make a free account. Click Sign up, enter your email, follow the steps. A free account is enough. You will not be charged.
3. Start a new chat. The box that asks how it can help is where you type. A chat is just a conversation with the AI, like texting.
4. Copy the grader instructions in Part 2 below (everything in the gray box, the whole block). Paste it into the chat. Do not send yet.
5. Press Enter twice for space, then paste the student's full submission underneath, in the same message. Include the student's three brands, the judgment pass, the contradiction, the build call, and the AI transcript.
6. Send it. The AI replies with a score for each of the four rubric rows, a total out of 100, and a short note per row.
7. Read the score and decide. It is a first draft for you to approve, change, or override. You are the grader of record. The grading guide (`ch02-rebrand-grading-guide.md`) holds the full answer key and tells you what each row is really worth.
8. Click New chat for the next student, so submissions do not blur together.

The two-second check you can always run yourself, no AI needed: did the student pick, as the louder "buy this," the brand whose buyer is essentially "everyone" (no one is excluded), the value-type label has no real mechanism behind it (calling a plain canned water "performance"), and the positioning statement could be copy-pasted onto a protein bar or a laptop? And did they name at least one specific tell from the framework in their own words? Both yes is an Excellent judgment pass; one yes is Proficient; neither is Developing or Not yet.

## Part 2: The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a second-year marketing student's individual assignment. The student picked a plain product (canned water, a notebook, a white t-shirt, table salt, and so on), asked a free AI to invent three different brands for three different buyers from that product, and was then told to judge which one is a real marketing idea and which is just a louder way of saying "buy this," using the chapter's framework. The brands are unique to each student's session, so do not look for one correct list. Look for the student's judgment of the brands, in their own words. Score the submission against the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback. Reward catching the AI's failure pattern over polished writing.
>
> The framework the student was asked to use (you need this to grade them):
>
> 1. The marketing concept versus the selling concept. The marketing concept means understanding one specific buyer so well the product almost sells itself to her. A real marketing idea picks a buyer and lines up all five decisions behind her. The selling concept means pushing harder on a product nobody specifically asked for, the same product with louder talk, a buyer of "everyone," and four of the five decisions essentially unchanged. Telling these two apart is the central skill being graded.
> 2. The five marketing decisions: what to make it feel like, who exactly it serves, what to charge, where to sell it, how to talk about it. They multiply, they do not add, so one slot that contradicts another (a premium price sold at the dollar store, a rebel brand aimed at "busy professionals") would break the whole brand.
> 3. The four value types: money saved, performance, an experience, belonging. A strong brand picks one and the other decisions back it up. Watch for a value-type label slapped on a product with no real mechanism behind it (calling a plain canned water "performance" when nothing in the can outperforms tap water is a label, not a value).
> 4. The positioning statement, in the form "For [buyer] who [wants something], [brand] is the [product] that [delivers one benefit]." The test is whether a rival could copy the statement word for word. "For high-quality customers who want the best, our product is the one that delivers excellence" fits a protein bar, a laptop, or a hotel room. That is filler, not positioning.
>
> The AI's most common failure pattern, across plain products and sessions, gives you the answer key. The model reliably produces two honest brands for two narrow buyers (the decisions agree behind each), and a third brand that names a category-wide audience ("anyone with ambition," "everyone who lives an active lifestyle"), labels the product with a value type it has no mechanism to deliver ("performance" is the most common), writes a positioning statement a rival could copy, and stitches together a high price with maximum-reach distribution. That third brand is the louder "buy this." A student who caught it names at least one of these tells in their own words.
>
> Score these four rows and add them for a total out of 100. For each, give a number, one sentence on why, and one sentence the student could act on. Be specific and encouraging. If something required is missing, say so plainly and score it low; do not invent credit.
>
> 1. Judgment pass, real idea versus louder "buy this" (40 points). Full marks: the student correctly picks which of the three is the real marketing idea and which is the louder "buy this," names at least one specific tell from the framework (an "everyone" buyer, a value-type label with no mechanism, a positioning statement a rival could copy, or a slot in the five decisions that fights another), and explains the call in their own words. Proficient (about 28): the call is right and the tell is named but generic. Developing (about 20): calls one brand "better" or "cooler" without the marketing-versus-selling distinction. Not yet (about 10): no real judgment, or the AI's verdict repeated as the student's.
> 2. Five-decision coherence and the contradiction (25 points). Full marks: walks the five decisions for the chosen brand and shows they agree, finds one slot in the louder brand that contradicts another, and says in one sentence what it would break. Cap at Developing if the student lists the decisions without checking coherence, or finds no contradiction.
> 3. Value type and positioning (20 points). Full marks: names what each brand leads with from the four value types and writes a positioning statement that names a real buyer and one benefit a rival could not paste over. Deduct for confused value types or "high quality" filler positioning.
> 4. The build call (15 points). Full marks: commits to one brand to build, names its buyer and lead benefit, two sound sentences (or rejects all three and fixes the strongest). Low marks for hedging across all three or not committing to a buyer.
>
> Watch for these failure patterns and score them down with a kind note: picking the brand with the best-sounding name and stopping (the names are the AI's, not the student's); calling all three "great" and refusing to choose; starting with a product that is already exciting so the branding does no work; handing in a pile of campaign tactics instead of judging which idea is real (the exact AI Misfire the chapter warns about); repeating AI-invented market sizes or competitors as if they were facts; copying the AI's own verdict on the brands rather than writing one in the student's own words.
>
> Do not penalize the student for using AI heavily. This is an open-AI assignment, follow the institution's policy on disclosure and attribution. Penalize only the absence of real judgment, not the presence of AI use.
>
> Output format:
> - Judgment pass: __ /40 (why) (one tip)
> - Five-decision coherence and contradiction: __ /25 (why) (one tip)
> - Value type and positioning: __ /20 (why) (one tip)
> - Build call: __ /15 (why) (one tip)
> - Total: __ /100
> - One-line summary for the student (warm, specific, what to do next time)
>
> The student's submission follows below this line.

## Part 3: Reading and trusting the result

- Skim the notes first; they tell you whether the AI understood the submission. Check that its note on the judgment row names which brand the student flagged as the louder "buy this" and which tell they used. That is the heart of the grade.
- You can push back. Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example "the student did walk the five decisions for Brand 3 and found a real slot-versus-slot break, look again at the five-decision row," and it will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- Remember the brand names are the AI's. Do not let a vivid name inflate the score; credit the student's judgment of it. Tell the grader this in a follow-up if its notes seem swayed by a catchy name.
- The judgment row is the one to double-check yourself. The brands change session to session, but the failure pattern does not: a buyer who is really "everyone," a value-type label with no mechanism, a positioning statement a rival could copy, a premium price with maximum-reach distribution. Any one of these named in the student's own words is a real tell.
- Keep a fresh chat per student so submissions do not blur together. The score is yours; the AI only assists.

## Part 4: Grade by hand instead (no AI at all)

Read the write-up against the rubric below; the full answer key (the AI's failure pattern, the failure modes to watch, the score bands) is in `ch02-rebrand-grading-guide.md`.

| Row | Points | What earns full marks |
|---|---|---|
| Judgment pass: real idea vs. louder "buy this" | 40 | Correctly picks the real marketing idea and the louder "buy this," names at least one specific tell from the framework, and explains the call in the student's own words |
| Five-decision coherence and the contradiction | 25 | Walks the five decisions and shows they agree; finds one slot that contradicts another in the louder brand and says what it would break |
| Value type and positioning | 20 | Names what each brand leads with from the four value types; positioning statement names a real buyer and one benefit a rival could not paste over |
| The build call | 15 | Commits to one brand to build, names its buyer and lead benefit, two sound sentences (or rejects all three and fixes the strongest) |
| **Total** | **100** | |

Fast bands: 90 to 100, real-versus-loud called correctly with a specific tell from the framework, a real contradiction in the five decisions, sharp value types and positioning, a committed build call; 80 to 89, a genuine call and a five-decision read with one thin row; 70 to 79, present but leaned on the AI (praised the brands with no distinction, listed decisions without checking coherence, hedged the call); 60 to 69, beauty contest or "all three are great," no real discrimination. Handle any academic-integrity concern through your institution's policy, not the rubric.

The single fastest signal of a strong paper: the student can say, in their own words, why one brand is a real reason for one buyer to choose and another is just a louder pitch at no one in particular. That value-versus-volume judgment is the whole card.

Chapter 7: Brands and Brand Management

Instructor Teaching Notes

A session-by-session guide for teaching Chapter 7. It ties the Ch7 bundle together (textbook chapter, lecture video, slide deck, test bank) into one plan you can walk into class with. Chapter 7 has a full bundle, so these notes lean on it and show how each piece carries a different part of the session.

Where this sits: Week 5 of the 15-week pacing guide, paired with Chapter 8 (Product Management) in a "Brands and product" week. Brand work is the first of the three mix decisions Project 2 makes real, so the chapter lands while teams are deep in P2 (the strategy, STP, and mix deck), specifically while they are drafting the positioning statement. The AI rung that advances this week is rungs 2 to 3, "Use and connect": students run a generative-engine-optimization and citation-share check on their brand across three AI assistants, and the chapter's positioning widget shows them how a sharp brief turns a vague AI answer into one they can defend. The chapter follows STP (Ch6) directly, so students arrive holding the segment-and-position vocabulary the positioning section builds on.

1. At a glance

The big idea, in one sentence. A brand is a network of memory connections in the buyer's head, not the logo at its center, and the work is to build the right connections, defend the cues that retrieve them, and grow by being thought of in more buying situations rather than by being thought of as better.

The chapter's running brand. The chapter opens and closes on the Tylenol puzzle, which the lecture video uses as its hook: in 1982, seven people died from cyanide-laced Extra-Strength Tylenol capsules, the 37 percent share leader pulled 31 million capsules at a cost above $100 million, share fell to about 7 percent, and it climbed back to roughly 30 percent within a year. The running puzzle is why Tylenol recovered while Perrier, facing its own 1990 contamination crisis with the same fast response, never did (the contrast that makes brand equity concrete in §7.5). Three brands then carry the argument as "three depths," and a fourth is the warning: Patagonia (the behavior is the asset, a fifty-year pattern from dropping steel pitons in 1972 to transferring the company to a climate trust in 2022), Costco (availability without advertising, six operational cues refreshed across 30 to 40 visits a year), Liquid Death (the story before the product, a $1,500 video that planted the can, the skull, and the voice before any shelf accepted it, zero to a $1.4 billion valuation in seven years), and Bud Light (a position with no operational backing, roughly $1.4 billion in lost revenue and the top-selling-beer spot). Recurring supporting cases worth keeping straight: Coca-Cola vs Dr Pepper (double jeopardy), Stanley (the $23 premium and a shape-audit cue that scores below the pass line), Kit Kat (CEP expansion, paired with coffee, grew from $300M to $500M), Snickers (asset-first consistency), Tropicana (the 2009 redesign that cost about 20 percent of sales), and Arm & Hammer, Hyundai's Genesis, and Porsche's Cayenne (the repositioning cases).

Learning objectives (from the test bank, nine LOs). A student who finishes this chapter can:

  • LO 7.1 Define a brand as a memory structure (network of associations) and explain the difference between mental availability and differentiation.
  • LO 7.2 Describe how mental availability works: category entry points, CEP coverage, and how big brands outcompete small ones without being perceived as better.
  • LO 7.3 Identify the six types of distinctive assets, use the Fame-and-Uniqueness 2x2 to score them, and apply the shape-first audit.
  • LO 7.4 Explain the two empirical regularities of brand growth (double jeopardy and the NBD-Dirichlet pattern) and derive the penetration-first priority.
  • LO 7.5 Define brand equity using the three customer-ledger levers (WTP, CAC, retention) and explain how the three ISO 10668-compliant measurement approaches work.
  • LO 7.6 Build a brand positioning statement using the Three-Cs (consumer, competition, company) scaffold; distinguish points of parity from points of difference; apply the swap test and the onliness statement template.
  • LO 7.7 Explain repositioning: when to move, the Keller-vs-Kapferer consistency-vs-change debate, and the identity-brand risk.
  • LO 7.8 Apply the four-step brand-building playbook (audit assets, audit CEPs, refresh weak assets, expand into untapped CEPs) and the curse-of-knowledge / SUCCES / valuable-virality tests.
  • LO 7.9 Explain how AI assistants alter brand visibility through generative engine optimization (GEO), citation share, and the three-name answer.

Prerequisites. Sophomore standing; no prior marketing or computing background assumed. Students should arrive having read Chapter 6 (STP), because the positioning section (§7.6) builds directly on the points-of-parity and points-of-difference vocabulary STP introduced, and the perceptual map returns here as the gap-finding tool. Chapter 4 (How Buyers Buy) also helps, since the memory-structure model leans on how buyers retrieve a consideration set. A working AI assistant account is needed for the hands-on moment, and students will want access to three assistants (Claude, ChatGPT, Perplexity) for the citation-share activity. No quantitative prerequisite beyond reading a scatter and a 2x2.

3. Per-section teaching guidance

For each framework: the talking points, the one thing students must leave with, the concrete example to use, and the slide/sketch/widget to demo.

§7.1 What a brand actually is (LO 7.1)

  • Talking points. A brand is a pattern of connected information in long-term memory, centered on a name, with links of varying strength. Activate one node and the activation spreads. Consumer psychology calls it a memory structure; practitioners call it mental availability; the two names point at the same thing. A brand encodes through several channels at once (Schmitt's five experiential modules), and more channels means deeper memory, which is why an Apple Store reaches you through five doors and why the Stanley premium buys the ritual and the recognition signal, not a colder drink. Keller's CBBE pyramid sets the build order: you cannot buy resonance before earning salience.
  • The one thing to leave with. The brand is the network, not any single node. Remove the can and the words still pull the network; that robustness is why a strong brand survives a crisis.
  • Example. Liquid Death as the network (words + can + skull + voice + festival). The friend who defends Stanley cups (resonance) vs the friend who grabs a Yeti on sale (salience).
  • Demo. Slides 02, 04; chapter §7.1 brand-network sketch. Widget 7.1 ("What comes to mind?") is client-side, no AI: press a cue and watch the whole brand rise into the thought bubble, then hide the name and press again. A clean 60-second illustration of spreading activation, not the AI moment.

§7.2 Mental availability (LO 7.2)

  • Talking points. Mental availability is the probability the brand comes to mind when a buying situation arrives, and it splits into two measurable parts: how many situations the brand is linked to, and how strong each link is. A category entry point (CEP) is a buying situation that triggers a category thought. A brand grows by being thought of in more situations, not more intensely in the one it owns. The 95-5 rule redirects the budget: most buyers are out-of-market in any week, so brand work plants memory in the 95 percent for the cycle when they come in. Mental availability is not awareness; a brand can have 95 percent awareness and almost no availability for the post-workout moment.
  • The one thing to leave with. McDonald's wins on nine doorways, not a better burger. The strategic move for a one-CEP rival is a second and a third doorway, not a better patty.
  • Example. McDonald's nine CEPs vs a burger rival on "lunch" alone. The 95 percent awareness / no post-workout availability gap.
  • Demo. Slides 05–06; chapter §7.2 CEP wheel figure and the 95-5 grid sketch. The §7.2 widget (7.2, live Claude Haiku 4.5) asks the same brand two ways, "how do I make this brand more popular?" (broad) vs "which weekly buying situation should this brand own, and which untapped one has the highest frequency?" (sharp). A strong demo of "the question changed, not the model," and it can search the live web.

§7.3 Distinctive assets (LO 7.3)

  • Talking points. A distinctive asset is a non-name cue that retrieves the brand, on color, shape, sound, character, packaging, font, or slogan fragment. The test that separates an asset from decoration works the same on every channel: show the cue with no name to category buyers and ask which brand. The shape-first audit is the cheapest version, flash the bare silhouette for 1.5 seconds against a 50 percent pass line (Coke contour bottle 89, Toblerone 71, Stanley tumbler 43, below the line). A cue is judged on two separate tests: Fame (how many recognize it) and Uniqueness (how many of those name this brand). Plot the two and every cue lands in one of four verdicts: lock the strong corner for a decade, invest behind the rising cue, retire the category cliche, drop the decoration. Then the distinction that trips up most teams: distinctive means recognizable; differentiated means seen as better; only about 11 percent of buyers call their favorite brand different.
  • The one thing to leave with. Distinctiveness wins the moment of choice at the shelf, and differentiation justifies the higher price once the buyer has already recognized the brand. Lock the load-bearing cue; vary the executions, never the cue.
  • Example. Stanley's 43 (a viral surge, not a locked cue, so the next dollars go to locking an asset). Tropicana's 2009 redesign that cost about 20 percent of sales in two months because the buyer's path ran through the orange-with-a-straw picture, not the name.
  • Demo. Slides 07–10; chapter §7.3 Fame-by-Uniqueness 2x2 (both the sketch and the full SVG figure with six cues plotted), the shape-audit sketch, and the six-asset gallery. Widget 7.3 (w73, live Haiku) audits a real feed's recurring cues two ways, vague ("audit this brand's distinctive assets") vs sharp ("score each cue only from the posts I show you, mark fame as relative rank, flag any cue competitors also use"); presets for Liquid Death, Stanley, and a student feed.

§7.4 The empirical regularities of brand growth (LO 7.4)

  • Talking points. Watch real buyers over years and the same patterns repeat. Penetration drives growth, not loyalty: new-buyer campaigns beat existing-buyer campaigns about three to one on real sales, and loyalty programs lift frequency only slightly because most members would have come back anyway. Double jeopardy: a smaller brand carries two penalties at once, fewer buyers and buyers who return a little less often (Coke bought by about three-quarters of cola buyers around nine times a year, Dr Pepper by maybe a quarter around seven), and the gap between seven and nine is far too small to rescue the small brand. Duplication of purchase: there are no brand tribes, only one shared pool, and your buyers also buy rivals in proportion to those rivals' share (about 72 percent of Pepsi buyers also bought Coke). The 80/20 folklore is really closer to 50/20. Mental and physical availability multiply, so a zero on either is a zero overall.
  • The one thing to leave with. A small brand is small because few people buy it at all, and frequency follows penetration, so the only way off the double-jeopardy line is up and to the right at once: win more buyers, and frequency follows.
  • Example. Coke vs Dr Pepper (double jeopardy). The "Fanta segment" that is mostly the Coke segment that grabbed a Fanta last week (duplication). Coca-Cola high on both availabilities vs Liquid Death strong in memory but still building distribution.
  • Demo. Slides 11–14; chapter §7.4 double-jeopardy scatter (full SVG figure with twelve brands on the rising line) and the mental × physical figure. Widget 7.4 (w74) is client-side: pick a cola brand, drop it where you think it sits, and the chart shows whether it lands on the line, which makes the "frequency is set by size" point physical.

§7.5 Brand equity, formally (LO 7.5)

  • Talking points. Equity is the dollar value of customer lifetime value attributable to the brand, summed across the base. The brand moves three levers on that calculation: it raises willingness to pay (Stanley's $35 against a $12 thermally identical generic), lowers customer acquisition cost (Coca-Cola need not convince a buyer that cola exists), and extends retention (Patagonia keeps outerwear buyers about fifteen years against an industry mean near six). Why the buyer pays the premium: information asymmetry, the brand-as-signal. A firm with millions in a name has a strong reason to ship a cup that holds, so the $23 premium is the price of the uncertainty the brand removed. The three valuation firms (Interbrand, Kantar BrandZ, Brand Finance) disagree by hundreds of billions because they answer three different questions (earns now, might earn, costs to rent), all ISO 10668-compliant, so track one publisher's year-on-year delta, not the cross-publisher absolute. Equity (the summed dollar figure) is not brand power (the per-encounter force), which is why Nissan's equity beats Porsche's while Porsche's power is larger.
  • The one thing to leave with. Brand equity is the dollar value of the uncertainty the brand has removed, banked across the base. The §7.5 instrument drawer (Aaker's four pillars, the identity system, the Kapferer prism, the brand-relationship spectrum, the BAV PowerGrid) is reference material; do not lecture it, point to it.
  • Example. The Tylenol vs Perrier contrast resolves the opener: same crisis, same fast response, opposite outcomes, because Tylenol had banked two decades of memory and Perrier's connection was transactional. Toyota's 1989 Lexus launch as the brand-relationship-spectrum rightward move.
  • Demo. Slides 15–17; chapter §7.5 three-levers sketch, the valuation-triad sketch (Apple valued three ways, a ~2x spread), the CLV-attribution stack, and the Tylenol recovery curve. No widget in this section; it is the lecture-and-resolve beat.

§7.6 Positioning, the Three-Cs, and repositioning (LO 7.6, LO 7.7)

  • Talking points. A position is the sentence you want to own in the buyer's head. The onliness statement is the swap-test discipline: "Our [brand] is the only [category] that [does what] for [whom]," and if the sentence still reads true with a rival's name swapped in, it is generic. But onliness carries only the value-claim leg; the full agency statement has four blanks, "for [target], [brand] is the only [frame of reference] that [value claim], because [reasons to believe]," and a real brief fills all four. The Three-Cs scaffold the blanks: consumer (the job hired), competition (the frame of reference, the set the buyer should compare against, the way Genesis named BMW and Mercedes), company (the reasons to believe, the way Avis paired "We Try Harder" with "We're only #2"). Points of parity are the category must-haves a buyer demands before considering any brand; points of difference are what win the choice. The perceptual map (§7.6, distinct from the Fame-by-Uniqueness 2x2) plots rivals on two perceived axes and finds the empty quadrant; the map finds the gap and the onliness statement claims it. Repositioning is harder than positioning from scratch because the team overwrites associations the buyer already holds; the four blanks point the way (Arm & Hammer changed "for when," Genesis changed "relative to whom"). Keller says consistency keeps associations strong; Kapferer says relevance to changing needs matters; both are right, so lock the assets and reposition the meaning only when the culture has moved. The danger case is the identity brand (Porsche Cayenne): change who the brand is for and the base reads it as betrayal.
  • The one thing to leave with. The onliness line is the sharp output of a structured Three-Cs analysis, not a guess, and the swap test is what proves it is ownable. A position you cannot back operationally is the position that backfires.
  • Example. Patagonia's onliness statement survives against Columbia and REI; Liquid Death's survives against Saratoga and Topo Chico. Genesis (frame of reference), Avis (reason to believe), Arm & Hammer and Porsche Cayenne (repositioning).
  • Demo. Slides 18–19; chapter §7.6 perceptual-map sketch and the repositioning subsection. Two live widgets pair perfectly with the activity: Widget 7.7 (w77, "Map the category gap") asks the model to fix two axes, place brands from public positioning, and name the emptiest quadrant, then draws the map; Widget 7.6 (w76, "Write and audit a brand statement") runs the Three-Cs and assembles the four-part statement, then runs the swap test on top, with a Haiku/Opus model toggle.

§7.8 The brand-building playbook and the message tests (LO 7.8)

  • Talking points. The playbook turns the chapter into four steps a team runs as a loop. One, audit the distinctive assets (score each cue on fame and uniqueness, lock the load-bearing, retire the decoration). Two, audit the CEPs (list the situations, find which the brand owns and which are open), and before acting compute the noise floor, one standard deviation of the metric over the trailing eight quarters, so a 1.5-point move inside a 2.0-point floor is read as noise, not a result. Three, refresh the weak assets without breaking the strong (vary executions, never the load-bearing cue). Four, expand into the biggest untapped doorway (Kit Kat paired itself with coffee and grew a third in a year on the same 1935 wafer). Then the message tests: the curse of knowledge (the 1990 tapper study, tappers predicted 50 percent recognition, listeners reached 2.5 percent) explains why a team hears "premium hydration" as obvious; the SUCCES filter explains why Subway's "Jared" story beat "7 Under 6"; the valuable-virality test asks whether the story can be retold without the brand (Evian's dancing babies travel without Evian and share fell about 25 percent while views hit 50 million; Blendtec's "Will It Blend?" cannot be retold without the blender).
  • The one thing to leave with. Lock the load-bearing cues and vary only the executions, and compute the noise floor before you call any tracker move a result. A campaign that goes viral without the brand inside the share buys reach you never see at the shelf.
  • Example. Kit Kat and coffee (CEP expansion). Subway "Jared" vs "7 Under 6" (SUCCES). Evian vs Blendtec (valuable virality).
  • Demo. Slides (the playbook maps onto the deck's growth and closing slides); chapter §7.8 playbook-loop sketch, the tapper sketch, and the valuable-virality fork sketch. Widget 7.6 (w76) lives here too and is the natural place to show the Three-Cs statement build if you did not run it in §7.6.

§7.9 / The GEO move: brand visibility through AI assistants (LO 7.9)

  • Talking points. The buying conversation is moving. Brand monitoring now runs in two lanes: the legacy social-listening incumbents (Brandwatch, Sprinklr) and the new generative-engine-optimization lane (Profound, Evertune, AthenaHQ). The share of pre-purchase brand mentions traveling through human conversation fell from roughly 93 percent in 2013 to roughly 78 percent in 2024, with the rest now passing between a buyer and an AI assistant that composes a three-name answer. Citation share across the major assistants is the new share-of-search, because a brand absent from the three-name answer never enters consideration even when its social dashboard looks healthy. The chapter's habit: each week, paste the same question into Claude, ChatGPT, and Perplexity and log whether the brand appears and in which position; the four-week trend is the brand's citation share, and it differs across assistants because each model trained on a different slice of the web.
  • The one thing to leave with. A healthy social dashboard does not protect a brand absent from the AI three-name answer. Citation share is a thing students can measure this week, on their own brand, in about fifteen minutes.
  • Example. The four-week tracker across three assistants. The HBR argument that brand work is moving from paid persuasion to AI-mediated recommendation, won by publishing original data the assistants will cite.
  • Demo. Chapter §7.5-to-§7.6 transition carries the GEO prose and the "Do this weekly, four weeks" callout with the exact prompt; the four-week citation-share tracker sketch. This is the chapter's signature rung-2-to-3 AI move and the basis of the in-class GEO step (see §6 and §7).

4. Discussion prompts

Use four to six of these to drive the chapter's real debates. They work as cold-call, think-pair-share, or small-group prompts. The first two are the chapter's central tensions.

  1. The Tylenol question. "Tylenol and Perrier both faced a contamination crisis and both responded fast, yet Tylenol recovered within a year and Perrier never did. Why?" (Target: Tylenol had banked two decades of memory across several channels, doctor recommendation, packaging, shelf presence, so the deep network bent and re-formed. Perrier's connection was largely transactional, a green bottle and a French-water story, so once it broke there was little to spring back. This is test-bank CYU item 1 and the chapter's opener.)
  1. Distinctive vs differentiated. "Only about 11 percent of buyers say their favorite brand is different from its rivals. So does being better even matter?" (Target: it matters, but for a different job. Distinctiveness, being recognizable, wins the moment of choice at the shelf; differentiation justifies the premium once the buyer has already recognized the brand. A team that audits only what makes it special and never locks its cues loses buyers who never recognized it in the first place.)
  1. Narrow assets vs broad reach. "Liquid Death dominates two CEPs, festival and gym, and is nearly absent from eight others. Is that the right call?" (Target: it is coherent for an identity brand, because the punk aesthetic only works where that self-expression is legible, but it creates a double-jeopardy vulnerability, because winning two CEPs out of ten means low penetration and, by the regularity, low frequency. Expanding into the family dinner table is the high-risk move, an identity-brand betrayal, not a distribution problem. This is test-bank SA-2.)
  1. Is being right a defense (the position-with-no-backing question)? "Bud Light's stance was a real position. Patagonia's was a real position. Why did one backfire and one compound?" (Target: Patagonia's position was verifiable on four channels, product, supply chain, hiring, audited charity, all built before the position was taken, so the authenticity test passed. Bud Light borrowed a world it had no standing in, then retracted, which read as cowardice to one audience and as the original offense to the other. This is test-bank CYU item 7.)
  1. Lock it or move it? "Keller says a brand stays strong by holding consistent. Kapferer says a brand survives only by staying relevant to changing needs. Which is right?" (Target: both, about different parts of the brand. Lock the distinctive assets the buyer uses to find you; reposition the meaning around them only when the culture has genuinely moved. The Porsche Cayenne shows the cost of moving the meaning of an identity brand too far.)
  1. Does a healthy dashboard mean a healthy brand? "A brand's Instagram metrics look great. Is the brand safe?" (Target: not necessarily. If it is absent from the three-name answer that AI assistants return when buyers ask category questions, it never enters consideration, however healthy the social dashboard looks. Citation share across Claude, ChatGPT, and Perplexity is the AI-era share-of-search. This is test-bank Item 21 and AI-3.)

5. Common misconceptions and pitfalls

The specific places sophomores get brand work wrong, and how to correct each on the spot.

  • Treating the brand as the logo. Students hear "brand" and point at the logo or the color. Correct it with the network model: a brand is the whole memory structure, and Liquid Death is the can plus the skull plus the voice plus the festival, not the words alone. Keep saying "the network, not the node." This is test-bank Item 1, the most common entry-level error.
  • Confusing distinctive with differentiated. Students assume a strong brand is one buyers see as better. Correct it: distinctive means recognizable and wins the shelf; differentiated means better and justifies the premium; only 11 percent of buyers name a real difference. A correct audit scores sensory cues on Fame and Uniqueness, not strategy claims. This is the heart of test-bank AI-1, where the AI lists "edgy personality" and "sustainability commitment" (positioning claims) instead of the tallboy can and the skull (measurable cues).
  • Confusing mental availability with awareness. Students treat "everyone knows the brand" as success. Correct it: a brand can have 95 percent awareness and almost no availability for a specific situation. The operating number is mental availability per CEP, because awareness says the buyer could name it in a quiz, availability says whether it shows up at the moment that pays.
  • Reading double jeopardy as a loyalty problem. Students think a small brand needs more loyal customers. Correct it: the small brand is small because few people buy it at all, and the frequency gap (seven vs nine) is far too small to rescue it. Growth comes from penetration, and frequency follows.
  • Calling any empty quadrant on a perceptual map an opportunity. Students see white space and call it a position. Correct it the way Ch6 did: the map finds candidate gaps, but a gap is only real if buyers want to live there, and the onliness statement plus the swap test is what proves the claim is ownable, not just empty.
  • Writing a value claim and thinking the position is done. Students write "premium quality for discerning customers" and stop. Correct it with the swap test: every rival could say that word for word, so it is generic. A real statement fills all four blanks, target, frame of reference, value claim, reasons to believe, and survives the swap. This is test-bank SA-1 and AI-2.
  • Assuming a viral campaign builds the brand. Students equate views with brand growth. Correct it with the valuable-virality test: Evian's dancing babies reached 50 million views while share fell about 25 percent, because the babies travel without Evian. Ask whether the story can be retold without the brand in it.
  • Refreshing the logo to "modernize." Students (and incoming CMOs) want to redesign the load-bearing cue. Correct it with Tropicana: dropping the orange-with-a-straw picture cost about 20 percent of sales in two months. Lock the cues that score high on fame and uniqueness; vary the executions, never the cue. This is test-bank CYU item 8.

6. The hands-on AI moment

The chapter's AI move (rungs 2 to 3, "Use and connect"). The week's rung is using a live model's real capability, including live web search, and connecting the brand to the AI assistants that now mediate consideration. Chapter 7 carries six interactive widgets; three call a live model through the course proxy (the §7.2 CEP widget, the §7.3 feed-audit widget, the §7.6 statement widget, and the §7.6 map widget) and two run in the browser (the §7.1 cue-retrieval widget and the §7.4 double-jeopardy widget). The signature move that advances the rung this week is the GEO citation-share check, which is a connect-the-real-world move, not a single widget. For a 75-minute block, run one live widget in class (the §7.6 statement widget is the strongest because it ties to P2 positioning) and assign the four-week GEO tracker as the rung deliverable.

The widgets and the GEO move, by name (use what exists; do not invent):

  • Widget 7.1 "What comes to mind?" (client-side, no AI). Press a cue, watch the whole brand rise into the thought bubble, then hide the name and press again. A 60-second illustration of spreading activation, not the AI moment.
  • Widget 7.2 "Which situations does the brand own?" (live Claude Haiku 4.5, web search on). The same brand asked broad ("how do I make this brand more popular?") vs sharp ("which weekly buying situation should this brand own, and which untapped one has the highest frequency?"). Presets for Snickers, Liquid Death, Costco, and Topo Chico.
  • Widget 7.3 "Audit a feed's distinctive assets" (live Haiku). Reads a feed's recent posts and sorts cues from decoration two ways: vague ("audit this brand's distinctive assets") vs sharp ("score each cue only from the posts I show you, mark fame as relative rank, flag any cue competitors also use"). The best demo of "the instruction changed, not the model," and it maps onto test-bank AI-1 and AI-4.
  • Widget 7.4 "Drop a brand on the double-jeopardy line" (client-side, no AI). Pick a cola brand, drop it, and see whether it lands on the line.
  • Widget 7.6 "Write and audit a brand statement" (live, Haiku/Opus toggle, web search). The sharp brief walks the Three-Cs and assembles the four-part positioning statement, then runs the swap test on top. Presets for Liquid Death, Stanley, and Patagonia, plus type-your-own.
  • Widget 7.7 "Map the category gap" (live, web search). Vague map uses axes you cannot defend; the sharp map fixes two axes, places each brand from public positioning, and names the emptiest quadrant, then draws it. Pairs with the activity's map step.
  • The GEO four-week citation-share tracker (the §7.6-transition "Do this weekly, four weeks" callout). The chapter gives the exact prompt: "Name the top three [category] brands for [specific use-case], with one line on why each." Paste it into Claude, ChatGPT, and Perplexity each week and log position (1, 2, 3, or absent) per assistant.

How to run the §7.6 widget live (about 8 minutes, inside the positioning block).

  1. Project Widget 7.6 and load the Liquid Death preset.
  2. Press the vague button ("Write a tagline for this brand") first and read what comes back. Ask the room: "Could a rival say this word for word?" They will see the slogan is undisprovable.
  3. Switch to the sharp button ("Run the Three-Cs and build the full four-part statement") and run it again. Walk the output: which blank is the frame of reference, which is the value claim, which are the reasons to believe, and what the swap test flags.
  4. Make the teaching point: the model did not get smarter between the two runs; the request did. A vague brief returns a slogan no one can disprove; a sharp brief returns a statement you can defend, and the swap test is the audit.

What students produce (the rung deliverable). Each student (or pair) runs the four-week GEO citation-share check on the brand they carry from P1, using the chapter's prompt across Claude, ChatGPT, and Perplexity. They log the brand's position in each assistant each week and write three or four sentences: which rivals took the slots, how the answer differed across assistants and why (different training slices and live sources), and what one move would lift the brand into the three-name answer. This is the rung-2-to-3 deliverable and doubles as their AI-use disclosure for the week. (For a single class meeting where four weeks is not possible, run one week live in class and have them complete the remaining three on their own.)

How to debrief it. Pull two or three student trackers onto the screen. Ask: "Where does this brand show up, where does it vanish, and what would change that?" Drive the chapter's standard: the AI read is a candidate, not a measurement, so a brand team validates against a real prompted-recall survey before acting. Connect forward: the §7.3 feed-audit widget and the §7.6 statement widget do the same vague-vs-sharp move on assets and positioning, and Chapter 18 shows how to save these audits as a reusable Skill the team reruns every quarter.

7. In-class activity: the distinctive-asset and positioning audit

Goal. Students run the chapter's brand audit on one real brand, in teams, in about 18 minutes, and leave having done the four-step diagnostic once rather than only heard it.

Setup. Pick one brand the whole room knows and project Widget 7.7 (the map) and Widget 7.6 (the statement) so the positioning steps are live. Good choices: a hydration or energy-drink brand, a campus coffee shop, or a sneaker brand. Use the brand the chapter and deck already work (Liquid Death or Stanley) only if the class needs the scaffolding; a fresh brand forces real thinking. Have three AI assistants open in tabs for the closing GEO step.

Instructions (give these as four timed steps, the chapter's Job Aid).

  1. Sort the cues on fame and uniqueness (4 min). List the brand's cues, color, shape, sound, character, packaging, font, slogan fragment. Place each in the 2x2. Which one is load-bearing (high on both, lock it) and which is decoration (low on both, drop it)? "If you cannot name a single cue you would recognize without the logo, the brand has built no distinctive asset yet."
  2. Name the doorways (3 min). Write the buying situations the brand already owns in your head, then name one untapped doorway with high weekly frequency the brand could plausibly own. Which one would you invest behind first?
  3. Write the onliness statement and run the swap test (5 min). Fill the line out loud: "Our [brand] is the only [category] that [does what] for [whom]." Then drop a competitor's name into the same sentence. If it still reads true, tighten it until only your brand fits. Use Widget 7.6 to assemble the full four-part version and let the swap test audit it.
  4. Check the citation share (6 min). On the open assistants, paste the chapter's prompt, "Name the top three [category] brands for [use-case]," into Claude, ChatGPT, and Perplexity, and record whether the brand appears and where. Is the brand in the three-name answer, and if not, which rivals took the slots?

What good looks like. A strong team names a genuinely load-bearing cue and a real piece of decoration rather than waving all the cues through, picks a high-frequency untapped doorway rather than a vague "be everywhere," writes an onliness statement that survives the swap test (not "high quality," which any rival claims), and reads its citation-share result honestly, noticing when the brand is absent from one assistant and present in another and asking why. A weak team lists the logo as the only cue, claims a generic point of difference, and treats a single strong week in one assistant as proof the brand is healthy. Use the gap between the two to re-teach distinctive-vs-differentiated and the citation-share point in the wrap.

Tie to the project. This activity is a dry run of the brand-and-positioning half of Project 2. Tell students that: the distinctive-asset audit, the onliness statement, and the citation-share read are all inputs to the P2 strategy deck. The brand they do here is practice; the brand they carry from P1 is the real thing, and the GEO check they run for homework is on that real brand.

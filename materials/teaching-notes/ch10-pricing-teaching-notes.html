Chapter 10: Pricing

Instructor Teaching Notes

A session-by-session guide for teaching Chapter 10. It ties the Ch10 bundle together (textbook chapter, the rendered lecture video, the coral slide deck, the test bank) into one plan you can walk into class with. Chapter 10 has the fullest material set in the course, so these notes lean on that bundle and show how each piece carries a different part of the session.

Where this sits: Week 6 of the 15-week pacing guide, paired with Chapter 9 (Services) in a "Services and pricing" week. Pricing is the second of the three mix decisions Project 2 makes real, so it lands while teams are deep in P2 (the strategy, STP, and mix deck). The AI rung that advances this week is rungs 3 to 4, "Connect and build": students run a willingness-to-pay and fairness audit on a real pricing rollout, and the chapter's flagship widget shows them how to route a pricing task to the right model tier. Midterm 1 (Ch1 to Ch9) lands around this point in the pacing guide, so this is also the week the pricing material first gets clustered for a real exam.

1. At a glance

The big idea, in one sentence. Price is the only marketing decision that brings in revenue while every other decision spends it, and setting it well means reading three references at once (cost is the floor, the competitor is the frame, and the buyer's willingness to pay is the ceiling), then watching the fairness ceiling that decides how long any price holds, more carefully than the algorithm that computes it.

The chapter's running example. Three brands carry the whole chapter as structural commitments, and the chapter opens and closes on a fourth case that frames the stakes. The opener and closer is the EpiPen: a two-pack climbed from about $100 in 2007 to $608 by 2016 in roughly fifteen small steps, each below the just-noticeable difference, and the climb only broke at a 2016 Senate hearing when the cumulative line became visible and the dual-entitlement story collapsed. The three worked brands are Costco (competitor-based pricing: a 14 percent markup cap keeps the shelf visibly cheap while the membership fee carries roughly 70 percent of profit), Patagonia (value-based pricing defended by purpose: the $279 Down Sweater held up by a lifetime repair guarantee, the 1% for the Planet pledge, and the Worn Wear resale program), and Liquid Death (value-based pricing defended by identity: $2.49 for canned water that costs under $0.30 to make, running on the Veblen effect). The chapter's recurring teaching product is the premium tumbler (Stanley Quencher at $45 against Yeti, Owala, and Hydro Flask), which threads through the elasticity math, the Van Westendorp band, and the flagship competitor audit. Caterpillar carries the value-based EVC walk ($90,000 base climbing to a $110,000 ceiling, closing at $100,000).

Learning objectives (from the test bank, eight LOs). A student who finishes this chapter can:

  • LO 10.1 Explain why price is the only mix element that creates revenue and how a small price change produces an asymmetric profit effect.
  • LO 10.2 Describe the four pricing approaches (cost-based, competitor-based, value-based, customer-determined) and choose the right approach for a given context.
  • LO 10.3 Distinguish markup from margin and apply breakeven and marginal-cost arithmetic to a pricing decision.
  • LO 10.4 Explain how willingness to pay is measured using Van Westendorp's PSM, conjoint analysis, price experiments, and scanner data.
  • LO 10.5 Identify the major psychological pricing mechanisms (reference pricing, decoy effect, price-quality inference) and predict how each shifts buyer choice.
  • LO 10.6 Describe how dynamic pricing and price discrimination work, and apply the six-principle fairness audit to a real rollout.
  • LO 10.7 Explain freemium, outcome-based, and gainsharing pricing models and connect them to AI-agent product economics.
  • LO 10.8 Analyze pricing fairness using the dual-entitlement principle and explain why shrinkflation and skimpflation carry detection risk.

Prerequisites. Sophomore standing; no prior marketing or computing background assumed. Students should arrive having read Chapter 9 (Services), because the chapter opens by handing the perishable-capacity problem forward from services (a seat or room that goes empty tonight is gone), and the yield-management section picks it up directly. A working AI assistant account is needed for the hands-on moment. Light comfort with percentages is the one quantitative prerequisite, since this chapter does the most arithmetic in the course (breakeven, contribution margin, markup-versus-margin, elasticity). If students have done some of P2 already, the value-based section lands faster because they have a real brand to price.

2. Timing plan: a 75-minute block

This maps the chapter to one 75-minute session. Pricing is the most framework-dense and most arithmetic-heavy chapter in the course, so the plan protects two things: the four-approach spine (because everything else hangs off it) and the in-class activity (because students learn pricing by pricing something, not by hearing nine frameworks). It runs lean on the deep machinery (the Nagle pyramid, the pocket-price waterfall, conjoint mechanics, the three degrees of discrimination) and pushes the arithmetic into the activity rather than lecturing all of it.

3. Per-section teaching guidance

For each framework: the talking points, the one thing students must leave with, the concrete example to use, and the slide/sketch/widget to demo.

§10.1 Asymmetric leverage (LO 10.1)

  • Talking points. Every other mix element spends money; price is the only one that brings it in. The arithmetic: $100 revenue, $97 cost, $3 profit. Raise price 1 percent at flat cost and profit goes to $4, a 33 percent gain on a 1 percent move. Matching that through volume takes 33 percent more units, because every extra unit drags its share of cost. The reason is marginal-cost asymmetry: selling the same unit higher costs zero more.
  • The one thing to leave with. A 1 percent price move beats a 1 percent volume move by an order of magnitude, yet most teams are paid to manage volume.
  • Example. Netflix over a long horizon ($7.99 in 2010 to $15.99 in 2024, the moves passing straight to the bottom line because one more streamed minute costs nothing). Contrast with a grocery chain raising margarine a dollar, where the next tub still has to be bought and shipped.
  • Demo. Slides 02–03; chapter §10.1 pricing-leverage and volume-vs-price sketches. The video's opening makes the same point with the two cost terms first.

§10.2 The four pricing approaches (LO 10.2, LO 10.3)

  • Talking points. Three references a firm can read: cost is the floor, the competitor is the frame, value is the ceiling. Cost is easiest to know and value is hardest, so most firms anchor on what they can measure and leave the ceiling unread. Cost-based adds a markup (honest home: regulated utilities). Competitor-based anchors to rivals and risks the reflexive match; teach the four-question check before reacting to a rival's move (why did they move, is it permanent, what does not responding cost, what does our response trigger). Value-based is the EVC walk. Customer-determined hands the buyer the pen (Priceline, Humble Bundle, reverse auctions) under three conditions: the firm accepts some low prices, WTP varies widely, and realized prices stay private.
  • The one thing to leave with. Only value-based pricing is tied to the buyer's wallet; the other three are tied to the firm's costs, a rival's sticker, or a mechanism. A better pricing conversation triangulates all three references.
  • Example. Caterpillar's dealer bid is the worked EVC case: a $90,000 competitor base, then $7,000 (longer component life) + $6,000 (fewer breakdowns) + $5,000 (same-day parts) + $2,000 (warranty), each verifiable against the buyer's own logs, summing to a $110,000 ceiling, closing at a $100,000 discount both sides read as a win. The discount is the closing move; the EVC walk is what made it possible.
  • Arithmetic to teach here. Breakeven (fixed cost / contribution margin; the $5 campus drink at $1.50 cost breaks even at 2,000 cups, and a $6 price drops it to ~1,556 while a $4 price pushes it to 2,800). Markup vs margin (a $60 cost at $90 is a 50 percent markup but a 33 percent margin). These are the two most testable mechanics in the chapter.
  • Demo. Slides 06–07; chapter §10.2 four-approaches price ladder (fig-1), the EVC waterfall sketch, and the breakeven sketch. The §10.2 "Pick the pricing approach for a real brand" live-AI widget turns a named brand and segment into one of the four approaches with dollar reasoning and a sample band, vague vs sharp; this is a strong demo for the AI moment (see §6).

§10.3 Willingness to pay and price elasticity (LO 10.4)

  • Talking points. Value-based pricing is only as good as the firm's read of WTP, the most a buyer would pay before walking away. WTP varies across buyers, which is what gives the demand curve its slope. Elasticity measures how much volume moves when price moves; the cross-category average is about -2.62, and below -1 a category is elastic (an increase loses more than it adds). Six conditions push a category toward inelastic (distinctive product, price the buyer does not notice, sticky habits, a cost-or-scarcity story, small spend relative to income, a third party paying), and each is also a lever to widen the acceptable band. The hard part is knowing which curve you are on; the escape is a matched-market test. For launches with no history, the four reservation-price procedures run cheapest-to-most-valid: direct asking, Van Westendorp, conjoint, scanner data.
  • The one thing to leave with. A brand that reads weak volume and keeps cutting price may have been on the cheap side of its own curve all along, so the cut was never the cure. Use Van Westendorp at launch because 200 respondents is what a campus project supports.
  • Example. The tumbler at -1.5 (raised $45 to $49.50, volume fell 200 to 170, so the increase was a net loss). The Van Westendorp meter on canned water priced $1–5, with the optimal price point where "too cheap" meets "too expensive."
  • Demo. Slides 11–13; chapter §10.3 Van Westendorp figure (fig-2). The PSM explorer widget lets you drag the four medians and watch the band's crossings move; the point is narrow and worth holding (the four medians are the four questions, and the band is whatever their crossings say). The conjoint material (the EINC roofing case, where aesthetics earned almost no premium while durability earned most of it) is a good drawer to open only if time allows.

§10.4 Psychological pricing (LO 10.5)

  • Talking points. Buyers read prices in System-1 mode against a stored reference point. The reference is a moving average of recent exposures, so a $600 dress reads moderate next to a $1,200 sibling and expensive next to a $200 one, and the firm can move the reference by choosing the comparison (anchoring). Buyers miss changes below the just-noticeable difference (5 to 15 percent). The decoy effect is the headline: add an option dominated on every attribute by one of two existing options, and choice shifts to the dominator; the decoy is never picked. Distinguish it from the compromise effect, which needs no dominated option (the middle just reads as the defensible choice). Charm pricing (9-endings) works where buyers do not comparison-shop and fails where they look the number up, which is why premium brands round to whole dollars.
  • The one thing to leave with. The surrounding options, not the item alone, set the buyer's read of a price. A dominated third option nobody buys can still earn its place by reframing the comparison.
  • Example. The Economist subscription page (web $59, print $125, print+web $125): with the decoy, 84 percent pick print+web; remove it and 68 percent pick web-only. Spotify's Free/Premium/Family for the compromise effect. Apple's $1,099 iPhone next to Patagonia's round $139/$199/$299 for why premium positioning avoids 9-endings.
  • Demo. Slide 15; chapter §10.4 decoy figure (fig-3). The decoy designer widget is the one to run live: place a decoy, move its price and features, and watch the predicted split shift, then break it (drop the decoy below ~$90 or give it real digital features and dominance collapses). This is a reveal-a-principle widget, not a calculator; it shows the student when the decoy works and when it stops working.

§10.5 Dynamic pricing and price discrimination (LO 10.6)

  • Talking points. The approaches so far assume one price held until the next cycle; dynamic pricing drops that, quoting a number near each buyer's WTP at the moment. The 2024–2026 academic record says the binding limit is governance, not computing power: managers hold back because the model's recommendation is a black box they cannot defend to a regulator, and the fairness exposure runs through the brand. Price discrimination comes in three degrees (first: each buyer's max; second: by quantity; third: by segment), and five conditions must hold, the binding one being that the rollout must not produce resentment severe enough to damage the brand. Yield management is the form dynamic pricing takes for fixed perishable capacity (the seat, the room handed forward from Chapter 9). What separates a tolerated surge from a rejected one is the six-principle fairness audit: explicability, transparency, cooperative framing, category tolerance, brand-promise compatibility, pre-launch communication.
  • The one thing to leave with. A 2026 dynamic-pricing decision is a brand-and-governance decision wrapped around an algorithm. Run the six-principle audit before launch; a rollout that breaks two or more is structurally vulnerable to the Wendy's-pattern reversal.
  • Example. The Indianapolis Zoo (single $16.95 price replaced by a published grid from an $8 Tuesday floor to a $30 Saturday peak; weekday attendance moved 57 to 67 percent, revenue rose 12 percent, and access widened for fixed-income families) versus Wendy's (announced a peak-hour add framed as a margin tool, reversed in twelve days). The two cases sit side by side because the mechanism is identical and only the framing and the off-peak floor differ.
  • Demo. Slides 16–18; chapter §10.5 six-principles 3-by-2 grid sketch (each cell names one 2024 failure mode), the yield-management sketch, and the Uber-surge tolerance sketch. The Stonegate pint case (a familiar, frequently bought product with a tight reference price) is the cleanest category-tolerance failure.

§10.6 Freemium, subscription, and pricing innovations (LO 10.7)

  • Talking points. When marginal cost rounds to zero, the problem inverts from rationing access to converting it. Freemium and subscription are both variants of two-part pricing. Freemium is a conversion problem before it is a pricing one: the converted cohort has to cover fixed development and acquisition across the whole free-plus-paid population (Spotify ~40 percent paid, Duolingo ~8 percent, Notion ~4 percent, team tools 25 to 30 percent). The means-and-ends frame asks whether the firm charges for the product (the means) or the outcome (the end), arrayed on a risk-transfer ladder: pay-per-use, tiered subscription, outcome-based, gainsharing. Outcome-based is the 2026 default for AI-agent products because an invoice tied to completed work fits a tool whose value is the work done.
  • The one thing to leave with. In freemium the lever is the conversion rate and the cohort economics, not the headline price, and a conversion lift that pulls in less-committed users who churn faster can flatten the lifetime-value math even as the dashboard shows a win.
  • Example. HubSpot Breeze at $0.50 per resolved support ticket (the buyer pays nothing when the AI fails). Adobe's move from a $2,500 boxed Creative Suite to $60-a-month Creative Cloud. The JPMorgan-Persado gainsharing deal, where Persado was paid a percentage of the documented copy lift.
  • Demo. Chapter §10.6 freemium funnel sketch and the means-and-ends risk ladder. The freemium widget runs a 24-month cohort, but it is built to expose one trap, not to total a number: the "show me the trap" controls bump conversion +10 points, then add 1 point of churn, so the lifetime-value reversal emerges. Run the two buttons in order if you project it; the reversal is the lesson, not any single LTV/CAC figure.

§10.7 The fairness ceiling (LO 10.8)

  • Talking points. Buyers reject economically efficient transactions they read as unfair, and they pay a personal cost to do it. The mechanism is the dual-entitlement principle: buyers grant the firm the right to defend its reference profit against rising costs (a cost-driven increase reads as fair) but not to capture windfall profit from a demand spike (the same increase after a heatwave reads as gouging). The same dollar produces opposite reactions depending on the story. A 2024 JACR study found buyers rate an algorithm-set demographic price gap as more fair than the identical human-set gap, because a human had the agency to choose otherwise (inviting an inference of unfavorable intent) while an algorithm reads as a low-agency rule-follower. The operational rule is binary: profit-driven personalized pricing belongs behind a published-rule algorithm; prosocial pricing (senior, hardship, student) keeps a human face, where the contrast reverses. When a posted increase is itself the brand risk, the firm has four routes: post it, absorb it, shrinkflation (hold price, shrink package), or skimpflation (hold price and size, substitute cheaper inputs). Routes 3 and 4 are covert by design, and detection risk depends on category recall.
  • The one thing to leave with. The fairness ceiling binds tighter than anything the algorithm can compute. The same dollar reads as fair after a cost rise and as gouging after a demand spike.
  • Example. Netflix price-hike emails citing content investment (a cost story). The campus tutor whose "Tuesday $20 study session" reads as fair but whose "$40 exam-week emergency rate" reads as gouging on the same spread. Shrinkflation in paper towels (low recall, slow detection) versus gasoline (high recall, immediate detection).
  • Demo. Slide 18; chapter §10.7 dual-entitlement line sketch. The shrinkflation/skimpflation distinction and the Casey-Warren regulatory trend are the spine of test-bank SA-4.

Brand applications: Costco, Patagonia, Liquid Death (synthesis, LO 10.2)

  • Talking points. The three brands show what happens when a firm picks one approach as a structural commitment and runs the rest of the business off it. Costco commits to everyday-low-price at the SKU level (14 percent markup cap), made viable by the membership fee that carries ~70 percent of profit and absorbs the margin penalty no unbacked low-price retailer survives. Patagonia commits to value-with-purpose, defended by structure (repair guarantee turns the high price into a competitive per-year cost, the pledge converts price into identity, Worn Wear cannibalizes new sales on purpose). Liquid Death commits to value-with-positioning, running on the Veblen effect (a higher price lifts demand among buyers signaling identity).
  • The one thing to leave with. A premium has to be defended by structure, not advertising. A values premium without real value creation is moralism; an identity premium without the distinctive-asset infrastructure is just an expensive bottle of water nobody wants.
  • Demo. Slide 19; chapter brand-applications figure (fig-4) mapping all three to their approaches. This section sets up the flagship competitor-audit widget (see §6).

4. Discussion prompts

Use four to six of these to drive the chapter's real debates. They work as cold-call, think-pair-share, or small-group prompts. The first two are the chapter's central tensions.

  1. Cost-plus vs value-based. "A founder says 'we just take our production cost and add a 40 percent markup, and that's the price.' What is wrong with that, and what would you do instead?" (Target: cost-plus reads the floor and leaves the ceiling unread, so it sets the price too low when WTP exceeds cost-plus or mispositions the brand when it overshoots. Value-based starts from what the buyer would otherwise pay. This is test-bank SA-1, the oat-milk-latte case.)
  1. When is a dynamic price fair? "Yield management and surge pricing use the same logic, but the Indianapolis Zoo's rollout succeeded and Wendy's reversed in twelve days. What separates a tolerated dynamic price from a rejected one?" (Target: the Zoo's off-peak price genuinely fell below the old single price and the calendar was published in advance, so budget-constrained families paid less; Wendy's announced only a peak-hour add framed as a margin tool. Run the six-principle audit. This is the chapter's central governance point.)
  1. The EpiPen question. "The drug never changed and the cost never moved, yet the price climbed fivefold over nine years and then came back down. What set the price, and what brought it down?" (Target: each step sat below the just-noticeable difference and the cost story absorbed it, so the climb worked until the cumulative line became visible at a Senate hearing. The fairness ceiling, not the marginal cost, brought it down, because parents of allergic children were the vulnerable group and the product was a necessity.)
  1. The algorithm-vs-human fairness flip. "A 2024 study found buyers rated an algorithm-set demographic price gap as more fair than the identical gap set by a human manager. Why, and what should a firm do with that finding?" (Target: the unfairness judgment follows from inferred intent, not the size of the gap; a human could have chosen otherwise, an algorithm reads as a low-agency rule-follower. The operational rule is binary: profit-driven personalized pricing behind a published-rule algorithm, prosocial pricing with a human face. This is test-bank Item 22 and AI-3.)
  1. Does a conversion win always help? "A freemium app raises its trial-to-paid conversion from 4 to 6 percent. Is that always good news?" (Target: not necessarily. Conversion drives paid-user volume, but if the lift pulls in less-committed users who churn faster, the lifetime-value math can flatten even as the dashboard shows a larger paid cohort. The lever is the cohort economics, not the conversion rate alone. This connects to test-bank Item 20 and the freemium widget's trap.)
  1. Covert price increases. "When a posted price increase would hurt the brand, firms can shrink the package (shrinkflation) or swap in cheaper inputs (skimpflation). Are these dishonest, and when does a firm get caught?" (Target: both are covert by design, and detection depends on category recall: paper towels hide a shrink for months, gasoline is caught immediately because the per-unit price is on a roadside sign and bought every few days. The 2026 Casey-Warren bill would force per-unit disclosure and erase the low-detection advantage. This is test-bank SA-4 and Items 19/23.)

5. Common misconceptions and pitfalls

The specific places sophomores get pricing wrong, and how to correct each on the spot.

  • Confusing markup with margin. The single most common arithmetic error in this chapter. Students compute a 50 percent markup, call it a 50 percent margin, and overstate their profitability. Correct it with the $60-to-$90 example every time: markup uses cost as the base (and can pass 100 percent), margin uses price as the base (and never can). Make them do one of each on a card before the activity, because the breakeven that follows depends on getting the margin right.
  • Treating cost-plus as value-based. Students hear "we charge a premium" and assume that is value-based pricing, when adding a markup to a more expensive product is still cost-based. Correct it: the question that makes pricing value-based is "what would this buyer otherwise pay," not "what did this cost us." Liquid Death's $2.49 is value-based; the same $2.49 justified by "aluminum cans cost more" would be cost-based.
  • Reading any empty quadrant on the value stick as money on the table. Students see consumer surplus above the price and conclude the firm should always raise the price to capture it. Correct it with elasticity: raising price moves volume, and below -1 the increase loses more than it adds. The surplus is what keeps the buyer buying.
  • Assuming the cheapest WTP method is the worst. Students dismiss direct asking and reach straight for conjoint. Correct it: the four procedures are a sequence, and Van Westendorp is the right launch-stage default because 200 respondents and twenty minutes is what a campus project supports, while conjoint costs three to five times more. The method fits the question and the budget.
  • Picking the biggest surge multiplier. Students assume dynamic pricing means charging the most the moment demand spikes. Correct it with the fairness ceiling: a surge that breaks category tolerance or reads as extraction reverses in days (Wendy's, Stonegate). The same mechanism framed as an off-peak discount with a published calendar succeeds (the Zoo).
  • Believing "the algorithm decided" removes responsibility. Students assume automation launders a pricing decision. Correct it: the binding limit on dynamic pricing is governance, and a price the firm cannot defend to a regulator is a Section 5 risk (the Amazon Project Nessie case). The fairness exposure still runs through the brand.
  • Thinking a higher conversion rate is always a win. Students read the freemium funnel as "more conversions, more revenue." Correct it with the widget's trap: a conversion lift that pulls in faster-churning users flattens the lifetime-value math. The cohort economics, not the funnel volume, decide whether the model funds itself.
  • Confusing the decoy with the compromise effect. Students call any three-tier menu a decoy. Correct it: a decoy is dominated on every attribute by one of the other options (and is never picked), while the compromise effect needs no dominated option, because the middle just reads as the safe choice. The Economist page is a decoy; Spotify's Free/Premium/Family is compromise.

6. The hands-on AI moment

The chapter's AI move (rungs 3 to 4, "Connect and build"). The week's rung is connecting a model to real pricing work and reading where the model's tier changes the answer. Chapter 10 carries several interactive widgets. For a 75-minute block, run one live in class (the flagship) and assign the Apply-box workflow as homework. Note up front: the freemium widget reveals a principle, not a number. It is not a calculator. It runs a cohort sim, but its purpose is to surface the conversion-versus-churn trap, so if you project it, run the two "show me the trap" buttons in order and let the lifetime-value reversal emerge, rather than treating it as a tool for computing LTV/CAC.

The widgets, by name (use what exists; do not invent):

  • §10.2 "Pick the pricing approach for a real brand" (live model). Name a brand and a buyer segment, or pick a preset (Caterpillar B2B bid, Spotify new market, a regulated utility), then ask the vague way ("what should this cost?") and the sharp way ("which of the four approaches fits, with the dollar reasoning and a sample band?"). The vague ask returns a generic verdict; the sharp ask names one of the four approaches and anchors it. The clean "the instruction changed, not the model" demo.
  • §10.3 Van Westendorp PSM explorer (client-side). Drag the four medians and watch the four cumulative curves redraw and the band's crossings move. A reveal-a-principle widget: the four medians are the four questions, and the band is whatever their crossings say.
  • §10.4 Decoy designer (client-side). Place a decoy, move its price and features, and watch the predicted buyer split shift, then watch dominance break when the decoy drops below about $90 or gets real features. Shows when the decoy works and when it stops working.
  • §10.6 Freemium funnel (client-side, reveal-a-principle). A 24-month cohort sim built to expose one trap: the "win 10 points of conversion" then "but they churn 1 point faster" buttons make the lifetime-value reversal emerge. Not a calculator.
  • The flagship: §10 competitor-price audit, two model tiers (live model). Sends one prompt to claude-haiku-4-5 (fast) and claude-opus-4-7 (deep) in parallel, both searching the live web for current shelf prices, and shows the two audits side by side. The preset audits Stanley's $45 Quencher against Yeti, Owala, and Hydro Flask.

How to run the flagship live (about 8 minutes, inside the brand-applications block or the activity).

  1. Project the §10 competitor-audit widget. Keep the Stanley preset (or type a brand the class knows).
  2. Click Run on both models. While they search and stream, tell the room what to watch for: not which AI is better, but where the cheap tier reduces the audit to a numeric comparison and where the deep tier pulls in the structure (the decoy framing, the Veblen signaling, the fairness ceiling, the brand-equity story).
  3. Read the two panes out loud. Haiku usually returns a price band and a one-line rationale, correct on the numbers and silent on the structure. Opus pulls the levers into one recommendation and names which lever the firm is actually choosing when it holds the price.
  4. Make the teaching point: the cost gap of roughly 15 times per token is the inverse of the use-case gap. Route routine Skill calls (the same procedure on every category) to Haiku, and a quarterly review that changes the brand strategy to Opus, because there the reasoning is the deliverable.

What students produce. Each student (or pair) runs the Apply-box workflow on a real product they buy or would launch: ask the model to pick the approach and a band, write and field the four Van Westendorp questions on five friends, ask for a three-option lineup that uses the decoy or compromise effect, then run the sharp competitor audit and the lazy "is my price good?" version and compare. They write two or three sentences on what the sharp ask returned that the vague one missed. This is the rung-3-to-4 deliverable and doubles as their AI-use disclosure for the week.

How to debrief it. Pull two or three student outputs onto the screen. Ask: "Where was the model right on the numbers but silent on the structure?" Drive the chapter's standard: the model is a fast first draft, and both tiers search the live web so the read is current, but every figure, name, and date has to be verified before you act. Connect forward: packaging the audit as a reusable Skill and wiring a competitor-price MCP server is the build work Chapter 18 takes up, which is the next rung on the ladder.

7. In-class activity: price a real product

Goal. Students run the chapter's pricing sequence on one real product, in teams, in about 14 minutes, and leave having priced something rather than only heard the frameworks.

Setup. Pick one product the whole room knows, or let teams use the brand they carry from P1. Good choices: the premium tumbler the chapter and deck already work (use it if the class needs scaffolding), a campus coffee drink, a streaming service, or an Etsy-style item a student would actually sell. Project the §10 competitor-audit widget so the audit step is live.

Instructions (give these as four timed steps).

  1. Place it on the value stick (3 min). What is the floor (roughly, the unit cost)? What is the frame (what do the nearest two or three rivals charge)? What is the ceiling (what would a buyer who would otherwise buy the rival pay for this one)? Each team writes a one-line floor, frame, and ceiling. "If you cannot name a buyer who would pay the ceiling, you do not have a ceiling yet."
  1. Pick the approach and run the arithmetic (3 min). Which of the four approaches fits, and why? Then one quick number: at your chosen price, what is the contribution margin, and how many units break even against a fixed cost you estimate? Check that your markup and your margin are not the same number.
  1. Write the four Van Westendorp questions (3 min). Too expensive to consider, too cheap to trust, getting expensive but worth it, a bargain. If you have a phone handy, ask one or two people in the room and see where the answers cluster. Five answers give a rough band off the crossings.
  1. Run the sharp competitor audit (5 min). On the projected widget (or each team's own assistant), ask: "audit my $X against these rivals, name the two or three levers the recommendation rests on (decoy, Veblen signaling, the fairness ceiling, elasticity, an anchor), and give the price to hold next quarter using current shelf prices." Then ask the lazy version, "is my price good?", and compare. The gap is most of the lesson.

What good looks like. A strong team names a real buyer behind the ceiling rather than asserting one, gets the markup-versus-margin distinction right without prompting, picks an approach that matches the product (value-based for a differentiated item, not cost-plus by reflex), and, the real tell, reads the sharp audit critically (catches a figure to verify, names which lever the model leaned on). A weak team sets the ceiling at "whatever feels premium," reports a markup as a margin, defaults to cost-plus, and takes the model's price at face value. Use the gap between the two to re-teach the value stick and the markup-margin distinction in the wrap.

Tie to the project. This activity is a dry run of the price decision in Project 2. Tell students that: the value-stick read, the approach choice, the WTP band, and the defended price are all P2 mix deliverables. The product they price here is practice; the brand they carry from P1 is the real thing, and the AI use on P2 must be disclosed and critiqued (what the model got right, what it missed, what the team changed).

Chapter 2: What Marketing Is

Instructor Teaching Notes

A session-by-session guide for teaching Chapter 2. It ties the Ch2 bundle together (textbook chapter, lecture video, slide deck, test bank) into one plan you can walk into class with. Chapter 2 has a full material set, so these notes lean on that bundle and show how each piece carries a different part of the session.

Where this sits: Week 1 of the 15-week pacing guide, the front door of the course. Per the pacing guide, Chapter 1 (AI Foundations) and Chapter 2 (What Marketing Is) share Week 1, so the AI frame and the definition of marketing land together. The AI rung that advances this week is rung 1, "Ask well", plus the whole-ladder preview from Chapter 1: students feel the difference a sharp prompt makes against a vague one on the same marketing question. The project arc opens here too: this is the week you introduce the P1-to-P3 arc, form teams, and start brand shortlists. Because Chapter 2 is the conceptual spine the whole course hangs on (marketing as five coordinated decisions), it is worth protecting its own clear 75-minute block even though it shares the calendar week with Chapter 1.

1. At a glance

The big idea, in one sentence. Marketing is not the ad at the end; it is five decisions a firm makes and has to keep pointing the same way (what to make, who to serve, what to charge, where to make it available, and how to talk about it), and because those decisions multiply rather than add, four good ones and one broken one do not average into a passing plan, they deliver the buyer straight to the friction the fifth one created.

The chapter's running brands. The chapter opens and closes on a two-brand puzzle: Patagonia gave its whole company away to a climate trust in 2022 (roughly three billion dollars) and kept selling jackets the next morning, while Bud Light approved one partnership in April 2023 and lost twenty-two years as the best-selling US beer in about eight weeks. One survived a three-billion-dollar giveaway; the other could not survive a single can. That gap is the whole chapter. Note the deliberate ordering difference between the bundle pieces: the video opens on Bud Light (the manager who made a one-billion-dollar decision) and then holds it against Patagonia, while the textbook opens on Patagonia and saves Bud Light for the hard half of the argument. Either hook works; pick one and commit. Three more brands carry the body and recur as a matched set: Stanley (the Quencher near-miss, $73M to $750M when targeting changed and availability scaled to the buyer the cup already served), Costco (the $65 membership fee and 92 percent renewal that fund a near-zero ad budget; the buyer-side cost-to-satisfy read), and Liquid Death (the $2.49 can defended by experiential and social value, the product-leadership play). Trader Joe's appears once as the second no-ad brand that reaches the same place by a different road.

Learning objectives (from the test bank, nine LOs). A student who finishes this chapter can:

  • LO 2.1 Define marketing using the AMA's 2007 definition and explain what an exchange requires.
  • LO 2.2 Name the five marketing decisions (the coordinated-decisions definition) and explain why they multiply rather than add.
  • LO 2.3 Distinguish the marketing concept from the selling concept, and explain how marketing myopia traps firms.
  • LO 2.4 Trace the five business orientations (production, product, selling, marketing, holistic) and connect each to a 2026 example.
  • LO 2.5 Apply the four Ps, seven Ts, and 4Cs frameworks to a real brand, and explain what the 4Ps-to-7Ts split fixed.
  • LO 2.6 Explain the four value types (economic, functional, experiential, social) and apply the four-part positioning statement.
  • LO 2.7 Explain how penetration, customer lifetime value, and retention determine whether a marketing configuration pays on repeat.
  • LO 2.8 Analyze the Bud Light case using the coherence principle and Situational Crisis Communication Theory.
  • LO 2.9 Evaluate AI output about a brand's marketing decisions, identify which framework an AI-drafted positioning or strategy applies, and flag what the output leaves out.

Prerequisites. Sophomore standing; no prior marketing or computing background assumed. This is the second chapter students meet, so the only real prerequisite is Chapter 1 (AI Foundations), which sets the five-rung AI ladder and the precise-prompt idea this chapter's widgets put to work. Students should have a working AI assistant account for the hands-on moment. No quantitative prerequisite beyond reading a percentage; the retention math in §2.6 is conceptual, not computational here (the arithmetic gets heavy later, in Chapter 10). Because this is the front door, do not assume any marketing vocabulary at all; the chapter teaches "targeting," "distribution," and "brand" from scratch with tooltips.

2. Timing plan: a 75-minute block

This maps the chapter to one 75-minute session. Chapter 2 is wide (one definition, the five decisions, the multiplicative rule, three framework families, the three-number payoff, the Bud Light failure, and three brand applications), so the plan keeps each framework to its one essential point, leans on the running brands to carry the load, and protects the in-class activity, because students learn the five-decision lens by running it once, not by hearing all the parts. The chapter shares Week 1 with Chapter 1, so the AI moment here is light by design (rung 1, "ask well"); the heavy AI build comes later.

If you have two sessions for this chapter, split at the 38-minute mark (end of the frameworks block) and open the second session with the read-a-brand activity expanded to 25 minutes.

3. Per-section teaching guidance

For each framework: the talking points, the one thing students must leave with, the concrete example to use, and the slide/sketch/widget to demo.

§2.1 Why marketing exists (LO 2.1)

  • Talking points. A buyer with money and a problem, a seller with the fix, and silence between them; marketing closes that gap so a voluntary trade happens, then does it again. Define marketing with the AMA's 2007 wording (creating, communicating, delivering, exchanging offerings that have value for customers, clients, partners, and society at large). Define the exchange as a voluntary, value-creating transfer where each side gives up something, gets something it values more, and is free to walk away. Drucker's 1973 limit: the aim of marketing is to make selling unnecessary (Spotify Wrapped is the everyday version). Close on the 2026 update: the buyer is now a person-plus-an-assistant (agentic commerce), so the offer has to read to the model, not just the human, which previews generative-engine optimization in Chapter 18.
  • The one thing to leave with. The exchange completes only when both sides feel they won; when one of the five conditions fails, the sale does not happen no matter how good the other four were.
  • Example. "She hands over fifteen dollars; he hands over a tallboy of Liquid Death; both walk away thinking they got the better end." The running-shoe buyer who now types her need into ChatGPT instead of Google.
  • Demo. Slide 06; chapter sk-exchange-mount and the sk-five-conditions five-gates sketch. The §2.1 "Close the gap" client-side gate demo flips any one condition off and shows the sale fail; use it as a 30-second illustration, not the AI moment.

§2.2 The five decisions (LO 2.2)

  • Talking points. A firm can change exactly five things about an offering: the offering, the buyer it serves, the price, the place, and the message. Older textbooks fold this into the four Ps but leave out targeting; the convention now keeps who-to-serve on its own, because choosing the buyer is a different question from choosing what to make. Walk each slot on the running brands (Patagonia's narrow shelf, Liquid Death's beer-shaped can fixing its price and shelf and voice). Then the positioning statement as the artifact that makes the who-decision checkable: "For [target customer] who [want], [brand] is a [product category] that [key benefit]." The statement names the buyer and the benefit, not the firm and the feature. Close on the five Cs as the analysis that feeds the five decisions, and the three nested layers (corporate strategy, marketing strategy, the marketing program).
  • The one thing to leave with. Targeting is the binding decision: it lines the other four up behind it, so a position you can only fill with a buzzword is the slot the brand has not actually decided.
  • Example. Liquid Death's four-part statement ("for concert-going young adults who want to skip alcohol without looking like they did, a canned water that turns staying sober into a statement"). The five Cs threaded so a weak customer read tilts the whole targeting choice.
  • Demo. Slides 04–05 and 07; chapter fig-process, sk-decisions-mount, sk-positioning-mount (the four-slot sentence), and sk-five-cs-mount. The §2.2 "Read a brand's five decisions" live-AI widget (w22) is the centerpiece AI demo and the spine of the in-class activity (see §6 and §7).

§2.3 The decisions are multiplicative (LO 2.2, and the mental/physical availability of LO 2.7)

  • Talking points. Correct the intuition first: students walk in believing a brand wins by being genuinely different. The Ehrenberg-Bass 15-category audit says only about 11 percent of a brand's own buyers see it as different; the other 89 bought it because it was familiar, in stock, and thought of first. So a brand competes on the two market-based assets, mental availability (easy to think of) and physical availability (easy to find), not on a unique meaning the buyer does not hold. A second finding kills the loyalty myth: across 880 IPA entries, campaigns that pulled in new buyers won about three times as often, and only 2 percent of big winners grew on loyalty alone. Then the rule: marketing is multiplicative, not additive. Four good decisions and one broken one do not average into a B; the four good ones pay to deliver the buyer to the friction the fifth created. Strategy calls this fit and trade-offs.
  • The one thing to leave with. Coherence is the whole game, because the decisions multiply; a narrow segment served perfectly is still too small to grow on, so the rule disciplines all five toward keeping the brand widely available.
  • Example. Stanley: the firm shelved the Quencher in 2018 for not fitting the construction-worker buyer, then a women-run account bought 5,000 to resell and sold out in five days. The president read the sellout as the cup's true buyer, kept the distinctive assets, and let availability scale (wardrobe colors, Target and Dick's, TikTok over outdoor-magazine ads). $73M to $750M in about three years, the base flipping to about 98 percent women 35–44.
  • Demo. Slides 08–11; chapter sk-mental-vs-physical-mount (the 2x2 grid with brands plotted) and sk-misalign-mount (the five-node diagram with one node struck through). The §2.3 client-side multiplicative meter (w23) is the clean illustration: click one decision to break it and the system meter multiplies down (about 40 percent on one break, 15 on two), it does not subtract to 80.

§2.4 Frameworks for the five decisions (LO 2.5, and the four value types of LO 2.6)

  • Talking points. The 4Ps are a 1960 mnemonic (McCarthy) that stuck for sixty years but hide decisions a firm now manages on purpose, so the current textbook splits them into seven Ts (Product, Service, Brand, Price, Incentives, Communication, Distribution). Two splits matter: Product was hiding the artifact, the service, and the brand; Promotion was hiding building the brand and running a sale. Fix the distinctive-asset-vs-differentiated-attribute confusion here (a distinctive asset lets a buyer spot the brand without the name; a differentiated attribute is a claim of superiority, and the record favors the first). The 4Cs (Lauterborn 1990) read the same five from the buyer's seat; the reframe matters most on cost-to-satisfy (time, search, switching, not just money) and communication (in 2026 she talks back). Costco reads cleanly: the fee is a money cost that drops four other costs by more than the fee. Name the four value types so students stop pricing identity purchases like spreadsheets. The four A's (acceptability, affordability, accessibility, awareness) are gates in a line, worth a mention if time allows.
  • The one thing to leave with. The split is the point: folding brand into product or short-term incentives into long-term communication lets a weekend coupon eat the budget meant to build the brand. The buyer never sees the firm's levers, only a need, a full cost, and the talk around it.
  • Example. Costco's $65 fee as a cost-to-satisfy reducer (test-bank Item 14). Liquid Death's premium price defended by experiential and social value, not functional (water is water; test-bank Item 12). The Trader Joe's value-prop loaded as the widget's default.
  • Demo. Slides 12–15; chapter sk-fourps-sevents-mount and sk-4cs-mount. The §2.4 "Audit a value-prop from the buyer's seat" live-AI vision widget (w24) grades each of the four Cs PRESENT / VAGUE / MISSING and has a third "sharp+" toggle that runs the four value types on the same input; this is the strongest in-class AI moment (see §6).

§2.5 How firms think: five orientations (LO 2.3, LO 2.4)

  • Talking points. How a firm thinks about its own job shapes all five decisions whether anyone names it or not, and the common answer moved through five stages. Production (cheap and available; Ford's Model T, $850 to about $260). Product (build the most refined thing; the classic refutation is marketing myopia, defining the firm by the product instead of the job, the railroads who kept perfecting locomotives while cars and planes took the trips, and Kodak after them). Selling (push hard on things nobody seeks, like life insurance or the welcome-week credit-card table). Marketing (the 1950s flip: find products for the buyers you have, not buyers for the products you made; the 2016 Jobs-to-be-Done reframing, the quarter-inch hole not the drill; Godin's smallest viable audience). Holistic (since about 2000, serve every stakeholder; Patagonia's purpose trust is the strong form). Close on Cialdini's seven levers as the dual-use mental levers communication pulls, and the 2026 blind spot: the AI a buyer asks before she sees your homepage sets her mood.
  • The one thing to leave with. Marketing myopia lives inside "great products speak for themselves"; the buyer was never loyal to the product, only to the job, and the job has other instruments.
  • Example. The railroads (test-bank Item 18, the JTBD-in-myopia case). Life insurance as the selling-orientation example (test-bank Item 9). The quarter-inch drill and the quarter-inch hole.
  • Demo. Slide 15; chapter sk-orientations-mount (the five orientations on a timeline) and sk-jtbd-card-mount (name the product and substitutes escape; name the job and they become your options). The §2.5 "Diagnose the orientation" live-AI widget (w25) places a one-line mission on the product-trapped-to-customer-job line and names the railroad-era parallel, vague vs sharp; a strong homework AI moment.

§2.6 What marketing's job actually pays for (LO 2.7)

  • Talking points. The five decisions cost money, and the firm pays because together they bring in more, on repeat. The mix is really doing two jobs at once, acquiring buyers who do not buy you yet and keeping the ones who do, and few actions do both well, which is why the three numbers split the way they do. Penetration is how many different people buy you, and it is the number that moves first when a brand grows (category-entry points are how a brand earns that reach). Customer lifetime value is what one buyer is worth over the relationship, and it sets the ceiling on what you can spend to win her; in 2026 the brand manager can pull the real retention number instead of guessing (a preview of the connected-tools rung and Chapter 24). Retention is the heavy lifter: the 1990 Bain study across about a hundred service firms found cutting losses five points lifted profit 25 to 95 percent, because each retained buyer adds years of margin you never spend to win again. The AIDA funnel is a fine check on one ad and a poor model of marketing overall.
  • The one thing to leave with. Retention does the heavy lifting because average tenure scales with the inverse of churn (80 percent kept is five years, 90 is ten, 95 is twenty), so a few points of retention swing lifetime value hard.
  • Example. Costco: grocery markups so thin a trip barely covers stocking, so the fee stack from renewing members roughly equals reported profit, which is why it can skip advertising (test-bank Item 14 and AI-3 both lean on this). The Bain 25-to-95-percent finding (test-bank Item 17).
  • Demo. Slides 16–17; chapter sk-penetration-vs-loyalty-mount (a crowd of new buyers vs squeezing the regulars) and sk-clv-mount (one buyer as a stack of yearly margins). The client-side CLV/retention widget (clv-widget) drags the retention rate and shows the acquisition ceiling move, with the marker at Costco's 92 percent up the steep part of the curve; a reveal-the-principle widget, not the AI moment.

§2.7 When one decision breaks: Bud Light (LO 2.8)

  • Talking points. The negative proof of the chapter's whole claim. On April 1, 2023, an Anheuser-Busch InBev manager approved a personalized can and promo video for Dylan Mulvaney, even though Bud Light had topped US beer for twenty years on conservative working-class men. Within about eight weeks it lost the top spot held since 2001; within nine months, more than a billion dollars in lost US sales; by early 2026, still down close to 30 percent, third behind Modelo and Michelob Ultra. The partnership aimed "who to serve" at a buyer the other four were not serving, and the decisions multiply, so one misaligned slot sank it. The surprise students need: the lasting damage was the response, a separate decision. Situational Crisis Communication Theory matches the response to what the audience feels; a peer-reviewed study coded the angry posts at 52.6 percent broken trust and 44.7 percent lost control, the textbook case for a Rebuild response (acknowledge the harm, announce a concrete fix). The company chose Bolstering (past good deeds, the patriotic workforce), which left both feelings untouched and read as dodging, the worst-case choice. The break then spread to all five decisions.
  • The one thing to leave with. "The model was right" or "we have always done good" does not settle a crisis; the response has to match the emotion the audience is actually feeling, and a real acknowledgment in the first ten days would have been the cheap fix.
  • Example. The full Bud Light timeline (test-bank Items 15 and 16). The Rebuild-vs-Bolstering choice (test-bank Item 16 and AI-4).
  • Demo. Slide 18 (the response pull quote); chapter sk-budlight-cascade-mount (five nodes turning together, then one yanked out and the other four seizing) and sk-crisis-fork-mount (the acknowledge-and-fix vs change-the-subject fork). The §2.7 client-side Bud Light collapse cascade (budlight-widget) runs the April 2023 partnership and walks the timeline as the system meter falls; an excellent live illustration and the mirror image of the §2.2 coherent-brand widget.

§2.8 Three brand applications (synthesis, LO 2.2)

  • Talking points. The three brands you have followed line up against the three value disciplines (Treacy and Wiersema 1995): a firm wins by being best at one and merely good at the rest. Patagonia wins on customer intimacy (knowing the buyer; the Worn Wear repair program feeds the next product cycle, and the firm published its own carbon footprint rising the wrong way because an honesty relationship does not survive dishonesty). Liquid Death wins on product leadership (the offer is mostly an idea; the four other decisions exist to protect what the can means). Costco wins on operational excellence (the fee is the engine, about 4,000 SKUs against a supermarket's 30,000 to 50,000, Kirkland private-label that cannot be price-shopped). Trader Joe's reaches the same no-ad place by a different road, which is the point: when the five decisions truly agree, the brand can drop the one lever everyone assumes it cannot live without. The strategy acid test: if the brand vanished tomorrow, could a rival easily replace it? For Costco and Trader Joe's the answer is no, because the cheap thing to copy is the no-ad rule, not the system underneath.
  • The one thing to leave with. Each brand picks a different discipline and the five decisions fall in behind that one choice; you cannot be middling at everything and expect anyone to notice you.
  • Example. Patagonia / Liquid Death / Costco mapped to the three disciplines. Trader Joe's as the second no-ad brand (test-bank context for Item 14 and the Costco AI-3 item).
  • Demo. Slide 19; chapter sk-three-disciplines-mount (three hills, one tall and sharp) and sk-brand-readout-mount (read any brand from the street, the five decisions fanning out with checks or crosses). 4. Discussion prompts

Use four to six of these to drive the chapter's real debates. They work as cold-call, think-pair-share, or small-group prompts. The first two are the chapter's central tensions.

  1. The two-brand puzzle. "Patagonia gave away a three-billion-dollar company and kept selling jackets the next morning. Bud Light approved one partnership and lost twenty-two years at the top in eight weeks. What does one have that the other did not?" (Target: coherence. Patagonia's five decisions all pointed at the same buyer for forty years, so the ownership change fit the configuration; Bud Light aimed one decision at a buyer the other four were not serving. The decisions multiply, so one misaligned slot sinks the rest. This is the chapter's whole argument and test-bank Item 5.)
  1. Marketing is not advertising. "Most people walk in thinking marketing is the ad at the end. After this chapter, where would you put advertising in the list of five decisions, and why is it not the most important one?" (Target: communication is one of five, and the smallest in revenue terms; no ad budget fixes a wrong product, buyer, price, or shelf. The useful reverse is that four coherent decisions do most of communication's work for free, the way Liquid Death's can says what it is before any ad.)
  1. Difference vs availability. "If only 11 percent of a brand's own buyers think it is different from the competition, what is the brand actually competing on?" (Target: mental availability, being easy to think of when the need shows up, and physical availability, being easy to find. The 89 percent bought it because it was familiar, in stock, and thought of first, so the firm gets paid to keep its distinctive cues famous and consistent, not to argue a difference buyers do not feel.)
  1. Was being right a defense? "Target's-style cases aside, Bud Light's bigger mistake came after the partnership. The company recited its history of supporting veterans and its patriotic workforce. Why did that make things worse?" (Target: the angry buyers felt broken trust and lost control, which call for a Rebuild response (acknowledge the harm, announce a fix). Bolstering, reciting past good deeds, addresses neither, so it read as dodging. The response has to match the emotion, not what executives feel like saying. This is test-bank Item 16 and AI-4.)
  1. Does loyalty grow a brand? "A brand has a small but intensely loyal base. Should it spend to make those buyers buy more often, or to reach new buyers?" (Target: reach. Across 880 IPA entries, campaigns that pulled in new buyers won about three times as often, and only 2 percent of big winners grew on loyalty alone. Growth comes from penetration, the buyers not buying you yet, not from squeezing the ones who already are. This connects to test-bank Item 13 and SA-2.)
  1. Does AI change the definition of marketing? "Your buyer now asks ChatGPT or Claude for a recommendation before she ever sees your homepage. Does that change what marketing is, or just where it happens?" (Target: the five decisions do not change, but the buyer is now a person-plus-an-assistant, so the offer has to read cleanly to the model too (agentic commerce), and the firm that ignores how the model describes it leaves the first impression to the machine. This previews generative-engine optimization in Chapter 18 and the AI thread the course runs on. It also sets up test-bank items AI-1 through AI-4, which test whether students can critique AI output against the chapter's frameworks.)

5. Common misconceptions and pitfalls

The specific places sophomores get this chapter wrong, and how to correct each on the spot.

  • Thinking marketing equals advertising. The single most common entering belief. Students arrive sure that marketing is the ads. Correct it on day one: marketing is five decisions, and communication is only one of them, the smallest in revenue terms. Keep saying "the ad is the last of five, and four coherent decisions do most of its work for free."
  • Averaging the five decisions instead of multiplying them. Students score a brand "four out of five, so 80 percent." Correct it with the multiplicative meter (w23): four good decisions and one broken one collapse the system to about 40 percent, not 80, because the four good ones pay to deliver the buyer straight to the friction the fifth created. The mantra: multiplicative, not additive.
  • Confusing a distinctive asset with a differentiated attribute. Students think winning means proving the product is better. Correct it with the 11-percent finding: only about a tenth of buyers think their preferred brand is different, so the firm gets paid to keep its cues famous and consistent (Tiffany blue, the tallboy can), not to keep arguing a superiority buyers do not feel.
  • Reading loyalty as the growth lever. Students assume a small brand needs more loyal customers. Correct it: growth comes from penetration, the buyers who are not buying you at all. Only 2 percent of the big IPA winners grew on loyalty alone. The loyalty gap is far too small to rescue a brand that few people buy.
  • Treating the 7Ts (or a 7Ps you saw elsewhere) as a different, conflicting model. Students arriving from another course may know a "7Ps" services mix. Correct it: the 7Ts are the Kellogg/Chernev split of the same five decisions (Product, Service, Brand, Price, Incentives, Communication, Distribution); the services 7Ps add People, Process, and Physical evidence and are a Chapter 9 topic. Both descend from the same five-decision spine.
  • Pricing an identity purchase like a spreadsheet. Students try to justify Liquid Death's price on functional grounds (the can preserves taste). Correct it with the four value types: water is water on functional and economic value, but the premium makes perfect sense on experiential and social value, the identity signal the buyer displays. Naming the kind of value is the fix (test-bank Item 12).
  • Confusing the marketing concept with the selling concept. Students think "we have a great product, sales will follow" is the marketing orientation. Correct it: that is the product orientation, and it walks straight into marketing myopia. The marketing concept flips the question to "what job is the buyer hiring this to do?" (test-bank Item 10, SA-1, AI-1).
  • Assuming a crisis is fixed by reminding people you are good. Students reach for "remind them of all the good we have done." Correct it with SCCT: when the dominant emotions are broken trust and lost control, Bolstering (reciting good deeds) is the worst-case choice; the audience needs a Rebuild response that acknowledges the harm and names a concrete fix (test-bank Item 16, AI-4).

6. The hands-on AI moment

The chapter's AI move (rung 1, "Ask well," with the whole-ladder preview from Chapter 1). The week's rung is the most basic and the most important one: a specific prompt gets a useful answer and a vague one gets mush, on the same model and the same bill. Chapter 2 carries seven interactive widgets; three call a live model through the course proxy and four run in the browser. For a 75-minute block that shares the week with Chapter 1, run one live in class and assign one as homework. The strongest in-class choice is the §2.4 value-prop vision audit (w24), because it shows a live model reading a real homepage and because the vague-vs-sharp contrast is unmistakable; the §2.2 five-decisions auditor (w22) is the close second and ties directly to the in-class activity.

The seven widgets, by name (use what exists; do not invent):

  • §2.1 "Close the gap: the exchange" (client-side, no AI). Five toggles for the five exchange conditions; flip one off and the sale fails. A 30-second illustration of the exchange, not the AI moment.
  • §2.2 "Read a brand's five decisions" (live model, Claude Haiku 4.5, web search on by default). Pick a sample brand (Patagonia, Liquid Death, Costco, Stanley) or type any real brand, then ask the same model the same question two ways: the vague "how should this brand market itself?" returns marketing mush, and the sharp "read its five marketing decisions and name the one that would break the other four if it changed" searches the live web and returns a coherence diagnosis. The cleanest "the instruction changed, not the model" demo, and the spine of the in-class activity.
  • §2.3 "Multiplicative, not additive" (client-side, no AI). Five decision toggles and one system meter; break one and the meter multiplies down, it does not subtract. The reveal-a-principle illustration of coherence.
  • §2.4 "Audit a value-prop from the buyer's seat" (live model, vision). Paste a value-prop or upload a real homepage-hero screenshot; the model grades each of the four buyer-side Cs PRESENT / VAGUE / MISSING with one line of evidence. A vague toggle ("is this a good landing page?") returns a compliment; a sharp toggle ("4Cs audit, name the missing variable") returns a decision; a third "sharp+" toggle runs the four value types (economic, functional, experiential, social) on the same input. The strongest in-class AI moment.
  • §2.5 "Diagnose the orientation" (live model). Pick a preset (generic SaaS, Patagonia, Liquid Death, a 1950s railroad) or paste a one-line mission; the model places it on the product-trapped-to-customer-job line and names the railroad-era parallel, vague vs sharp. A strong homework choice.
  • §2.6 "What can you spend to win one buyer?" (client-side, no AI). Drag the retention rate and watch average tenure and the acquisition ceiling move, with the marker at Costco's 92 percent. A reveal-a-principle widget, not a calculator to total.
  • §2.7 "Watch the Bud Light collapse" (client-side, no AI). Run the April 2023 partnership and watch one decision pull out of line and the other four fall, with the real timeline. The mirror image of the §2.2 coherent brand.

How to run it live (about 7 minutes, inside the frameworks block).

  1. Project the §2.4 widget. Keep the Trader Joe's value-prop already loaded, or paste a brand the class knows (or upload a screenshot of a homepage on the projector).
  2. Press the vague toggle ("is this a good landing page?") and read what comes back. Ask the room: "Did that tell us anything we can act on?" They will see it is a compliment.
  3. Switch to the sharp toggle ("4Cs audit, name the missing variable") and run the same input. Walk the scorecard out loud: which C came back PRESENT, which VAGUE, which MISSING, and the one line of evidence for each. If time, press sharp+ to run the four value types on the same input.
  4. Make the teaching point: the model did not get smarter between the two runs and the bill was identical; the request changed. A vague brief returns a platitude, a sharp brief returns a diagnosis. That is rung 1 of the ladder, and it is the discipline the whole course is built on.

What students produce. Each student (or pair) runs the §2.2 five-decisions auditor (w22) on the brand they picked for the in-class activity (or their P1 shortlist brand), once vague and once sharp, and writes two or three sentences: what the vague ask returned, what the sharp ask returned, and the one decision the sharp ask flagged as the brand's exposure. This is the rung-1 deliverable and doubles as their AI-use disclosure for the week, per the course AI policy.

How to debrief it. Pull two or three student outputs onto the screen. Ask: "Where was the model right, and where did it miss?" Drive the course's standard from the first week: the model is a fast first draft, not the answer, and (because these widgets search the live web) every name, figure, and date has to be verified before you act on it. Connect forward: the §2.5 orientation widget and the §2.2 auditor do the same vague-vs-sharp move on orientation and strategy, and the four AI-integration test items (AI-1 through AI-4) are exactly this skill, recognizing when AI output is well-specified versus generic and which framework it is silently applying.

7. In-class activity: read a real brand's five decisions

Goal. Students run the chapter's four-step diagnostic on one real brand, in teams, in about 13 minutes, and leave having read the five decisions once rather than only heard them.

Setup. Pick one brand the whole room knows, or let teams use a brand from their P1 shortlist. Good choices: a campus coffee shop, a hydration or drinkware brand, a streaming service, or a sneaker brand. Use one of the chapter's worked brands (Patagonia, Costco, Liquid Death) only if the class needs the scaffolding; a fresh brand forces real thinking. Project the §2.2 "Read a brand's five decisions" widget (w22) so the AI step is live, and put slide 19 (the three value disciplines) on screen as the scoring scaffold.

Instructions (give these as four timed steps, the chapter's own diagnostic).

  1. Fill the five slots (4 min). Write one plain line each for what the brand makes, who it serves, what it charges, where a buyer finds it, and how it talks. "If a slot is hard to name from the outside, that vagueness is itself a finding."
  2. Name the binding decision (2 min). Targeting usually sets the other four, so write down who the brand is pointed at, then check whether the price, the shelf, and the message all fit that buyer.
  3. Hunt for the contradiction (3 min). Read the five lines together and find the slot that points somewhere the others do not. Because the decisions multiply, a single mismatch is where the brand is most exposed, the way the 2023 partnership aimed Bud Light at a buyer its other four decisions never served.
  4. Score the coherence, and check it against AI (4 min). If all five point at one buyer doing one thing, the brand coheres and the team can say why. Then run the §2.2 widget on the same brand: ask the vague version and the sharp version, and compare the model's read to the team's. Catch one thing the model got wrong or left out.

What good looks like. A strong team writes five slots a stranger could act on, correctly names targeting as the binding decision, finds a real contradiction rather than waving the brand through, and (the real tell) reads the AI output critically, catching a figure to verify or a decision the model missed. A weak team writes "everyone who likes coffee" for the who-slot, calls every slot coherent without friction, finds no contradiction, and takes the model's read at face value. Use the gap between the two to re-teach the multiplicative rule and the "marketing is not advertising" point in the wrap.

Tie to the project. This activity is a dry run of the customer-and-brand read at the front of Project 1. Tell students that: reading a brand's five decisions, naming the binding decision, and a first read of who the brand's customers are all feed P1 (Research and the brand audit). The brand they do here is practice; the brand the team shortlists this week and locks in Week 2 is the real thing, and AI use on the projects must be disclosed and critiqued (what the model got right, what it missed, what the team changed), per the course AI policy.

8. Assessment pointers

For a chapter quiz (low-stakes, after this week). Pull 8–10 multiple-choice items spread across the objectives. A balanced quick quiz: Item 1 or 2 (LO 2.1, the AMA definition / what an exchange requires), Item 4 or 5 (LO 2.2, the five decisions multiply / Patagonia coherence), Item 6 (LO 2.5, the 4Ps-to-7Ts split), Item 7 (LO 2.3, marketing myopia), Item 8 or 9 (LO 2.4, the production / selling orientation), Item 12 (LO 2.6, the four value types on Liquid Death), Item 13 (LO 2.7, penetration), Item 14 (LO 2.5, Costco and cost-to-satisfy), Item 15 or 16 (LO 2.8, the Bud Light failure / SCCT), Item 20 (LO 2.7, the three numbers). All are answerable from the chapter; difficulty is tagged per item (five are Diff 1, eleven Diff 2, four Diff 3).

For a midterm (Midterm 1 covers Ch1–Ch9, landing around Week 6). This is the foundation chapter, so its harder items anchor the opening block of the exam. The four scenario/short-answer items carry the most weight: SA-1 (the product-vs-marketing orientation on a software startup, LO 2.3/2.4), SA-2 (the three-number diagnosis of a candle brand losing money on retention, Diff 3, LO 2.7), SA-3 (the Stanley five-decision realignment, which decision changed first, LO 2.2/2.6), SA-4 (the 5C situation analysis on a fast-casual launch, naming the weakest C, Diff 3, LO 2.2/2.6). The four AI-integration items (AI-1 through AI-4) test whether students can critique AI-generated marketing output: AI-1 spots the product orientation in an AI's "great products speak for themselves" advice; AI-2 catches an AI-drafted positioning statement whose target and benefit are buzzword filler; AI-3 catches an AI that optimizes one variable (remove the Costco fee) without modeling the five-decision system; AI-4 recognizes a Bolstering crisis draft and prescribes Rebuild. They pair naturally with the rung-1 AI moment and are suitable for AI-assisted grading via the course proxy.

Stanley's Quencher: Cut It, or Re-Aim It?

A teaching case for Chapter 6 (Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning). Students sit on Stanley's brand team in 2018, facing a recommendation to discontinue the 40-ounce Quencher tumbler, and have to decide what the firm's own data is missing. This is the pilot case for the course's Cases material type, and it is built on the chapter's running brand example. Grounded in the real Chapter 6 facts; any teaching simplification is flagged in the instructor note.

PART ONE: STUDENT-FACING CASE

Stanley's Quencher: Cut It, or Re-Aim It?

Chapter: 6, Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning Format: Teaching case for in-class discussion (not graded) Read before class: about 7 minutes for the case and the exhibits

The situation

Stanley has been making insulated drinkware since 1913. For more than a century the brand meant one thing: a rugged green bottle that kept coffee hot on a job site or a hunting trip, sold to a buyer the company described, in its own planning, as outdoorsmen roughly 35 to 55 years old. That buyer is who the marketing spoke to, who the catalog photographed, and who the sales dashboard was built to track.

It is 2018, and one product in the line is not pulling its weight against that picture. The Quencher is a 40-ounce tumbler with a handle and a straw. On the dashboard that measures how the core outdoors buyer responds, the Quencher looks minor. It does not photograph like the heritage bottles, it does not sell through the channels the outdoorsman shops, and inside the buyer the company has chosen to serve, the numbers are weak enough that the recommendation on the table is to stop making it. The data is not wrong. Against the segment Stanley targets, the Quencher really is a soft product.

There is one fact the dashboard is not built to show. The Quencher has quietly found an audience nobody at Stanley aimed at. A shopping blog run by three women, with a readership that is almost entirely women aged 25 to 50, has been recommending the Quencher to its audience, not as a thing to take coffee to a worksite but as the cup you drink water from at your desk all day. When the blog placed a large wholesale order to sell the tumbler to its own readers, the order sold out in days.

So the brand team has two readings of the same product in front of it. The dashboard says the Quencher is a weak performer in the market Stanley serves. The wholesale sell-out says there is a different buyer, in a place Stanley was not looking, answering a question Stanley was not asking, who wants this exact product. Both readings are true at the same time.

The quarterly review is in two days.

Exhibit 4. The buyer, in her own words (composed).

The decision

You sit on Stanley's brand team. In two days the quarterly review will consider a recommendation to discontinue the Quencher, supported by the dashboard in Exhibit 1's left column. You have also seen the wholesale sell-out in the right column. Do you sign the recommendation to cut the product, or do you argue for something else? Whatever you recommend, you have to defend it with the evidence in front of you, and you have to say what it would cost Stanley if you are wrong.

Discussion questions

  1. Stanley's data was accurate. Against the buyer the firm had chosen to serve, the Quencher really was selling poorly. So how does an accurate dashboard lead a competent team toward a decision that, in hindsight, would have been a large mistake?
  1. The desk-water buyer is a different demographic from Stanley's target, but the chapter argues the deeper difference is not age or gender. Using the idea of the demand space (the occasion that triggers the need), what is the Quencher actually being hired to do for this buyer, and how is that a different job from the one Stanley's product was built around?
  1. Run the desk-water buyer through the five-criteria filter (Exhibit 2). Four criteria pass cleanly. The fifth, actionable, is described as "the firm's call" rather than a fact about the buyer. What makes actionable different from the other four here, and what would Stanley have to be able to do for it to pass?
  1. If Stanley keeps the Quencher and re-aims it at the desk-water buyer, the firm has to decide how to position it. Stanley has a century of rugged-outdoors heritage. Should the brand drop that heritage to speak to the new buyer, keep it, or do something in between? Use the idea that positioning is work done in the buyer's mind, not work done to the product.
  1. Look at the map in Exhibit 3. Stanley and Yeti compete on price and heritage. Is the desk-water buyer choosing on those two axes at all? If she is choosing on a different pair (say, "does it fit my daily life" and "do I like how it looks"), what does that change about where the opportunity is and who Stanley's real competitor is?
  1. Suppose you win the argument and the Quencher becomes a hit with the new buyer. The chapter also teaches that brands grow by reaching more buyers, not fewer, and that a segment found on social media can read as "full" and cool off fast. What would you watch for, and what would you do differently the day the boom starts to fade?

PART TWO: INSTRUCTOR-FACING TEACHING NOTE

Do not distribute. Everything below this line is for the instructor only. Delete this entire half before you hand the case to students. It contains the analysis, the board plan, and the answers.

Where this case fits

  • Chapter: 6, Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning. Week 4 of the 15-week pacing guide, the single-chapter week that feeds Project 2.
  • What it is for: this case makes the chapter's whole funnel do work on one real decision. Students do not just define segmentation, targeting, and positioning; they watch an accurate data system point a good team at the wrong answer, then use the chapter's tools to find the buyer the dashboard could not see. It is the chapter's opening hook (the Stanley near-miss) turned into a decision the students have to make themselves.
  • When to run it: after students have read the chapter, as the in-class application block (about 25 minutes) or as a stand-alone discussion session (about 50 minutes). It can also open the chapter as a cold hook before any reading, run only through Q1 and Q2, with the rest deferred until after the frameworks are taught.
  • How it relates to the rest of the Ch6 bundle: the lecture video opens on this same Stanley near-miss, and the textbook closes on it in the Apply box, so students arrive primed. The slide deck's perceptual-map step (slide 15) pairs with Q5. The case adds what the others cannot: a live argument where students have to commit to a recommendation and defend its cost, rather than watch the story resolve. It is a natural companion to the in-class segment-and-position sprint in the teaching notes (Section 7); run the case first to motivate the method, then the sprint to practice it on a fresh brand.

Learning objectives this case serves

Copied from the Ch6 test bank's nine LOs; the discussion reaches seven of them.

  • LO 6.1 Customer heterogeneity makes segmentation necessary , reached by Q1 (the dashboard serves one chosen group and is blind to another).
  • LO 6.2 The four segmentation bases, with the demand space as the most actionable behavioral cut , reached by Q2 (the desk-water occasion is the real cut, not the demographic).
  • LO 6.3 The five-criteria usability filter , reached by Q3 (the buyer passes four criteria; actionable is the firm's call).
  • LO 6.4 Target compatibility before attractiveness , reached by Q3 and Q4 (can Stanley win this buyer without breaking the brand).
  • LO 6.5 Positioning as work in the buyer's mind; the three positioning moves , reached by Q4 (re-aim the position without changing the 1913 product).
  • LO 6.7 Build and read a perceptual map; the empty-quadrant rule and the buyer's real axes , reached by Q5 (are Stanley's axes the buyer's axes).
  • LO 6.8 Double jeopardy and reconciling narrow targeting with broad penetration , reached by Q6 (grow by reaching more category buyers; watch the social-found segment cool).

The analysis: how an expert reasons through this

Q1, the failure of an accurate dashboard (LO 6.1). The lesson here is not that Stanley's data was wrong. It was right. The Quencher genuinely sold poorly to the buyer Stanley had chosen, outdoorsmen 35 to 55. The failure is upstream of the data: the dashboard measured the segment Stanley had already decided to serve and was never built to ask whether a different segment existed. This is customer heterogeneity biting in the most expensive way. A market is a scattered population of buyers who want different things, and Stanley's measurement system quietly assumed the population was the one slice it had picked. A data system can miss a segment entirely when that segment was never in the dashboard's question list. The strong student says some version of: the numbers answered the question Stanley asked, and Stanley asked the wrong question.

Q2, the demand space (LO 6.2). Push the room past "the new buyer is a woman, not a man." The chapter's deeper claim is that the benefit, or the occasion, is the cause of the choice, and demographics only describe the buyer after the fact. The Quencher's original buyer hires it for one occasion: keep coffee hot, away from home, on a job or a trip. The desk-water buyer hires it for a different occasion entirely: drink enough water across a whole day without refilling, and carry it from desk to car to a kid's practice without thinking. That is a different demand space, and it is the same move the chapter credits to Knorr in India (same product, reframed onto an occasion buyers were already trying to fill, sales up 127 percent on a product never reformulated). The Quencher was not reformulated either. The 40-ounce size and the handle, incidental features for the worksite, turn out to be the whole point for the desk. The product did not change; the occupied occasion did.

Q3, the five-criteria filter and compatibility (LO 6.3, LO 6.4). Walk the filter (Exhibit 2). Measurable, accessible, substantial, and differentiable all pass on the facts: the blog readership is sized and described, it is reachable through the channel that surfaced it, the wholesale order sold out in days, and the buyer wants a different thing and responds to a different message. The chapter is explicit that the desk-water segment passes every test but the last. Actionable is different in kind from the other four: the first four are facts about the buyer, while actionable is a fact about Stanley, namely whether the firm has the resources and the freedom to build and run a program for this buyer. This is where targeting compatibility enters. The question is not "is this a good segment" (it plainly is) but "can Stanley serve it better than a rival could, given what Stanley is." That is a judgment about Stanley's brand, channels, and willingness to move, which is exactly why the chapter calls it the firm's call. The strong student separates the four buyer-facts from the one firm-fact and sees that the whole decision lives in that fifth box.

Q4, positioning without changing the product (LO 6.5). This is the chapter's central positioning idea: positioning is work you do to the buyer's mind, not to the product. Stanley did not have to choose between its heritage and the new buyer. What it actually did is the instructive answer: it kept the 1913 heritage mark, brought in leadership from outside (a president who had come from Crocs), and re-pointed the same product with pastel colorways and short-supply drops aimed at the new buyer. The heritage stayed as the reason-to-believe (this is a real, durable, century-old product, not a fashion cup that will crack), while the colorways and the scarcity drops spoke to the desk-water buyer's actual decision (does it fit my life, do I like how it looks, is it a little bit special to own). The name and the category never changed; only the buyer and the way the brand asked to be remembered did. A student who argues "drop the heritage entirely" should be pushed on what reason-to-believe is left, and a student who argues "change nothing" should be pushed on why the buyer would notice. The defensible answer is the in-between move Stanley made.

Q5, the buyer's real axes (LO 6.7). Exhibit 3's map is drawn on Stanley's axes: price and heritage-or-ruggedness. The chapter's hardest point about perceptual maps is that you do not get to declare the axes; you recover them from the buyer, and the pair the boardroom would draw may not be the pair the buyer uses. If the desk-water buyer is really choosing on "does it fit my daily life" and "do I like how it looks," then the price-and-heritage map predicts her choice badly, and Stanley's real competitor for her is not Yeti (premium adventure) but the designed-for-daily-life brands like Owala. Reframing the axes also reframes where the opportunity is: it is not an empty corner on the old map, it is a different frame that the old map cannot see. This is the same logic the chapter uses for Liquid Death, an opening that was invisible until someone named a different frame as the one that matters. The strong student notices that the map Stanley was using is part of why the dashboard missed the buyer in the first place.

Q6, reconciling the flip with penetration, and the over-fit (LO 6.8). Once the re-aim works, the chapter's penetration evidence governs what comes next. STP picked the category and the meaning; the double-jeopardy and duplication-of-purchase laws say the brand now grows by reaching more category buyers, not by digging deeper into the few it has. So the growth move is mental and physical availability: more occasions, more places to buy, more buyers who think of the Quencher, not harder loyalty from the early fans. The case also seeds the cautionary half of the real story, which the instructor can reveal at the close: a segment found and fed through one social channel can read as full and cool fast, and Stanley's Quencher demand did slide as the channel saturated and rivals like Owala rose. The chapter's discipline for catching that is the regular segment refit (re-cut the segments each quarter and treat a sharp drop in stability as the signal to re-cut), so the team should be watching the mix, not assuming the 2018 buyer is permanent. The strong student says: ride the penetration laws to grow it, and keep re-cutting so you see the next shift before the dashboard turns it into a crisis.

How to lead the discussion (a board plan)

Board layout. Draw two columns at the start, headed "What the dashboard sees" and "What the dashboard misses." Fill the left from Q1 (accurate, weak Quencher sales, outdoors buyer) and the right as the discussion surfaces the desk-water buyer. Keep a third strip at the bottom for "the firm's call," which you fill during Q3. On a second board (or the lower half), draw the empty perceptual-map cross for Q5 and let students place the brands and argue the axes.

Sequence. Open with Q1 and let the room defend the cut decision honestly; you want students to feel that signing the discontinuation was reasonable on the data. That is the trap, and it has to be real before you spring it. Move to Q2 and let the demand-space reframe land (this is the "aha," so give it room). Use Q3 to slow down and separate the four buyer-facts from the one firm-fact; this is where the framework becomes precise. Q4 and Q5 are the richest arguments; let them run. Reserve Q6 for the close.

The pivot. The discussion turns at the moment you ask, after the room has fully argued that the data says cut: "Whose question was the dashboard answering?" That single question reframes everything from a data problem into a segmentation problem, and it is the chapter's whole opening hook. Hold the reveal of Exhibit 1's right column (the wholesale sell-out) until the room has committed to the obvious read, so the new buyer arrives as a genuine surprise rather than a hint.

The close. Land on the takeaway below. Then, and only then, give the room the real outcome: Stanley kept the product, re-aimed it, and revenue moved from roughly $73 million to past $750 million within a few years, before sliding as the channel saturated. Name plainly what was a judgment call (whether Stanley could act on the segment without breaking the brand, the actionable box) and what the evidence settled (the buyer was real and the demand was there). Do not let the happy ending erase the lesson that a competent team nearly made the opposite call on accurate data.

The key takeaway

A data system can miss an entire segment when that segment was never in its question list, and the cheapest growth a firm ever finds can be a buyer it already has and is not counting. Stanley re-pointed a 1913 product at a buyer it had been blind to, without changing the product at all, and that is segmentation, targeting, and positioning doing their work on one decision.

Timing

As a 25-minute in-class block:

  • Setup and Q1 (defend the cut, surface the trap): 6 minutes.
  • Q2 (the demand-space reframe, the pivot): 6 minutes.
  • Q3 (five criteria, the firm's call): 4 minutes.
  • Q4 and Q5 (positioning and the map), pick one to run long: 6 minutes.
  • Q6 and the close (penetration, the reveal, the takeaway): 3 minutes.

As a 50-minute stand-alone session, double the discussion questions and run the perceptual map live on the board, then bridge into the in-class segment-and-position sprint from the teaching notes on a fresh brand.

If you are short on time, protect Q1, Q2, and the pivot; those carry the chapter's core lesson. Q5 (the map) is the one to cut, since the slide deck and the chapter both cover the map elsewhere.

Common student moves (and how to handle each)

  • "The data was just wrong / Stanley had bad analysts." The most common miss, and it dodges the lesson. Redirect: the data was right, and the analysts were competent. Make them sit with that, because the failure is in the question the dashboard was built to ask, not in the numbers. This is the whole point.
  • "They should have known women would buy it." Hindsight talking. Redirect: nothing in Stanley's century of data or its chosen target pointed there, which is exactly why a segment outside your question list is invisible until something (here, a wholesale sell-out) forces it into view.
  • "Just target everyone, men and women." The everybody trap. Redirect with the chapter: a brand that tries to mean something to everyone means something specific to no one, and the two buyers want different things from the product. Targeting is choosing, and choosing has a cost.
  • "Pick the bigger segment." Students default to size. Redirect with compatibility-before-attractiveness: the desk-water segment is attractive, but the real question is whether Stanley can serve it better than a rival can without breaking the brand. Size is not the deciding test.
  • "Drop the heritage, it is old-fashioned for this buyer." Tempting but it discards the reason-to-believe. Redirect: positioning changes what the buyer values about the brand without changing the product; the heritage is what makes the cup credible and durable rather than a disposable fashion item. Stanley kept it on purpose.
  • "Any empty space on the map is an opportunity." The chapter's single most common map error, likely to surface on Q5. Redirect: an empty quadrant is an opening only when buyers actually want to live there, and the real move here is recognizing the buyer is on different axes, not finding white space on the old ones.
  • Treating the win as permanent. On Q6, students assume the flip is the end of the story. Redirect with the over-fit: a social-found segment can read as full and cool fast, and Stanley's demand did slide, which is why the chapter insists on a regular segment refit.

An AI-integration angle (optional)

The chapter advances rung 2 of the AI ladder (use a live model's real capability and check what it returns), and two of its named widgets fit this case cleanly.

  • Before Q4, run the chapter's positioning widget (Section 6.4, "Write and audit a positioning statement") on the Quencher. Fill the blanks for the re-aimed product (brand: Stanley Quencher; target: the desk-water buyer; the one thing it does best: a durable, heritage-grade cup sized and styled for all-day hydration; one rival: Owala). First ask it the vague way ("write a tagline"), then the sharp way ("write the statement in the template and flag every clause the firm cannot prove"). Have the room read the clause audit out loud.
  • The debrief is the teaching point, not the output. Ask: where was the model right, and where did it miss? Drive the chapter's standard, that the model is a fast first draft and not the answer. The model will likely produce a clean statement but cannot tell you whether the actionable box passes, because that is a judgment about Stanley the model has no way to make. That gap is the lesson: the human owns the compatibility call.

If you are running the short 25-minute block, skip the live widget and just point students at it for their own practice; the case stands without it.

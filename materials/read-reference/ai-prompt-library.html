AI Prompt Library

Principles of Marketing: An AI-Integrated Approach

How to use this library. Each section below is one chapter's hands-on AI moves. Every prompt is drawn directly from that chapter's interactive widgets, Apply-box exercises, or named AI loops. Copy the block, fill in the bracketed fields with your brand or topic, and paste it into any chat model with web search enabled (Claude, ChatGPT, or Gemini). The prompts are organized on the course's ask -> use -> connect -> build -> publish ladder: earlier chapters sit on the ask rung, later chapters climb toward connecting live data and publishing outputs.

AI-use policy (one paragraph). Use AI as a thinking partner, not a ghostwriter. Verify every specific claim, statistic, date, and name against a real source before it appears in any deliverable. Never paste customer data, PII, confidential financials, or trade secrets into a public tool. Disclose AI assistance when your instructor or employer asks. The course's benchmark: an AI-assisted answer you can defend line by line beats an AI answer you cannot explain at all.

Chapter 1: AI for Marketers

What this chapter teaches. How a language model works, the context-window ceiling, temperature, the rung ladder (ask / Skill / MCP / agent), and why fluent output is not the same as accurate output.

1-A. Vague vs. Specific: Prompt-Builder Demo

What it does. The chapter's core interactive widget. It runs two versions of the same request through a live model so you can see how precision changes the output.

Vague request (button 1):
Summarize this brand for me.

Brand context: Liquid Death is a canned mountain water brand that launched in 2019 
and reached a unicorn valuation in 2024. It is sold in 16-ounce tallboys and is 
known for its heavy-metal-style packaging, irreverent advertising, and a "death to 
plastic" environmental message that sets it apart in the bottled water category.

---

Sharp request (button 2 — add constraints):
Summarize in exactly three bullets, each under 12 words, plain English, no jargon.

Brand context: [same as above]

When to use it. Any time you need to understand why two people asking "the same question" get wildly different answers. Run vague first, then constrain.

Tool. Any chat model (Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini) with no special setup.

Expected output. Vague: a paragraph of brand description. Sharp: exactly three short bullets, disciplined by the constraint.

Disclosure/verification note. Confirm any specific claim about the brand (founding year, valuation) against a public source before using it in an assignment.

1-B. Build the Rung-Two Version (Apply Box)

What it does. Turns a one-off task into a reusable Skill (rung two). The step that takes the Apply box from reading about the ladder to climbing it.

I want to build a reusable assistant for this task:
[Describe the task in one sentence, e.g., "summarize a year of customer reviews 
and flag the top three complaints."]

Step 1: Tell me how many tokens my file is and whether it fits in your context window.
Step 2: Answer one specific question from the file: [state the question].
Step 3: Produce three versions of [specific output, e.g., "a tagline"] at low 
temperature, then three more at high temperature, and describe what changed.
Step 4: Which of the four rungs does this task belong on — ask once, save as a 
Skill, connect live data, or run on a schedule?
Step 5: If rung two, write me the system instructions I should paste into a 
Custom GPT or Claude Project so this task is reusable.

When to use it. After you have run a task manually once and want to make it repeatable without writing code.

Tool. ChatGPT (for Custom GPT builder) or Claude (for a Project). Rung-two setup takes about five minutes.

Expected output. A ready-to-paste system prompt for a Custom GPT or Claude Project, plus a one-sentence description of what rung-four would look like.

Disclosure/verification note. Do not upload files with PII or confidential data as knowledge sources in any shared GPT.

Chapter 2: What Marketing Is

What this chapter teaches. The five marketing decisions (what / who / how much / where / how to talk), the 4Cs buyer-side lens, and how a mission statement either names the buyer's job or talks about the product.

2-A. 4Cs Value-Proposition Audit

What it does. Audits any homepage hero or value-proposition text against Lauterborn's four buyer-side variables. Flags which one is missing.

You are auditing a value proposition against Lauterborn's 4Cs: Consumer wants & 
needs, Cost-to-satisfy, Convenience-to-buy, Communication. 

For each C, output one line: the C name, a verdict of PRESENT / VAGUE / MISSING, 
and one short clause of evidence drawn from the input. End with one sentence naming 
the single buyer-side variable the message most leaves missing. Plain English, no 
preamble.

Value proposition to audit:
[Paste the homepage headline + subhead, or describe the brand's main pitch in 
2-3 sentences.]

When to use it. When a brand's messaging feels off and you cannot name why. Works on competitor sites, your own brand, or a client's draft copy.

Tool. Any chat model. No web search needed.

Expected output. Four labeled lines (one per C, each with verdict + evidence) plus a closing sentence naming the weakest variable.

Disclosure/verification note. If you audit a competitor's live page, verify the text you paste is current before drawing conclusions.

2-B. Mission Rewrite: Buyer-Job Lens

What it does. Rewrites a product-centric mission statement so it names the buyer's job, then identifies which of the five marketing decisions the rewrite would change first.

Rewrite this mission so it names the buyer's job instead of the product, then say 
which of the five marketing decisions (what to make / who to serve / how much to 
charge / where to sell / how to talk) the rewrite changes first.

Mission to rewrite:
[Paste the brand's current mission statement.]

When to use it. At the start of a brand analysis when a mission statement is product-in, buyer-out. Also useful when evaluating whether a rebrand actually changed anything.

Tool. Any chat model.

Expected output. A rewritten mission statement in one sentence, followed by one sentence naming the decision the rewrite would shift.

Disclosure/verification note. The rewrite is a starting point, not a deliverable. Verify the brand's actual positioning before presenting the rewrite as accurate.

Chapter 3: Marketing Environment

What this chapter teaches. PESTEL analysis, competitive signals, the Flip Test (reading your strengths as your rival's read of your weaknesses).

3-A. Live PESTEL Scan with Quarter-over-Quarter Diff

What it does. The chapter's environmental-scanning widget (vague vs. sharp). The sharp version produces a real-time PESTEL read with source citations and a binding-force verdict.

Run a PESTEL scan of the current environment for this brand / industry. Search the 
live web for current 2026 sources. For each of the six axes (Political, Economic, 
Social, Technological, Environmental, Legal), give me the top two dated 2026 signals, 
each with a source and a 1-10 impact score, framed as a quarter-over-quarter diff 
(what moved since last quarter). Then name the single force moving fastest — the 
binding force. Be specific, name dates and sources, no preamble.

Brand / industry: [e.g., "U.S. plant-based meat category, mid-2026"]

When to use it. At the start of a situation analysis, before writing a marketing plan, or whenever a major external event (regulation, tech shift, macro shock) may have changed the environment since you last looked.

Tool. Chat model with web search enabled (Claude with search, ChatGPT with browsing, Gemini).

Expected output. Six labeled sections (one per PESTEL axis), each with two sourced, dated signals and an impact score, plus a one-line binding-force verdict.

Disclosure/verification note. Verify every date and source the model cites. Models can hallucinate article titles or misattribute quotes. Click through before you cite.

3-B. The Flip Test (SWOT from the Rival's Seat)

What it does. Flips your SWOT by reading each strength the way your strongest rival would read it, revealing vulnerabilities your own analysis misses.

You are a competitive-strategy coach. Search the live web for current 2026 sources 
when a market claim needs one. No preamble, no em dashes, plain language.

Brand: [your brand]
Named rival: [strongest competitor]
Strengths: [list your brand's top 3 strengths]
Weaknesses: [list your brand's top 2 acknowledged weaknesses]

Run the Flip Test: for each strength I listed, write one sentence describing how 
the named rival reads it as a vulnerability or as a signal of where we are NOT 
competing. Then name the single competitive opening the flip reveals.

When to use it. After completing a standard SWOT. The flip catches blind spots that polite internal analysis leaves invisible.

Tool. Chat model with web search enabled.

Expected output. One flipped sentence per strength, plus a one-sentence competitive-opening verdict.

Disclosure/verification note. The rival's read is the model's inference, not confirmed strategy. Triangulate against public statements, pricing, and distribution data before acting on it.

Chapter 4: How Buyers Buy

What this chapter teaches. The consumer decision journey, involvement levels, and heuristics. No distinct AI widget in this chapter; use the Apply box below.

4-A. Decision-Journey Map (Apply Box)

What it does. Maps a buyer's decision process for your category, stage by stage, and identifies where the brand is absent.

Map the consumer decision journey for a buyer choosing [product category, e.g., 
"a new laptop under $1,000"] in [year, e.g., "2026"]. For each stage — 
need recognition, information search, evaluation of alternatives, purchase, 
and post-purchase evaluation — name the top two touchpoints where a brand can 
appear, and flag the one stage where most brands in this category are absent. 
Plain English, 5-6 sentences per stage, no preamble.

When to use it. When designing a campaign and you need to know which stages are over- or under-invested.

Tool. Any chat model. Web search helpful for current touchpoint data.

Expected output. Five labeled stage sections, each naming two touchpoints and one absence flag.

Disclosure/verification note. Journey maps are hypotheses. Validate with real buyer interviews or behavioral data before building a budget around them.

Chapter 5: Marketing Research

What this chapter teaches. Research design, survey methods, and coding open-ended responses.

5-A. Code Open-Ended Survey Responses (Vague vs. Specific)

What it does. The chapter's real-AI widget. Sends the same set of open-ended responses through a vague and a sharp coding request to show how instruction quality drives analysis quality.

Vague version:
What themes appear in these customer responses?
[Paste 8-12 open-ended survey responses, one per line.]

---

Sharp version (paste responses into the same prompt):
You are a survey-research analyst coding open-ended responses. Be concise. Use 
plain English. Do not exceed 240 words.

Code these open-ended responses into no more than four mutually exclusive themes. 
For each theme: name it in 3-5 words, cite the response numbers that support it, 
and give a one-sentence explanation of what drives it. Flag any response that does 
not fit a theme. Then name the single theme a product manager should act on first.

Responses:
[Paste 8-12 open-ended survey responses, one per line, numbered.]

When to use it. After running a survey with open-ended questions. Reduces coding time from hours to minutes on sets up to a few hundred responses.

Tool. Any chat model. Upload a CSV for larger sets.

Expected output. Sharp version: four labeled themes, each with cited response numbers, a one-sentence explanation, and a flag for outliers. Closes with the highest-priority theme.

Disclosure/verification note. AI coding introduces its own biases. Spot-check 10-15% of coded responses manually before reporting results.

Chapter 6: STP (Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning)

What this chapter teaches. Cluster-based segmentation, the five usability criteria, persona building, the positioning template, and POP/POD audit.

6-A. Name the Segments from Cluster Output

What it does. The chapter's first real-AI widget. Reads raw cluster rows, proposes plain-English segment names, and scores each on the five usability criteria.

You are a segmentation methodologist helping a marketing student read raw cluster 
output. Be concise and use plain English. Never invent attributes the columns do 
not contain.

Here is cluster output from a k-means run on customer transaction and survey data. 
Name each cluster with a 3-5 word strategic label, cite the column values that 
justify the name, and score each cluster on the five usability criteria 
(measurable / accessible / substantial / differentiable / actionable) with a 
pass/fail for each. Flag any cluster the columns cannot support.

Cluster data:
[Paste cluster summary table: columns = attributes, rows = clusters, values = means]

When to use it. After running a segmentation analysis in Python, R, or a tool like Qualtrics. Turns statistical output into names a brand team can debate.

Tool. Any chat model. No web search needed.

Expected output. One label per cluster with column evidence, a five-criteria scorecard, and a flag for any cluster that is analytically unsupportable.

Disclosure/verification note. The names are hypotheses based on the data you paste. Validate with real buyer interviews before presenting segment names to a client.

6-B. Draft a Persona from the Strategic Segment

What it does. Turns a one-line strategic segment into a buyer persona with media-buyable descriptors.

Turn this strategic segment into a buyable media profile: "[paste your segment 
description, e.g., 'women aged 28-44 who treat daily hydration as part of a 
health-and-identity practice']". List the Meta and TikTok interest categories, 
the geo and income filters, and flag where the profile reaches non-target buyers.

When to use it. After naming a segment and before buying media. The strategic segment alone cannot be bought on any platform; the persona is the translation layer.

Tool. Any chat model. No web search needed.

Expected output. A media-buyable profile with platform-specific interest categories, demographic filters, and a flag for waste reach.

Disclosure/verification note. Platform audience sizes and interest categories change. Verify estimates in the platform's own Audience Insights tool before building a media plan.

6-C. Write and Audit a Positioning Statement

What it does. The chapter's positioning widget. Given four inputs (brand, target, best-differentiator, rival), it writes a statement in the standard template and flags any clause the firm cannot prove or that a rival could say word for word.

You are a brand-positioning coach helping a marketing student. Be concise and use 
plain English. Never invent facts the student did not provide.

Write a positioning statement in the standard template (For [target] who [need], 
[brand] is the [category] that [biggest difference] because [reason to believe]). 
Then audit every clause: flag any claim the firm cannot prove, any point of 
difference a rival could say word for word, and any missing point of parity that 
disqualifies the brand from consideration. Be specific.

Brand: [brand name]
Target: [one-line strategic segment]
The one thing it does best: [one specific differentiator]
One rival: [competitor name]

When to use it. Drafting or pressure-testing a positioning statement. The audit clause is the part most students skip and most brand teams need most.

Tool. Any chat model. No web search needed.

Expected output. A positioning statement in the full template, followed by a clause-by-clause audit naming what can and cannot be proven, plus a missing-POP flag.

Disclosure/verification note. The model's "reason to believe" is inferred from what you provide. Verify that every RTB claim is something the firm can actually substantiate.

Chapter 7: Brands

What this chapter teaches. Distinctive assets, brand equity, GEO (generative engine optimization), perceptual maps, and repositioning.

7-A. Distinctive-Asset Audit

What it does. The chapter's asset-audit widget. For any brand, it lists non-name cues (color, shape, sound, character, font, slogan fragment), scores each on fame and uniqueness, and flags assets at risk of erosion.

You are a brand strategist auditing distinctive assets for a marketing course. 
A distinctive asset is a non-name cue (color, shape, sound, character, font, 
packaging, slogan fragment) that buyers tie to one brand. Be concise. No preamble.

For this brand, list its top five distinctive assets, score each on a 1-5 scale 
for fame (how many category buyers recognize it) and uniqueness (how strongly 
it is tied to this brand rather than shared with rivals), and flag any asset 
that a competitor is eroding or that the brand itself is diluting through 
inconsistent use.

Brand: [brand name and one-line description]

When to use it. Before any rebrand decision, before briefing a creative agency, or when evaluating whether a brand extension is safe.

Tool. Any chat model. Web search helpful for current competitive-erosion examples.

Expected output. Five labeled assets, each with a fame score, uniqueness score, and a one-line erosion flag where applicable.

Disclosure/verification note. Asset fame and uniqueness scores are the model's inference from public knowledge. Commission a real brand-tracking study before a major brand investment.

7-B. GEO Weekly-Check Prompt

What it does. The chapter's generative-engine-optimization move. Monitors how AI search engines describe your brand this week and tracks citation share over a four-week window.

Search the web for how AI-powered search engines (Perplexity, Google AI Overview, 
ChatGPT search) describe [brand name] when someone asks "[the category question 
your buyer asks, e.g., 'what is the best running shoe for marathon training']" 
right now. 

Report: (1) whether the brand appears in the AI-generated answer at all, 
(2) what it says when it does appear, (3) which sources it cites that mention 
the brand, and (4) one piece of content the brand could publish this week to 
improve its citation share. Be specific and dated.

When to use it. Weekly, or before any content-strategy decision. Run the same query four weeks in a row and note whether the brand's citation share moves.

Tool. Chat model with live web search enabled. Run in a new conversation each week so results are not contaminated by prior context.

Expected output. A brief report on AI-engine visibility, citing sources, with one actionable content recommendation for the week.

Disclosure/verification note. AI-search results change daily. Screenshot the output with the date before sharing. Never present a snapshot as the permanent state.

7-C. Perceptual-Map Read + Competitor Positioning

What it does. For any brand and two axes, places the brand and its named rivals on a map from their public positioning, then reads the empty quadrant.

You are a brand strategist helping a marketing student build a perceptual map. 
Reason about the actual named brands and their current real positioning, using 
what you know and any live web information. Be concrete and place brands only 
from their public positioning. No preamble, no marketing cliches.

Category: [e.g., "U.S. athletic footwear"]
Axis 1 (x): [e.g., "performance focus — lifestyle focus"]
Axis 2 (y): [e.g., "premium price — value price"]
Brands to place: [list 4-6 brand names]

Place each brand at approximate coordinates on a -5 to +5 scale for each axis, 
name the evidence (one phrase from their current marketing), and identify the 
one quadrant that is either empty or occupied only by weak brands. Then say 
whether buyer ideal points cluster there.

When to use it. STP analysis, competitive review, or when evaluating a potential repositioning move.

Tool. Chat model with web search enabled.

Expected output. Coordinate estimates for each brand with one-phrase evidence, plus a named empty quadrant and a verdict on whether it represents real demand.

Disclosure/verification note. Positioning is inferred from public marketing. Validate with real buyer surveys before using a perceptual map in a business decision.

Chapter 8: Product Management

What this chapter teaches. The product lifecycle, Rogers' innovation diffusion, packaging as strategy, and the 9x adoption-resistance rule.

8-A. Product-Lifecycle Stage Detector

What it does. The chapter's live-AI widget. Given any product or brand, it searches the web for current signals and places it on the PLC curve with evidence.

Search the live web for current signals about this product or brand, then place 
it on the product lifecycle curve (introduction / growth / maturity / decline). 
Name the specific signals that justify the placement: revenue trend, competitive 
intensity, price trajectory, and category penetration rate. Then name the one 
marketing move that fits this stage and one that does not. Be specific and dated.

Product / brand: [e.g., "Apple AirPods, mid-2026"]

When to use it. Before building a marketing plan or allocating budget. The right move depends on the stage.

Tool. Chat model with web search enabled.

Expected output. A PLC stage verdict, four sourced signals, one appropriate and one inappropriate marketing move.

Disclosure/verification note. PLC signals can be ambiguous. The model reads public data; verify revenue figures against the company's own filings.

8-B. Packaging-as-Strategy Audit (Apply Box)

What it does. Evaluates a product's packaging on its five jobs: attract attention, communicate quality, enable purchase, build brand recognition, and support sustainability claims.

Audit the packaging of [brand + product, e.g., "Liquid Death 16oz tallboy can"] 
on its five strategic jobs: (1) attract shelf attention, (2) communicate quality 
or brand personality, (3) enable purchase (ease of handling, information clarity), 
(4) build distinctive recognition at distance, (5) support any sustainability 
claim. For each job, give a pass/fail verdict and one sentence of evidence. 
Close with the one job the packaging handles worst and what a fix would cost.

When to use it. New-product development, competitive analysis, or when a brand's packaging feels misaligned with its positioning.

Tool. Any chat model. For better results, paste a link to the product page or describe the packaging in detail.

Expected output. Five labeled jobs, each with a pass/fail and one-sentence evidence, plus a lowest-scoring job and a fix.

Disclosure/verification note. The audit is based on described or publicly visible packaging. Physical inspection and shopper research are required before a redesign.

Chapter 9: Services Marketing

What this chapter teaches. SERVQUAL five-gap model, three justices (distributive, procedural, interactional), the customer journey map, and the service blueprint.

9-A. SERVQUAL Gap Diagnosis (Sharp Version)

What it does. The chapter's gap-diagnosis widget. Given one real customer complaint, it identifies which of the five SERVQUAL gaps it belongs in, cites the upstream cause, and prescribes a fix.

You are a service-quality advisor diagnosing a customer complaint with the 
SERVQUAL five-gap model. The brand and failure may be real and recent; search 
the live web for the current facts of the case before you judge, and cite a 
date where you can.

The five gaps are: Gap 1 knowledge (what management thinks buyers want vs 
what they want), Gap 2 standards (the spec written for staff vs the expectation), 
Gap 3 delivery (what the front line does vs the spec), Gap 4 communication 
(what the advertising promised vs what was delivered), Gap 5 perception 
(expected vs perceived service, the only gap the buyer feels). Gap 5 is 
downstream of the other four; do not default to Gap 5 unless you have ruled 
out the upstream four.

Output exactly four labelled sections and nothing else:
GAP: Gap N, its name.
EVIDENCE: the literal phrase from the complaint that points to this gap.
UPSTREAM CAUSE: the operational pattern inside the firm that produced it, 
one or two sentences.
FIX: the specific recovery move, one or two sentences.
Do not exceed 180 words.

Complaint to diagnose:
[Paste the complaint text. Can be a verbatim review, a support ticket, or a 
description of the failure.]

When to use it. Service-failure analysis, mystery-shopper debrief, or when a complaint has landed in your inbox and you need to route it to the right team.

Tool. Chat model with web search enabled (for real-brand cases).

Expected output. Four labeled sections: GAP, EVIDENCE, UPSTREAM CAUSE, FIX. Under 180 words.

Disclosure/verification note. Do not paste real customer PII (names, order numbers, emails). Replace with "[Customer]" and "[Order #]" before running.

9-B. Journey Map to Service Blueprint (Apply Box)

What it does. Converts a customer journey description into a service blueprint with frontstage and backstage actions, and flags the highest-risk failure point.

Convert this customer journey into a service blueprint. For each stage of the 
journey I describe, name: (1) the frontstage action (what the customer does or 
sees), (2) the frontstage employee action, (3) the backstage action the customer 
never sees, and (4) the supporting systems or tools. Then flag the single point 
where a failure is most likely to destroy the experience.

Journey stages for [brand + service, e.g., "a hotel check-in through departure"]:
[Describe 4-6 journey stages in plain English, one per line.]

When to use it. Service design, operations review, or building a training module. The backstage column reveals where the customer-facing experience actually breaks.

Tool. Any chat model.

Expected output. A table-like blueprint with four columns per stage, plus a one-sentence highest-risk-point flag.

Disclosure/verification note. The blueprint reflects your description of the process, not observed operations. Validate with frontline staff before using for training.

9-C. Score the Queue on Three Justices (Apply Box)

What it does. Given a service failure and a recovery response, scores the response on distributive, procedural, and interactional justice and identifies the justice leak.

Score this service recovery on the three justices:
- Distributive justice: was the compensation fair relative to the harm?
- Procedural justice: was the process fast, transparent, and consistent?
- Interactional justice: was the communication warm, personal, and respectful?

For each, give a score of 1-5 and one sentence of evidence from the recovery. 
Then name the one justice that is the leak — the lowest score that most explains 
why the customer left unhappy despite being compensated.

Failure: [describe the service failure in 1-2 sentences]
Recovery: [describe what the brand did in response]

When to use it. After a service recovery, when reading review data, or when diagnosing why compensation programs are not generating goodwill.

Tool. Any chat model.

Expected output. Three scored sections (1-5 with one-sentence evidence each) and a named justice leak.

Disclosure/verification note. Justice scores are based on the description you provide. Do not use real customer information. Verify the recovery details before presenting.

Chapter 10: Pricing

What this chapter teaches. The four pricing approaches (cost-based, competitor-based, value-based, customer-determined), price anchoring, and competitor pricing audit.

10-A. Pricing-Approach Picker (Sharp Version)

What it does. The chapter's core AI widget. Given a brand and segment, identifies which of the four pricing approaches fits, explains the dollar reasoning, and provides a sample price band.

You are a pricing advisor matching a real brand to one of the four pricing 
approaches. The brand and segment may be real, so you may search the live web 
for current facts and cite a date where you can. The four approaches are: 
cost-based (markup over unit cost, the floor, common in regulated utilities), 
competitor-based (anchor to rivals and adjust for differentiation, common in 
commodity and grocery categories), value-based (price to what the product is 
worth to a buyer who would otherwise buy the next-best alternative, the ceiling, 
the only approach tied to the buyer wallet), and customer-determined (the buyer 
names a candidate price and the firm accepts or rejects).

Output exactly four labelled sections and nothing else:
APPROACH: the one approach that fits, named.
WHY THIS ONE: one sentence on why it fits this brand and segment over the 
other three.
DOLLAR REASONING: the value or cost or competitor logic behind the number, 
one or two sentences.
SAMPLE BAND: a plausible price range with the unit, flagged as illustrative.
Do not exceed 170 words.

Brand and segment: [e.g., "Caterpillar excavators sold to mid-size construction 
firms bidding on municipal contracts"]

When to use it. Pricing strategy, competitive analysis, or the pricing section of a marketing plan.

Tool. Chat model with web search enabled.

Expected output. Four labeled sections under 170 words. SAMPLE BAND is always flagged as illustrative, not a recommendation.

Disclosure/verification note. The sample band is a model estimate, not a market study. Validate against actual transaction data or a pricing survey before setting a price.

10-B. Competitor Pricing Audit (Apply Box)

What it does. Scans current competitor prices, identifies the pricing anchor in the category, and flags where your brand is under- or over-positioned relative to the category frame.

Search the live web for current pricing of the top four competitors in 
[product category, e.g., "U.S. premium wireless headphones, 2026"]. For each 
competitor, report: the brand name, the flagship product SKU, the current 
list price, and one sentence on the value claim that justifies the price. 
Then name the price anchor in the category (the brand buyers use as the 
reference point), flag any brand priced above anchor without a clear value 
premium, and flag any brand leaving value on the table. Be specific and 
cite a source and date for each price.

When to use it. Before setting a price, launching a new SKU, or responding to a competitive price move.

Tool. Chat model with web search enabled.

Expected output. A four-row comparison table plus a named anchor, an over-priced flag, and an under-priced flag, each with source and date.

Disclosure/verification note. Prices change. Verify every price on the retailer's or brand's own site the day you use the data.

Chapter 11: Distribution

What this chapter teaches. Channel design, markup vs. margin arithmetic, the Iron Law of Distribution, and the four forces of channel conflict.

11-A. Channel Arithmetic: Markup vs. Margin (Apply Box)

What it does. Runs the markup-vs-margin calculation for any channel and flags where a brand's pricing math breaks down when it adds an intermediary.

A brand sells a product at a manufacturer's suggested retail price of $[MSRP]. 
The brand's cost to produce is $[unit cost]. A retailer who carries the product 
takes a [X]% margin on the retail price. A distributor between the brand and 
retailer takes a [Y]% margin on the price it charges the retailer.

Calculate: (1) the brand's net revenue per unit after both intermediaries, 
(2) the brand's contribution margin per unit, and (3) whether the brand can 
still cover its fixed costs at this channel structure if it sells [Z] units 
per month. Then flag the one intermediary that is taking the most value and 
name one alternative channel structure that would recover it.

When to use it. Before adding a new retail or distribution partner, evaluating a channel conflict, or building the distribution section of a marketing plan.

Tool. Any chat model (arithmetic is simple but the model handles the framing).

Expected output. Three calculated outputs plus a named margin-taker and one alternative channel structure.

Disclosure/verification note. Fill in real numbers from your brand's cost sheet. Do not use publicly confidential channel terms without authorization.

11-B. Four-Forces Channel Conflict Triage (Apply Box)

What it does. Given a channel conflict situation, identifies which of the four conflict forces is driving it and prescribes the appropriate response.

Classify this channel conflict and prescribe a response. The four forces are: 
(1) goal incompatibility — brand and channel partner want different things, 
(2) role ambiguity — unclear who owns which customer, territory, or SKU, 
(3) power imbalance — one side has leverage the other cannot match, 
(4) perceptual differences — brand and partner read the same data differently.

For this conflict: name the primary force, name the secondary force if one 
exists, and prescribe the response from the conflict-response taxonomy 
(collaboration, compromise, accommodation, avoidance, or competition with 
reasoning). Close with one sentence on the risk of inaction.

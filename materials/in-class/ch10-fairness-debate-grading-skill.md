# Grading Skill: The Price-Fairness Debate on a Real Case

*Chapter 10 group activity. This file lets you grade a student's work with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste a block of instructions into Claude, paste the work under it, and read back a score for each part of the rubric with a sentence of feedback. The students debated and wrote their briefs with no AI; your optional AI use here is only to speed your grading. Because this is a group activity with a per-member individual brief, you grade one member at a time. You stay in charge: the AI gives you a first-pass score, you decide the grade. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom in case you would rather not use AI at all.*

---

## Part 1. If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install anything or pay anything. This takes about five minutes the first time and under two minutes per student after that.

1. **Open a web browser** (Chrome, Safari, Edge, whatever you use) and go to **claude.ai**. (ChatGPT at **chatgpt.com** works the same way if you prefer it.)
2. **Make a free account.** Click **Sign up**, enter your email, and follow the steps. A free account is enough for this. You will not be charged.
3. **Start a new chat.** After you log in you will see a box that says something like *"How can I help you today?"* That box is where you type. A "chat" is just a conversation with the AI, like texting.
4. **Copy the grader instructions.** In Part 2 below, everything inside the gray box is one block. Select it all, copy it, and paste it into the chat box. Do not press send yet.
5. **Add the work underneath.** This is a group activity, so paste the **joint debate summary** and then **one member's own one-page position brief** right below the instructions, in the same message. Grade one member per chat so their individual brief is scored on its own and you can spot near-duplicates across the group.
6. **Send it.** Press Enter (or click the arrow). The AI will read both and reply with a score for each of the four rubric rows, a total out of 100, and a short note for each row.
7. **Read the score and decide.** The AI's score is a **first draft for you to approve, change, or override.** You are the grader of record. If a score looks off, trust your own read, the grading guide (`ch10-fairness-debate-grading-guide.md`) tells you what each row is really worth.
8. **Grade the next member.** Click **New chat**, paste the grader block again, then the same joint summary plus the next member's brief. A fresh chat each time keeps one member's work from bleeding into the next and helps you catch copied briefs.

That is the whole process. The block in Part 2 does the work; you paste, read, and sign off.

> **One quick thing to look for yourself, no AI needed.** This activity lives or dies on whether the brief argues from the **principle** rather than gut feel. Skim for the dual-entitlement test: did the student ask whether the increase defended a real cost (fair) or captured a demand windfall (gouging), and did they name the strongest counter to their own side? And remember the rule the AI is told to follow too: grade the **quality of the argument, not which side won**. A student who argued the harder side well earns full marks.

## Part 2. The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a second-year marketing student's work from an in-class price-fairness debate, held with no AI. A group of four to six split into a "fair" side and an "unfair" side and debated the same embedded case: Meadowbrook Gardens, a family theme park that replaced a long-standing flat $52 gate price with a published calendar charging $38 on slow weekdays and $74 on peak summer Saturdays, on a brand advertised as "a day out the whole family can afford, any weekend." The case states that the park's costs did not rise; the change was driven by crowding and demand, not cost. The case is genuinely two-sided, so neither verdict is the "right answer." Each member wrote their own one-page position brief arguing their assigned side. Your job is to score ONE member's contribution (their individual brief, read against the group's joint summary) using the rubric below, and give short, kind, specific feedback. This is a first-year-friendly course.
>
> **Grade the quality of the argument, NOT which side the student argued or whether you agree with it.** A student assigned the harder side who argues it well earns full marks. Do not reward a confident verdict over a principled argument.
>
> **Background you need (the chapter's frameworks):**
> - The **dual-entitlement principle** is the core test: a price increase that **defends a real cost** reads as fair, while one that **captures a demand windfall** (charging more just because demand spiked) reads as gouging. A strong brief runs this test on the real facts.
> - **Category tolerance:** some categories carry a tight reference price buyers treat as a promise (a theme park that posted one flat price for a decade), others a loose one where prices already move (airfare). The tighter the reference price, the more a peak increase stings. For this case, the long-standing flat $52 makes the reference price tight.
> - **The better-than-static test:** dynamic pricing reads as fairer when some buyers get a genuinely better deal than the old single price gave them (a real off-peak discount), not just a peak surcharge. For this case, the weekday price genuinely fell to $38, below the old flat $52, which is a real fair-side point.
> - **Brand promise and pre-launch communication:** a change consistent with the brand's promise and published in advance reads as fairer than a surprise surcharge that breaks an implied promise. For this case, the calendar was posted a season ahead (a fair-side point), but the brand had promised affordability "any weekend," which a peak-Saturday surcharge strains (an unfair-side point).
> - The **six dynamic-pricing fairness principles** are explicability, transparency at the moment of encounter, cooperative framing, category tolerance, brand-promise compatibility, and pre-launch communication. A strong brief applies at least two correctly to this case.
> - A strong brief **argues from the principle and names the strongest counter to its own side honestly.** A weak brief asserts a verdict by gut feel ("it cost more, so it was unfair") with no principle behind it.
>
> **How to score.** Give a number for each of the four criteria below, then add them for a total out of 100. For each, write one sentence saying why, and one sentence the student could act on. Be specific and encouraging. If something required is missing, say so plainly and score it low; do not invent credit.
>
> 1. **The fairness argument, built on the principle (44 points).** Does the brief argue its assigned side from the dual-entitlement principle (cost defense vs demand windfall), apply category tolerance and the better-than-static test to the real case, AND name the strongest counter honestly? Full marks: the principle applied to the facts plus an honest counter. Low marks: a gut-feel verdict with little of the principle.
> 2. **Use of the case facts and the six principles (22 points).** Does the brief marshal the real case facts and apply at least two of the six dynamic-pricing principles correctly for its side? Deduct when the case is mentioned but the principles are not applied, or the argument floats free of the facts.
> 3. **Individual position brief (22 points).** Is this member's brief their own, does it take a clear side, and does it name one supporting AND one opposing piece of evidence? If it reads like a near-duplicate of a teammate's brief, or names only supporting evidence, say so and score it low; the individual brief is the anti-slacker check.
> 4. **Joint summary and writing (12 points).** Does the joint summary fairly capture both sides and where the room landed, in plain language with correct terms? Deduct for a one-sided summary that reports only the winning side.
>
> **Watch for these patterns and score them down (with a kind note):** a gut-feel fairness claim with no dual-entitlement reasoning; a brief that ignores the actual case facts; a brief that names only supporting evidence and ducks the counter; a near-duplicate of a teammate's brief; a one-sided joint summary. **Do NOT** mark a brief down for arguing an unpopular side, that is the assignment.
>
> **Output format:**
> - **Criterion 1, The fairness argument: __/44.** (why), then (one tip)
> - **Criterion 2, Case facts and the six principles: __/22.** (why), then (one tip)
> - **Criterion 3, Individual position brief: __/22.** (why), then (one tip)
> - **Criterion 4, Joint summary and writing: __/12.** (why), then (one tip)
> - **Total: __/100**
> - **One-line summary for the student:** (warm, specific, what to do next time)
>
> The joint debate summary and one member's brief follow below this line.

## Part 3. Reading and trusting the result

- The AI returns four scores, a total, and short notes. **Skim the notes first**, they tell you whether the AI actually understood the brief. If the notes match what you saw, the total is trustworthy.
- **You can push back.** Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example *"This member argued the harder side and applied dual entitlement well, look again at Criterion 1"* or *"Check whether the brief named a counter, not just supporting evidence."* The AI will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- **The fairness-argument row is the one to read closely yourself.** It is worth the most, and it is where a confident verdict can fool the AI into generosity. A brief that runs the dual-entitlement test on the real facts and names an honest counter is strong even if its conclusion is unpopular.
- **Hold the line that the grade is for the argument, not the side.** If the AI seems to reward the side it finds more sympathetic, correct it: a well-argued unpopular side earns full marks.
- **Catch near-duplicates yourself.** Because you grade one member per chat, lay the group's briefs side by side once. If several are the same with a word swapped, score the copies down on the individual-brief row.
- **Keep a fresh chat per member** so briefs do not blur together.
- Remember the framing for any feedback you pass on: *the instructor grades; the AI assists.* The students used no AI; the score is yours.

## Part 4. Grade by hand instead (no AI at all)

If you would rather not use AI, grade straight from the rubric. Read the joint summary once, then score each member's brief on the four rows:

| Criterion | Points | What earns full marks |
|---|---|---|
| **The fairness argument, built on the principle** | 44 | Argues the assigned side from the dual-entitlement principle (cost-defense vs demand-windfall), applies category tolerance and the better-than-static test to the real case, and names the strongest counter honestly |
| **Use of the case facts and the six principles** | 22 | Marshals the real case facts and applies at least two of the six dynamic-pricing principles correctly for the side argued |
| **Individual position brief** | 22 | The member's own brief, taking a clear side, naming one supporting AND one opposing piece of evidence (not a near-duplicate of a teammate's) |
| **Joint summary and writing** | 12 | Joint summary fairly captures both sides and where the room landed; plain, clear, correct terms |
| **Total** | **100** | |

**Fast bands:** 90 to 100 a brief that runs the dual-entitlement test on the real facts, applies category tolerance and the better-than-static test, names the strongest counter, uses at least two of the six principles, with every member's own distinct brief and a fair joint summary; 80 to 89 a principled argument with one thin row; 70 to 79 the case engaged but leaning on gut feel, or only supporting evidence named; 60 to 69 a gut-feel verdict with no principle, an abstract brief, or near-duplicate briefs; below 60 a missing joint summary or missing briefs.

The single fastest signal of a strong brief: **the dual-entitlement test applied to the real facts plus an honest counter.** A student who argues "here is why my side is right, and here is the best point against me" has done the chapter's core move, whichever side they were assigned. Grade the argument, not the verdict, and never mark a brief down for arguing an unpopular side. Because the individual brief is the anti-slacker rule, grade each member's own work and score copied briefs down. Full details and failure modes are in `ch10-fairness-debate-grading-guide.md`.

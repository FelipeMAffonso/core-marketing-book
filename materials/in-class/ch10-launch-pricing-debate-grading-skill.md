# Grading Skill: Skim or Penetrate: A Live Launch Debate

*Chapter 10 in-class activity, no AI account needed by students. This file lets you grade a student's individual brief with the help of a free AI account, even if you have never used one. You paste a block of instructions into Claude, paste the student's brief under it, and read back a score for each part of the rubric with a sentence of feedback. You stay in charge: the AI gives you a first-pass score, you decide the grade. Because this is a per-member brief written live in class, you grade one student at a time. A grade-by-hand version is at the bottom in case you would rather not use AI at all.*

---

## Part 1. If you have never used AI before, start here

You do not need to install anything or pay anything. This takes about five minutes the first time and under two minutes per student after that.

1. **Open a web browser** (Chrome, Safari, Edge, whatever you use) and go to **claude.ai**. (ChatGPT at **chatgpt.com** works the same way if you prefer it.)
2. **Make a free account.** Click **Sign up**, enter your email, and follow the steps. A free account is enough for this. You will not be charged.
3. **Start a new chat.** After you log in you will see a box that says something like *"How can I help you today?"* That box is where you type. A "chat" is just a conversation with the AI, like texting.
4. **Copy the grader instructions.** In Part 2 below, everything inside the gray box is one block. Select it all, copy it, and paste it into the chat box. Do not press send yet.
5. **Add the student's brief underneath.** Press Enter twice to make space, then paste the student's one-paragraph brief right below the instructions, in the same message. Grade one student per chat so each brief is scored on its own and you can spot near-duplicates across the group.
6. **Send it.** Press Enter (or click the arrow). The AI will read the brief and reply with a score for each of the four rubric rows, a total out of 100, and a short note for each row.
7. **Read the score and decide.** The AI's score is a **first draft for you to approve, change, or override.** You are the grader of record. If a score looks off, trust your own read. The grading guide (`ch10-launch-pricing-debate-grading-guide.md`) holds the full answer key and tells you what each row is really worth.
8. **Grade the next student.** Click **New chat**, paste the grader block again, then the next student's brief. A fresh chat each time keeps one brief from bleeding into the next and helps you catch copied briefs.

That is the whole process. The block in Part 2 does the work; you paste, read, and sign off.

> **The two-second check you can always run yourself, no AI needed.** The posture-call row (worth 44 points) comes down to one test: did the student name the q/p ratio from the scenario (approximately 3) and note that it falls below the chapter's threshold of 4? If yes, the argument is principled. If no, it is gut feel. The single-number question (28 points) has a clear best answer: the student should name a specific number from the scenario, say which direction it would need to move, and explain why. Any of q, p, the q/p ratio, the competitor price, or the unit cost is fair game. If the student names "a number" without specifying, or says "if the cost were lower" without an amount, that is Developing.

## Part 2. The grader block (copy everything in the gray box)

> You are helping an instructor grade a second-year marketing student's work from an in-class launch-pricing debate, held with no AI. A group of four to six was split into two sides at the bell: half argued for a skimming launch at $115, half argued for a penetration launch at $68. The student wrote their own one-paragraph brief in the last 15 minutes of class. Your job is to score ONE student's brief using the rubric below and give short, kind, specific feedback. This is a first-year-friendly course.
>
> **Grade the quality of the argument, NOT which side the student was assigned or whether you agree with it.** A student assigned the harder side (penetration, which the q/p math does not favor in this scenario) who argues it well earns full marks. Do not reward a confident posture call over a principled one.
>
> **The scenario the student debated (you need this to grade the brief):**
> Maison Tula is a regional indie cosmetics brand in Austin, Texas, launching its first prestige fragrance, Tula No. 7, a cedar-and-citrus eau de parfum. Unit cost is $28. The two postures being argued: a skimming launch at $115 or a penetration launch at $68. Comparable indie fragrances from Dedcool and Skylar price at $95 to $98. The diffusion estimates from the scenario: q &asymp; 0.12 (imitation coefficient), p &asymp; 0.04 (innovation coefficient), giving a q/p ratio of approximately 3. The chapter's Bass regime rule says: q/p below about 4 favors skimming (imitation cascade too weak to recover sacrificed margin); q/p above about 7 favors penetration (imitation cascade dominates). This product's q/p of approximately 3 falls below the threshold of 4, so the chapter's math favors skimming. The diffusion horizon is 24 months.
>
> **The brief asks two questions:**
> 1. Which side was more persuaded by the chapter's q/p logic and margin math? The student is not required to agree with their assigned side; they are required to explain why the chapter's numbers favor one posture.
> 2. What is the single number from the scenario that would change their mind if it were different? Name the number, the direction it would need to move, and explain why.
>
> **Margin arithmetic you need:** a 30% margin on a $28 cost requires price = $28 divided by (1 minus 0.30) = $28 divided by 0.70 = approximately $40. That is the floor. Markup is different: a 30% markup on a $28 cost is $28 times 1.30 = $36.40. The two are not the same. A student who uses markup instead of margin has made a mechanical error; note it and score the margin row down.
>
> **How to score.** Give a number for each of the four criteria below, then add them for a total out of 100. For each, write one sentence saying why, and one sentence the student could act on. Be specific and encouraging. If something required is missing, say so plainly and score it low; do not invent credit.
>
> 1. **Posture call rooted in the q/p logic (44 points).** Does the brief name the posture their side argued, apply the q/p ratio from the scenario (approximately 3), note that this falls below the chapter's threshold of 4, and explain the implication for the 24-month diffusion horizon? Full marks: the ratio named, the threshold applied, and the connection to the diffusion window explicit. Low marks: a posture picked by gut feel with no q/p reasoning ("prestige fragrance should cost more" or "sharing helps penetration" with no ratio).
> 2. **The single-number question answered correctly (28 points).** Does the brief name a specific number from the scenario (the q/p ratio, the imitation coefficient q, the competitor price, the unit cost), state the direction it would need to move, and explain the mechanism? Full marks: a named number, a direction, and a causal explanation. Low marks: "if the cost were different" with no amount, or the question skipped entirely.
> 3. **Margin arithmetic applied correctly (16 points).** Does the brief use the margin formula correctly at least once? Price equals unit cost divided by (1 minus margin rate). At 30%, that is $28 divided by 0.70, which equals approximately $40. The student does not need to write the formula; a correct calculation with a reference to a specific price and margin rate is enough. Deduct if the student uses markup instead (cost times (1 plus margin rate)) and calls it margin.
> 4. **Brief quality: own voice, clear argument (12 points).** Is this clearly the student's own paragraph, taking a position, reading as an argument rather than a recap of what both sides said? Deduct if it reads neutral, or like a near-duplicate of a tablemate's brief.
>
> **Watch for these patterns and score them down (with a kind note):** a posture picked by gut feel with no q/p reasoning; the single-number question skipped or answered with a gesture; markup used in place of margin and the error not noticed; a paragraph that recaps the debate rather than taking a side; a near-duplicate of another student's brief.
>
> **Do NOT** mark a brief down for arguing the penetration side, even though the q/p math favors skimming. A penetration brief that honestly reckons with the q/p threshold and argues the estimate is uncertain given Maison Tula's distribution gap is doing real intellectual work. That is the assignment.
>
> **Output format:**
> - **Criterion 1, Posture call rooted in q/p logic: __/44.** (why), then (one tip)
> - **Criterion 2, Single-number question answered: __/28.** (why), then (one tip)
> - **Criterion 3, Margin arithmetic: __/16.** (why), then (one tip)
> - **Criterion 4, Brief quality and own voice: __/12.** (why), then (one tip)
> - **Total: __/100**
> - **One-line summary for the student:** (warm, specific, what to do next time)
>
> The student's one-paragraph brief follows below this line.

## Part 3. Reading and trusting the result

- The AI returns four scores, a total, and short notes. **Skim the notes first.** They tell you whether the AI actually understood the brief. Check that the note on Criterion 1 says something about the q/p ratio and the threshold of 4; if it does not, the AI may have read a different posture logic into the brief than the chapter uses.
- **You can push back.** Type a follow-up in the same chat, for example *"The student named the q/p ratio and the threshold but did not connect it to the 24-month window; look again at Criterion 1"* or *"The student wrote 1.30 times cost and called it a margin; is Criterion 3 scored correctly?"* The AI will re-score. You are calibrating it, not obeying it.
- **The posture-call row is the one to read closely yourself.** It is worth the most points, and it is where a confident-sounding brief can fool the AI into generosity. A brief that names the q/p ratio and notes the threshold is principled; a brief that argues "luxury fragrance should be priced high" without mentioning the ratio is not, even if it sounds more assured.
- **Check the single-number row yourself.** The AI sometimes accepts a vague gesture ("the cost") as a named number. Read the brief yourself: did the student name a specific value from the scenario, give a direction, and explain the mechanism? If not, the row should be Developing or Not-yet.
- **Catch near-duplicates yourself.** Because you grade one student per chat, lay the group's briefs side by side once. If several are the same paragraph with a name swapped, score the copies down on the brief-quality row.
- **Keep a fresh chat per student** so briefs do not blur together.
- Remember the framing for any feedback you pass on: *the instructor grades; the AI assists.* The students used no AI; the score is yours.

## Part 4. Grade by hand instead (no AI at all)

If you would rather not use AI, grade straight from the rubric. Read each student's brief once and score the four rows.

| Criterion | Points | What earns full marks |
|---|---|---|
| **Posture call rooted in the q/p logic** | 44 | Names the posture, applies the q/p ratio (approximately 3) from the scenario, notes the chapter's threshold of 4, and explains the implication for the 24-month diffusion horizon |
| **The single-number question answered** | 28 | Names a specific number from the scenario (q, p, q/p ratio, competitor price, or unit cost), states the direction it would need to move, and explains the mechanism |
| **Margin arithmetic applied correctly** | 16 | Uses price = cost divided by (1 minus margin rate) correctly at least once; does not confuse markup (cost times (1 plus markup rate)) with margin |
| **Brief quality: own voice, clear argument** | 12 | The student's own paragraph, taking a clear position, reading as an argument rather than a neutral recap |
| **Total** | **100** | |

**Fast bands:** 90 to 100 is a brief that names the q/p ratio of approximately 3, notes the threshold of 4, connects it to the 24-month diffusion window, names a specific number for the flip question with a direction and a mechanism, and uses margin arithmetic correctly, all in the student's own voice; 80 to 89 is a principled posture call with one thin row (threshold named without the diffusion-horizon link, or the single-number question answered with a plausible number but thin mechanism); 70 to 79 is a posture call leaning on category intuition with thin q/p reasoning, and the single-number question vague; 60 to 69 is a gut-feel posture with no q/p reasoning and the single-number question skipped or a near-duplicate brief; below 60 or an integrity flag for a missing brief or a brief that is clearly pre-generated without any reference to the actual debate. Handle integrity questions through your institution's academic-integrity and AI-use policy, not the rubric.

The single fastest signal of a strong brief: **the q/p ratio of approximately 3 named alongside the chapter's threshold of 4, with an explanation of why that ratio means the imitation cascade will not arrive fast enough.** A student who writes that has done the chapter's core move.

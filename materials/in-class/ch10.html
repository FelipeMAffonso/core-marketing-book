The Fitness-Chain Pricing Consultants

A Chapter 10 in-class group activity that runs on paper. The room becomes the pricing team for a small chain of fitness studios that has tried one flat membership rate for three years and is leaving real money on the table. Each team is handed one of the four price-customization levers from the chapter and has thirty minutes to design a price tier for that lever, present the case for it, and defend it against the other three. Each member turns in their own one-paragraph verdict on which lever should anchor the chain's new price structure. Done in groups of three or four, with no AI.

  • TypeGroup of 3-4
  • Time~45 hrs
  • Points100 pts
  • ChapterCh 10

Student instructions

You are stepping into the pricing-team chair for one room. Pulse Studios is a small chain of seven boutique fitness studios in one metro area, charging one flat membership of $89 per month that buys unlimited access to every studio at every hour. The owner has hired your team because the flat rate is not capturing the value the gym actually creates: some members come every day at 6 a.m., some come twice a month at noon, some sit through every spin class while others only want the sauna, and a few corporate clients want a bulk rate for their teams. The flat $89 leaves the heavy users underpaying and the light users overpaying, which is exactly the problem price customization is built to fix.

Your team is one of four sets of consultants in the room. Each set is assigned one of the chapter's four price-customization levers and has half an hour to design the price tier that uses that lever, present the case for it to the other three teams, and answer the room's questions. The four levers, one per team:

  1. Controlling availability. Set the price by what time the member can train (peak versus off-peak, weekend versus weekday, hold a few hours back from the basic tier). Design at least two tiers tied to time-of-day or day-of-week, and propose a number for each. Reason from why a 6 a.m. weekday slot is more valuable than a 2 p.m. Tuesday slot.
  2. Buyer characteristics. Set the price by who the member is (student, senior, healthcare worker, corporate partner). Design at least two buyer tiers and propose a number for each. Reason from why each group's willingness to pay differs and why the chain can verify the category cleanly (a student ID, a corporate domain in the email, a confirmed senior age).
  3. Transaction characteristics. Set the price by how the member buys (a month-to-month plan versus a six-month commit versus an annual paid up front; a single-studio plan versus the full network). Design at least two transaction tiers and propose a number for each. Reason from why a member who commits to a year is worth a discount and why a member who shows up week to week is not.
  4. Product-line good/better/best. Set the price by the bundle the member chooses (basic gym access, a mid-tier that adds group classes, a top tier that adds spa and a guest pass). Design three tiers and propose a number for each. Reason from why the good-better-best ladder lets the chain serve a price-sensitive student and a high-willingness-to-pay professional from the same studio.

The chapter's four customization levers are the menu. Each team has one of them; no team designs two.

The chain's numbers (what every team needs)

Pulse Studios, a small fitness chain. Seven studios in one metro area, one flat membership at $89 per month, unlimited access at every studio and every hour.

What it costs the chain per active member per month (the floor): about $22, in instructor time, cleaning, towels, equipment depreciation, software, and the share of rent and utilities allocated to one member. A price beneath that loses money on every membership.

What rivals charge (the frame): the budget chain across town runs about $15 per month for off-peak basic access only. A mid-tier studio across the metro charges about $129 per month for unlimited access plus group classes. A high-end boutique nearby charges $199 per month for unlimited access plus group classes, a sauna, and one guest pass per month.

What the chain knows about its members (the value signal): the top quartile of members come 18 or more times a month and would pay noticeably more for the same access; the bottom quartile come two or three times a month and many of them would drop the membership if it rose another $10; corporate partners have asked for a per-employee rate for groups of 20 or more; students at the nearby university have asked for a cheaper plan with limited hours.

How the activity runs (the runnable kit)

  1. Setup (5 min). Class splits into four teams of three or four. The instructor assigns one of the four levers to each team. Every team receives the chain's numbers above and the worksheet below.
  2. Design the tier (15 min). Each team designs the price tier for its lever using the worksheet. The work is to name at least two (or three, for the good-better-best team) sub-tiers, set a number for each that sits between the $22 floor and the $199 high-end ceiling, and write the one-sentence reason each tier earns its number from the chain's value signal.
  3. Present and question (15 min). Each team gets three minutes to present its tier to the room and three minutes to take questions from the other three teams. The questions to expect, and to bring: which sub-tier captures the most member willingness to pay, which sub-tier is the easiest to enforce without gaming, and which fairness risk the tier introduces. The room keeps a public tally of who answered cleanly and who did not.
  4. Room vote (5 min). After all four teams present, the room votes on which of the four levers should be the anchor of Pulse's new price structure (the one the chain leads with) and which two should be the secondary levers stacked on top. The instructor records the vote on the board.
  5. Individual verdict (5 min, written quietly). Each member writes their own one-paragraph verdict naming the lever they would anchor on, one specific reason from the chain's numbers, and one fairness risk they would have to manage.

Submit

A team packet that contains:

  1. Your team's design worksheet (the lever, the sub-tiers with their prices and reasons, the fairness risk and its mitigation).
  2. A one-page room summary that records the four levers, the team that designed each, the price tiers each proposed, and the room's vote on which lever should anchor the new structure. One team takes this on, or a volunteer scorekeeper does.
  3. Each member's own one-paragraph verdict (separate paragraphs, one per member, not copied across the team).

Follow your institution's academic-integrity and AI-use policy for any tool you use along the way. The activity itself does not need any AI; the value is the team's own argument in the room.

On the job. Designing a price tier is one of the most common pricing moves a brand or revenue team makes, and the half-hour you spend defending one lever against three rivals is exactly the meeting a price change has to survive before it ships.

    Grading Guide: The Fitness-Chain Pricing Consultants

    Instructor-facing. This is the answer key for the in-class consultants activity, and students never see it. Grade against the assignment's rubric: tier design 40, lever used correctly 18, fairness risk 18, individual verdict 24.

    What this card tests

    Whether the team can take one of the chapter's four price-customization levers and turn it into a defensible tier on a specific chain with specific numbers, and whether each member can carry their own one-paragraph reading of which lever should anchor the structure. The activity is inherently AI-resistant because the team has to take live questions from a rival team in the room and adjust on the spot, which a model cannot do for them.

    The answer key: what good and weak tier designs look like, by lever

    The chain's numbers anchor every team's design: the floor is $22 per member per month, the rival frame runs $15 (budget) to $129 (mid) to $199 (high-end), the value signal is the heavy-user top quartile at 18-plus visits per month, the light-user bottom quartile at two or three, the corporate ask for 20-plus employees, and the student ask for a cheaper limited-hours plan. Every team's prices have to sit between $22 and the high-end ceiling and have to earn the price from one of those signals.

    Team 1, controlling availability (peak versus off-peak). A strong tier sets a basic off-peak plan at about $49 to $59 per month (weekday hours outside 6 to 9 a.m. and 5 to 8 p.m., or weekend-only access) and the original unlimited plan at about $99 to $109. The strong reasoning ties the lower tier to the budget rival's $15 plan (anyone who would have churned now has a Pulse plan that fits their schedule) and the higher tier to the heavy-user signal (the 6 a.m. user is the high-willingness-to-pay slot). A weak tier sets off-peak at $30 (beneath the $22 floor when discounted further) or sets both tiers above $89 (no real off-peak discount, the lever is not used).

    Team 2, buyer characteristics (student, senior, healthcare, corporate). A strong tier sets the student plan at about $49 to $55 per month (verified by a current .edu email and a student-only off-peak window), the senior plan at about $59 to $65, the healthcare/first-responder plan at about $69, and the corporate per-employee rate at about $59 to $69 for groups of 20-plus. The strong reasoning ties each price to a willingness-to-pay signal (a student would otherwise pay the budget chain's $15 plan, a corporate buyer has budget for $80-plus but is buying volume) and names how the chain verifies the category. A weak tier sets a senior price beneath the $22 floor, or fails to verify the category (a student plan with no student check is gamed by every adult in the metro).

    Team 3, transaction characteristics (month-to-month versus annual, single-studio versus full-network). A strong tier sets month-to-month at the current $89, six-month commit at about $79 per month ($474 paid up front), annual at about $69 per month ($828 paid up front), with the option of single-studio access for about $15 to $20 less per month at each tier. The strong reasoning is that an annual commit is worth a 22% discount because the chain captures cash up front and locks in 12 months of revenue. A weak tier sets a one-year discount of 5% (which fails to compensate the member for losing flexibility) or sets the annual price beneath the floor.

    Team 4, product-line good/better/best. A strong tier sets a basic plan at about $59 to $69 (access to one home studio plus weekday classes), a mid-tier at about $99 to $109 (full network access, all classes, mobile app workouts), and a top tier at about $149 to $169 (full network, all classes, the sauna and steam room, one guest pass per month, and a quarterly performance assessment). The strong reasoning ties each price to a separable value signal and names what the chain takes away from the lower tier (a strong good-better-best tier is built on what the basic plan does NOT include, not just what the top plan adds). A weak tier sets three prices close to each other (no real spread) or names "premium feel" as the differentiator without a concrete cut.

    The vote and the room summary

    After all four teams present, the room votes on which lever anchors Pulse's new price structure. There is no single right answer; the strongest cases the chapter would defend are:

    • The good-better-best lever as the anchor is the most defensible pick for a small chain because it sets up the whole price ladder in one structure that the member can read in fifteen seconds; the other three levers stack on top of the basic-mid-top spine (the student tier is a buyer-characteristics modifier on the basic plan, the annual discount is a transaction modifier on each tier, the off-peak option is an availability modifier on the basic plan).
    • The buyer-characteristics lever as the anchor is defensible if the chain's biggest revenue opportunity is the corporate ask, which the team can show from the chain's numbers (a 200-employee corporate deal at $69 per employee per month is $13,800 of monthly recurring revenue from one signed contract).
    • The availability lever as the anchor is defensible only when the chain has a lot of off-peak capacity, which Pulse's data does not strongly suggest.
    • The transaction lever as the anchor alone is weak because it gives the chain no way to address the corporate ask or the student ask.

    A room that votes for the buyer-characteristics lever or the good-better-best lever as the anchor has read the chain's numbers correctly. A room that votes for the availability lever or the transaction lever as the anchor without explaining why is reading the chapter and not the case.

    Fairness risks per lever (what the rubric rewards)

    • Availability: an off-peak tier that effectively prices out shift workers who can only train at 6 a.m. (a dual-entitlement risk: the chain is charging the people with the least schedule flexibility the most). Mitigation: cap the peak premium at a transparent number tied to the cost of staffing the peak hours.
    • Buyer characteristics: a student tier that adults game by claiming to be students (gameable verification), or a senior tier that reads as ageist by a buyer in their 50s who is healthier than many in their 30s. Mitigation: verify the category with a real signal (a .edu email plus a current term, a state-issued ID with a date) and avoid moralistic category framing.
    • Transaction: an annual plan that locks in members who lose income mid-year and cannot cancel without a real reason (a dual-entitlement risk: a windfall to the chain when a member stops using the gym but still pays). Mitigation: a pause-or-refund clause for genuine hardship, like the gym version of an airline's bereavement fare.
    • Good-better-best: a basic tier that removes value the chain claimed was part of the membership all along (a member who has been paying $89 for sauna access now finds sauna is in the top tier only). Mitigation: grandfather current members on the original feature set for the renewal cycle and apply the new tier structure only to new members for the first six months.

    A team that names a real risk and a real mitigation earns full marks on the fairness row; a team that names "people might feel cheated" without naming a specific risk earns Developing.

    Three ways to grade

    1. By hand against this key (fastest). Read the team's worksheet and check the prices are within band, the reasoning is from the chain's numbers, and the team answered at least one of the room's questions cleanly. Read each member's verdict for a named lever, a specific reason from the chain's numbers, and a named fairness risk.
    2. Hand it to a TA after calibrating together on one strong and one weak team's packet.
    3. Use the grading skill (ch10-fitness-chain-consultants-grading-skill.md): paste it into a free Claude or ChatGPT account with the team's packet for a first-pass score. It carries this same key.

    Score bands

    • 90 to 100: the tier prices sit cleanly within the $22-to-$199 band, every price ties to a specific signal in the chain's numbers, the team held up against the room's questions, and the fairness risk was real and the mitigation worked.
    • 80 to 89: the tier is well-designed and within band, but one price is asserted rather than reasoned, or one of the room's questions was not answered cleanly.
    • 70 to 79: the tier was within band, but the reasoning slid from the chain's data into general arguments the team could have made about any gym.
    • 60 to 69 or below: the tier slipped its lever (the time-of-day team designed a student plan instead), or the prices were not anchored to the chain's numbers, or every member's verdict was a near-duplicate of one teammate's paragraph.

    Failure modes to watch

    The most common failure is lever drift: a team assigned the transaction lever quietly turns its plan into a good-better-best with a small discount layered on. Catch this in the question round by asking the team to name the lever and the sub-tier in the same sentence; if they cannot, dock the lever-used-correctly row. The second most common is prices outside the band: a team that sets a tier at $25 has forgotten the floor, and a team that sets one at $249 has forgotten the high-end ceiling. The third is identical individual verdicts, where every member writes the same paragraph because they copied the team summary; the rubric scores each member separately on this row to enforce the individual artifact.

The Restaurant Pricing Game

A Chapter 10 group activity that runs in the room on paper. Every team runs a competing campus taco stand at the same unit cost, every team writes a price each round, and the market is so elastic the cheapest stand walks off with the crowd. The lesson is felt, not computed: a race to the bottom wins customers and destroys margin, and a team that holds or differentiates is the one with the better story at the end. Done in groups of three or four with an individual debrief from each member.

  • TypeGroup of 3-4
  • Time~30 hrs
  • Points100 pts
  • ChapterCh 10

Student instructions

You are running a campus taco stand against the other teams in this room. Every stand sells one identical taco at the same unit cost, and the market is very elastic, so the team that posts the lowest price each round walks off with most of the crowd. You will play five rounds, set a price each round, and watch what happens when everyone keeps undercutting. The whole thing runs on paper: each team writes a price on a slip, the slips are revealed at once, and the profits are worked out by hand on a shared class scoreboard. You will not need an AI account or a computer for this activity, because every material is on this page.

The game (the runnable kit)

The class splits into four to six competing teams of three or four students each. Every team runs the same campus taco stand, selling one identical taco. The numbers are the same for everyone:

  • Unit cost. Every taco costs the stand $2.00 to make. You earn no profit at or below that price.
  • The market each round. There are 120 customers in the market.
  • The demand rule (how customers split). The market is very elastic, so customers go to the cheapest stand. The team (or teams) posting the lowest price that round wins 60 customers, split evenly across the teams tied for that lowest price. The other 60 customers split evenly among the rest of the teams (the ones that did not post the lowest price). If every team picks the same price, all 120 split evenly across the room.
  • Profit each round. For each team, profit is (your price minus $2.00) times the customers you won. A price at or below $2.00 earns zero profit even when it wins the crowd.
  • Allowed prices. Any price at least $2.10, in whole-dime increments (so $2.10, $2.50, $3.20, and so on). You do not need to go above about $6.00 in practice.

How to play

Play five rounds. Each round:

  1. Each team privately writes its taco price on a slip of paper. Do not show the other teams.
  2. Reveal every team's slip at once at the front of the room.
  3. Apply the demand rule: identify the lowest price (or the tie for it), hand those team(s) 60 customers split evenly, split the other 60 evenly among the remaining teams, then compute each team's profit as (its price minus $2.00) times the customers it won.
  4. One team (or a volunteer scorekeeper for the room) writes every team's price, customers won, and profit for that round on the class scoreboard below, so the whole room sees the price war form in real time.

After the fifth round, every team adds up its profit across the five rounds and the room talks it over: did a price war start, who started it, did anyone hold a higher price and lose the crowd, and which strategy made the most total money over the five rounds. The lesson the game is built to show is the one the chapter names: in a very elastic market where everyone just matches the lowest price, a race to the bottom benefits no team, and the smarter move is to not match, to differentiate, or to think two rounds ahead.

The team debrief (answer as a team on the team results sheet)

In a few sentences, answer these as a team:

  2. Which team cut first, and which team (if any) held a higher price?

  4. Over the five rounds, did the lowest-price strategy or a hold-higher strategy make the most total profit on the scoreboard?
  5. What would have beaten the race to the bottom? (Hold the line, differentiate, think two rounds ahead, something else.)

What each member turns in (the individual artifact)

Each member writes their own one-paragraph debrief, which names their team's price in each of the five rounds, the price war (if one started) and who started it, and the one lesson they personally drew about competitor-based pricing and elasticity. Do not copy a teammate's paragraph; each member is graded on their own.

Submit

A team packet that contains:

  1. Your team's round-tracking sheet (the five rounds of your team's price, customers won, and profit).
  2. The class scoreboard for the room (every team's price, customers won, and profit per round, with totals). If two teams turn this in, that is fine; the grader only needs one copy.
  3. The team debrief (the five short answers above, in a few sentences).
  4. Each member's own one-paragraph debrief (separate paragraphs, one per member, not copied across the team).

If your institution allows AI for this activity, follow your institution's academic-integrity and AI-use policy on what you may use and how to disclose it. The game itself does not need any AI at all, and the debrief has to be your own reading of the room you played in.

On the job. Knowing when not to match a rival's price cut is a real call a growth or pricing team makes, and feeling a price war erase everyone's margin here is what keeps you from starting one later.

  • LO 10.4 Explain price elasticity and read its effect: in a very elastic market, a small price difference swings most of the volume to the cheapest seller.

The work is played in the room on paper and the debriefs are written by hand, so the reasoning the rubric rewards is the student's own reading of the specific game the room played: which round the cuts started, who held, what the scoreboard ended up looking like, and what would have beaten the race to the bottom. That is a debrief an AI cannot write for the student because it does not know what happened in the room.

    Grading Guide: The Restaurant Pricing Game

    Chapter 10 group activity. This guide is for whoever runs and grades the game: you by hand, a TA, or an AI you direct. It says how to run the game in the room, what the activity is really testing, what good and weak work look like row by row, the failure modes to watch for, and how to turn the rubric into a fair score fast. The students used no AI on the game itself; this is a paper game plus a hand-written debrief from each member. The companion ch10-restaurant-pricing-game-grading-skill.md turns this same rubric into something you can paste into a free Claude account if you want a first-pass score; this guide stands on its own without it.

    How to run it (you are the facilitator)

    The card ships as a runnable paper kit, so the mechanics are simple and the same for every team. Split the class into four to six competing teams of three or four students each. Every team runs the same campus taco stand selling one identical taco, and the numbers are fixed for everyone:

    • Unit cost: $2.00 per taco. Zero profit at or below that price.
    • Market each round: 120 customers.
    • The demand rule: the team (or teams) posting the lowest price wins 60 customers, split evenly across the teams tied for lowest. The other 60 split evenly among the remaining teams. If everyone ties, all 120 split evenly.
    • Profit each round: (a team's price minus $2.00) times the customers it won.
    • Allowed prices: at least $2.10, in whole-dime increments. In practice, the room rarely needs to go above about $6.00.

    Play five rounds. Each round, teams privately write a price on a slip, you reveal all slips at the front of the room, you (or a volunteer scorekeeper) apply the demand rule on the board, and every team's price, customers, and profit go on the class scoreboard (the shared sheet in the student card). The scoreboard is the artifact that lets the grader verify the demand rule round by round, and it is required in the submission. The whole game fits in about twenty to thirty minutes.

    The point to steer toward: in a very elastic market where everyone just matches the lowest price, the cuts win customers but destroy margin, so the race to the bottom benefits no team. The smarter move is to not match, to differentiate, or to think two rounds ahead.

    What this activity is really testing

    Not whether students can multiply price minus cost by quantity; the arithmetic is the easy part. This game tests whether a student can read competitor-based pricing and elasticity off a market they just played: explain why the price war happened on the scoreboard, name who would have won by holding or differentiating, and draw the right lesson. The individual debrief is the anti-slacker artifact: it keeps every member on the hook, so each member's paragraph is graded as their own.

    Keep the chapter's idea in front of you while grading: in a very elastic market, a small price difference swings most of the volume, which is exactly what tempts everyone to cut, and exactly why matching to the bottom is a trap. A debrief that narrates "we charged $3, then $2.50, then $2.20" without explaining the mechanism has missed the point. A debrief that says "the market was so elastic that whoever undercut grabbed the crowd, so we all kept cutting until the margin was gone, and the team that held at $3.50 lost volume but would have won the room if we had agreed not to chase price" has hit it.

    You own the grade. Pick the path that fits your section and your time.

    1. By hand. Read the team results sheet and each member's debrief against the four rubric rows, then check the class scoreboard once for the demand rule. Budget about five minutes per member plus a quick check of the team sheet and the scoreboard. The score bands below let you land a number quickly. Read the game-reading row first; it decides most of the grade.
    2. Hand it to TAs. Give your TAs this guide and the rubric. Calibrate first: grade one strong debrief and one weak one together so everyone anchors the bands the same way. The most common TA drift is rewarding a tidy round-by-round narration over a debrief that actually explains the price-war mechanism, so calibrate on that specifically. Remind TAs to grade each member's debrief separately and to flag near-duplicates.

    The answer key (the lesson every strong submission lands on)

    The scoreboard differs every time the room plays, so there is no single "right" set of numbers to match a submission against. The lesson, on the other hand, never changes, and that is what you grade against:

    • The mechanism. A very elastic market sends most of the volume to whoever posts the lowest price, so any team that matches a rival's cut is dragged toward the floor with everyone else, and a team that does not match loses volume but holds margin. When every team matches, the cuts keep coming until margin is gone. That is the price-war trap of competitor-based pricing.
    • What would have beaten it. Hold the price line (lose some customers, keep margin per unit, and total profit can still beat the cut-to-the-bottom team), differentiate (find a reason for buyers not to compare on price alone), or think two rounds ahead (refuse to start the cut, or signal a hold to the room).
    • The "we won by undercutting" trap to disarm. A team that won the most rounds at a rock-bottom price typically did not end with the most total profit, because the margin collapsed. Seeing that on the scoreboard is part of the lesson; a debrief that calls undercutting "the winning strategy" without checking total profit has missed it.

    The signal of a strong submission is a debrief that names the mechanism and the better move (and ideally points to the row on the scoreboard where the cuts started). The signal of a weak one is a play-by-play of the rounds with no explanation.

    What to look for, row by row

    The rows below match the card's rubric exactly. Grade the game-reading row first; it is the heaviest at 42 points.

    Reading the game: why the price war happened and what beats it (42 pts), the heaviest row, grade it first.

    • Excellent: the student explains the mechanism, an elastic market sends most of the volume to the lowest price so matching pulls everyone to the bottom and margin disappears. They name who would have won by holding or differentiating (often a team that held a higher price for a round or two), and they draw a correct lesson about competitor-based pricing.
    • Look for the mechanism and the better move. The single best signal is a debrief that explains why the price war happened and names what would have beaten it. Narrating the rounds is not the same as reading them.
    • Proficient: the mechanism is there but the better move is fuzzy, or the better move is named but the mechanism is described only in passing.
    • Developing: a play-by-play of the prices with no real explanation of why the cuts kept coming. That is the Developing band no matter how neat the recording.
    • Not yet: the rounds are barely described and no lesson is drawn.

    Round play and scoreboard recorded (22 pts).

    • Excellent: round-by-round prices, customers won, and profits recorded correctly for every team on the class scoreboard, and the arithmetic matches the demand rule (lowest team(s) take 60 evenly, the rest split 60 evenly, profit is margin times customers).
    • Proficient: one small gap or one slip in the arithmetic on the scoreboard.
    • Developing: a scoreboard with several gaps, or profits that do not match the demand rule (a team credited with customers it did not win, or profit computed on price rather than on price minus cost).
    • Not yet: the scoreboard is missing and the team turned in only its own per-team sheet, which leaves the demand rule unverifiable.

    Individual debrief (each member, 24 pts), the anti-slacker artifact.

    • Excellent: every member's one-paragraph debrief is their own, names their team's prices across the five rounds and the price-war dynamic on the room's scoreboard, and states a real lesson about competitor-based pricing and elasticity.
    • Proficient: most members submit a real, distinct debrief.
    • Developing: paragraphs are thin or read like one debrief with names swapped.
    • Not yet: debriefs are missing across the team.
    • How to grade it. Read the paragraphs side by side. Near-duplicate paragraphs cap this row low even if the team sheet and scoreboard are excellent. This is the row that protects the members who did the work.

    Writing and terms (12 pts).

    • Excellent: plain, clear writing that uses the terms correctly, elasticity, competitor-based pricing, price war.
    • Proficient: mostly clear, one or two loose phrasings.
    • Developing: disconnected sentences, or terms used loosely (calling any price change a "price war," or confusing elastic with inelastic).
    • Not yet: unclear.

    Common failure modes (and how to score them)

    • The play-by-play. The debrief lists every round's price and stops. The most common miss. The game-reading row lands in Developing; the scoreboard row can still earn full marks if the recording is correct. Net: usually a low-to-mid range total.
    • The near-duplicate debriefs. Three or four members turn in the same paragraph with a word changed. Grade each member's own paragraph and score the individual-debrief row low for the copies; this is the anti-slacker rule doing its job.
    • The arithmetic that does not match the demand rule. The scoreboard credits a team with customers it did not win, or computes profit on the full price instead of price minus cost, or splits the 60 unevenly when there was no tie. Score the scoreboard row on whether the numbers follow the demand rule, and note the slip in feedback.
    • The missing better move. The student explains the price war but never says what would have beaten it. That caps the game-reading row at Proficient, because naming the smarter play (hold, differentiate, think two rounds ahead) is half the lesson.
    • The "we won so cutting works" read. A team that won the most rounds concludes that undercutting is the right strategy, missing that the total profit was tiny because the margin collapsed. Note this in feedback: winning the crowd at no margin is the trap, not the win. Score the game-reading row on whether they saw that.
    • The missing scoreboard. Some teams turn in only their own per-team sheet and skip the class scoreboard. Cap the scoreboard row low (Developing at best), because the demand rule cannot be verified without it. Note in feedback that the scoreboard is the team-level artifact.

    Score bands (for a fast, fair landing)

    Use these to convert the four rows into a defensible total. Grade the team sheet and the scoreboard once, then each member's debrief separately.

    • 90 to 100 (Excellent). A debrief that explains the elastic-market mechanism and names what would have beaten the price war, a correctly recorded scoreboard with every team's rounds and totals, every member's own distinct debrief, and clean use of the terms.
    • 80 to 89 (Strong/Proficient). A real reading of the game and a recorded scoreboard, but one row is thin: a partial lesson, a small gap in the scoreboard, or one debrief weaker than the rest.
    • 70 to 79 (Developing). The game was played and recorded, but the debrief mostly narrates rather than explains, or the better move is missing.
    • 60 to 69 (Weak). Near-duplicate debriefs across the team, a scoreboard that does not match the demand rule, or no real reading of why the price war happened.
    • Below 60. A missing scoreboard or missing debriefs across the team. Handle any academic-integrity concerns through your institution's process, not the rubric.

    A note for graders

    The lesson to reward is the one the source and the chapter both name: a price war in a very elastic market benefits no team. A debrief that records the prices but never explains the price war has missed it; a debrief that names what would have beaten the price war, holding, differentiating, or thinking two rounds ahead, is the signal of a strong submission. When two members hand in the same paragraph, that is the anti-slacker rule catching a free rider; grade each member's own work and score the copies down on the individual-debrief row. The class scoreboard is what lets you verify the demand rule round by round, so make sure it came in with the submission; if it did not, cap the scoreboard row low and note it in the feedback.

Skim or Penetrate: Judge an AI Launch Call

Student instructions

Below is a memo an AI produced for a campus student union launching a co-branded signature cold brew with a local roaster. The first run is a launch kit: one bottle, a co-branded insulated tumbler, packaging, and the roaster's per-kit licensing fee, all in at a $40 unit cost. The union asked the AI which launch posture to use, what comparable kits sell for, and what price earns a 40% margin. It reads the way a confident analyst writes, and in two places it is wrong in ways that would cost real money at launch. Your job is to work the chapter's launch logic on it, catch what it got wrong, and argue the call out as a group. You will not need an AI account of your own, because the memo is on this page.

The launch memo to judge

Should you skim or penetrate?

I recommend a skimming posture: open at a higher price and bring it down later. A signature drink kit is a premium, identity-driven product, and the students who most want to show campus pride will pay to be first with the new tumbler. Launching high captures that willingness to pay before the novelty fades, and you can step the price down for the spring re-order once the early adopters are served. A skimming launch also signals quality, which protects the co-brand's premium image.

What competitors charge for a comparable kit: Co-branded drink kits in the campus and lifestyle space cluster between roughly $45 and $75. Stanley sells a co-branded drink-and-tumbler bundle at $59, and a comparable university-bookstore cold-brew launch kit runs about $52. Your product sits in the middle of that band, which gives you room to open at a premium without looking out of line.

Your launch price at a 40% margin: You asked for a 40% margin on your $40 unit cost. That gives you a launch price of $56.00 ($40 cost plus 40% = $40 × 1.40 = $56). This sits right in the competitor band, so you can open at a premium and still earn a healthy 40% margin on every kit.

Margin check: Margin is 40%, you are earning a 40% margin on every kit at $56. Markup is also 40%, since you added 40% to your cost.

Is your opening price fair? Yes. At $56 you are priced in line with the $52 to $59 comparable kits, and a 40% margin on a real, disclosed cost is a transparent and defensible way to price a launch. You are not exploiting scarcity; you are charging a normal premium for a premium product. This is a fair opening price.

Start with the launch fork. Is this a high-q/p product, where every party photo and group chat and tumbler carried across the quad is an imitation channel pulling buyers in fast, or a low-q/p one, where adoption is slow and word of mouth is weak? A high q/p favors penetration, pricing low to ride the imitation wave; a low q/p favors skimming, pricing high while the early buyers who must have it pay up. Decide whether the posture the memo picked actually matches the q/p read for this product, or whether it asserted a posture without doing that read at all.

Then check the memo's numbers and claims, because it has two real errors. Redo the margin arithmetic first: the union asked for a 40% margin on a $40 cost, so work out what a true 40% margin price should be and what margin the $56 the memo landed on actually earns, and you will find it has reported a markup as though it were a margin. Next, take each competitor price the memo states as fact and decide whether it was ever verified, marking each one confirmed or unconfirmed. Finally, give a one-line dual-entitlement read on whether the opening price reads as fair, remembering that a cost story reads as fair while a demand windfall reads as gouging. The group meets, argues the posture out, and turns in one decision sheet.

Each member turns in individually: your own one-paragraph verdict stating which launch posture you would pick (skim, penetrate, or neutral), the one piece of evidence that moved you, and one specific thing the memo got wrong. Do not copy another member's paragraph; the group turns in one shared decision sheet on top of the separate verdicts.

Submit: one group decision sheet (the posture the group chose, the q/p reasoning, the two errors caught and corrected, and a one-line fairness read on the opening price) plus each member's own one-paragraph verdict.

On the job. Choosing whether to launch high or low is a real decision a product marketing team argues out before a new release, and the skill you build here is reading an AI's confident launch memo well enough to catch the error in it and still hold your own posture in the room.

The Price-Fairness Debate on a Real Case

A Chapter 10 group activity that uses no AI at all. The group is handed the same case file on a real pricing move, a theme park that switched to weekend-peak ticket pricing, splits into a fair side and an unfair side, and argues the move out loud using the chapter's fairness tools. Then each member writes their own position brief. The fairness ceiling is the binding constraint on any 2026 pricing move, and arguing it well on messy real facts, including the side you may not personally believe, is the judgment the chapter trains. Done in a group of four to six, split into two sides, with an individual brief from each member.

Student instructions

The case below describes a real kind of pricing move, a family theme park that replaced one flat ticket price with a calendar that charges more on peak weekends and less on slow weekdays. Half the people who hear this call it a fair way to manage crowds, and half call it a surcharge on the families who can only come on Saturday. Both reads are arguable, and the chapter gives you the tools to argue either one well. Your job is to take the side you are assigned, build the strongest case for it from the dual-entitlement principle and the dynamic-pricing fairness checklist, debate it live against the other side, and then write your own position brief. You hold the fairness logic in your head and apply it to these facts; there is no tool to paste anything into.

The case to debate

Meadowbrook Gardens moves to calendar pricing

Meadowbrook Gardens is a mid-size regional theme park whose advertising has promised the same thing for years: "a day out the whole family can afford, any weekend." For a decade it charged a single adult gate price of $52, the same number every day of the year, printed on the billboards off the interstate.

Two problems pushed the park to change. Saturdays in summer were so crowded that families waited ninety minutes for the headline ride and left unhappy, while Tuesdays and Wednesdays ran near-empty and lost money on staffing a full park for thin crowds. The park's costs did not rise; what changed was that the single price was over-pricing the empty weekdays and under-pricing the packed weekends.

Starting this season the park replaced the flat $52 with a published calendar grid. A slow weekday now costs $38, well below the old single price. A peak summer Saturday now costs $74. Every other day falls somewhere between, and the whole calendar is posted on the website and the app a full season in advance, so a family can see every day's price before they choose when to come. The park kept the $52-equivalent days on the shoulder dates of spring and fall.

The park's public explanation, in a press release and on the app: "Calendar pricing lets us spread out the crowds so everyone has a better day. Come on a weekday and pay less than you used to. Come on a peak Saturday and you will pay more, but you will also find shorter lines because the price helps level the crowd."

The reaction split. Some season-pass parents thanked the park for the cheaper weekdays, which finally let them visit on a day off without fighting the crowd. Others were angry: a working family whose only free day is Saturday now pays $74 for the exact same park that cost $52 last year, and a local columnist wrote that "a park that built its whole brand on 'any weekend the family can afford' just put a surcharge on the one weekend most families actually have."

This is the case both sides argue. The park did two things at once, and that is what makes it a real debate: it cut the off-peak price genuinely below the old flat price, and it raised the peak price above it, on a brand that had promised affordability on any weekend.

How the debate runs

With four to six members, split into two sides. Roughly half argue that the move was fair, and half argue that it was unfair. Take the side you are assigned rather than the side you happen to hold, because arguing the harder side well is the skill the chapter trains. Each side prepares its case from the tools below, then the two sides debate it live, and afterward the group writes one joint summary and each member writes their own brief.

Build your side's argument from the chapter's fairness tools, using the ones that fit the facts:

  • The dual-entitlement principle, the core test. Buyers grant a firm the right to defend its profit when costs rise, but not the right to capture a windfall from a demand spike. Was the higher Saturday price defending a real cost, which reads as fair, or capturing a demand windfall, which reads as gouging? The case tells you the park's costs did not rise, which is a fact each side has to reckon with.
  • The better-than-static test. A dynamic price reads as fairer when some buyers get a genuinely better deal than the old single price gave them, not just a peak surcharge stapled on. Did anyone actually come out ahead here, and does a real off-peak discount change the fairness read on the peak increase?
  • Category tolerance. Some categories carry a tight reference price that buyers treat as a promise; others let prices move without complaint. Where does a family theme park with a long-posted flat price sit, and how does that shape what the peak Saturday price feels like?
  • Brand-promise compatibility and pre-launch communication. Can a brand built on "a day out the whole family can afford, any weekend" absorb a weekend surcharge without breaking its own promise, and does posting the full calendar a season in advance do enough to make the change land as fair?

Debate it live: each side makes its case, then answers the other side's strongest point. After the debate, the group writes one joint summary, and each member writes their own brief.

Each member turns in individually. Write your own one-page position brief arguing your assigned side, fair or unfair. Run the dual-entitlement test on these facts, bring in at least one other tool from the list, and name honestly the single strongest point against your own side along with how you would answer it. Do not share a brief with another member. A strong brief argues its side from the principle and faces the best counter squarely. A weak one asserts "it is unfair because it costs more" or "it is fair because weekdays got cheaper" with no principle underneath. You are graded on the quality of the argument and not on which side wins, so argue your assigned side as well as you can even when it is the harder one to defend.

Submit: one joint debate summary, holding the strongest argument each side made, the principles each side leaned on, and where the room landed, plus each member's own one-page position brief arguing their assigned side with the dual-entitlement reasoning and the strongest counter to that side named and answered.

On the job. When a price change goes public and customers push back, a brand or PR team has to judge fast whether the move reads as fair and decide whether to hold it or walk it back, which is the exact call this debate rehearses.

    Grading Guide: The Price-Fairness Debate on a Real Case

    Chapter 10 group activity, no AI account needed. This guide is for whoever runs and grades the debate: you by hand, a TA, or an AI you direct. Because the case is embedded in the assignment and the same for every group, this guide doubles as the answer key: it tells you the strongest fair-side and unfair-side arguments the case supports, how the six fairness principles read on these facts, what good and weak work look like row by row, the failure modes to watch for, and how to land a fair score fast.     What this activity is really testing

    This is the no-AI judgment card. No group generates anything and there is no planted error to catch, because the skill here is not catching a model out; it is arguing the fairness ceiling from the principle, on a real case, against a live opponent. Every group debates the same embedded case: Meadowbrook Gardens, a family theme park that replaced a long-standing flat $52 gate price with a published calendar that charges $38 on slow weekdays and $74 on peak summer Saturdays, on a brand whose advertised promise is "a day out the whole family can afford, any weekend." The case is built to be genuinely two-sided, so neither verdict is the "right answer."

    Keep the chapter's idea in front of you while grading. The dual-entitlement principle says buyers grant a firm the right to defend its profit when costs rise, so a cost-driven increase reads as fair, but not the right to capture a windfall from a demand spike, so a demand-driven increase reads as gouging. The graded skill is whether a student can run that test on these actual facts, bring in the better-than-static test and category tolerance and the brand-promise read, and name the strongest counter to their own side honestly. The individual brief is the anti-slacker rule: each member's brief is graded as their own, so one member cannot carry four names.

    The grade is for the quality of the argument, not for which side the student argued or which side won the room. A student assigned the harder side who argues it well earns full marks.

    The answer key: the strongest case each side can make (the case is the same for every group)

    Because the case is fixed, you can check any brief against the reads below in seconds. There is no single correct verdict; a strong brief is one that argues its assigned side from the principle and faces the best counter. What follows is the strongest honest version of each side and the facts each has to reckon with.

    The fair side, at its strongest. The move reads as fair under the better-than-static test, which is the chapter's strongest fair-side tool here. A real off-peak discount existed: the weekday price fell to $38, genuinely below the old flat $52, so a price-sensitive family that can shift its visit to a weekday now pays less than it ever did, exactly the structure that separated the Indianapolis Zoo's fair calendar rollout from a pure surge. The whole calendar was published a season in advance on the website and the app, so there is no surprise at the gate and the transparency principle is satisfied. The cooperative-framing story is real rather than cosmetic, because the higher Saturday price does level the crowd, and a family that pays the peak price also gets the shorter line the case describes, which is a genuine benefit and not just a charge. The fair side should concede openly that the park's costs did not rise, then argue that a cost increase is not the only fair basis for a price difference: temporal and capacity-driven pricing, charging more for the scarce peak slot and less for the empty one, is a recognized fair basis under the explicability principle, the same logic a toll lane or an off-peak transit fare runs on.

    The unfair side, at its strongest. The move reads as a demand windfall under the core dual-entitlement test, which is the unfair side's strongest tool. The case states plainly that the park's costs did not rise, so the higher Saturday price is not defending a cost; it is capturing more from buyers at the moment demand peaks, which is the textbook demand-windfall pattern that reads as gouging. Category tolerance cuts hard here: a family theme park that posted one flat price for a decade has a tight reference price that buyers treat as a promise, so a jump from $52 to $74 on the busiest day is screenshot-visible and reads as a betrayal rather than a normal market move. The brand-promise principle is the sharpest point: a park advertised as affordable "any weekend" cannot raise the price on the one weekend most working families can actually come without breaking its own promise, which is the chapter's named Legoland-style failure where a family-accessibility promise cannot absorb a weekend surcharge. The unfair side should concede the genuine weekday discount, then argue it does not cure the harm, because the families most squeezed are precisely the ones with no weekday to shift to.

    The honest counter each side has to name (this is half the skill). A strong fair-side brief names the brand-promise and category-tolerance objection as its strongest counter and answers it, for instance that the promise is preserved on the cheaper and shoulder days and the peak price buys a better experience. A strong unfair-side brief names the genuine off-peak discount and the season-ahead transparency as its strongest counter and answers it, for instance that a real discount for the flexible does not make the surcharge on the inflexible fair. A brief that names only the evidence for its own side and ducks the counter has done half the work, and the rubric caps it for that.

    How the six fairness principles read on this case (for reference, calls can differ with good evidence). Use these to judge whether a brief's application of a principle holds, not as a required answer set.

    • Explicability: arguable, and this is where the sides genuinely diverge. A peak price maps to a capacity-and-time-of-use story, which buyers accept, but it also maps to a demand-capture story, which they reject; a strong brief argues which frame the facts support rather than asserting one.
    • Transparency at the moment of encounter: pass. The full calendar is posted a season ahead on the website and the app, so the buyer can see every day's price before choosing when to come.
    • Cooperative framing: arguable. The park's "spread out the crowds so everyone has a better day" framing reads as a shared interest, and the shorter peak-day line is a real benefit; the unfair side can still argue the framing dresses up a margin move.
    • Category tolerance: fail, and this is the unfair side's structural anchor. A family theme park with a decade-long flat price carries a tight reference price bought infrequently enough to feel like a promise, so peak variation stings.
    • Brand-promise compatibility: fail (or weak), the sharpest unfair-side point. "Affordable any weekend" sits close to a stability commitment that a weekend surcharge contradicts. The fair side's best answer is that affordability survives on the discounted and shoulder days.
    • Pre-launch communication: pass. The calendar and the explanation were published in advance, which is exactly the step Wendy's skipped, so this episode does the thing the chapter's failure case did not.

    A move that passes some principles and fails others is the normal shape of a real fairness question, which is why the verdict is arguable and the grade is for the argument.

    How to run the debate (you are the facilitator)

    The students all have the same embedded case, so you do not hand out anything. Then:

    1. Split the sides. Roughly half the group argues the move was fair, half that it was unfair. Assign the sides rather than letting members pick, so students argue the case rather than their preference, and sometimes the harder side.
    2. Prep time, about ten to fifteen minutes. Each side builds its argument from the chapter's tools: the dual-entitlement principle, the better-than-static test, category tolerance, and the brand-promise and pre-launch-communication principles.
    3. The live round. Each side makes its case, then answers the other side's strongest point. Keep it to a few minutes a side plus rebuttals.
    4. The briefs. After the debate, the group writes one joint summary, the strongest argument each side made and where the room landed, and each member writes their own one-page position brief arguing their assigned side.

    The whole thing fits in about an hour. The point you are steering toward: the fairness ceiling is the binding constraint on any 2026 pricing move, and the test is whether a student can argue it from the principle on real facts, not whether the class reaches a "correct" verdict.

The answer key above is all you need for the heaviest row: check whether the brief runs the dual-entitlement test on the fact that costs did not rise, brings in at least one more tool, and names and answers the strongest counter. Read that row first; it decides most of the grade.

    What to look for, row by row

    The fairness argument, built on the principle (44 pts), the heaviest row, grade it first.

    • Strong: the brief argues its assigned side from the dual-entitlement principle, runs the cost-defense-versus-demand-windfall test on the actual facts (reckoning with the stated fact that the park's costs did not rise), brings in the better-than-static test or category tolerance or the brand-promise read, and names the single strongest counter to its own side with an answer to it. For the unfair side, that looks like "costs did not rise, so the Saturday increase captures a demand windfall in a tight-reference-price category, and the genuine weekday discount does not cure the harm to families with no weekday to shift to." For the fair side, it looks like "the off-peak price genuinely fell below the old flat price and the calendar was published in advance, so this is the better-than-static structure that reads as fair, and the affordability promise survives on the cheaper and shoulder days."
    • Look for the principle plus the answered counter. The single best signal of a strong brief is the dual-entitlement test applied to the real facts with the strongest opposing point named and met, not hidden.
    • Weak: "it is unfair because it costs more," or "it is fair because weekdays got cheaper," with no dual-entitlement reasoning, or a verdict asserted by gut feel. That is the Developing or Not-yet band no matter how confident the prose.

    Use of the case facts and the fairness checklist (22 pts).

    • Strong: the brief marshals the specific facts of this case, the decade of a single flat price, the genuine cut to $38, the rise to $74, the "any weekend" promise, the season-ahead published calendar, and applies at least two of the six dynamic-pricing principles correctly for its side.
    • Weak: the case is mentioned but the principles are not applied, or the brief argues fairness in the abstract without the facts of this episode. Mentioning without applying caps this row at Developing.

    Individual position brief (each member, 22 pts).

    • Strong: every member's brief is their own, takes a clear side, and names one piece of evidence that supports their side and one that cuts against it.
    • Weak: near-duplicate briefs across the group, or a brief that names only supporting evidence and ducks the counter. Grade each member's own; do not give the group one shared score on this row.

    Joint summary and writing (12 pts).

    • Strong: the joint summary fairly captures both sides, the strongest argument each made, and where the room landed, in plain language with correct terms.
    • Weak: a one-sided summary that only reports the winning side, or a thin summary that does not capture the actual arguments.

    Common failure modes (and how to score them)

    • The gut-feel verdict. "It costs more so it is unfair," or "weekdays got cheaper so it is fair," with no dual-entitlement reasoning. The most common miss. The fairness-argument row lands in Developing or Not-yet; note in feedback that the chapter's test is cost defense versus demand windfall, not whether the price went up or down.
    • The abstract brief. The student argues fairness in general and never engages the specific facts, the flat-price history, the genuine off-peak cut, the affordability promise. Score the case-facts row down: the brief has to argue this episode.
    • The one-sided brief. The student names only the evidence for their side and never the counter. Cap the individual-brief row, because naming and answering the strongest opposing point is half the skill this assignment asks for.
    • The brief that ignores the no-cost-rise fact. The single most decisive fact in the case is that the park's costs did not rise. A fair-side brief that never reckons with it has dodged the core test, and an unfair-side brief that never names it has missed its strongest point. Read for it on the heaviest row.
    • The near-duplicate briefs. Several members turn in the same brief with a word changed. Grade each member's own and score the copies down on the individual-brief row; this is the anti-slacker rule doing its job.
    • The "wrong side" penalty that should not happen. A member argued the harder side, say defending the surcharge, and argued it well. Do not mark this down for reaching an unpopular conclusion. Arguing the assigned side well, even the harder side, is the skill, not landing on a "correct" verdict.
    • The one-sided joint summary. The summary reports only the side that won the room. Score the joint-summary row down; the summary's job is to capture both sides fairly.

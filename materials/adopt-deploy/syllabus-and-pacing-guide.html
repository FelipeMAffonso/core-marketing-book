Course description

Marketing is the coordinated set of decisions a firm makes about what to make, who to serve, what to charge, where to make it available, and how to communicate it. This course teaches those decisions the way a working marketer faces them, and it teaches them in the world we actually market in today, where buyers research with AI assistants, where a brand has to be legible to a model and not just to a person, and where a single marketer can now build and automate work that used to take a team.

The course is built for second-year students. It assumes no prior marketing background and no computing background. Each idea is taught from everyday intuition, with one concrete brand example, and then put to work. The writing is plain and direct. The goal is not to memorize definitions but to be able to make a marketing decision and defend it.

The textbook reads two ways. Every chapter ships at two reader-selectable depths in one place: Comprehensive, the full chapter for students who want the complete treatment, and Concise, a shorter self-contained edition of the same chapter. Students choose the depth that fits their week. You can assign one depth as the baseline and leave the other as an option, or let students choose.

The AI-integrated approach

AI is not a single guest lecture in this course. It is a thread that runs through all 27 chapters as a ladder of five rungs, and the course climbs one rung at a time:

  1. Ask well. Prompting that gets a useful answer. The difference a specific prompt makes.
  2. Use real capabilities. Vision to audit an ad, source-grounded research, image and video generation,

transcription.

  1. Connect real tools. Point an AI at your own stack in plain language: a CRM, a spreadsheet, an

analytics property, a design tool.

  1. Build and automate. Reusable skills, no-code agents, scheduled routines. Work a non-developer can

stand up.

  1. Publish and consume. Distribute a capability in a marketplace, not just use one privately.

Chapter 1 introduces the whole frame and the evidence behind it: marketing is an early-adopter profession, AI fluency is among the most-requested workplace skills, and the tools are reachable by people who do not write code. Each later chapter then advances one rung, woven into that chapter's running brand example, so the AI thread compounds instead of repeating. Every chapter carries one hands-on AI exercise tied to its own framework, and the course ends with students building a working marketing agent.

The point is fluency, not novelty. A student who finishes this course can frame a marketing problem, choose the right tool for it, do real work with that tool, and say clearly where the human judgment still has to live.

Learning outcomes

By the end of the course, a student will be able to:

  1. Define marketing as a set of coordinated decisions and explain why those decisions multiply rather

than add, using the four Ps, the seven Ts, and the value framework on a real brand.

  1. Read the marketing environment with the 5C, PESTEL, Porter's Five Forces, and a scored SWOT, and

identify the binding force on a category this quarter.

  1. Build a segmentation, targeting, and positioning argument, including a usability filter on

segments, a points-of-parity and points-of-difference positioning statement, and a perceptual map.

  1. Manage a brand and a product as a system: brand as memory structure, distinctive assets, the

product life cycle, packaging and portfolio decisions, and the economics of brand equity.

  1. Set price and design distribution using value-based pricing, breakeven and margin arithmetic,

channel structure, physical availability, retail format logic, and omni-channel design.

  1. Plan and run marketing communications across paid, owned, and earned media, including search and

generative-engine visibility, content, social, the creator economy, and lifecycle retention.

  1. Close the marketing loop with measurement and finance, tying marketing activity to customer asset

value, attribution, the P&L, and a budget you can defend in a finance review.

  1. Conduct marketing research and reason about evidence, choosing the right method for the question,

sizing a study, and recognizing the common ways research misleads.

  1. Apply AI across the marketing workflow by climbing the five-rung ladder, from precise prompting

through building and publishing a working marketing agent, and stating where human judgment must remain.

  1. Act on the ethical and regulatory floor of modern marketing, including privacy regimes, dark

patterns, algorithmic fairness, disclosure of AI-generated content, and human-fallback design.

The three team projects (P1 to P3)

Teams pick one real brand in the first weeks and carry it through the whole course. Each project is a milestone in a single arc, so the work compounds the way a real engagement would.

  • Project 1: Research and the brand audit. The team chooses its brand, reads the environment around

it, and audits the brand and its customers. Output: a brand-and-environment audit grounded in real evidence, using the 5C and PESTEL frames, a Five-Forces read, and a scored SWOT, plus a first read of who the brand's customers are.

  • Project 2: Strategy, STP, and the mix. The team segments the market, chooses a target,

writes a positioning statement, and makes the core mix decisions for the brand: product and brand, price, and channel. Output: a strategy deck the team could present to the brand's marketing lead.

  • Project 3: The marketing plan and the build. The team turns the strategy into a full marketing plan

with a communications program, a measurement and budget section that ties to customer asset value, and a working AI capability the team built for the brand (the capstone build from Chapter 27). Output: a marketing plan and a demonstrated agent or automation, presented in the final weeks.

The AI-integration assessment angle

Because AI is woven through the course, it is assessed through the course rather than in a separate AI unit. Three concrete angles:

  1. Climb the ladder, show your work. Each chapter's hands-on AI exercise asks the student to do real

work with the rung that chapter teaches (a precise prompt, a vision audit, a connected tool, a built skill) and to show the input and the output. The assignment is graded on the marketing judgment, not on whether the tool ran.

  1. Disclose and critique, do not hide. Students are expected to use AI on the projects. They are also

expected to disclose what they used and to critique what it produced: what the model got right, what it missed, and what they changed. The disclosure and the critique are part of the grade. An AI draft taken at face value scores below a draft the student interrogated.

  1. Build something real at the end. Project 3 includes a working capability the team built, the

top rung of the ladder. The course's final demonstration of learning is not an essay about AI; it is a marketing agent the team can run.

See the AI-use policy below for what is expected, what must be disclosed, and what is off-limits.

AI use and academic-integrity policy

This is an AI-integrated course. Using AI well is a learning outcome, so the policy is not a blanket ban and it is not a free-for-all. The standard is simple: AI is a tool you are learning to use with judgment, and the work you submit must reflect your own thinking and be honestly disclosed.

What is expected and encouraged

  • Use AI on the hands-on exercises and the projects. That is the point of the course. Climb the ladder

the chapters teach: prompt well, use real capabilities, connect tools, build and automate, publish.

  • Use AI to learn: to explain a concept a second way, to quiz yourself, to get unstuck, to draft and

then improve. Treat it like a fast, tireless study partner that is sometimes confidently wrong.

  • Interrogate what it gives you. The course rewards students who check the output against the chapter,

catch what the model missed, and revise. Taking an AI answer at face value is the mistake the course is designed to train out of you.

What you must disclose

  • On every assignment and project, include a short AI-use note: which tools you used, for what, and

what you changed. One honest paragraph is enough. Many chapters' exercises ask you to paste the prompt and the output, which doubles as your disclosure.

  • Cite AI as a source when it shaped your content, the way you would cite any other source.
  • Disclosure is graded as part of the work. A clear, honest account of how you used AI and what you

did with it scores well. A hidden use that is discovered is an integrity violation.

What is off-limits

  • Submitting AI output as if it were your own unedited work, with no thought added and no disclosure.
  • Using AI on assessments where it is explicitly disallowed, such as a closed-book exam or an in-class

quiz. Each assessment states whether AI is allowed; when in doubt, ask before you use it.

  • Pasting confidential, personal, or proprietary data into a public AI tool. This includes real

customer personal data and anything a project partner shared in confidence. Use only public information or properly handled data, and never paste personally identifiable information into a consumer chatbot.

  • Fabrication. AI can invent facts, quotes, statistics, and citations. You are responsible for every

claim you submit. If you cannot verify it, do not submit it.

  • Passing off another person's or team's work as your own, with or without AI. The usual rules of

academic honesty apply in full.

Why the policy looks like this

Marketing teams already use AI every day, and the workplace expects new hires to use it well and responsibly. A course that banned it would train students for a job that no longer exists, and a course with no standards would teach them to outsource their judgment. This policy splits the difference the way the profession does: use the tools, own the output, disclose the use, and keep the human judgment where it belongs.

Week-by-week pacing guide

This guide maps all 27 chapters across a standard 15-week semester. The logic: Chapter 1 is the front door and shares week 1 with Chapter 2 so the AI frame and the definition of marketing land together; from there the course moves at roughly two chapters per week, following the book's order; the final weeks shift weight onto the capstone build and the project presentations. Each week shows the chapters and their topics, the AI ladder rung that advances that week, an in-class activity hook, and which project milestone is in motion.

Reading depth. A reasonable default is to assign the Concise depth as the baseline read and leave Comprehensive as the deeper option, or assign Comprehensive for the chapters most central to the projects (research, STP, brands, pricing, the plan and finance chapters) and Concise elsewhere. Compressing to a quad or 8-week term, or expanding

The 15-week map is the reference. To fit other calendars:

  • 8-week / quad term. Double the pace to roughly three to four chapters per week and collapse the

project arc into two milestones instead of three (merge P1 into the front of P2, keep P3 as the plan and build). Keep the single-chapter weeks for research (Ch5) and STP (Ch6) if at all possible, since the projects lean on them; let the channel chapters (Ch13 to Ch23) move fastest, since they share a structure. Assign the Concise depth as the baseline read throughout to keep the load realistic.

  • 10-week term. Run about three chapters per week and keep all three project milestones; drop the two

single-chapter weeks back into pairs.

  • Expanding past 15 weeks (a 16-week term with a finals week, or a slower track). Give Ch5 Research,

Ch6 STP, Ch10 Pricing, Ch24 Plan and Analytics, and Ch27 Capstone their own full weeks, and add a dedicated work-in-class session before each project deadline. This is the most comfortable pace for a class new to both marketing and AI.

Whatever the length, hold three things fixed: Chapter 1 opens the course and sets the AI frame; the research and STP chapters land before the project work that depends on them; and the capstone build and the presentations anchor the end.

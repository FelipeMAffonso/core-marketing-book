The line working comms directors draw between buying attention and earning credibility was drawn almost exactly a hundred years ago, and the two functions still answer different questions a century later.     PR, Marketing, And Comms: Who Controls The Message

    Every brand pays for three communication functions and confuses the lines between them about weekly. Marketing buys attention and convinces buyers to act, with its measurement tied to revenue and the buyer as the unit. Corporate communications manages the message across every internal and external audience, with its measurement tied to coherence.     02 // the modern pitch

    The Modern Pitch

    A pitch is a written message to a journalist proposing a story. It is not a press release, which is the formal company-issued document with a dateline and a quote. The pitch is the email that asks a journalist to consider covering something, and the release is what the journalist receives once the pitch is accepted and the briefing happens. Most stories in the trade and consumer press start as a pitch from a PR person to a specific reporter, not as a release blasted to a list. A 2026 pitch goes to a named human, not a publication’s general inbox, because the beat reporter who covers the category, not the twelve other reporters at the same publication, is the one who will see why the story fits.

The newsroom era of “PR sends to a press list, the press writes the story” gave way around 2018 to a beat-reporter era, where individual journalists with personal newsletters and social followings carry more brand-relevant audience than the masthead they nominally work for. Beat reporters like Patrick Coffee at the Wall Street Journal (advertising and marketing) and Maja Pawinska Sims at PRovoke Media (the PR industry) drive coverage at the individual level.

    The structure of a 2026 pitch email is fixed enough to work from a checklist rather than improvising each one, and it has four parts that each answer a question the reporter has in the first seconds of reading. The subject line sits under sixty characters and names the news in plain English, because most reporters first see the email on a phone where the subject cuts off near sixty characters, and a subject that truncates before the news arrives gets discarded. The first paragraph runs under fifty words and answers what the news is and why this reporter rather than the others should cover it, which is the part that separates a personalized pitch from a list blast. The proof-point paragraph runs under seventy words and gives the journalist something to verify or quote, such as a number tied to a source or a customer they can call. The next-step paragraph runs under sixty words and offers a specific path forward the brand can deliver inside the reporter’s normal cadence, often with a short line on how long the offer stays open.

    Embargo Embargo is the agreement that the journalist will not publish before a stated date and time. The PR person briefs multiple journalists in parallel under embargo so coverage arrives as a wave at, say, 6 a.m. Eastern on a Tuesday, instead of trickling out over a week. The cost is that any one reporter can break the embargo (most do not, because they want the next briefing), and the benefit is that the news appears big enough to be visible against the day’s other stories.

    The second routing decision is the choice between an exclusive and a broad release.     The most common pitch failure is the misnamed reporter, and the mechanism is mechanical. The big PR software vendors all let a person merge one pitch to every name labeled as covering a category with a click, so the pitch arrives, the reporter sees that the beat is technically right but the last six stories were on supply-chain economics while the brand was pitching a flavor launch, and it is discarded unread. The fix adds about thirty seconds per pitch: before sending, the PR person opens the reporter’s three most recent pieces, pulls one specific detail from each, and weaves it into the first paragraph as the reason this reporter specifically should cover the story. On a list of fifty reporters that thirty seconds is the difference between a 5 percent read rate and a 40 percent one, which is the difference between two reporters reading the pitch and twenty doing so.

    03 // building the media list

    Building the media list

    A media list is the spreadsheet of journalists a brand keeps relationships with, and it runs from about fifteen rows for a niche brand to three hundred for one with a wide category footprint. Each row holds the reporter's name, beat, publication, email, the last story you read, the date and substance of your last contact, and a notes field for personal context such as a vacation week or a kid's name. A PR person curates the list rather than buying it wholesale, because the value of a contact scales with how recently the brand was useful to that reporter, not with whether the email still resolves. The list is a record of relationships, and a bought spreadsheet of valid addresses is not the same asset.

    04 // measuring earned media

    Measuring earned media

    The measurement problem in PR is older than the field, and it has the shape Cutlip first named in the 1950s. Marketing has revenue as its terminal metric, so an executive can defend a campaign by pointing at sales lift. PR has coverage that may or may not have moved revenue, with no clean line between a Wall Street Journal feature this week and a sales number three months out. Because of that gap, the field spent decades building proxy metrics for what the coverage actually did, and the 2026 stack settles on three as load-bearing. Each answers a different question about the same coverage cycle, and the trick is to read them together rather than one at a time.

    Share of voice (SOV) The percentage of category mentions in earned media that name your brand. If a thousand articles in the consumer-cup category last quarter mentioned a brand name and 230 of them mentioned Stanley, Stanley's SOV is 23 percent. SOV is the closest PR comes to a market-share equivalent: it asks how big the brand looms in the category conversation. Tracked weekly, the trend line either matches or diverges from market share, and divergence is itself a finding.

    Computing share of voice is the same arithmetic as a class-participation percentage, and it runs on a free tool or a paid platform depending on the budget. The formula is one line: SOV equals brand mentions divided by brand mentions plus all named-competitor mentions in the same period, times 100. The same arithmetic gives a campus coffee cart its SOV against the other carts on the union plaza: count the Instagram tags for each over the same seven days, divide your tags by the total, and the answer is the share of the conversation your cart is winning. For the mention counts themselves, a small brand or a student team starts at free Google Alerts and runs the formula in a sheet, while a paid Talkwalker or Brandwatch subscription does the same counting automatically the next morning, which is what the annual fee buys.

    Sentiment monitoring runs on the same free-versus-paid split. The free path reads each alert email, labels it positive, neutral, or negative in a sheet, and totals the columns: a 70-positive, 25-neutral, 5-negative week is a plus-65 net-sentiment week, while a 20-positive, 30-neutral, 50-negative week is a minus-30 week and a signal to act. The paid path enters the brand name and the messaging pillars into the platform, which then scores each mention against those pillars with a language model. The trade is hand-labeling time against dollar cost, and the right choice depends on which the brand has more of.

    Sentiment scoring (the 2026 method) Sentiment is a 0-100 score (or a +/- bipolar score) attached to each mention reflecting how positively the brand comes off. The 2024-era lexicon-based scoring (count positive and negative words, take the difference) regularly flagged sarcasm wrong and missed category-specific tone. The 2026 method uses LLM scoring against a brand-supplied rubric: each mention goes to a model with the rubric and prior examples, and the model emits a score plus a one-sentence justification. Costs roughly 0.002 dollars per mention with prompt caching, against the older method's 0 cost but lower accuracy.

    05 // the crisis playbook

    The Crisis Playbook

    A crisis is a moment when the conversation about a brand moves faster than the brand can normally talk back. A product recall closed in seventy-two hours, with calls to retailers and a press release, is not a crisis. The brand is working at its normal speed, and seventy-two hours is fast enough to settle the matter before the conversation builds. The same recall becomes a crisis when the failure video has reached ten million impressions in the first twelve hours. The brand is doing the same work at the same speed, but the audience has already decided what the recall means. Internet conversation moves at one velocity and corporate communication moves at another, and the gap between them is what turns an incident into a crisis.

      Job Aid. The first-hour crisis card and the pitch skeleton. These are the chapter’s two operating sequences pulled out of the spine so you can run them cold, on your own brand or a competitor’s. Work top to bottom. Each block is a move the chapter taught.

      1. In the first hour, acknowledge before you explain.

      You do not need to know what happened yet. Within sixty minutes, say that you are aware something is happening, that the team is investigating, who is leading the response, and when the next update will come. The audience in hour one is not waiting for facts. It is waiting for proof that the company is awake, so silence is the one answer that always loses.

      2. Write the holding statement in five sentences.

      Acknowledge the situation, express concern for anyone affected, state that you are investigating, name the person or team responsible for the response, and give a specific time for the next update. Do not assign blame, do not speculate on cause, and do not promise an outcome you cannot control. Change the crisis and the same five slots still hold.

      3. Match the response family to what the audience already blames.

      Read who the audience holds responsible, then pick the family. If an outside actor caused the harm (a victim crisis), deny is credible. If the cause was an accident or a thin link to the brand, diminish fits. If the audience attributes the harm to the brand itself, only rebuild works, meaning apology plus compensation plus a structural change. The audience’s attribution chooses the family, not the brand’s preference.

      4. Build the pitch as a four-part email a reporter reads in twelve seconds.

      Keep the subject under sixty characters, the first paragraph under fifty words, then a proof-point paragraph, then a clear next step, all under 220 words total. Send it to the beat reporter who covers this story, not the masthead, and open the first paragraph with one specific detail from one of their last three stories. That thirty-second personalization is the difference between a 5 percent and a 40 percent read rate.

      Key Takeaways

      • Earned media is the distribution channel you do not own, do not pay for, and cannot pause. The work is knowing how it functions before you need it, because the brand has the least control over the channel that carries the most credibility.
      • The three communication functions split by who controls the message after it leaves the building. Marketing buys attention it controls, corporate communications keeps the story coherent, and PR works the audiences a brand cannot pay, while PESO sorts every channel into paid, earned, shared, and owned.
      • A pitch is a routing problem solved with a four-part template a reporter reads on a phone in twelve seconds. Naming the reporter's last story in the first paragraph is the difference between a 5 percent and a 40 percent read rate, because a generic merge reads as spam and a specific detail reads as homework.
      • Earned media is measured with share of voice, sentiment, and message pull-through. Those three answer how big the brand looms, how positive the coverage runs, and whether the strategy landed, and the relationship underneath, communal versus exchange, decides whether the brand survives a bad quarter.
      • The first hour of a crisis is louder than thirty days of advertising. Acknowledge fast, match the response family to what the audience already blames, and never workshop a statement past the point of saying anything, because the audience in hour one wants proof the company is awake, not a finished investigation.
      • The worst answer to the first-hour rule is no answer. Silence reads as the brand being asleep or hiding, and once that reading locks in it is very hard to dislodge, which is why Bud Light's ten-day silence cost a two-decade position while Stanley's same-day response gained share.
      • AI can draft a pitch and red-team a holding statement, and a sharp question with the rules written down beats a vague one. The model shifts the PR director from drafter to editor, the audit costs cents against a five-figure agency retainer, and the human stays the editor who signs off.

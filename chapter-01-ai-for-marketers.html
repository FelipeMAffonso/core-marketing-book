    01 // the file

    The File

    If you grew up on a phone, you may never have thought about a file. You tap an app, your photos are just there, your messages are just there, and nothing ever asks you where they live. A computer is more honest about it. On a computer, everything you keep is a file, meaning one named thing you can find, open, and hand to someone else, whether that is a photo, a song, or the 8,000-row spreadsheet from the opening story. The plainest picture is a labeled folder in a filing cabinet, with a name on the tab so you can find it again and stuff inside it.

    If you grew up on a phone, you may never have thought about a file. On a computer, everything you keep is a file: one named thing you can find, open, and hand across, like the 8,000-row spreadsheet from the opening story. When you upload one to an AI, you do exactly that, so it pays to know what you are holding.

    On a phone the files are still there, hidden under the apps that open them, which spare you from ever handing one to anyone. The moment you upload something to an AI, you are doing exactly that, picking one file out of the cabinet and passing it across. So it pays to know what you are holding. Every file carries four simple facts: a name (so you can find it again), a size (how much room it takes up), a place it lives (which folder), and a format (what kind of thing it is). Of those four, the format is the one most people never think about, and it is the one most likely to wreck an AI’s answer.

    Every file carries four facts: a name, a size, the folder it lives in, and a format. The format is the one most people never think about, and the one most likely to wreck an answer.

    You can read a file’s format off the end of its name. After the last dot comes a short tag, called the extension, and it tells you what kind of file you are holding. A name ending in .csv is a plain grid: rows of text, with commas marking where one column stops and the next begins. A name ending in .xlsx is an Excel workbook, with cells and formulas and tabs packed into one sealed file. A name ending in .pdf is a fixed page, the kind of thing you would print, where the layout is locked in place even if it started life as a spreadsheet.

    The format matters more than the other three facts because it decides what shape the AI sees when the file arrives. A .csv arrives as a clean grid, so the AI can line up the columns and count the rows the way you would read a table. A .pdf arrives the way a page arrives to your eyes, read top to bottom as a wall of text, so the column structure is gone even when the page started as a printed spreadsheet. The AI can still read the numbers off a PDF, yet it can no longer sort them, filter them, or do math on them, because to the AI they are no longer a table at all. Handing an AI a PDF when it needed a spreadsheet is the most common quiet mistake in marketing AI work, and nothing pops up to warn you because the answer comes back looking just as confident either way.

    One more word matters before the next section, because it is the unit the AI charges by. The AI reads your text in small chunks called tokens, where a token is roughly three-quarters of a word, so a short two-hundred-word note works out to about 260 tokens and a ten-page brief to about 4,000. Think of a token as a mouthful of text, the amount the AI takes in at one bite, and the bigger the file you send, the more tokens it holds and the more each answer costs. Numbers and code take more tokens per page than plain English, which is why a spreadsheet of 8,000 rows can swell into hundreds of thousands of tokens even though it looks small on screen.

    This is the part that explains the opening story. Every model has a ceiling on how much it can hold in mind at one time, and that ceiling is called its context window. Picture a desk with room for only so many open pages spread across it, where once the desk is full, the next page has nowhere to land. A file that fits on the desk gets read in full, but when you hand the model a file bigger than its window, the part past the edge is cut off before the model ever looks at it. The model cannot tell that anything is missing, the way you cannot miss a page you never knew existed, and the chat window shows you nothing at all. That is exactly what burned the brand manager, whose spreadsheet ran past the ceiling so the 67 percent came from the slice that fit while the rest of the survey sat unread.

    The good news for 2026 is that this ceiling got enormous.     02 // the wire

    The Wire

    The file does not stay on your laptop. The company that built the AI runs it on their own computers, far away, so when you type a question and press Enter, your words leave your machine, travel across the internet, get answered, and come back. The whole trip takes a second or two. The chat window makes it feel like a conversation happening right there in front of you, but almost nothing happens on your laptop. Your laptop only types the question and shows the answer, while a powerful computer in a server does the thinking.

    The file does not stay on your laptop. The company that built the AI runs it far away, so when you press Enter, your words travel across the internet, get answered, and come back in a second or two. Your laptop only types the question and shows the answer, while a powerful computer in a server does the thinking.

    That round trip has a name and a shape worth knowing, because every app on your phone and laptop runs on the same one. The trip out is called a request, the trip back is called a response, and together they travel over a shared set of rules called HTTP. Picture the request as a piece of mail you drop in a box, addressed where it is going, labeled with what kind of mail it is, and sealed around the actual message. The reply mail that comes back carries a short status up front, like a delivery receipt, with the answer itself underneath.

    That round trip has a shape worth knowing, because every app runs on the same one. The trip out is a request, the trip back a response, and both travel over a shared set of rules called HTTP.     The doorway your request knocks on is called an API. An API is just a way for one program to ask another program for something, with an agreed-on address to send to and an agreed-on shape for the answer. The company that runs the AI publishes an address, and anything that sends a properly shaped request to it gets a reply. The chat app you use is one such sender, and a short script a marketing analyst writes is another, knocking on the very same door. The friendly chat window and the analyst's code are two ways of reaching the same model through the same doorway.

    The doorway your request knocks on is called an API, a way for one program to ask another for something at an agreed address.     The door has a lock, and your key to it is, almost literally, a password. Every request has to say who is calling, so the company knows both that you are allowed in and whose account to charge. For this the company gives each account a long secret string called an API key, which the request carries the way a letter carries a return address. Anthropic keys start with sk-ant- and OpenAI keys start with sk-, though the exact characters do not matter to you. The thing to remember is that anyone holding your key can spend your money, so you treat it the way you treat the password to your bank.

    The reason to take the key seriously is that careless keys get drained fast. A developer once pasted a key into a public code page by accident. Automated scanners that comb the internet for exactly this found it in about forty minutes and ran up a bill over five thousand dollars before anyone noticed. Keys get scanned for constantly, so the rule is simple: never paste a key into a Slack message, never save it in a file you might share, never email it. The safe way is to store it once in a named slot on your computer (an environment variable) and let your programs read it by that name, so the actual secret never sits inside a file you could accidentally hand to someone. A public page on GitHub is the most common place this leaks.

    03 // the model

    The Model

    You type a question into ChatGPT and an answer slides back a few seconds later. It feels like you asked a very well-read librarian who went and looked something up, but that is not what happened, and the gap between the feeling and the reality is the most useful thing to understand about AI before you hand it any real work.

    You type a question into ChatGPT and an answer slides back. It feels like you asked a well-read librarian who looked something up, but that is not what happened, and the gap between the feeling and the reality is worth understanding before you hand it real work.

    The thing that answered you is called a model, and underneath it runs one small trick. Your phone already does a tiny version of it. When you start texting “I’m running five minutes,” the keyboard offers “late” before you type it, because it has watched how your sentences usually end. A model is that same guess-the-next-word trick, scaled up almost past believing. It read an enormous slice of the internet, plus books and articles, one time, and from all that reading it learned which word tends to follow which. That learning is stored as billions of internal numbers called weights. When you ask it something, it guesses the next word, then the word after that, then the next, until the answer is done. Every reply you have ever gotten was built one guessed word at a time.

    Two facts fall out of that trick, and both change how you use it. The first surprises people who expect a chatbot to be stuck in the past. Open ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini today, ask about a phone that launched last week, and the app searches the web on the spot, reads the live pages, and answers from what it just found, usually deciding to look on its own without being told. For everyday questions you can treat the assistant as up to date. Under the hood the model itself is more limited, because it learned from an enormous pile of reading that stopped on a fixed day, a date called its knowledge cutoff. The memory it carries inside is frozen on that day, like a student who crammed from the whole library up to one date and never read again. The current answers you get come from the search step sitting on top of that frozen memory, so a tool with search switched off drops back to whatever it memorized.

    04 // the agent

    The Agent

    In Section 1.3 you stayed in the driver's seat, typing a question, reading the answer, and typing the next one, with the whole thing moving only as fast as you could type. The version of the assistant you meet in 2026 does more than answer. When a question needs current facts it searches the web on its own, the way you saw a moment ago, and when you hand it a bigger job and connect it to your files or apps, it takes several steps to finish that job for you while you do something else. The name for the assistant working in that mode is an agent.

    Picture it as the difference between asking a coworker a question and handing that same coworker a task.     You can see the shape of the loop in one tiny example. Give the model a single tool that can open a web page and ask it for the top headline on a news site. That back-and-forth (think, use a tool, read the result, decide the next step) is the loop, and a real agent just runs it more times with more tools until the job is done. Think of it as a recipe card the assistant follows, a plain-language note in a folder on your computer that says what the task is and how to do it, started by typing a short command like /competitor-pestel Stanley. Building the card is an afternoon's work, and using it is one command every week for the rest of the year.

    05 // the stack

    The Stack

    You have met four things so far, taught one at a time so each would stick: the file, the wire, the model, and the agent. Set them on top of each other, because that is how they actually fit together, and what you get is a ladder. Each rung sits on the one below it and takes one more chore off your hands than the rung beneath it did, so as you climb you do less while the work does more.

    You have met four things: the file, the wire, the model, and the agent. Set them on top of each other and you get a ladder, where each rung takes one more chore off your hands than the one below.

    07 // failure modes

    Four failure modes the chapter prevents

    No rung in the ladder is safe just because you climbed it. The same four parts you have been learning, the file, the wire, the model, and the agent, each break in one repeatable way, and a marketer who can name the four breaks catches them before they reach a client. Three of the four go wrong with words on a screen, while the fourth goes wrong with a picture of your brand. None of them involves anyone doing anything wrong on purpose, because the tool just fails quietly, and that quiet is what makes it dangerous.

    1. The model read only part of your file

    You paste a long spreadsheet into a chat and ask for a summary. The model summarizes the part it could hold and answers in the same confident voice it would use for the whole thing. You read the summary and trust it for the whole file. That is the story this chapter opened with: eight thousand rows of customer comments pasted in, the model reading down to about row 3,800, and a tidy summary of the first 47% sold as the summary of all of it.

    You might think a 2026 model has solved this, and most days it has. The newest models hold about a million words at once, so a normal file slides in whole and nothing gets cut. The trap is the genuinely big file, the full year of reviews or the whole call log, where you cannot eyeball whether it fit. The fix takes one question. Before you trust a summary, ask the model in plain words how many tokens your file is, and check that against its limit. If the count sits comfortably under, you are fine, and if it is close or over, you feed the model a smaller slice you picked yourself or let a tool break the file into pieces and summarize each one. The chat will never warn you on its own, so the warning has to be you.

    09 // apply, review, and where this goes

    Apply What You Have Learned

    Do this, about ten minutes

    Run the four parts on one real file

    Pick a long document you actually have, like a year of customer reviews, a call log, or a forty-page brief. Then walk it through the chapter's machinery.

    1. Check whether it fits. Open Claude or ChatGPT, paste or upload the file, and ask in plain words how many tokens it is. Compare that number to the model's window. If it is comfortably under, the model read all of it. If it is close or over, the model read only a slice, and the summary you trust is a summary of part of the file.
    2. Ask, then verify. Ask the model for one specific number from the file, a statistic, a date, or a named source. Then click through to the real source. A claim you cannot trace back to a source does not exist, so it does not go on a slide.
    3. Change one control. Ask for three taglines at a low temperature, then ask again at a higher one. Watch the low setting repeat itself and the high setting spread out. A single dial just steered the model for you, with no clever wording involved.
    4. Place it on a rung. Decide which of the four rungs this task belongs on. A one-off question stays on rung one. A weekly version belongs on rung two as a Skill. A version that reads your live data belongs on rung three. A version that runs Monday morning while you sleep belongs on rung four.

    Key Takeaways

    • The model is a well-read autocomplete. It guesses the next word, then the next, from a huge slice of text it read once, so every answer is built one guessed word at a time.
    • Its memory is frozen, but it searches the live web. The training data stops on a knowledge-cutoff date, so current answers come from a search step sitting on top of that frozen memory. Switch search off and it drops back to what it memorized.
    • A fluent answer can still be wrong. The model reaches for the most natural-sounding word, not the most truthful one, so it has no built-in sense of true or false. Every specific number, date, name, and citation gets a real source clicked before it ships.
    • A file is read in chunks, and a chunk past the limit never arrives. The context window is a fixed ceiling measured in tokens, and overflow is cut silently before the model looks at the file. The only warning is you asking whether the whole thing fit.
    • You steer with words and one dial. Your prompt, the hidden system prompt above it, and the temperature setting do more to shape the answer than any clever wording, and the bill is metered in tokens.
    • An agent is the model put in a loop with tools. You state the goal once, the model thinks, uses a tool, reads the result, and repeats until the job is done, while you keep your hand on the steps that touch real money or real customers.
    • The stack is a four-rung climb. Ask by hand on rung one, save a task as a Skill on rung two, connect live data through an MCP plug on rung three, and run a scheduled agent overnight on rung four. The same model runs all four, and only the wiring changes.

    Check Your Understanding

    Answer each in your head, then click to check.

    1. The model answers a question about a phone that launched last week. How is that possible if its training data is frozen?

    The model itself learned from text that stopped on its knowledge-cutoff date, so its built-in memory cannot know about last week. The current answer comes from a web-search step sitting on top of that frozen memory. Switch search off and the model drops back to whatever it memorized, then guesses about anything newer.

    2. A model hands you a clean statistic, a real-looking journal name, and a link. Why is the calm, confident tone no proof the number is real?

    The model reaches for the most natural-sounding next word, not the most truthful one, and it has no inner check asking whether a claim is true. A fluent wrong answer is the model working normally, not breaking. The one-second test is to click the link or DOI, because an invented citation fails to resolve or lands somewhere unrelated.

    3. You paste a full year of reviews into a chat and get a tidy summary. What single question protects you, and why does the chat never warn you on its own?

    Ask how many tokens the file is and compare that to the model's window. Overflow past the window is cut before the model ever looks at it, so the cut happens outside the model and produces no error message. The summary covers only the slice that fit, in the same confident voice it would use for the whole file.

    4. Hand the same CSV to a model as a .csv and as a .pdf. Why does the PDF version often produce a worse answer?

    A CSV arrives as a clean grid, so the model can line up columns and count rows. A PDF arrives as a printed page read top to bottom, so the column structure is gone even though it started as a spreadsheet. The model can still read the numbers off the PDF, but it can no longer sort, filter, or do math on them.

    5. What does the system prompt do, and why does rewriting it change behavior more than rewording your own message?

    The system prompt is a hidden instruction the app sends before your prompt, setting the model's persona, rules, and output format for the whole conversation. It frames every reply that follows. A clever sentence in your own message nudges one turn, while a rewritten system prompt resets the manners and guardrails for all of them.

    6. What turns a plain language model into an agent, and which step should you never switch off on work that touches real money?

    An agent is the same model placed in a loop with a short list of tools. You set the goal once, then the model thinks, uses a tool, reads the result, and decides the next step until the job is done. The review step, where the app shows you a risky action before it sends an email or changes a file, is the one to keep on.

    7. A weekly competitor brief lives on rung two as a Skill and rung four as a scheduled agent. What is actually different between the two rungs?

    The model stays the same, because the same model can run every rung. What changes is the wiring around it, meaning how your request reaches the model and where the answer is sent. On rung two you type one command to run the saved task, while on rung four a timer runs it Monday morning as you sleep and drops the result where you told it to.

    How Buyers Actually Buy

    Working Draft

    A buyer reaches for whatever her memory hands her first, and people rarely notice what they are purchasing.

    1. Hoyer, MacInnis, and Pieters' canonical case that implicit memory, not conscious recall, drives brand retrieval.

    A construction-equipment buyer drives the same commute every weekday morning. Somewhere along that route stands a billboard for Caterpillar. After fifty passes she still cannot tell you what was on it. Asked whether she has seen any Caterpillar advertising in the past month, she shakes her head. Two weeks later her crew foreman asks her to name a heavy-machinery brand for a rental, and Caterpillar arrives in her mouth before she has finished hearing the question. She would call that a guess.1

    It was not a guess. The billboard had encoded itself fifty times into a memory she could not introspect on, and the foreman's question pulled the brand out. A recognition test (have you seen Caterpillar advertising?) would have produced an honest no. The category prompt did what recognition could not, because implicit memory does not need awareness to retrieve.1

    Buyers in the moment of need rarely deliberate. They match the situation against whatever they remember, and they buy whatever the match returns. If the brand retrieved fastest is the brand bought, then marketing's first job is to be the brand most easily retrieved.

    Consumer behavior is the buyer's full set of decisions across three stages: acquisition (researching, comparing, paying), consumption (using the product across the days that follow), and disposition (throwing out, reselling, donating, handing down).2,3 Disposition belongs to the same study because what the buyer does with the item shapes whether they return to the brand for the replacement. Patagonia's repair-and-resale program treats end-of-life as part of the brand experience, not as a service-desk problem. Most of the marketing payoff sits on what the literature calls the being side. The identity the brand confers stays attached to the buyer long after the receipt is forgotten and the day-one fit fades.3

    Two ideas from the consumer-behavior literature frame the rest of the chapter. The first is that buyers vary how hard they think. A first car gets weeks of research; a gas-station Coke gets none. Section 4.3 returns to that range and what sets it. The second is that the textbook decision process runs through seven stages, from need recognition to divestment, and the stages stay useful as a diagnostic even though most buyers skip them.4 When sales fall, the question is which stage is leaking: buyers who never search, buyers who reach checkout and abandon, or buyers who finish and pick a competitor next time. The closing exercise turns that into a hands-on audit you can run on any brand.

    01 // buyers retrieve, then choose

    4.1 Buyers Retrieve, Then Choose

    2. Nedungadi (1990, U. Florida): manipulated only pre-choice cues; preferences held constant, choices shifted toward primed brands. JCR December 1990.

    A buyer who calls Pepsi her favorite cola can still hand a Coke to the cashier when the menu opens. Retrieval is unconscious and quick, and whichever brand comes to mind first usually ends up in the order. Forty years of consumer-psychology research keep returning to the same one-sentence finding: choices shift when researchers manipulate which brands a buyer recalls, even when stated product preferences hold constant.5 The Pepsi-preferring buyer reaches for Coke because Coke came to mind when the menu opened. Nothing about her preference moved in the seconds before she ordered. Buyers in the moment of need rarely deliberate. They match against whatever they remember, which makes the marketer's central job ensuring the brand is the one that comes to mind fastest.

    Long-term memory stores brand knowledge as an associative network, a web of ideas tied together by links the buyer has built through experience and exposure.2 Each brand sits inside a bundle of associations called a schema, and that schema is what comes to mind when a situational cue appears. The density and strength of those links is what scientists at the Ehrenberg-Bass Institute call mental availability: a brand tied to many situations strongly outruns one tied to a few weakly.6 Coca-Cola wins because its schema is tied to morning, lunch, party, pizza, and many other cues with thick connections. Pepsi's net of cues is narrower.

    3. Robert Zajonc's 1968 mere exposure experiments measured the implicit-memory channel directly: subjects shown nonsense words and Chinese characters they could not consciously remember still rated those stimuli as more pleasant than novel ones. The effect peaked between 10 and 25 exposures, then plateaued. Modern out-of-home advertising frequency targets sit in that range for a reason.

    The construction-equipment buyer in the chapter opener never recalled the Caterpillar billboard, yet the brand arrived in her mouth the moment her foreman asked for one. Implicit memory does not require conscious recall to influence behavior, which is why recognition surveys miss most of what advertising actually does.2 A brief earlier exposure makes a concept easier to retrieve later, a process the literature calls priming. A buyer who scrolls past a Coca-Cola post in the morning is measurably more likely to order Coca-Cola at lunch, whether or not she remembers the scroll. Activation spreads automatically across linked ideas, so hearing the word cola brings Coca-Cola, pizza, and party to mind together. The buyer experiences the whole retrieval as a single fast guess. Awareness and retrieval are different memories, and a brand that has secured the first while neglecting the second loses the order anyway.

    Two diagnostics decide whether a brand's distinctive assets are doing the work. Fame is the share of buyers who recognize the asset and connect it to some brand. Uniqueness is the share of recognizers who attach the asset to the correct one. A high-Fame, low-Uniqueness asset signals a logo or color that has bled into its category and is now subsidizing competitors. A 2026 benchmark of 1,162 distinctive assets across twenty-one categories found that shape-based assets reached roughly forty percent Fame and seventy-one percent Uniqueness, while color-based assets reached only twelve and thirty-nine percent.7 Shape survives the low-resolution conditions where most exposures happen: thumbnail icons, glanced billboards, motion-blurred packaging. A fresh asset library should weight shape first, with color in a supporting role while shape carries the recognition load.

    Distribution of exposures across time matters because most exposures never reach deliberation in the first place. An Australian study of 143 weeknight television ads found that only forty percent achieved any post-broadcast recognition. Among those, only forty percent were attributed to the right brand, leaving the noticed-and-correctly-branded share near sixteen percent.8 Small exposures spread across many situational cues beat a single large exposure that hits the same buyer once.2,9 The brand that meets the buyer at the morning gym, the afternoon commute, and the Friday grocery aisle ties its schema to three cues. The brand that buys one blockbuster Super Bowl spot ties its schema to a couch the buyer will not be sitting on when she chooses.

    The audit that decides which assets to keep used to need a research vendor and several weeks of fieldwork. A 2024 study by the Ehrenberg-Bass team showed that the Fame-and-Uniqueness coding rubric now runs inside a large language model with no loss of accuracy. The validation passed 4,600 brand names and roughly 600 non-name assets through the pipeline across the United Kingdom, the United States, and Australia. GPT-4-class systems coded the assets thirty-four percent more accurately than GPT-3.5, and forty-four percent more accurately than human coders, at roughly one percent of the survey cost.10 The audit now runs in an afternoon for a few thousand API tokens. The team that does not run it concedes the diagnostic to whichever competitor does.

    A brand absent from the model's response is in the same position as one absent from the buyer's own set: invisible at the moment of choice. The buyer who delegates the retrieval has handed the consideration set to a system with its own associative network. That network was built from press coverage, reviews, and the schema markup the brand may not even know it is publishing.11 The retrieval problem now has two storefronts, and they share the same architecture. Most retrievals do not even feel like decisions: they happen on autopilot, which is where the next section starts.

    02 // most buying runs on autopilot

    4.2 Most Buying Runs On Autopilot

    4. The 27-62% range spans conservative and broad research traditions; even the lower bound undercuts the rational-deliberator image.

    A college senior walks the same route from her apartment to campus five days a week. By week three of the semester she arrives at the building without remembering a single turn. The route never required a decision. Her brain stored the leaving-the-apartment cue and stitched it to the sequence of turns until the cue alone triggered the behavior, with no conscious thought in the middle. The buyer-behavior version of that walk runs in grocery aisles, coffee lines, and checkout funnels every day of the year. Most category choices are not decisions in the textbook sense. They are stored responses that the environment triggers when the matching cue appears, and the buyer notices the choice only after she has already made it.2

    The numbers say so plainly. Search is sharply limited even for purchases the buyer would call important, and experience-sampling diaries find that roughly forty-three percent of daily action repeats in the same context while the actor is thinking about something else entirely.12 The diary protocols that produced that figure have replicated across cohorts, age groups, and product categories since the 1990s. Estimates put impulse buys between twenty-seven and sixty-two percent of consumer purchases, and even the floor of that range undercuts the deliberation model.2 Reasons-based campaigns aimed at habitual buyers in stable contexts therefore produce no measurable lift, because the buyer is not listening to the argument. She is answering the cue.

    The cue-context-response loop is the basic unit of habitual buying.12 A trigger in the environment sets off a learned behavior the buyer never re-evaluates, because repetition with reward has linked the cue directly to the response. Walking past the campus coffee shop on a Tuesday morning is a cue. Ordering the same medium oat-milk latte is the response. The buyer is inside the shop holding the cup before she has decided to buy coffee. The point of entry for a brand is therefore the cue layer, not the message layer, because a buyer on autopilot does not hear arguments. She answers cues, and cues are the only point in the loop a brand can cheaply enter or cheaply block.

    The asymmetry runs in one direction. Adding a cue to a buyer's stable context is cheap, but breaking a habit that the context still supports is expensive, and persuasion against an active cue almost always loses. The exception is a habit discontinuity window. When the buyer's context changes through a move, a job switch, a new baby, a graduation, or a divorce, the old cue layer dissolves and the buyer has to re-deliberate by default for roughly four to twelve weeks.12 Inside that window a targeted promotion produces structural lift, because the autopilot has no route to fall back on. Outside the window the same promotion reaches a buyer whose cues are intact, and the lift collapses to zero.

    The cleanest population-scale test of the cue-disruption mechanism in three decades is the GLP-1 prescription wave. Semaglutide and tirzepatide damp the appetite signals the gut sends to the brain, and roughly one in four United States households now has at least one member on a prescription. A December 2025 Journal of Marketing Research household-panel study tracked grocery spending before and after adoption.13 Total spend dropped 5.3 percent within six months of starting the medication. Savory snacks fell 10.1 percent and fast-food runs fell 8.0 percent. Higher-income households cut more than lower-income did (8.2 percent versus a smaller drop), which rules out income elasticity as the mechanism. Users report that the quiet pull they used to feel walking past a Dunkin' sign simply does not arrive anymore. The cue is still there in the environment, but the interoceptive signal that used to trigger the response has been damped at the source.

    4b. Rocklage, Berger, and Boghrati (2026, JMR) tracked category confidence across 11,994 Sephora reviewers and 50,000+ beer drinkers. Confidence dips between the eighth and 240th purchase, and low-confidence buyers switched brands at 53% against 43% for high-confidence buyers. A CRM follow-up that asks for a specific dimensional observation (what did you notice about the texture?), not a generic satisfaction rating, speeds confidence recovery and lifted retention 4.5-10pp in field trials.14

    The categories that took the hit were the ones built on the cue-reward loop, and the brands built on those categories felt it on the income statement. Mondelez North America (Oreo, Chips Ahoy, Ritz, Trident) reported a 4.1 percent net revenue decline in early 2026. Walmart launched GLP-1 Nutrition Zones in 1,200 stores by the third quarter of 2025, clustering protein bars and smaller-portion meals where the cue-driven snack shelves used to dominate. The lesson generalizes past pharmaceuticals. Any cohort whose cue layer collapses simultaneously becomes a leading indicator on a category's structural revenue exposure, and a brand built on a single cue, with no non-cue distinctive asset behind it, is exposed the moment the cue disappears.

    The deliberation threshold does not belong to the buyer alone. Kurt Lewin's 1936 equation captures the joint logic: behavior is a function of the person and the environment acting together, B = f(P, E).15 Any observable behavior is the joint product of person-side factors like motives and identity and environment-side factors like situational cues and available alternatives. The Stanley Quencher arc from 2019 through 2024 is the equation in the marketplace. The same young professional who would not have considered a forty-ounce industrial tumbler in 2019 considered it in 2024, although her motives and attitudes stayed roughly constant across those five years.16 The change happened on the environment side. TikTok built a hydration culture that gave the cup social meaning, color-drops converted it from kitchenware into a gift category, and the cup's presence at offices and gyms created a field tension toward purchase that the 2019 environment did not contain.

    A brand that models only segments and personas is therefore modeling only the person side of Lewin's equation. The environment side is where most of the change happens, because that side is the cue layer the buyer's autopilot actually runs on. The next section turns from autopilot to the rarer state: the buyer who has the motivation, the resources, and the situation to actually think a decision through.

    03 // when buyers actually think

    4.3 When Buyers Actually Think

    5. Petty and Cacioppo developed the Elaboration Likelihood Model across a decade of attitude-change experiments at Ohio State (1979-1986). Schumann's 1983 advertising study (high-involvement viewers process arguments; low-involvement viewers respond to celebrity cues) is the cleanest field replication and has held under three decades of meta-analysis.17

    A college junior signs a lease for her first apartment on a Tuesday morning. She has read seven listings, toured four buildings, asked her parents about utilities, and stared at a Google spreadsheet of comparable rents for three nights running. That same evening she buys a Coke from the lobby vending machine without registering that she chose it. The two purchases happen six hours apart inside one head. The classroom model that imagines a buyer who always weighs options carefully cannot accommodate both moments at once, because the effort she spent on the lease is the effort she did not spend on the soda. Buyer deliberation is a scarce resource the buyer rations across her day.2

    Howard and Sheth's 1969 typology organizes that rationing into three modes. Extensive problem solving (EPS) is the heavy mode the lease decision sits inside, where the buyer builds a category schema from scratch through outside-the-head research because she has no prior knowledge to draw on. A first-time car buyer, a small-business owner picking her first SaaS subscription, and a freshman choosing between competing laptops the week before college starts all run in EPS. Limited problem solving (LPS) is the middle mode she runs when she replaces a familiar appliance after a few years, comparing a short consideration set on a few attributes she already knows how to weigh. Routinized response behavior (RRB) is the autopilot mode the Coke runs on, where the choice is retrieved from internal memory in under a second the way she retrieves her own phone number.18 The brand-strategy consequence is that an undergrad picking a first laptop and an undergrad replacing a toaster are not the same buyer, even when they share the same body, because the marketing emphasis on each mode sits in a different place.

    The mode a buyer occupies is not a property of the buyer alone. The same student might run RRB on a Coke at the vending machine and EPS on a Coke for a sponsorship contract her student government is negotiating, because the second purchase carries financial and social stakes the first never reaches. Consumer-behavior research catalogs the conditions that decide which mode triggers through the MAO triad: motivation, ability, and opportunity.2 Motivation is whether the buyer cares enough about the decision to focus on it. Ability is whether she has the financial, cognitive, and emotional resources to evaluate alternatives. Opportunity is whether the situation gives her the time and quiet to actually do that work. All three gates must open at once for deliberation to run, because if any one closes the buyer falls back on a shortcut even when she would prefer not to. The Decision Effort Switchboard widget below lets you toggle the three gates against a worked example and watch the mode flip.

    When the gates do open, the buyer runs the central route through Petty and Cacioppo's Elaboration Likelihood Model (ELM). She evaluates the actual arguments in the message: the lease's per-square-foot cost, the laptop's processor benchmarks, the apartment's commute time.17 Attitudes formed this way bind tightly to the brand because the buyer constructed them herself out of evidence she chose to weigh. Counter-persuasion bounces off them, and a competitor's argument has to clear a higher evidentiary bar to dislodge the position the buyer has already built. When the gates close, she runs the peripheral route instead, forming her opinion from cues that have nothing to do with the argument: the warmth of the background music, the attractiveness of the spokesperson, the familiarity of the logo at the shelf. The peripheral attitude is faster to assemble and faster to abandon, because it never sat through the work that anchors a central attitude in place. Most category choices most buyers make most of the time route peripherally, which is why mental availability is the binding constraint Section 4.1 names and not a tiebreaker for buyers who already deliberated.

    The diagnostic move for a brand manager is to ask which mode the population of buyers in a category is running in at the moment of choice. A category that has been stable for a decade has trained its buyers into RRB and peripheral processing, and a competitor cannot dislodge an incumbent there with an argument because the buyers are not listening to arguments. The cheap window opens when a category shock disrupts the routine, because the shock pushes the population out of RRB and back into LPS or EPS where deliberation runs by default.18 Regulatory changes, category-wide pricing resets, generational handoffs, and new buying interfaces all qualify as shocks at different scales. The AI-mediated discovery shift reorganizing buyer search is the live example sitting underneath every category right now. A buyer who used to retrieve her tumbler brand from memory in a second is asking ChatGPT for a recommendation instead, and the model's consideration set is not hers, which means Stanley and YETI now compete against whatever set the model assembles before the human sees the options. Section 4.6 returns to that shift, and the rest of the chapter builds the machinery you need to read it.

    04 // shortcuts shape the choice

    4.4 Shortcuts Shape the Choice

    A buyer walks into Target on a Tuesday evening for a tumbler and a bottle of sunscreen. She passes a Stanley endcap where a TikTok montage swears by the Cosmopolitan-pink Quencher, and a clerk in a uniform answers her question about lid sizes. The shelf next to the Quencher reads "two left at this price," while her phone shows a Klarna offer that splits the sunscreen into four payments of twenty-five dollars. Beside the bottle a seventy-five-dollar tier sits next to a thirty-five-dollar one, nudging her toward the middle option. None of those cues argued with her, because each one borrowed a piece of cognitive machinery that buyers run on by default. Four families cover most of what was running in her cart on the way to checkout. The social family runs on other people, and the authority family on credentialed roles. The loss-and-scarcity family turns on the asymmetric pain of missing out, while the reference-point family rests on whichever number or label comes first. Earlier sections covered attention and decision effort, and this section covers what runs the moment the buyer can no longer wait.

    Social shortcuts

    6. Cialdini's Influence (1984, expanded 2006 and 2021) names seven principles. Three of them (social proof, liking, and unity) form the social family used here, and authority becomes its own family. The remaining three (reciprocity, commitment, and scarcity) fall under loss-and-scarcity, while the reference-point family draws on a separate Kahneman and Tversky tradition.19,20

    Buyers read the behavior of similar others as evidence about their own future satisfaction, and that reading is the engine behind social proof, liking, and unity.19,20 A freshman who picks the coffee counter with a line is treating the queue as data the same way she would treat a Yelp rating. Liking adds the pull of a familiar voice, so a friend's recommendation outperforms the same line delivered by a stranger. Unity adds in-group identity on top of liking, because a fellow climber's nod carries weight a similar stranger's never will. The three cues stack hardest under uncertainty and similarity, and they collapse under reactance once the buyer reads the move as manufactured.

    7. The Tupperware party famously ran all seven of those levers at once. At its late-1990s peak a party started somewhere on Earth roughly every 2.75 seconds, on an advertising-free model that moved about $2.5 million in sales a day.19

    The Tupperware party of the 1990s ran the social family at full strength. A neighbor opened the door, a friend ran the introductions, and a room of similar women placed visible orders inside a shared kitchen identity. Field studies put the friendship bond at roughly twice the predictive weight of the buyer's own preference for the product on the table.19 Marketers borrow the engine by staging visible-crowd evidence such as Amazon's review counts or Duolingo's streak community, and by recruiting in-group voices in place of studio spokespeople. The audit question asks whether the visible crowd is real, whether the spokesperson sits genuinely inside the in-group, and whether the intensity stays under the buyer's reactance threshold. A staged review pile reverses the cue the moment the buyer reads the pile as staged.

    Authority shortcuts

    Buyers defer to titles, uniforms, and trappings because slow verification of expertise is expensive, and the symbol arrives faster than the credential check.19 A pedestrian in Brooklyn handed a dime to a stranger forty-two percent of the time when the asker wore street clothes, and the same request in a security-guard uniform pulled ninety-two percent.19 Nothing else about the request changed, so the deference loaded onto the uniform and took effect before deliberation could intervene. Hospital nurses ran the same pattern on phone orders that broke four hospital rules at once, because the title alone carried the compliance through a gate where careful evaluation would have stopped.

    The deployment splits into three modes a brand team can recognize on sight. Credentialed-expert mode runs a real title in front of the camera, as when a board-certified dermatologist fronts a skincare brand. Role-coded mode borrows the role without the title, as when a frozen-meal package puts a chef in whites on the box. Affiliation mode borrows authority from a third-party seal, such as the ADA stamp on a dental brand or the SOC 2 logo on a software pricing page. The audit asks whether the credential is real, whether the role actually matches the buyer's question, and whether the seal still means what the buyer assumes it means. Borrowed authority unravels fast once the buyer notices the seam, because reactance against a fake credential runs steeper than reactance against an absent one.

    Loss-and-scarcity shortcuts

    A dollar on the loss side weighs roughly twice what the same dollar feels like on the gain side, and that asymmetry, loss aversion, is the engine underneath reciprocity, commitment, scarcity, endowment, and present bias.21 Reciprocity sets a small obligation in motion before any ask arrives, which is why Costco's sample tray converts indifferent shoppers at a rate no markdown could match. Commitment converts a tiny early agreement into a larger later one, so a save-your-progress tap at a lesson's end pulls the next session forward. Scarcity tells the buyer the option is about to disappear, although a socially scarce option outperforms a logistically scarce one because other buyers racing for the cup carry proof an empty shelf cannot. Endowment kicks in once public ownership has been claimed, so a TikTok post of the cup raises the buyer's valuation before the receipt prints, and present bias finishes the family by steepening the felt discount on right-now relative to the future self.

    8. The CFPB's January 2025 report found 53 percent of buy-now-pay-later users acknowledged a purchase they knew was outside their budget. The U.S. category ran near $70 billion in 2025.22

    The canonical lab evidence is Worchel's 1975 cookie jar, where identical cookies were judged most desirable when the jar emptied because other participants had taken them.19 The brand version runs through Stanley's color drops, Booking's "two rooms left at this price," and Patagonia's lifetime warranty framed as protection against loss. Klarna and Afterpay ship present bias at internet scale, splitting a hundred-dollar basket into four installments of twenty-five so the steep right-now discount applies to each piece and not the whole.22 The audit asks whether the scarcity is real, whether the obligation is fair, and whether the installment widget pairs with a one-click cancel the buyer's future self would still endorse.

    Reference-point shortcuts

    Buyers do not feel a price, a probability, or a quantity in absolute terms. They feel each one against whichever number, label, or comparison was placed in front of them first.21,23,24,25 Anchoring pulls the estimate toward the first value seen, so a seventy-five-dollar tier on a rate card makes a forty-five-dollar option read as the modest one. Framing decides whether the same forty-five dollars reads as a spend or as a hedge against losing access. Mental accounting then decides which envelope the dollars come from, so a hundred-dollar tumbler coded as a collectible draws against a different ceiling than the same insulating function coded as kitchenware.

    9. Johnson and Goldstein (2003): opt-in countries register about 15 percent of citizens as organ donors and opt-out countries about 90 percent, a 75-point gap that reflects the form, not the preference.25

    Defaults steer the share of buyers who passively continue, which is why opt-in countries register about fifteen percent of citizens as organ donors while opt-out countries register about ninety percent on similar underlying preferences.25 The seventy-five-point gap reflects the architecture of the form, not any real difference in what citizens want, and designing that architecture on purpose is choice architecture. Joint-versus-separate evaluation completes the family, because rivals on a comparison grid supply the reference point an isolated detail page cannot, so a brand sold inside a comparison table competes on a different attribute set than the same brand on its own product page.

    The worked case is the Drunk Elephant SPF on Sephora's product page versus Amazon's compare-similar grid. On the standalone page the hundred-dollar bottle sits in separate evaluation, and price-per-ounce stays invisible because no rival supplies the reference point.26 On the comparison grid the same bottle sits beside cheaper options, joint evaluation engages, and the dominant attribute swaps from brand cachet to dollars-per-ounce. When ChatGPT's shopping responses began shipping side-by-side comparison tables in March 2026, the default evaluation mode for a generation of buyers swapped from separate to joint, so the attributes the model chooses for its rows now help decide the sale. The audit asks which reference point the buyer meets first, whether the brand authored it or a rival did, and whether the default the buyer sails through is the one her reflective self would have picked. Availability and representativeness round out the family, because easily-retrieved examples read as evidence of how often something happens while prototype-fit reads as evidence of what category something belongs to.

    Reading about four families of shortcuts is one thing, and spotting them in a real ad is the skill that transfers.     Shortcuts, whether they arrive from other people or from the buyer's own mind, can only operate on the brands the buyer already carries into the store, since a shortcut chooses among a short list it did not assemble. So the prior question is how a brand reaches that short list before the buyer ever begins to search.

    05 // consideration sets built before search

    4.5 Consideration Sets Are Built Before Search

    10. First-recall premium has a name. Ehrenberg-Bass calls the share of unaided choices that go to the first-named brand the retrieval advantage; the thirty-percent figure is a category average, higher in low-involvement categories.8

    A buyer standing in front of a beverage cooler at a gas station does not survey the shelf. She reaches for one of two or three brands her memory already volunteered before her hand moved. That handful of brands is the consideration set, sometimes called the evoked set in older textbooks. Ehrenberg-Bass panel data places the set at two to eight brands across categories, with the modal count closer to three.8 Roughly half of buyers consider only one brand at the actual moment of purchase. A brand outside this set effectively does not exist for that buyer in that moment, no matter how good the product is on attributes the buyer never gets to evaluate.

    The brand recalled first wins about thirty percent of unaided choices in the category before any feature or price comparison runs. That first-recall premium is why mental availability functions as the load-bearing brand asset across categories. Growth depends on widening the set of moments at which the brand comes to mind, because retrieval order propagates into purchase share even when the set is otherwise identical. The figure shifts higher in low-involvement categories like soft drinks and salty snacks, where buyers rarely deliberate, and lower in high-stakes purchases like cars and life insurance, where buyers run additional retrieval cycles before deciding.8

    An attribute drives the choice only when it is both salient and diagnostic.2 "Tells time" is salient for a watch though every watch does it, which leaves it useless for discrimination. The marketer's lever is the diagnostic-not-salient cell, an attribute the brand already owns but buyers do not yet retrieve. Apple ran this move on the iPhone by making camera quality diagnostic for a phone, releasing low-light and computational-photography improvements in tight cadence and naming each one in launch copy until camera became the attribute buyers reach for when comparing handsets. Negative information is also more diagnostic than positive at this stage, because a single dealbreaker filters the set faster than any number of nice-to-have features can build it.2

    A buyer at a Manhattan trattoria glances down the wine list and almost picks the eighteen-dollar house red, then sees a four-hundred-dollar Barolo at the top of the page she will not order. By the time her server returns she has chosen the thirty-two-dollar Brunello. The Barolo's only job was to sit there. Decades of evidence catalog this family of choice-context effects, where preference between two options shifts when a third option enters the set even though no one selects it.27 Each effect works by handing the buyer an easy verbal reason to pick one option over another, because the assortment supplies the justification the buyer's deliberation circuit reaches for under any System 2 pass. A 2024 retrospective treats decoy, compromise, phantom, and upscaling as variants of one underlying mechanism.28

    11. Choice-context effects at scale. A 2025 study of roughly 3.6 million wine transactions across a regional grocery chain found about one percentage point of share lift on occasional shoppers, redirecting roughly 36,000 bottles a year through the silent presence of a non-purchased alternative.29

    A second context effect turns on evaluation mode, not set composition. When the buyer evaluates options separately, one webpage or shelf-tag at a time, she leans on the attributes that are easy to picture in isolation. When she evaluates them jointly, on a comparison grid, she leans on attributes that are easy to compare across columns. A higher-quality sound system loses to a louder one that is technically worse when the two are seen apart, because loudness reads alone and quality only registers in comparison. Drunk Elephant ran the inverse on sunscreen by foregrounding ingredient transparency, which buyers can only weigh against rival labels in joint mode. Channel design follows from the mode: a direct-to-consumer comparison page should foreground the diagnostic-not-salient attribute, while a roadside billboard cannot.

    The prior question is which situations put the brand into the set in the first place. Category Entry Points (CEPs) are the situations, occasions, emotions, and cues that prompt a buyer to access the category.8 For beverages, CEPs include morning, post-workout, with pizza, commute, party, road trip, and gift. The Ehrenberg-Bass framework organizes the inventory along seven W-spokes: why (the goal), when (time of day), where (location), with whom (social context), while doing what (parallel activity), with what (paired product), and how feeling (emotional state). Each spoke is a category of retrieval cue covering many moments, which is why a brand can hold many CEPs along one spoke. Stanley owns several when cues at once: morning, gym hour, school drop-off, and the desk-water break.

    Market share approximates the polygon area a brand draws when its CEP links are plotted as spokes, roughly the count of CEPs the brand owns multiplied by the average link strength.8 A brand that owns many weak links can beat a brand that owns a few strong ones, because the buyer's retrieval moment is unpredictable. Adding a new situation the brand could plausibly serve is usually cheaper than out-competing for one an incumbent already owns. The Ehrenberg-Bass tradition separates growth CEPs, situations the brand is not yet retrieved at where the spend recruits light buyers, from refresh CEPs, situations the brand already owns where the spend re-cues heavy buyers who would have bought anyway. Almost all category growth lives in light-buyer recruitment, so roughly sixty to seventy percent of brand-building spend should route to growth CEPs.30

    The 8-second window

    A 2025 attention benchmark reports average focus per social post fell from 12.1 seconds in 2015 to 8.25 seconds in 2025, with seventy percent of viewers deciding whether to stay or scroll within the first three seconds.31 The recognition cue has to trigger on roughly the second frame, which favors shape-based distinctive assets (the Coca-Cola contour bottle, the Stanley Quencher tapered cylinder) over text or color. A field study of 23,958 branded-video moments found an affect-cognition-affect arc beat the default cognitive structure: open with the splash, cut to a one-second feature beat, close with the user's smile.32

    A 2026 cluster opens an affective CEP class the original inventory does not name. The retrieval cue is not a place or a time but an internal state, such as loneliness at 11 p.m. or stress before a presentation.33 Brands adjacent to those cues (hydration, journaling, sleep, fitness, mental-health apps) gain a class of moments to audit, alongside a safety risk if they ship without crisis-detection language, because one unsafe response damages brand perception far more than thousands of safe ones help it.34 Conversational brand surfaces in particular accumulate these affective cues without a category-norm playbook, which is why the audit table needs new rows for them.

    The audit now needs a second column for the model. Each CEP can be rendered as a query the buyer would type into ChatGPT or Claude, run on a schedule, with the brand's rank tracked over time. When the buyer outsources the shortlist to a model, the same cardinality holds, three to five brands, though the set is assembled from a training corpus the buyer never built.11 The 2026 tooling for this is answer-engine optimization, which tracks AI mentions and tunes content to lift rank inside model answers. A brand absent from the model's set sits in the same position as a brand absent from a buyer's. Until recently the short list lived only inside the buyer's head. Now part of it lives inside the model she just asked.

    4.6 AI Is Becoming Part of the Buyer's Mind

    12. Algorithm aversion, defined. People often prefer a human recommender over an algorithm even when the algorithm is provably more accurate, and the preference sharpens after one visible error. On subjective tasks the mechanism is uniqueness neglect: the buyer believes her case is special in ways the algorithm cannot see.35

    A college senior opens ChatGPT and asks for a forty-ounce insulated tumbler under fifty dollars for weekend hiking. Three brands come back ranked, each with a one-sentence fit reason. She never typed Stanley into a search bar, scrolled a product grid, or compared reviews across tabs. The model wrote her consideration set in eleven seconds, and the brands that surfaced will anchor every comparison she makes next.36 The associative network from section 4.1 now runs in parallel with a second network the buyer never trained, built from patterns a large language model learned online. Mental availability still wins, but it now lives inside the model's weights as much as inside the buyer's head.

    The buyer's first instinct is to distrust the second network even when its picks are better. A student asked to choose between a human advisor and an algorithmic one will pick the advisor.35 The preference sharpens after the algorithm makes a single visible mistake, and it is sharpest on tasks the buyer reads as subjective, because she believes her case is special in ways the algorithm cannot see.37 The same buyer accepts the algorithm without hesitation on tasks she reads as objective: the fastest driving route, the cheapest flight, the top-rated charger. Identity-laden categories trigger the algorithm aversion, while functionally-bounded ones leave the buyer indifferent to who or what made the choice.

    The aversion inverts whenever the human alternative reads as corruptible. A loan applicant choosing between a human officer and a fixed-rule algorithm often picks the algorithm.38 The algorithm cannot quietly approve a faster decision for a cousin who looks like the loan officer. The same inversion appears in bail decisions and entry-level hiring once the human reviewer reads as self-interested. A 2024 cross-cultural replication shows the moderator is an attention state, not a stable trait, because exposing readers to a corruption-scandal article before the task activates the same inversion inside a single population.39 Stitch Fix layers a human stylist's commentary on top of the algorithmic pick, and the hybrid beats either pure version because the buyer treats each one as a hedge against the other's blind spot.

    A 2021 review proposed a four-type taxonomy of buyer-AI interactions that holds up well enough to walk through a single student's afternoon.36 Her Instagram feed quietly logs which posts she lingered on, which is data capture. Spotify drops her into a college-indie-folk-Sunday-evening bucket and tunes the next playlist, which is classification. She types a question into ChatGPT and asks it to draft the first email to her professor, which is delegation. At night she opens a companion chatbot and talks through a hard day, which is the social type. The four types stack inside any AI-first storefront, and a brand audit should name which type each touchpoint runs on before discussing optimization, because the disclosure obligations and trust dynamics differ across them.

    A 2025 frontier paper flipped the original premise, because the algorithm is increasingly the buyer itself: an autonomous shopping agent is the entity actually browsing, reading reviews, comparing prices, and clicking buy.40 A 2026 follow-up specialized the framework into a buildable agent specification organized around three phases of any delegated task.41 The preactional phase sets expectations and gathers consent before the task begins. The actional phase keeps the buyer able to intervene while the agent is executing. The postactional phase evaluates the outcome and logs the chain of actions for audit. Walmart's Sparky shipping with a fixed scope-of-action pre-commitment is the preactional lever in operation, and Costco's member-services chatbot exposing which membership rules it consulted before issuing a refund is the actional version.

    When the buyer is an agent, the brand's mental-availability assets change shape. Distinctive shapes and colors still matter for the human eye. Training-data presence, citation graph, schema markup, review depth, and clean structured-data endpoints now matter for the agent eye. LLM shopping audits show recommendations concentrate on a handful of modal products across thousands of independent queries, top-of-list positions stay over-weighted even in text-only interfaces, and seller-copy tweaks produce ten-to-thirty-point share swings.42 Walmart reported in 2025 that Sparky lifted average order value by roughly thirty-five percent across half the Walmart app's user base.43 An eighty-dollar basket became a hundred-and-eight-dollar basket through proactive complement suggestions the buyer would not have searched for.

    Agents also restrict the buyer's experience in three ways a marketer should be able to name.44 Agency transference is the algorithm pre-filtering options the buyer would otherwise have seen, and the TikTok For You feed is the canonical case, because the user never sees the ninety-nine percent of videos already filtered out. Parametric reductionism is the compression of the buyer's identity into a numerical embedding, so two buyers whose embeddings sit close together get the same recommendation regardless of how different their identities are. Regulated expression is the AI interface reshaping the buyer's articulation of need through suggested rephrasings, which is distinct from output moderation because it controls what the system accepts as input in the first place.

    Aversion intensifies in identity-laden categories like clothing, gifts, books, and music, and softens in functionally-bounded ones like commodities, scheduling, and search. The things a buyer owns extend her sense of self, so an agent's choices in those categories register as identity violations even when functionally adequate.45 One specific reversal cuts across the gradient: when inventory is vanishing, consumers welcome the higher-autonomy agent.46 The question shifts from whether the agent's taste is good enough to whether the agent will get the option before it is gone. Stanley limited-edition tumblers sell out in roughly four minutes, and the shopper who would refuse an AI clothing recommender on a Tuesday scroll will accept an autonomous bidding agent inside that four-minute window. A practical first step is to rank category-context cells by inventory volatility and ship the higher-autonomy flow in the top quintile.

    The conversational layer also carries a revenue logic the buyer rarely sees. Platforms running ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, and Gemini cannot cover compute on subscriptions alone, so the next layer is paid inclusion of brand names inside the model's reply.47 The conversational turn is the priced unit the way the impression was priced on Google. The format is a contextual native ad embedded inside the answer the buyer asked for, with no visual boundary and no banner shape, arriving in the same conversational register the model uses for everything else, which slips past the persuasion-knowledge defenses buyers trained against display formats. The merchant infrastructure is shipping fast. Shopify rolled out agentic storefronts to millions of merchants by March 2026, OpenAI launched Instant Checkout inside ChatGPT in late 2025, and Adobe telemetry placed generative-AI shopping trips at roughly forty-seven times their year-earlier volume.

    The monetization question forces a disclosure question, and the Interactive Advertising Bureau answered it on January 15, 2026, with a framework naming six categories of AI-generated content that require consumer-facing labels, including synthetic images of fictional events and chatbots simulating human interaction.48 A Patagonia campaign makes the rule concrete. The team shoots a real climber on a real rope wearing a real Patagonia jacket, then post-production replaces the flat overcast sky with a dramatic dawn backdrop. Only the dawn is synthetic, but it falls inside the first disclosure category, so the brand discloses. Content merely suspected of being AI-generated lost roughly half its trust and fourteen percent of purchase consideration in a 2025 study, with the damage showing up on suspicion alone. A brand that defaults to disclosure now moves ahead of a regulatory cycle tightening faster than its own creative refresh. Stanley, Duolingo, and Costco show the whole chapter at work, so the close walks through them.

    Apply What You Have Learned

    Do this, about ten minutes

    Audit a real brand on the new shelf

    Pick a brand you actually bought in the last week, then run the chapter's machinery on it.

    1. Map its category entry points. List the situations where this brand comes to mind for you: the times, places, moods, and activities. That list is its mental availability inside your own head.
    2. Ask the AI shelf. Open ChatGPT or Claude and ask for a recommendation the way a buyer would, naming one of those situations: "recommend a [category] for [your situation], three picks with a reason each." Read the short list it returns.
    3. Find the gap. Is your brand on the AI's list? If it is, note which situation won it the slot. If it is not, you have found a mental-availability gap, the brand is invisible at that moment to the model that is now half of your buyers' memory.
    4. Name one fix. Pick one category entry point the brand could plausibly own but does not, and one distinctive asset, a shape, a color, a sound, that would make it recognizable inside the eight-second scroll.

    Run it once and the chapter stops being abstract. Stanley did exactly this audit in 2018, when its dashboard read low cup sales among hunters and contractors as product weakness, while the real buyer was a young professional at school drop-off and the gym, activating entry points the brand was not even measuring. Stanley invested behind those situations, and cup-line revenue moved from $73 million to $750 million in five years.

    Key Takeaways

    • Memory is the first storefront a buyer reaches. The brand that arrives first when a buyer needs the category wins more than the brand that scored highest on a survey, because retrieval at the moment of need beats stated preference at the desk.
    • Awareness is not retrieval. A buyer can recognize a brand on a list and still fail to produce it in the moment that matters; category entry points are the links that do the surfacing.
    • Most buying runs on autopilot. Roughly forty-three percent of daily behavior is habitual, so the dominant mode is routinized response, not extensive problem-solving.
    • Decision effort scales with risk. Routine, limited, and extensive problem-solving are not personality types but mode-states set by familiarity, money risk, social risk, and urgency.
    • Shortcuts come in families. Social, authority, loss-and-scarcity, and reference-point shortcuts map the Cialdini principles and the behavioral-economics levers onto one picture.
    • Consideration sets are built before the search. The brands competing for the choice are the ones that came to mind inside the buyer's first few seconds of attention.
    • AI is becoming a second memory. Shopping agents now browse, compare, and click buy, so brand-asset work optimizes for the agent's eye as well as the human's.

    Check Your Understanding

    Answer each in your head, then click to check.

    1. What separates mental availability from brand awareness, and why does it change where the budget goes?

    Mental availability is the strength and breadth of the links between the brand and the situations where a buyer might need the category. Awareness is only the ability to recognize the brand on a list. Awareness without entry-point links does not surface the brand when the buying moment arrives, so spend that builds links beats spend that builds recognition alone.

    2. Why does focusing on heavy buyers produce weak growth, even though the heavy 20 percent generate most current revenue?

    Heavy buyers already retrieve the brand, so extra spend on them produces little lift. Light buyers, who buy the category occasionally and the brand rarely, are where category growth comes from. Loyalty programs structurally miss them because light buyers do not engage with the program.

    3. A car ad lists six engine specs, three transmissions, and a torque curve. Why will it underperform in a thirty-second slot?

    A buyer scrolling at home is on the peripheral route, so central-route arguments cannot be processed in the time available. Shape, color, and feeling carry the encoding. Save the spec sheet for the dealership floor, where motivation, ability, and opportunity all run high.

    4. Why does an algorithm-rendered approval often produce lower satisfaction than the same approval from a human?

    The gap appears on favorable decisions, because the buyer reads acknowledgment by a human as recognition of her personhood. It closes for unfavorable decisions. A brand can wrap an algorithmic approval in a human-attribution frame and measure the difference.

    5. What is a category entry point, and how is it different from a positioning statement?

    A CEP is a situation, need, or trigger that brings the brand to mind. A positioning statement is the brand's claim to a strategic place in the market. A CEP lives inside the buyer's head and is measured by what she recalls first when the situation arrives.

    6. When does scarcity flip the usual algorithm-aversion pattern?

    When inventory is vanishing fast, buyers reverse the preference and welcome a higher-autonomy agent. The question shifts from "is the agent's taste good enough" to "will the agent get the option before it is gone." Stanley's four-minute color drops live inside that window.

    7. Why is "which feature matters most to you?" a poor predictor of shelf choice?

    The survey recruits System 2 to compose a verbal ranking. The shelf decision runs on System 1 retrieval driven by mental availability and distinctive assets. Stated preference and revealed preference disagree because different cognitive systems produce them.

    8. A startup wants to launch a tumbler with no ad budget. Which shortcut family should it build into first?

    The social family, because reciprocity, social proof, and unity scale through earned exposure with no ad budget behind them. Costco substitutes membership commitment, weekend sample reciprocity, and meme-grade word-of-mouth for ad spend. Map the entry points, find the most underserved situation, and seed the social-proof loop.

    The Duolingo case is one instance of the definition this chapter works from. Mobile marketing is the coordinated set of decisions about how a product is found, installed, opened, retained, and re-monetized inside an app-store ecosystem. The phrase rules out treating mobile as a smaller web, because Apple and Google own both the channel installs happen through and the channel engagement gets measured through. Found, installed, opened, retained, and re-monetized are five jobs that fail in different ways, and the brand that wins runs the listing, the push channel, the in-app surface, the measurement stack, and the paid mix as one connected system.     01 // why mobile is its own marketing surface

    Why Mobile Is Its Own Marketing Surface

    A buyer who wants a flashcard app on a Tuesday morning does not type a query into Google. They unlock the phone, swipe to the App Store, type two or three letters, and tap whichever icon looks most like the thing they want. The website that ranks first on Google for flashcard app is not on the screen. The buyer is inside a closed retail environment whose owner, Apple on iPhone and Google on Android, decides what gets shown, in what order, with what description, and at what price. The premise of marketing here is that the storefront and the inventory belong to the same two firms, so the brand's job is to win shelf space on a shelf it does not own.

    Every conversation a mobile growth team has returns to one number, because that number is the input every other number depends on. That number is the install, the moment a user downloads the app and opens it for the first time. An install is not a store-impression, where someone merely sees the listing, and it is not a tap-through, where someone clicks to read more. It is the first open, and it is what every other mobile metric is defined against.

    LTV minus CPI (the mobile unit-economics floor) Every mobile business reduces to a two-number sentence: the lifetime value (LTV) the average install produces versus the cost per install (CPI) the brand paid to acquire it. If LTV beats CPI with margin, every additional install compounds the business; if it does not, every additional install drains cash. A healthy consumer-app business in 2026 runs LTV at roughly 2.5 to 4 times CPI, and anything below 1.5 times is a unit-economics problem no growth tactic can paper over.

    Because installs are the unit the business is built on, the next question is how much one install costs and how much one is worth. The cost question is the cost per install, or CPI, the dollars spent on advertising divided by the installs that advertising produced. The value question is the lifetime value, or LTV, the total revenue one user produces across their whole history with the app. Put cost on one side of the page and value on the other, and the equation every mobile business reduces to comes into view: if the average install eventually pays back more than the brand spent to acquire it, the business compounds, and if not, it burns cash.

    Between the install on day zero and the lifetime value months later sits the question of whether the user keeps coming back, which is what retention answers. The industry reports day-1, day-7, and day-30 retention, the share of installed users still opening the app one day, seven days, and thirty days after first open. These three checkpoints matter because a user still around at day thirty is far likelier to pay than one who left by day seven, and a user who left on day one almost never returns. The day-30 median sits near 4 percent across non-gaming apps and near 14 percent for subscription apps, which sounds small because it is. Even the best apps lose most of their users within a month, so the chapter spends its attention on the surfaces that decide whether a user crosses the day-7 and day-30 cliffs.

    The retention numbers come from a cohort, the group that installed in a given week, and the dashboard asks how many of them opened the app again on day 7, then plots the share still active against the days since install. That plot is the retention curve, and it almost always falls fast in the first week and then flattens. A related pair on the same page is MAU and DAU, whose ratio, called stickiness, reads above 50 percent for a daily habit like Duolingo and lower for a weekly app. The three numbers compose into one picture: the brand pays a cost per install today, the user generates lifetime value over the next several months, and retention is the bridge that decides how much of that value the brand actually collects.

    2. Work the bridge through one case. A flashcard app pays $3 per install and earns about $9 of lifetime value from a $4.99 monthly subscription that lasts on average two months, a comfortable 3-to-1 ratio. Now retention slips because the onboarding got worse, and the average subscription lasts one month instead of two. Lifetime value falls to $4.50, the ratio falls to 1.5-to-1, and a campaign that was profitable a week earlier is now only breaking even on the same ad spend. Every section in this chapter is, at root, about defending one of the three numbers in that equation.

    Pull the subscription-length slider from two months down to one and the ratio crosses the line the same way it does in the worked case, which is the whole reason retention earns a section of its own later in the chapter. The presets fail in recognizable ways. Overpaying for installs at a thin retention level burns cash on every acquisition, and a healthy ratio can tip to break-even from a single month of lost subscription life. The equation is the destination, but the work lives in the surfaces the brand operates to defend each piece of it.

    That equation is the destination, and the work of defending each piece of it lives in the surfaces the brand actually operates.

    App Store Optimization (ASO) ASO is the practice of optimizing every element a user sees in the App Store or Google Play (title, subtitle, description, keywords, screenshots, video, ratings, reviews, category) so the listing both ranks higher in search and converts more of the users who see it. The two halves matter independently: visibility without conversion fills the impressions column without filling the install column, while conversion without visibility ships a beautiful listing nobody finds.

    The 2026 mobile stack has four surfaces, and the brand that wins runs them as one instrument where a change in one layer ripples through the other three. The first layer is the store listing, the page that turns a store-impression into an install, and it answers Will users find us, and will they tap install? The second is the engagement surface, the retention layer the brand owns after the install, and it answers Will the user come back tomorrow, next week, and next month?

    The third layer is the Mobile Measurement Partner (MMP), the third-party software that attributes installs back to the ad that drove them, and it answers Did our spend work, and which channel did the working? The fourth is the paid-acquisition channels that buy traffic into the listing, and it answers Can we buy more growth, and at what cost per install? The four questions chain into each other, because whether the spend worked depends on the user finding the listing, which depends on the keyword indexing, so a broken listing makes the measurement layer report misleading numbers. Brands lose when they hire four teams to own four layers and let the seams open up. Section 14.2 starts with the listing, because it is the layer the brand controls most directly and the one every other decision points back to.

    02 // app store optimization

    App Store Optimization

    3. Apple's search index is closed and proprietary, so the weights in Figure 14.1 are recovered through large-scale regression on ranking shifts after controlled metadata changes, the methodology Phiture, AppTweak, and Sensor Tower use in their respective ASO benchmarks. Treat the percentages as approximations within roughly five percentage points.

    The store is the shelf Apple and Google own, so the next question is the practical one: how does a brand move its listing up the rankings, and how does it turn the buyers who land on the listing into installs? App Store Optimization does two mechanically separate jobs, and the brand that wins runs them as one workflow. The first job is keyword indexing, which gets the listing to appear when a buyer searches a relevant term. The second job is conversion-rate optimization, which turns the buyer who landed on the listing into an install. The first half is invisible to the buyer, while the second half is everything the buyer sees.

    Keyword research as three-axis optimization

    Keyword research weighs every candidate term on three axes at once, because traffic, difficulty, and relevance each pull toward something the brand wants and the three fight each other. High-traffic keywords are usually high-difficulty too, because everyone competes for them, while the easiest keywords to rank for are often the lowest-traffic ones, because nobody bothers to compete. Relevance is the axis that quietly disqualifies the rest, since a flashcard app that ranks for a high-traffic but unrelated term wins installs from users who wanted something else and churn within a day. The keyword every team hunts scores well on all three at once, high traffic and low difficulty and high relevance, and that keyword almost never exists in the clean form a marketer hopes for. So the work is a principled approximation, and the asymmetric bet a team can actually win is a term with enough traffic to matter, low enough difficulty to rank without paid support, and tight enough relevance that the installs it brings actually stay.

    Duolingo shows the tension at scale. The head term spanish carries enormous traffic, somewhere between 90,000 and 130,000 daily App Store searches in the United States, but its difficulty sits near the ceiling because every language app on Earth bids on it. The relevance is perfect, since Duolingo teaches Spanish, yet the difficulty makes the term unwinnable on organic work alone without paid Apple Search Ads behind it. The long-tail variant learn spanish for travel carries roughly a tenth of the traffic, but its difficulty is far lower, and it converts about two-thirds better per impression because the search intent is sharper. The buyer who types learn spanish for travel has a specific goal, which makes them more likely to install once they see the listing.

    The fix is to spread the listing's metadata across thirty to forty long-tail variants of that kind, so the listing indexes against the widest reasonable surface without leaning on any single high-difficulty head term. A 2026 program packs those variants into three places at once: the iOS hidden-keyword field, the title, and the subtitle. The weights in Figure 14.1 then show roughly how each surface feeds the ranking signal, with the title carrying about 30 percent of the keyword weight, the subtitle 25 percent, and the hidden field 20 percent. The remaining weight sits in the description, the developer name, and the in-app-purchase names, which is why a single field is never the whole strategy. The same listing indexes differently on Google Play, which reads the description text directly and has no hidden field, so a team running both stores writes the metadata twice.

    Screenshots and video, the conversion half

    4. The 60 percent figure for screenshots draws from Phiture's Mobile Growth Stack 2024 conversion-attribution analysis across 240 listings; the top-quartile listing recovered roughly 31 percent install conversion versus the bottom-quartile listing's 7 percent on identical traffic.

    Once a buyer is on the listing, the visual carries roughly 60 percent of the install decision, and the rest of the metadata goes nearly invisible. The same traffic converts at very different rates depending on the screenshots, where a top-quartile subscription listing turns 25 to 35 percent of viewers into installs while a poorly designed listing sits closer to 7 percent. The first three screenshots are the only ones an iOS buyer sees without scrolling, and the autoplay preview video runs silent in the top tile, so the brand cannot rely on sound or on frames the buyer never reaches. The work that turns a store impression into an install is screenshot strategy. Each frame answers one question about what the app does, in a sentence the buyer reads in under two seconds, with a single feature highlighted and a coral overlay pulling the eye to it. Duolingo's first screenshot reads Learn Spanish in 5 minutes a day, the second reads Practice with bite-size lessons, the third reads Free, fun, effective, so three frames carry three sentences, and a buyer who installs spends about eleven seconds on the listing.

    Ratings and reviews, the flywheel

    The single largest outside factor on conversion is the star rating. A 4.7-star app converts roughly 2.4 times the rate of a 3.4-star app on identical traffic, and the 2025 ranking algorithm weights review recency heavily, so an app with a six-month-old 4.8-star history loses ground to a competitor with a one-month-old 4.6-star history. The professional move is to ask for the rating right after a satisfying action, the way Duolingo waits for a seven-day streak before it asks, to answer negative reviews within about two days through the developer console, and to refresh the listing with a new build description every few weeks because the algorithm rewards continued activity.

    You cannot stuff every keyword into every field, because the algorithm penalizes keyword cannibalization and the buyer's eye penalizes a title that reads like a robot wrote it. So the craft is spreading coverage so each field carries one or two strong terms while the listing still reads as English to the human and indexes across thirty-plus query variants to the algorithm. You can see that trade-off directly by editing a draft listing and watching how the coverage moves.

    The listing wins the install. Keeping the user past the install is a different surface, and it starts the moment the app first opens.

    03 // engagement: push, onboarding, the paywall

    Engagement: Push, Onboarding, And The Paywall

    5. The opt-in rate on iOS for top-quartile apps runs 50 to 65 percent on first prompt and 35 to 45 percent on average; Android historically defaulted to opted-in but Android 13+ requires explicit opt-in matching iOS.
    Push notification A push notification is a short message the brand sends to the user's device that appears on the lock screen or notification tray even when the app is closed. On iOS the user must grant explicit permission before the brand can send anything; on Android the default behavior shifted in Android 13 to require the same explicit opt-in. The opt-in moment, the segmentation of who receives which message, and the cadence of sending are the three levers that decide whether push lifts retention or trains users to disable notifications altogether.

    The store listing wins the install, and the surfaces inside the app decide whether the user stays. The first of those surfaces is the push notification, a short message that arrives on the lock screen even when the app is closed. The biggest lever on push is the opt-in moment, because iOS shows the permission prompt only once in the life of an install. The brand gets one chance to ask, and the moment it picks decides whether half the audience can ever be reached or three-quarters can. Asking at first open is the wrong moment, because the user has done nothing inside the app yet and has no reason to trust the brand with their lock screen. Asking right after the user feels value is the right moment, which is why Duolingo asks after the first complete Spanish lesson, by which point "would you like reminders to keep going?" reads as a favor rather than an interruption. That post-delight prompt lifts opt-in by roughly 18 percentage points over the first-open prompt, a gap in audience size that compounds for the life of the install.

    Once enough users have opted in, the next lever is segmentation, because a generic message broadcast to everyone converts at about a third the rate of a message written for a specific cohort. A push that says "Come back to Lingua!" reaches a brand-new installer, a daily power user, and a lapsed subscriber whose payment failed, and it addresses none of their actual situations. The fix is to write distinct copy for cohorts the brand can name, such as new installs in the first 48 hours, users who lapsed seven days, paying subscribers, and high-value power users. A push that names what the user did ("You finished lesson 12 last Thursday; lesson 13 takes four minutes") earns the open that the broadcast loses, and that gap accumulates campaign after campaign into the difference between a retention curve that holds and one that crumbles. The last lever is the frequency cap, because the optimal cadence sits between three and five pushes per user per week. Below that, the brand leaves engagement on the table, and above roughly seven a week, unsubscribes climb and the retention curve flattens as users disable notifications, so the platform itself enforces a hard cap that protects the audience even when a manager schedules an over-eager promotion.

    Push reaches the user when they are away from the app, and in-app messaging reaches them while they are inside it, which gives it sharper context because the brand already knows what screen the user is on and what they just did. The in-app surface takes three forms. The onboarding modal is the welcome flow that walks a first-time user to the first valuable action. The contextual prompt is a banner that appears on a specific screen to enable a feature or ask a permission, shown at the moment the feature would actually help the user. The paywall is the screen that turns a free user into a paying subscriber. The principle that runs through all three is to delay friction until after value, which is why Duolingo collects the user's language, level, and goal across the first nine screens, then routes them into a 60-second lesson before it asks for a sign-up, because every screen that asks the user to type or commit loses some of them, and the brand that delivers a complete unit of value first keeps more of them through the ask.

    6. The paywall placement decision determines roughly 60 to 80 percent of the variance in subscription conversion in 2026 subscription apps; an A/B test on paywall position lifted Duolingo Super conversion by 21 percent in 2024.
    Paywall A paywall is the in-app screen where a free user is asked to start a paid subscription. Its placement decides roughly 60 to 80 percent of the variance in subscription conversion: placed at first open it kills retention by demanding payment before the user has experienced the product; placed at the moment a free-tier limit is hit (Duolingo: hearts ran out, language streak about to break) it converts at three to four times that rate because the user already values the thing being gated. Every 2026 subscription app runs at least three placement variants in continuous A/B testing.

    The paywall is the most A/B-tested screen in a subscription app, and where it sits decides most of the conversion. Placed at first open, it is catastrophic to retention and often kills more than half of installs before they see the product. Placed after onboarding, it is the conservative middle. Placed at the moment a free-tier limit is hit, the way Duolingo shows it when the user runs out of hearts, it converts at three to four times the first-open rate because the user has felt the value and just hit the wall. The brand that wins instruments at least three positions, runs the test for several weeks, and ships the winner.

    04 // measurement: who drove the install, after privacy

    Measurement: Who Drove The Install, After Privacy

    At the end of every quarter the chief marketing officer asks one question, which is which channel actually drove the installs the brand paid for. Answering it needs a measurement layer, and the structural problem on mobile is that Apple and Google do not provide one, because they deliberately do not tell a brand which ad drove which install. The reason is worth seeing clearly. On the open web an ad network drops a cookie when a user clicks, and the cookie rides along through the install, so the network can report back that this click became that purchase. Apple and Google put a privacy fence around the mobile user that strips that signal before any outside company can read it, which leaves the ad network selling the click and the brand buying the install looking at each other across a wall neither one can see through.

    That gap is what the Mobile Measurement Partner (MMP) industry fills.

    Mobile Measurement Partner (MMP) An MMP is the third-party referee that fills the install-attribution gap Apple and Google leave open. The brand's engineering team installs the MMP's software kit once, and from then on the MMP receives ad-click data from every paid channel the brand spends on, watches every install that registers in the app, and reports which click drove which install. AppsFlyer, Adjust, Singular, and Branch are the four firms that lead the 2026 market.

    An MMP is a neutral referee standing between the ad networks and the brand. It watches every click the brand pays for, watches every install that registers in the app, and records which click goes with which install. Four firms run that role today. AppsFlyer is the market leader and the default choice for most consumer-app growth teams in the United States. Adjust is the European leader, owned by the ad network AppLovin since 2021, and the common pick for apps that spend heavily on AppLovin's own network. Singular is the analytics-heavy option, preferred by brands that want install data joined to revenue and lifetime-value modeling in one warehouse. Branch began as the deep-linking specialist and grew into measurement, so it is the typical choice for apps whose growth runs on cross-channel links.

    An MMP is the neutral referee standing between the ad networks and the brand: it watches every click the brand pays for, watches every install that registers in the app, and records which click goes with which install.

    7. Attribution cost scales with the business, not the other way around. The student who ships a flashcard app with 200 monthly installs runs on Branch's free deep-linking tier, because at that volume the deterministic numbers can be eyeballed from Apple Search Ads' own dashboard and the MMP is needed mostly to route incoming campaign clicks to the right screen. A mid-market subscription app at 50,000 to 500,000 monthly installs pays roughly $2,000 to $15,000 a month, about one to three percent of paid-acquisition spend, while Duolingo at close to a billion cumulative installs runs an AppsFlyer enterprise contract because even a one-cent-per-install fee becomes meaningful spend at that scale. The same logic governs the modeling layer below: a brand with 26 weeks of varied spend across Apple Search Ads, Meta, and TikTok can fit a regression that estimates each channel drives roughly four, six, and five dollars of install spend respectively, while a brand with one week of data cannot pull the channels apart at all.

    One boundary is worth naming directly. Installing the MMP's software kit (SDK) is your engineering team's job, not yours. Your job is the dashboard. You log into the MMP, read the install and retention rows the kit has been quietly filling in, and decide which channel to spend more on next week. The dashboard shows one table, which for every install last week lists which network drove it, what it cost, and what the day-7 and day-30 retention curves look like by source. The MMP also pipes that signal back to the ad networks, which is how Meta, Google, and TikTok learn which clicks turned into installs that stayed.

    The MMP also carries one piece of plumbing the marketer specifies directly, which is where a campaign click sends the user. A user taps a TikTok ad for a flashcard app, gets sent to the App Store, installs, opens the app, and arrives on the exact deck the ad promised rather than the generic home screen. That is a deferred deep link doing its job, and you can see the plain version in two minutes. Open the iPhone Notes app, paste https://open.spotify.com/track/4cOdK2wGLETKBW3PvgPWqT, and tap it. The Spotify app opens straight to that song rather than its home page, because the link pointed at a specific screen. Your job is not to write the code that wires these links. Your job is to name the destination each campaign needs and to tap a draft link to test it before the campaign goes live.

    The MMP described above was built on an assumption that held for a decade, which is that every install could be tied to a specific click by a specific user, deterministically and almost in real time. In April 2021 that assumption broke. Apple released iOS 14.5 with a framework called App Tracking Transparency, abbreviated ATT, and it changed mobile measurement permanently. Every app that wanted to track a user across other companies' apps now had to show a system dialog on first open, reading roughly "App X would like permission to track your activity across other companies' apps and websites." The user could tap "Ask App Not to Track" or "Allow," and you have almost certainly tapped through this prompt yourself on Instagram or TikTok. Tapping "Ask App Not to Track" silently denies the app the device advertising identifier (IDFA), so the operating system returns a string of zeros whenever the app's measurement code asks for it. Tapping "Allow" returns the real identifier, and you become one of the roughly 25 percent the brand can still track deterministically across apps.

    The opt-in rate cratered, and the ecosystem reorganized around the constraint because there was no path around it. Apple did not roll the rule back or exempt the largest advertisers. The replacement is a privacy-preserving framework, and it carries a fundamental trade-off, because the brand gets less precision in exchange for the user getting more privacy.

    SKAdNetwork and AdAttributionKit (Apple's privacy-preserving attribution) Where the old model let an ad network see which click became which install in near-real time, Apple's privacy-preserving frameworks release attribution only in aggregate, delayed by 24 to 72 hours, with a limited set of preset values, and only once a campaign clears a privacy threshold of conversions. SKAdNetwork was the first version of this; AdAttributionKit, introduced at WWDC 2024 and upgraded in 2025, is its 2026 successor and works the same way. This is the floor every iOS measurement stack now builds on, paired with statistical modeling to fill the gap deterministic tracking used to cover.

    The degradation has four parts, and together they are the trade. First, the data comes only in aggregate, so Apple reports "this campaign produced fifty installs this week," never "this user installed." Second, it is delayed 24 to 72 hours, so the signal that once updated every few seconds now updates about once a day. Third, it carries only a small set of preset postback values the brand defines in advance to encode events like "reached the paywall" or "finished the first lesson." Fourth, it is withheld entirely until a campaign clears a privacy threshold, because releasing data on a campaign with three installs would let someone work backward to the individual user. You never read this raw. Your MMP collects every report automatically and shows it as install counts and conversion breakdowns in the same dashboard you already use.

    The picture the 2026 brand operates inside is two parallel modes running at once. For the roughly 25 percent of iOS users who allowed tracking, the old deterministic mode still works, and the dashboards look the way they did in 2020. For the other 75 percent, only the privacy-preserving mode is available, reporting at the campaign level but never the user level. The brands that kept growing through the change are the ones whose teams learned to run both modes side by side and to bridge the gap with media mix modeling, usually shortened to MMM. MMM does not see which click became which install. It sees the total install count and the total spend in each channel, week by week, then fits a curve through that history to estimate how much of each week's installs each channel drove. The estimate is a population-level average rather than a per-install attribution, which is exactly the privacy-preserving trade-off Apple's framework was built to enforce.

    Some brands skip the modeling because they own an identity layer that pre-dates the install. The Costco app sidesteps the ATT problem because its users overwhelmingly arrive logged in to a membership account Costco has owned for decades, which makes Costco's loyalty database the deterministic identity layer Costco controls itself rather than the one Apple took away. One more layer is coming. Once you connect your MMP through an MCP server, the audit pulls live install and retention data with no copy-paste step, and Section 14.6 shows the AI side of that.

    A brand that owns a login predating the install, such as a membership or loyalty account, can sidestep this entirely, because its own database becomes the deterministic identity layer Apple took away.

    05 // paid acquisition: buying installs

    Paid Acquisition: Buying Installs

    Measurement is the diagnostic that tells the brand whether its spend is working, and the next question is where that spend should go. Apple's privacy reset shrank the precision of the measurement window, but it did not shrink the paid channels themselves, so the brand still has many places to put a dollar. Two of those channels are unusual because they live inside the stores where the install actually happens, which means the user is already in the right room when the ad reaches them. Those two are Apple Search Ads and Google App Campaigns, and a brand that takes mobile growth seriously buys on both for a structural reason, because the buyer searching the App Store for a flashcard app has higher purchase intent than the buyer scrolling Instagram, so the install they produce is worth more per dollar.

    8. The same channel reads differently at three scales. A Greek-life chapter launching a co-branded drink app might bid on five keywords for a week with a $100 test budget, which at roughly four dollars per install buys about 25 installs, enough to learn whether the listing converts. A small subscription app might run a $5,000 monthly budget at the same rate, buying a little over a thousand installs to feed a funnel that ends in a $4.99-per-month subscription. Duolingo might run hundreds of thousands of dollars a month against the keyword spanish alone, paying near the top of the band because the auction is so competitive, and the math still works because its lifetime value per install is large enough to absorb the higher cost. Same dashboard, same mechanics, three scales, with the per-install price shifting on auction density rather than on anything the channel does differently.

    Apple Search Ads is the simpler channel to describe, because it works the way Google Search ads have worked for two decades. The brand picks the keywords it wants, sets a maximum bid, uploads a creative variant, and Apple's auction places the brand's promoted listing at the top of the results when a user types one of those keywords. A Duolingo bid on spanish puts a promoted Duolingo listing above the organic results when someone searches spanish, with a small "Ad" badge marking it as paid. Through 2026 Apple is widening this surface, rolling out additional promoted slots on the search page, which raises both the available inventory and the competition for the top spot.

    The benchmarks let the brand forecast what it would pay and get back, and the percentages matter only once you translate them into a real campaign. Start with the tap-through rate, the share of users who see the promoted listing and tap it. Apple Search Ads runs about 4 to 9 percent, so four to nine of every hundred users who searched the keyword tapped through. That is far above the typical web banner or feed ad near 0.5 percent, because the user searching "spanish" is already shopping for a Spanish app and the ad meets them inside that intent. The next number is the install conversion rate, the share who tapped through and then installed, which runs roughly 32 to 48 percent because the user has pre-filtered themselves twice. Compound the two and about 2 percent of users who see the ad install the app, which is the efficiency that justifies the channel even at premium pricing. The price for that efficiency is the cost per install, which in the United States now centers around four dollars and climbs higher on the most competitive keywords as the auction fills in.

    Google App Campaigns runs the cross-Google equivalent. The brand uploads one budget and a set of creative assets, and Google's machine learning allocates the spend across Search, YouTube, Google Play, and the Display Network, with installs as the target. The brand provides headlines, descriptions, video, and images, and Google's system assembles them. The 2026 cost per install runs a few dollars on Android for non-gaming categories, climbing for subscription apps where the lifetime value is higher.

    Once a brand saturates its bidding inside the two in-store channels, the rest of the paid mix is where it goes for more installs, and each added channel reaches users who are not currently searching for the product. Meta combines Facebook and Instagram into one ad surface and stays the largest non-Google channel for consumer apps. Its strength is targeting depth, because the brand can reach users by interest, by lookalike audiences that resemble its existing high-value installs, or by recent ad engagement. Meta fits a brand whose buyer is not searching but would respond to a well-targeted feed ad, which is why a Stanley reach campaign fits it and a flashcard app for one course does not. TikTok became the second-largest paid mobile channel after 2023, especially for Gen Z apps, and its strength is creative velocity, because the platform rewards short native video and lets a brand test twenty variants in a week. Snapchat runs at smaller scale and skews younger still, so it fits a brand targeting users 13 to 24 where Snap is the dominant messaging app, and a brand outside that profile is better off skipping it than spreading spend thin.

    The programmatic-network layer is structurally different. AppLovin, Unity Ads, and Mintegral aggregate ad inventory across hundreds of smaller apps and sell it as a single buy, which fits brands that need install volume at a controlled cost without much demographic precision. These networks have the worst attribution problem of any channel, because the ad shows up inside another app rather than inside a search result, which is exactly why the MMP layer from Section 14.4 matters most for programmatic spend.

    The student running a 200-install flashcard app skips most of these channels, because the audience is too small for any of them to reach efficiently. The floor of paid acquisition for a tightly scoped app is having a bid where users searching for the product already are, then graduating to Meta or TikTok only once the App Store search auction is saturated.

    07 // shipping an app

    Shipping An App: From Idea To The Store

    You can take a working mobile app from an idea to the App Store in a weekend, for roughly $124 in store fees plus a builder subscription. The number matters less than what running the loop once does to a marketer's judgment. The marketer who has shipped an app reads every benchmark in this chapter differently, because the install graph, the retention curve, and the review queue stop being abstractions and become a system she has operated from the inside. The fee is fixed, at $99 a year for the Apple Developer Program and a one-time $25 for the Google Play Console.

    The first fork is whether to build without code or with it. The no-code builder path is the fastest route to a published app, and the four tools worth knowing each answer a different question. Pick Glide if your data already lives in a spreadsheet, Bubble if the app needs full custom logic, FlutterFlow if you want a real native binary with the option to drop into code later, and Adalo for a marketplace-style app on flat pricing. The 2026 difference is that each builder layered an AI assistant on top, so the student types a request like "generate a screen showing a list of decks with a search bar and a New Deck button" and gets a working draft to refine instead of a blank canvas.

    One 2026 caveat shapes the choice. Apple began enforcing Guideline 2.5.2 in March 2026, blocking a wave of AI prompt-to-app tools that let users generate and run software inside the host app. The rule is not aimed at AI-built apps as a category, because it targets any app that runs code which alters its own behavior. The four builders above predate that wave, compile to a real native binary, and stay compliant, which is why they remain the safe 2026 choices for store distribution.

    The coded path is for the feature a visual editor cannot model. In 2026 it looks almost nothing like it did two years earlier, because two AI development tools turned the writing-the-code half of the work into a stream of plain-English instructions. The student uses Cursor for line-level work where a human confirms each suggestion, and Claude Code for delegated multi-file work where naming an outcome and walking away is cleaner. The cross-platform framework underneath is either React Native or Flutter, both of which have crossed the good-enough line for non-game consumer apps, and either choice is defensible.

    10. On the coded path, the one rule worth getting right on day one is data isolation, where each user should only ever read their own rows. In a backend like Supabase that is a single policy statement applied to every table the user owns. A mistake here leaks one user's data to another, so handling it on the first day makes security a property of the schema rather than a feature bolted on later.

    Both paths end at the same door. App Store Connect is where the listing goes live, and submission is a fixed sequence of forms: a bundle ID, the listing metadata, screenshots at the required device sizes, a privacy questionnaire, an age rating, a TestFlight beta, and the submit button. The two screens that catch most first-timers are the screenshots, which demand exact pixel dimensions for every required device, and the privacy questionnaire, which forces an honest inventory of every category of user data the app touches. Walking the sequence once turns shipping the app into a concrete checklist, which is what the Job Aid at the end of the chapter lays out screen by screen.

    The honest 2026 reality of the review queue is that it has slowed. The flood of AI-built submissions pushed App Store volume up sharply through 2025 and into 2026, and a first submission now usually takes a few days rather than the same-day turnaround of two years earlier, with longer waits when volume spikes. Updates to an app already approved clear faster than a first submission. The practical lesson for the student shipping a flashcard app is to submit with a few days of slack before the deadline rather than counting on an overnight approval.

    Wiring the engagement surface is the last build step. A push platform like OneSignal handles the permission prompt, the segments, and the A/B test, and the setup is the same short loop every time: connect the platform keys, install the SDK, send the first push to your own phone to confirm it works, then segment and schedule. Once the wiring is confirmed, the A/B test the chapter described earlier is a few clicks in the composer, not a line of code.

    Put the pieces on a calendar and the weekend is real. Friday is the brief, one paragraph that locks the scope so the Saturday self does not feature-creep. Saturday is the build in a no-code editor plus the push wiring, with the first test push sent to the student's own phone by evening. Sunday is the listing, the screenshots, the privacy answers, and the submit button. The constraint across the weekend is decision-making rather than coding throughput, because the AI assistant and the no-code editor absorb the typing-heavy work while the student decides what to cut. A few weeks after launch the app has a few hundred classmates installed, a real retention curve in the dashboard, and a line on the student's resume.

    Shipping the listing is the baseline the chapter has been building toward. The frontier moves in the next section change what the listing has to do, as the visual surfaces get tested the way text keywords already are and the app starts answering an assistant's request without the user ever opening it.

    08 // what's next: visual aso, ai creative, agent-controlled apps

    What's Next: Visual ASO, AI Creative, And Agent-Controlled Apps

    The weekend deployment in the last section ran on the 2026 playbook as it stands today. A student who ships an app this semester will be optimizing a different channel in two years, so it helps to see where the channel is heading. Three frontier moves are reshaping mobile marketing through 2026, and each one extends a method the chapter already taught onto a surface the channel could not reach cleanly a year ago.

    The first move is visual ASO. The text playbook from Section 14.2 treats the words of the listing as the variables to tune. Visual ASO treats the pictures the same way. A marketer tests the icon, the first screenshot, and the palette across all five frames, at the scale that mature text-ASO programs have run for years. The method does not change, only the lever. Apple's 2025 product-page-optimization tool opened icon and first-screenshot testing to every developer, which removed the one barrier that had kept this work inside the largest growth teams. The flashcard launch from the weekend story is too small for this today, while a Duolingo or Stanley campaign is exactly the scale at which visual ASO becomes the obvious next lever.

    11. Teams running 30 to 100 visual variants a quarter report 12 to 28 percent install-conversion lifts on listings whose text ASO has already maxed out, a sizable second wave once the words run out of headroom.

    The second move is AI-generated creative for the visual test. Production cost is what used to cap visual ASO, because a human designer charges hundreds of dollars and weeks of turnaround per screenshot round. Image-generation models drop that cost to cents and seconds, so a marketer can produce dozens of variants without hiring a designer for each one. Apple's review still prohibits screenshots that misrepresent the real product, so the practical 2026 pattern is hybrid. You use the AI tool for the supporting graphics, the background art and the badge and the colored callout, while the actual UI shot stays literal, captured from a real device running the real app. The lift comes from the framing around the screen, not from a faked screen.

    12. A 2025 Phiture survey of 240 growth teams found 38 percent had already shipped at least one AI-generated screenshot, the median team running three or four AI variants alongside three or four human ones per test cycle.

    The third move changes the question, because the listing stops being the path the user takes. A growing class of apps can be operated by an AI assistant on the user's behalf, through Apple's App Intents or Google's App Actions. The user never sees the listing because the user never opens the app.

    A 2026 user might tell a Siri- or Gemini-powered assistant book me a flight to Stillwater for the OSU homecoming game, and the assistant calls an exposed intent on the airline's app, prefills the dates and the departure airport, and returns the result without the UI ever opening. The implication is structural, not tactical. The install graph now rewards apps whose actions are well-named and discoverable to the assistant layer, while an app that ships without exposing its core actions becomes invisible to this new traffic source. A growth team that wins in 2027 instruments App Intents the way the team that won in 2017 instrumented deep links, which means early and thoroughly, on the assumption that the channel goes dark for brands that ship late.

    The frontier moves all sit on top of the same install graph the chapter has been reading. The next chapter asks what happens to that graph when the app crosses a border.

    Looking Ahead

    The Duolingo PM from the opener is still watching the same install graph, and now she reads it as a five-stage loop. She optimizes the listing so learners find it and install, she wins the engagement surface with the right push and paywall placement, she measures who drove each install after privacy broke deterministic tracking, she buys installs first where intent is highest, and she ships the next thing in a weekend when she needs to test an idea. The two-thirds of learners who arrived through the App Store door, not the website the team had optimized for a decade, came through every one of those stages.

    The next chapter takes up the layer that sits on top of every install graph the chapter just walked. A push that goes out at 8 a.m. Eastern arrives at 1 a.m. in Mumbai and 9 p.m. in Sydney, and the keyword index she tuned for the US storefront may not rank in Brazil, Germany, or Japan, where the store carries different localizations and the cultural reading of an icon changes the conversion math. Chapter 15 works through the standardize-or-adapt decision and the frameworks marketers use to make it, asking which parts of the listing, the push copy, the paywall, and the onboarding travel across a border without change and which have to be rebuilt for the buyer in each market.

    Job Aid. The four-layer mobile-growth audit (and how to ship the app behind it). This worksheet is the chapter’s operating sequence pulled out of the spine so you can run it on any app, your own or a competitor’s. Work top to bottom. Each block is a move the chapter taught, and the last three blocks are what it takes to put a new app on the shelf in the first place.

    1. Optimize the listing (the find-and-convert layer).

    Pick the app’s three target keywords by scoring candidates on traffic, difficulty, and relevance, and spread them across the title (about 30 percent of the indexing weight), the subtitle (about 25 percent), and the hidden keyword field (about 20 percent), using 30 to 40 long-tail variants instead of stuffing one field. Then read the first three screenshots as a stranger would, because the visuals carry roughly 60 percent of the install decision, and request a rating only after the user finishes something satisfying, because a 4.7-star listing converts about 2.4 times a 3.4-star one.

    2. Win the engagement surface (the retention layer).

    Ask for push permission after the user feels value, not at first open, which lifts opt-in by roughly 18 points and only gets one shot per install. Cap notifications at three to five a week at peak and segment by cohort so the message fits where the user is. Place the paywall at the moment the user hits a limit, because the at-limit paywall converts three to four times the first-open one.

    3. Measure who drove the install, after privacy.

    Stand up a mobile measurement partner (AppsFlyer, Adjust, Singular, or Branch) as the referee Apple and Google left out, remembering that installing the SDK is engineering’s job while the dashboard is yours. Read attribution in two parallel modes: deterministic for the roughly 25 percent who opted into tracking, and the aggregated, delayed, threshold-withheld postbacks plus media mix modeling for the rest. Once you connect the measurement partner through MCP, an AI audit can read live install and retention data with no manual export.

    4. Buy installs where intent is highest.

    Start with the in-store channels, Apple Search Ads and Google App Campaigns, because the buyer is already shopping in the right room and the tap-through runs 4 to 9 percent against roughly 0.5 percent on a web banner. Layer Meta, TikTok, and programmatic on top once the in-store auction saturates, matching each channel to its strength (Meta for targeting depth, TikTok for creative velocity with younger users).

    5. Pick the build path (when you are shipping the app, not auditing it).

    Choose no-code when the visual editor can model the app: Glide when a spreadsheet sits behind the data, Bubble for full custom logic, FlutterFlow for a native binary with a code-eject option, Adalo for marketplace-style apps. Choose the coded path (React Native or Flutter) only for features the editor cannot reach, and let Cursor and Claude Code do the typing-heavy work while you decide. Either way, an AI assistant generates the bulk of the components from your description.

    6. Walk the eight App Store Connect screens.

    Submission is a fixed sequence of forms, and the screenshots and the privacy questionnaire catch most first-timers. In order: enroll in the Apple Developer Program, create the app record, fill the metadata, upload screenshots and the preview, answer the privacy nutrition labels, set the age rating and pricing, push a TestFlight build, then submit. App Store Connect is the door, and the Google Play Console runs a close parallel. Plan for review to take a few days in 2026, sometimes longer when submission volume spikes, and remember that updates clear faster than a first submission, so submit with slack rather than counting on a same-day approval.

    7. Wire the first push and confirm it.

    Connect a push platform (OneSignal is a common free starting point), send the first notification to your own phone to confirm the plumbing works, then build your cohort segments before you send to real users. Hold a control group out of any push test so you can read the lift against users who got nothing.

    Apply What You Have Learned

    Do this, about ten minutes

    Audit a real app the way a growth team would

    Pick an app you actually use, or an app idea you would genuinely ship, then run the chapter's machinery on it in Claude or ChatGPT one move at a time.

    1. Find its three target keywords.

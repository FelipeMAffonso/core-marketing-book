    Marketing is reaction to a moving world. You cannot build a strategy on a snapshot.

    02 // micro vs macro

    Micro and macro environment

    The marketing environment splits into two layers, and the split is about who you can argue with: the people and companies a brand deals with face to face, and the big forces that push on every brand at once where no single brand gets a say.

    The marketing environment splits into two layers, and the split is about who you can argue with: the people and companies a brand deals with face to face, and the big forces that push on every brand at once where no one gets a say.

    Microenvironment The actors a brand deals with directly: customers, the company itself, collaborators (channel partners, suppliers, agencies), competitors, and publics (regulators, media, advocacy groups). These are the parties a brand can call, sign a contract with, take to court, or buy lunch for. The brand negotiates with them, and they negotiate back.
    Macroenvironment The forces that push on every brand at the same time and that no single brand can negotiate with: political, economic, sociocultural, technological, environmental, and legal. A marketing team can react sooner or later, but it cannot bargain with a recession, a change in the public mood, or a rule pending in Brussels.

    One handy way to scan the first layer is the 5C framework, five words that start with C. Customers are the people you serve, Company is what you are actually able to do, Collaborators are the suppliers and agencies that help you reach buyers, and Competitors are everyone else chasing the same buyer. Context is the whole macro layer wrapped around the other four as the outer ring.

    03 // pestel

    PESTEL: the six macro forces

    Six forces sit outside your brand, and you cannot call any of them and ask for a better deal. You can sign your supplier and email your agency, but you cannot phone a tariff, a recession, or a new privacy law into changing its mind. All you can do is read each force early and move while the cheap options are still on the table. PESTEL is the checklist that keeps you from missing one. It stands for Political, Economic, Sociocultural, Technological, Environmental, and Legal, and the point is the habit: walk all six on a regular clock so none becomes the blind spot that catches you flat.

    Legal EU AI Act: four enforcement waves, not one switch

    The Act enforces across four waves, each a different binding obligation. The first took effect 2 February 2025, when the Article 5 prohibitions on unacceptable-risk uses bound firms under a ceiling of 7 percent of worldwide turnover or 35 million euros. The second, 2 August 2025, made providers of General-Purpose AI models document their training data and publish technical specifications. The third, 2 August 2026, brings the full high-risk regime plus the Article 50 transparency rules, where the deepfake-disclosure and AI-content-labeling obligations that affect marketing campaigns bind. The fourth runs to 2 August 2027 for high-risk systems in regulated products. A team that read the 2024 passage as a single 2026 problem misread the structure, because the third wave is written against creative pipelines that have to be in production six months before the August date.

    08 // failure modes

    Five failure modes

    Even a brand that owns every framework in this chapter can still miss the shift. The misses fall into five shapes, and each one is a good read of the world gone stale. Naming them is the cheapest way to catch yourself before the loss arrives.

    Snapshot strategy You read the world once, build a plan on that single photo, and defend the plan when the world moves. Tropicana's 2009 redesign dropped the orange-with-a-straw image; sales fell 20% in two months, a $35M loss, and the box was back within six weeks. The fix is to test any change against real buyer reactions before you ship it, and to keep a way to undo it fast.
    Ignoring weak signals You see an early sign, but it moves no number this quarter, so you file it as noise. Blockbuster turned down the chance to buy Netflix in 2000 and kept building stores; by 2007 the chance was gone. Kodak read its own future early and looked away. The fix is a standing quarterly habit: name three signals that could reshape the category in eighteen months, even the ones easy to wave off today.
    Over-reading short-term noise This is the opposite mistake. You treat a one-week storm as a long-arc force and rebuild the strategy around it. Bud Light's reaction to a 2023 partnership controversy turned a passing event into a lasting loss by over-correcting. The fix is to sort weak signals (long-arc, still moving in a year) from noise (one cycle, gone inside ninety days), and to change strategy only for the first kind.
    Judging a new force by old rules You measure a new thing with the test that fit the old world, and it fails, so you dismiss it. Nokia ran the 2007 iPhone through its own drop test, decided it was too fragile, and was right about a 2005 phone and wrong about what a phone had just become. The fix is to write down, for every category-shifting force, which of your own rules quietly assume the world that is being replaced.
    Vendor-concentration blindness You never check which outside software your revenue is sitting on. A faulty CrowdStrike update on 19 July 2024 crashed about 8.5 million Windows machines worldwide; Delta lost $500M over five days because its crew system could not reseat pilots once the hosts came back. The force was technological; the failure was nobody auditing it. The fix is a quarterly check listing every outside dependency, what happens when it breaks, and the written fallback.

    1. Ogilvy set down the Hathaway craft in Ogilvy on Advertising (1983). The brand-image principles still hold, although the channel economics that once made them affordable have collapsed.

    2.     Mental availability matters because buyers do not shop the way the old theory assumed. Pre-2010 advertising treated the buyer as a careful deliberator weighing attributes, the way an economics textbook describes a rational consumer. The panel data show the opposite. Most buyers, in most categories, match against whatever brand comes to mind in a few seconds, and the brand that comes to mind first is the one whose memory links got refreshed most recently and most often. Two literatures back this up. The elaboration likelihood model holds that persuasion runs through a central route when the buyer thinks hard and a peripheral route of cues and repetition when she does not. Daniel Kahneman's System 1, the fast and automatic mode of thought, names the same split from another angle, and the useful fact is that almost every buyer, in almost every routine purchase, is on the peripheral route almost all the time.

    The first axis is awareness: what does the buyer know right now? It runs up five rungs. Most Aware buyers know the brand and the price and need only an offer. Product-Aware buyers know the product but doubt how well it works. Solution-Aware buyers want the outcome but do not yet know a product delivers it.     03 // stepps: what gets shared

    STEPPS: What Gets Shared

    An ad refreshes the buyer's memory once. A buyer who repeats that ad to a friend refreshes her own memory and the friend's at the same time, and the brand pays nothing extra for the second one. That gap is why word-of-mouth is worth so much more per impression than a paid ad. The numbers are large. A buyer's recommendation produces at least ten times the per-impression sales lift of traditional advertising, a single new-customer restaurant recommendation lifts sales by roughly $200, and the gap between a five-star and a one-star review sells about twenty more books per review. So the question a brief used to ask, what claim wins the buyer?, gives way to a sharper one: what does the buyer want to be seen sharing with her friends? A claim can be perfectly true and never travel, because the buyer has no reason to repeat it. STEPPS is the checklist for answering the newer question.

    8. The framework gathers a decade of research on why things spread. Only about 7% of word-of-mouth happens online, so most of a brand's recommendations live in conversations no analytics tool will ever see.

    STEPPS names six independent levers that predict whether content spreads. Social currency asks whether sharing it makes the sender look smart or in-the-know. Triggers asks what everyday cue brings the brand back to mind. Emotion asks whether it stirs a strong feeling. Public asks whether people can see others using it. Practical value asks whether the buyer can forward it to genuinely help a friend. Stories asks whether the brand rides inside a narrative people want to retell. The levers are independent, and a brand almost never wins on all six. The honest answer is usually one or two it can actually pull, which is fewer than the brief asks for.

    The emotion lever has a finding sharper than the trade-press version, and it surprises most students. A six-month study tracked which of roughly 7,000 New York Times articles made the most-emailed list, holding the topic, length, and section constant and coding the dominant feeling in each one. Awe lifted sharing by 30 percent. Anger lifted it by 34 percent. Anxiety lifted it by 21 percent. Sadness cut sharing by 16 percent. The thing that drives sharing is not whether the feeling is positive or negative. It is whether the feeling is high-arousal or low-arousal. Awe, anger, and anxiety all leave the body keyed up, so they all push the buyer to talk, while sadness and contentment settle the body down and quiet it. A sad ad can genuinely move a buyer, which is why some charities win on sad creative, but it will not move her to share. A campaign that needs people to pass it along has to work in an awe, anger, or anxiety register instead.

    The 2023 Grimace Shake shows the rule at full scale. In June 2023 McDonald's released a purple birthday shake to mark the character Grimace's 52nd birthday, which looked like an ordinary brand promotion. Within two weeks an unplanned horror trend took over TikTok, where users filmed themselves drinking the shake and then cut to mock-gruesome scenes as if Grimace had attacked them. It sits alongside STEPPS rather than replacing it. STEPPS tells you what content to engineer; earned-trend riding tells you what to do when the audience does the engineering for you.

    SUCCES and the attention problem: what sticks, and whether anyone is looking

    STEPPS asks what gets shared. The companion question is what sticks, because a buyer cannot repeat an ad she cannot remember. The SUCCES framework names the six traits that turn a message the buyer merely hears into one she can repeat a week later in her own words. A working brand needs both halves. JFK's 1961 man on the moon line sticks without being shared for status, while Friends Don't Let Friends Drive Drunk gets shared on its triggers but carries its weight through concreteness and story.

    SUCCES (Heath & Heath 2007) Six traits that make a message lodge in memory. Simple means one core idea, kept compact. Unexpected means it breaks the buyer's guessing machine, then fills the gap. Concrete means sensory, examinable, human-scale. Credible means the buyer can test it for herself, not just trust the speaker. Emotional means the buyer cares about it, not just analyzes it. Stories means the message travels inside a narrative she wants to retell.

    The clearest worked case is the Center for Science in the Public Interest's 1992 reframing of movie-theater popcorn, because it isolates the concreteness lever from all the others. CSPI measured a medium AMC popcorn at 37 grams of saturated fat. As a raw number, 37 grams meant nothing, because almost no one in the audience carries an internal benchmark for what that does to a body. So the press team rewrote the same fact as a picture, telling readers a medium popcorn held more artery-clogging fat than a bacon-and-eggs breakfast, a Big Mac and fries, and a steak dinner combined. The fact never changed. What changed was the reader's ability to see it, because the comparison anchored to meals she already pictured. Headlines ran Popcorn Gets an R Rating, and within months AMC, United Artists, and Loews each dropped coconut oil. The same lever drives Patagonia's Don't Buy This Jacket in Section 16.6, where the cost of a jacket became 135 liters of water and 20 pounds of carbon dioxide, a number the reader could hold in her head.

A message that scores well on most of the six traits and zero on one usually fails at the step that trait controls, which is why the framework works best as a checklist a writer runs before the brief leaves her desk. The discipline is to score the draft honestly on each trait and rewrite the one that scores lowest, because a single weak trait is enough to keep the message from lodging.

    10. Persuasion is a separate question from stickiness. Seven principles reliably shift behavior across categories: reciprocity, commitment and consistency, social proof, authority, liking, scarcity, and unity. Two experiments produced bigger lifts than most marketers expect. Mints left with a personalized server comment lifted tips 23 percent through reciprocity, and a hotel card naming the share of prior guests who reused their towels lifted reuse 49 percent over the standard appeal through social proof. The working diagnostic is to pick the one load-bearing principle for an ad before defending the brief, because the autopilot from Section 16.1 cannot read seven recognition signals in a second and a half.

    08 // what goes wrong

    What Goes Wrong

    Most ads do not fail because someone made a bad ad. They fail in a handful of predictable ways, and the same five show up across the whole record.

    Aiming to build when refresh is the right move. The brief asks for a campaign that creates a new memory, but the brand already owns one the new work ignores. The fix is to audit the brand's existing distinctive assets first and ask whether the memory you want is genuinely new, or just the old one wearing new clothes.
    Generic visuals any competitor could run. The ad is well shot and well lit, and it looks like every other ad in the category. Tropicana 2009 is the teaching case, since a glass of orange juice could belong to any brand. Test the ad with the brand name removed, and if buyers cannot tell whose it is, the money is gone.
    The wrong awareness level. The headline pitches a Most-Aware buyer when the audience has never heard of the category, or it explains the category to a buyer at checkout who only needs the offer. Name the awareness rung before you write the headline, and remember the same buyer sits at different rungs in different channels in the same week.
    Low-arousal emotion when the goal is sharing. The brief wants the ad shared, and the creative sits in sad or gentle territory. Sadness cuts sharing by about sixteen percent. Sad and calm ads can work for paid brand refresh, but an earned-media ad has to move to awe, anger, anxiety, or amusement.
    Product-not-brand focus, the eighty-four percent failure. The ad shows the product and closes on a feature, so the buyer remembers the feature and forgets the brand. The Ehrenberg-Bass study put the number on it, with about eighty-four percent of TV impressions leaving no correctly branded memory. Lead with the distinctive asset before the product appears, and keep it on screen until the brand registers, even if the product gets less time.

    Job Aid. The one-page promo audit. Run this on any campaign brief before it goes into production. Each step takes a few minutes, and the order matters.

    1. Awareness rung.

    Write down the rung the headline assumes, whether Unaware, Problem-Aware, Solution-Aware, Product-Aware, or Most-Aware, and the rung the target audience is actually on. If they differ, fix the headline before anything else.

    2. Sophistication stage.

    Note how worn-out the category's claims are. A first-in-market claim and a fifth-generation category need different copy, even for the same product.

    3. STEPPS score.

    Rate the work on social currency, triggers, emotion, public visibility, practical value, and stories, and circle the lowest lever. If the goal is sharing, confirm the emotion is high-arousal, such as awe, anger, anxiety, or amusement, rather than sad or calm.

    4. SUCCES check.

    Confirm the core message is simple, unexpected, concrete, credible, and emotional, and that it carries a story. Then ask the blunter question: in the second or two of attention this will actually get, is a distinctive asset on screen?

    5. Distinctive asset.

    Name the one asset the work should refresh, and check that it appears before the product does and stays on screen until the brand registers. Test the ad with the brand name removed, and if no one can tell whose it is, stop.

    6. Build or refresh.

    Decide honestly which one the campaign is doing, and default to refresh.

    7. Brand-to-activation split.

    Confirm the plan sits near 60/40, adjusted for the category, and that nothing in-quarter has quietly renegotiated it.

    8. If it is generative.

    Attach the brand's reference images on every prompt, keep a person in the loop to reject off-brand variants, and sign and watermark whatever ships. If a brief fails any single step, the spend on that campaign is at risk, so fix the failing step before the money goes out.

    Key Takeaways

    • Advertising refreshes memory the brand already owns. Its job is to rehearse the memory structures a brand already carries rather than to argue the brand is better, because most buyers buy on autopilot in about two seconds.
    • A brand grows on mental and physical availability. Coming to mind at the cue is mental availability, being buyable at the moment is physical availability, so the highest-return move is rehearsing memory the brand owns instead of building new memory from scratch.
    • A headline has to match the buyer and the category. It has to match what the buyer already knows, which is her awareness rung, and how worn-out the category is, which is its sophistication stage, or it wastes the spend no matter how much media weight sits behind it.
    • Shared content refreshes two memories at no extra cost. Six STEPPS levers predict whether it travels, and high-arousal emotion such as awe, anger, or anxiety moves people to share while low-arousal sadness does not.
    • The message has to fit a second or two of attention. Most digital impressions get that long, which is why a visible distinctive asset on screen matters more than the strategy deck behind it.
    • Buyers recognize a brand by its distinctive asset. They recognize it far more than by any argued difference, so a brand audits each asset on fame and uniqueness, defends the strong ones, and never rebrands a winning asset just to mark a new director's arrival.
    • AI drops production cost to cents but collapses to the average without a brand reference. A raw prompt returns generic output, and the brand-to-activation budget split near 60/40 is the survival floor rather than a matter of taste.

    Check Your Understanding

    Answer each in your head, then click to check.

    1. The Hathaway eyepatch sold shirts for twenty-five years and never once argued the shirt was better made. What was the eyepatch doing, and what does that tell you about advertising's real job?

    The eyepatch was the brand's distinctive asset, a nonverbal cue buyers recognized Hathaway by before any product claim. Advertising's job is to refresh the memory the brand already owns, not to win an argument about quality. A reader in 1973 knew Hathaway by the eyepatch, not by a feature.

    2. An Ehrenberg-Bass study found forty percent of viewers recognized a recent ad, and forty percent of those credited it to the right brand. What share of the ad money produced a branded memory, and why does it matter?

    About sixteen percent, since forty percent of forty percent is sixteen, so roughly eighty-four percent of the TV ad money refreshed nothing. The ads were not bad, but they argued when they should have reminded, or led with the product instead of the brand. The fix is to lead with the distinctive asset before the product appears.

    3. Liquid Death's 2017 ads and its 2024 Costco-endcap offer reached the same brand's buyers with completely different headlines. Why?

    In 2017 the audience was Unaware, so the ads built identity around an attitude rather than a feature. By 2024 the buyer was Most-Aware and already knew the can, so a price-and-availability hook worked. The headline has to match the buyer's awareness rung, and the same buyer sits at different rungs in different channels.

    4. Berger and Milkman found awe lifted sharing thirty percent and anger thirty-four percent, but sadness cut it sixteen percent. What is the real variable, and what does it mean for an ad meant to be shared?

    The variable is arousal, not whether the feeling is positive or negative. High-arousal emotions such as awe, anger, and anxiety travel, while low-arousal ones such as sadness do not. A sad ad can move a buyer emotionally without moving her to share, so an earned-media campaign needs an awe, anger, or anxiety register.

    5. Pepsi paid about thirty million dollars to redesign Tropicana's packaging and lost twenty percent of sales in two months. The new package was not ugly. What went wrong?

    It was unfamiliar. Buyers were pattern-matching against the orange-with-a-straw image, a thirteen-year distinctive asset, and the new generic glass failed the match, so they reached for Florida's Natural or Minute Maid because those still looked like orange juice. Never destroy a winning distinctive asset, and never rebrand one just to mark a director's arrival.

    6. A marketer asks an AI “how do I make this ad better?” and gets generic advice. What should she ask instead, and why does the answer change?

    She should ask a sharp, situational question, such as “for this brand's current campaign, name the awareness rung it is pitched at, its weakest STEPPS lever, and the one distinctive asset it should refresh, using its current positioning.” That returns an anchored, buildable audit, especially when the model searches the live web for the brand's current work. The answer's quality follows the question's quality, so the skill is not asking AI, it is knowing what to ask.

    7. AI can now make a thirty-second video for a few hundred dollars instead of a quarter-million. So why does a brand still need a reference image or a fine-tuned model?

    Because every model drifts toward the average of its training data, so a raw prompt gives generic output any brand could have made. A “ketchup bottle” prompt returns a Heinz-shaped bottle only because Heinz dominates the training data, while a smaller brand gets a generic bottle. Brand-consistent work needs reference-image conditioning on every prompt or a per-brand fine-tune to escape the average, plus a consent-and-watermark step.

    8. A junior marketer's activation dashboard refreshes overnight and shows click-through to two decimals; the brand-building line is one number measured nine months ago. The finance chief asks why the slower-number budget is bigger. What is the trap, and how does she get out of it?

    This is the effectiveness trap, where the budget drifts to whatever metric scores fastest even though that metric is not the one that earns long-term profit, and the cuts drop reach below the brand-building floor until the long-term effect zeroes.

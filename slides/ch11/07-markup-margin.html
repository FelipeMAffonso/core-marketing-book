A $12 shirt sold at $24 is a 100% markup but only a 50% margin, so the channel is priced backward, not forward.

Markup · base is cost 100%

($24 − $12) ÷ $12

Measures the $12 spread against what the unit cost. The base is smaller, so markup can run past 100%.

Margin · base is price 50%

($24 − $12) ÷ $24

Measures the same $12 against the price. The base always includes the cost, so margin never reaches 100%.

You start from the shelf price the buyer will pay, then feed each tier its margin on the way down, so a partner who quotes markup while you assume margin ends up fighting over a price neither of you computed.

Source: Kotler, Keller & Chernev, Marketing Management 16e (2022), distribution chapter

Part 1

01

Price leverage

Why a one-percent move on the price line lifts profit far more than the same move on any other line of the income statement.

Margin and markup

Three dollars of profit yield two different ratios, because the two formulas use different denominators.

One sale · $7 cost + $3 profit = $10 revenue

Why the gap

  • Margin reads against revenue
  • Markup reads against cost
  • Same dollars, two denominators

Where it matters

  • Channel deals quote markup
  • P&L statements report margin
  • Confusion misstates channel profitability

Source: cost-accounting convention; Marn & Rosiello, HBR (1992) on the channel-margin error.

Asymmetric leverage

At a three-percent margin, a one-percent price move lifts profit by about thirty-three percent.

Profit lift from a 1% move · $100 revenue, $97 cost

Why the gap

  • A price increase incurs no marginal cost
  • Each additional unit incurs additional variable cost
  • Volume requires roughly three times the move to match

When it matters most

  • Thin-margin businesses
  • Subscription and software
  • Anything with near-zero marginal cost

Source: Marn & Rosiello, HBR (1992), three-percent net-margin illustration.

Netflix, 2010 to 2024

Six price increases moved the US standard plan from $7.99 to $15.99, because the marginal cost of one additional viewer is near zero.

Netflix US standard-plan monthly price

Source: Netflix US public price history, 2010 to 2024, standard HD plan.

A price increase produces a larger profit lift than an equal-percent volume increase, because price incurs no marginal cost.

The four pricing approaches

The four approaches (cost-plus, competitor-match, value-based, and dynamic) each read a different line of the value stick, and each one fails when the firm misreads its anchor line.

The four pricing approaches

Each approach anchors price on a different element of the value stick.

Cost-plus, competitor-match, value-based, dynamic

Source: synthesizes Bertini & Koenigsberg, HBR (2024); Oberholzer-Gee (2021); 2025 IJRM dynamic-pricing consensus.

Cost-plus

Price is set as unit cost plus a chosen markup, anchored on the cost floor of the value stick.

Food-truck burrito · markup of 40 percent on unit cost

When it fits

  • WTP cannot be measured reliably
  • Unit-cost data is well-defined
  • Buyers do not compare alternatives

Where it fails

  • WTP exceeds the cost-plus price
  • Differentiation goes uncaptured
  • A competitor captures the surplus

Source: Marn & Rosiello, HBR (1992); standard cost-accounting formulation.

Competitor-match

Price is set within the competitive band the buyer is already comparing against.

Four gas stations on one corner

When the frame holds

  • Commodity categories
  • Posted-board pricing
  • Side-by-side comparison

Where matching backfires

  • Differentiated firms train buyers down
  • Price wars drop below sustainable floor
  • Following misses the chance to re-anchor

Source: 24-hour gas-station survey, May 2025; numbers illustrative of the corner-cluster pattern.

Value-based

Price is set as a function of the buyer's willingness-to-pay, built from the competitor reference upward.

Economic value to the customer (EVC) walk · one heavy-duty truck

Source: Forbis & Mehta, Industrial Marketing Management (1981); Anderson, Jain & Chintagunta, JBBM (1993).

Dynamic pricing

The same seat clears at four prices across one week, because each booking window faces a different WTP curve.

One DFW to LGA seat · posted price by day before departure

Part 4

04

Pricing psychology

How buyer evaluation departs from purely numerical comparison. Three named effects (decoy, compromise, charm) carry the bulk of the chapter's experimental evidence.

The menu around a price does work the price itself does not.

Three effects we will name in Part 4

The decoy effect

A dominated option pulls choice toward the option that dominates it.

Cinema popcorn · share of buyers by menu

The setup

  • Two strong options, one trade-off
  • A dominated third option
  • Buyer selects the option that dominates the decoy

Where you see it

  • Cinema popcorn pricing
  • SaaS three-tier pricing
  • Apple storage tiers

Source: Huber, Payne & Puto, JCR (1982); asymmetric-dominance experimental paradigm.

The compromise effect

Three ordered tiers shift the modal choice toward the median option.

Spotify US plans · market share by tier

The pull

  • The bottom tier signals low quality
  • The top tier signals overspend
  • The middle tier minimizes both risks

Where you see it

  • SaaS three-tier pricing
  • Coffee shop sizes
  • Mid-tier wine selection

Source: Simonson, JCR (1989); Spotify share figures representative of post-2022 US market mix.

Charm pricing

The buyer encodes the leftmost digit as the price signal before fully parsing the trailing digits.

Anderson & Simester 1996 catalog experiment · same dress, three prices

The mechanism

  • Reading scans left to right
  • The leftmost digit anchors price magnitude
  • Trailing digits register only after

Where it works

  • Catalog and supermarket retail
  • Just-below thresholds ($9, $19, $99)
  • Goods buyers scan rather than evaluate

Source: Anderson & Simester, Quantitative Marketing and Economics (1996), catalog field experiment.

The decoy, compromise, and charm effects shift buyer choice without changing the underlying value created.

Part 5

05

Dynamic pricing and the fairness ceiling

Why the same surge-style logic is accepted at Uber and rejected at Wendy's. Three degrees of price discrimination frame one side, while a foundational perception study frames the other.

Three degrees

How finely the seller can sort buyers before the price reaches them.

First degree

One price per buyer

A unique price for each transaction, set close to each buyer's WTP.

car-dealer haggle, art-gallery quote

Second degree

By self-selected quantity

Buyers sort themselves by what they choose to buy, and per-unit price changes with the choice.

bulk discounts, two-part tariffs

Third degree

By observable segment

Seller posts different prices to groups defined by a signal: age, geography, channel, time.

student tickets, off-peak rates, geo-priced SaaS

Source: Pigou, The Economics of Welfare (1920), Part II, Ch. 17, §3.

Indianapolis Zoo

The same dynamic logic applies, but it serves an equity goal rather than maximizing margin.

Demand-based admission · 2014 launch · daily price band

Source: Indianapolis Zoo demand-based pricing program (launched 2014); WSJ coverage (2015), zoo annual report.

The fairness ceiling

A price below willingness-to-pay can still violate the buyer's fairness threshold.

Kahneman-Knetsch-Thaler snow-shovel survey · share judging the price "fair"

Source: Kahneman, Knetsch & Thaler, AER (1986), Table 1; phone survey n ≈ 100.

Uber surge vs Wendy's

The same dynamic logic produces an opposite reception.

Uber surge

Fare rises during a storm or a concert, against an imbalance between rider demand and driver supply.

Riders attribute the higher price to scarcity, which exceeds the fairness threshold.

launched 2012, tolerated for thirteen years

Wendy's dynamic menu

The announcement framed digital menu boards as a tool to raise prices at peak demand times.

Buyers interpreted it as charging the same person more for the identical item, the windfall pattern below the fairness threshold.

announced Feb 26, 2024; reversed Mar 9, 2024

Source: Kahneman, Knetsch & Thaler, AER (1986); Wendy's Q4 2023 call (Feb 26, 2024); clarification Mar 9, 2024.

Pricing decisions determine how the value created by a transaction is divided between the firm and its customers.

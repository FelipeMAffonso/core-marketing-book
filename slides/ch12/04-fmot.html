Most of the brand decision is made at the shelf, in the seconds the buyer is looking at the package. The retailer controls that moment.

Walk into a Target meaning to grab shampoo. The choice between Pantene, Garnier, and Mielle was not settled on the drive in. It is a fast match against whichever bottle the eye sees first, and the retailer controls which bottle that is. The three-to-seven-second window has a name, the first moment of truth, and it is why a major consumer-goods firm runs a merchandising operation as large as its advertising one.

  • ZMOT: the research the buyer does online before any trip
  • FMOT: the three-to-seven seconds at the shelf when she decides
  • SMOT: first use at home, shaped by service and the package
  • TMOT: the reaction she shares with peers afterward
  • The retailer owns FMOT outright and partly owns ZMOT

Source: POPAI 2014 Shopper Engagement Study (in-store decisions ~76% of category and brand choices); P&G (2005); Google (2011)

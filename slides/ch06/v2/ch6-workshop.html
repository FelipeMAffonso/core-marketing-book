Chapter 6 Workshop

Workshop: build STP for a real brand, then have an AI critic stress-test the position.

By the end, every student has a written positioning statement, a five-criteria score on the target, and a perceptual map.

MKTG 4333 / Principles of Marketing An AI-Integrated Approach
02 / 17
Setup

Pick a real brand you buy, or one you would actually launch.

Strong picks: Stanley, Liquid Death, the campus coffee shop, a tutoring service, an Etsy product you would sell. Weak picks: a phone, a car.

03 / 17
Activity 1 / 6 minutes

Activity 1: Name your brand's target segment in one plain sentence.

One sentence. Who is the buyer, and what do they actually want from the category. 6 minutes.

Discussion prompts

  • Can you name three people who fit your segment sentence, and three who do not?
  • Is the sentence a real buyer doing a real thing, or marketing-deck words?
04 / 17
Activity 1 debrief

Debrief: pull three segment sentences and ask the room which one is winnable.

Strongest sentence names a buyer the room can picture. Weakest sentence is broad enough to mean nobody specific.

Discussion prompts

  • Which sentence reads as a real buyer? Which reads as a marketing deck?
  • What single word would tighten the weakest sentence?
05 / 17
Activity 2 / 8 minutes

Activity 2: Score your segment against the five criteria, and find the one most likely to fail.

Measurable, accessible, substantial, differentiable, actionable. Score 1 to 5 on each. The lowest score is where the work is. 8 minutes.

Discussion prompts

  • Which criterion is your lowest score? What would have to change for that score to rise?
  • Did you give yourself a high score where the answer is honestly unclear?
06 / 17
Activity 2 caveat

Common pitfall: actionable is the criterion teams over-rate to make the segment look usable.

Identifying a perfect segment the firm cannot afford to serve is not a strategy. It is research.

07 / 17
Activity 3 / 8 minutes

Activity 3: Write your positioning statement in the four-clause template, then audit each clause.

For target who need, brand is the category that biggest difference because reason to believe. 8 minutes.

Discussion prompts

  • Could your closest rival say your RTB clause word for word? If yes, rewrite it.
  • Does your difference clause pass all three POD tests?
08 / 17
Activity 3 debrief

Debrief: read three positioning statements and ask the room which RTB clause is the strongest.

Strongest RTB is something the firm can prove and a rival cannot match on a normal product-development calendar.

Discussion prompts

  • Which RTB do you believe? Why that one?
  • Which one could a rival copy by next quarter?
09 / 17
Activity 4 / 10 minutes

Activity 4: Build a perceptual map for your brand and three rivals, then test the empty quadrant.

Pick two axes the buyer cares about. Plot four dots. Find the empty corner. Ask whether buyer ideal points actually live there. 10 minutes.

Discussion prompts

  • Where do the buyer ideal points actually cluster on your map?
  • Is the empty corner an opening or dead air? Defend your answer in one sentence.
10 / 17
Activity 4 caveat

Common pitfall: declaring the axes in the boardroom instead of recovering them from buyer ratings.

If buyers sort the category on different attributes than the ones you drew, the map predicts choices badly. A real PCA on rating data costs a few hundred dollars in 2026.

11 / 17
Activity 5 / 10 minutes

Activity 5: Run the AI critic on your positioning, sharp prompt first.

One prompt with the four clauses, three rivals, and the explicit ask to flag any clause a rival could match. 10 minutes.

Discussion prompts

  • Which clauses did the model flag? Did it catch anything you missed?
  • What is the gap between the sharp and vague responses, in your own words?
12 / 17
Activity 5 prompt

The sharp prompt: audit each clause against the three POD tests, name three rivals, flag any clause the rival could say.

Audit my positioning for [brand]. The target is [X], the need is [Y], the difference is [Z], the RTB is [W]. Three named rivals: [A, B, C]. Score each clause.

13 / 17
Activity 5 debrief

Debrief: what did the model get right, and what would you still verify?

Almost always right: naming weak clauses, surfacing rivals' current claims. Always verify: specific rival prices, share figures, anything where the training cutoff matters.

Discussion prompts

  • Did the model name a weak clause you would not have caught yourself?
  • What single fact from the response would you double-check before relying on it?
14 / 17
Activity 6 / 6 minutes

Activity 6: Run your target through the ethics check before you call it done.

Does the cut reconstruct a protected trait? Could the audience include children? Would the buyer feel watched? 6 minutes.

Discussion prompts

  • Does your target fail any of the three questions? Which one is hardest to satisfy?
  • If you were a regulator, would you investigate your own brand?
15 / 17
Deliverable

Final 6 minutes: write your one-page positioning brief.

Brand, segment, five-criteria score, positioning statement, map sketch, AI audit verdict, ethics check. One page.

Discussion prompts

  • What is the one sentence you would say to your CMO to defend this positioning?
16 / 17
Cross-review

Share: trade one-pagers with your partner and red-team each other.

Find the weakest clause in the positioning statement. Find the rival the AI audit missed. 5 minutes total.

Discussion prompts

  • Which clause in your partner's positioning needs the most defense?
  • What rival did your partner's AI audit miss?
17 / 17
Close

You have a defensible positioning now, with the segment named, the clauses audited, and the map tested.

Take the one-pager home. Run the same procedure on the next brand you buy.

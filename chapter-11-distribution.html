    Working Draft

    A buyer who can’t find you can’t buy you.

    1. Walmart entered Germany in 1997 by acquiring Wertkauf and Interspar. The 85 stores were sold to Metro AG in 2006.

    In 1997 Walmart bought 85 hypermarkets in Germany and announced plans to bring the cheapest-prices-in-the-country playbook from Bentonville to Bavaria. The pricing was right, the layouts were modeled on Supercenters in Arkansas, and the supply chain integrated cleanly with Walmart’s global procurement. Nine years and more than a billion dollars later, Walmart sold the German operation to Metro AG and walked away.

    The Germany failure had nothing to do with product, price, or brand. German shoppers were already buying staples cheaper at Aldi and Lidl, domestic discounters with smaller stores closer to where people lived. German labor law required eight-hour shifts that broke the Supercenter scheduling model, local zoning made it almost impossible to put a 200,000-square-foot store near a residential area, and the morning chant Walmart trained associates to perform was banned by works councils as a violation of dignity.

    The failure had nothing to do with product, price, or brand. German shoppers already bought staples cheaper at Aldi and Lidl, local labor law broke the Supercenter shift model, and zoning blocked the giant store near homes. The home playbook was right and the channel was wrong for the market.

    What Walmart got wrong in Germany was distribution, the work of being available to the buyer in the place, time, and form they expect, with a system that makes the transaction feel ordinary. This chapter runs on one principle: a buyer who cannot find you cannot buy you, and finding-you is a system, not an address.

    Three brands carry the chapter, each showing the principle from a different angle. Costco builds the channel into the strategy itself, so its distribution and its competitive advantage are one decision. Trader Joe’s turns a deliberately narrow assortment into a moat that wider rivals cannot copy without losing their own economics. Stanley stumbled into a channel it never commissioned and rode it to a billion-dollar product line. Liquid Death, Patagonia, and Duolingo return for the same lesson in later chapters.

    Distribution channel The set of interdependent organizations that participate in making a product or service available for use or consumption. The terms marketing channel, trade channel, and distribution channel are interchangeable. Interdependent means each member’s success is bound to the others, so channel conflict is structural, not a personality problem. Channels do more than transport units; they make the market by signaling availability, financing inventory, and standardizing what the category looks like to the buyer. The 1960 Marketing Myopia framing names the strategic version, because firms defined by their product rather than the buyer-need they serve (railroads, Eastman Kodak, Blockbuster, Borders) miss the channel substitution that takes the category away.
    01 // why distribution exists

    Why Distribution Exists

    Production and consumption rarely happen in the same place. A coffee bean roasted in Buffalo one morning is consumed by a buyer in Tulsa three days later in a 16-ounce paper cup with cream. Four gaps separate production from consumption, and a working channel closes all four. Place utility closes geography. Time utility closes timing. Form utility closes configuration. Possession utility closes ownership and payment. A channel that closes all four well is invisible, because the cup is just there at 7 a.m. and the buyer thinks about the coffee rather than the chain that put it in front of her.

    2. Costco (merchant), a manufacturer’s rep (agent), UPS (facilitator).

    Three legal types span every channel arrangement. Merchants (wholesalers, retailers) buy, take title, and resell, accepting inventory risk and markup upside. Agents (brokers, manufacturers’ reps) broker on commission without taking title. Facilitators (transportation, warehouses, banks, ad agencies) move pieces of the transaction so others can complete it. Merchants clear inventory at margin and agents close the volume they are paid on, whereas facilitators move units regardless of price, so confusing one for another in compensation design produces the wrong levers.

    The marketing channel sits inside a broader system worth naming carefully, because the two phrases get used interchangeably in trade press and on whiteboards even though they describe different things. The supply chain is the larger system, and the channel is the slice of it that points toward the buyer. A marketing channel covers the downstream flow, which is how the product moves from the manufacturer to the buyer once it exists. The supply chain adds three flows the channel does not own: the upstream raw-material flow that turns bauxite into aluminum and aluminum into the can, the finance flow that moves working capital and trade credit sideways to keep every node solvent between shipments, and the information flow that carries scanner data back up to the factory so production matches demand instead of guessing.

    The channel sits inside a larger system the two phrases get confused for. The supply chain is the whole thing, and the marketing channel is the downstream slice that points at the buyer. So an empty Walmart shelf can be a channel problem or a supply-chain problem, two different fixes for the same gap.

    This distinction is not academic. A stockout at a Walmart endcap is not a marketing failure even though marketing is the one fielding the angry brand-manager email. It is a supply-chain coordination failure that marketing inherits at the point of sale. The brand manager who walks into a category review pointing at empty shelves needs to know whether the gap is the channel team’s problem (a slotting fee that did not buy the placement promised) or the supply-chain team’s problem (a missed replenishment window upstream). Same empty shelf, two different fixes, two different P&Ls that absorb the cost.

    3. Read the buyer’s behavior segment first, then match the supply-chain archetype to it. The brand-loyal repeat buyer wants a continuous-replenishment supply chain built for steady shipments, the promotion-driven buyer wants a lean chain built for lowest unit cost, and the deal-driven buyer with unpredictable demand wants an agile chain built for fast response. The innovation-seeking buyer with lumpy, high-stakes demand wants a fully flexible supply chain built for project-style execution. Costco runs all three under one roof, lean on Kirkland Signature, agile on the specialty-foods program, and fully flexible on business services. A Greek-life chapter selling co-branded apparel runs the same three without naming them, and forcing all three through one vendor on one lead time means the rivalry-week shirt arrives late, the custom formal-dance hoodie eats the spring budget, and the standard navy tee sits over-ordered in a closet.
    02 // channel structure

    Channel Structure: How Long, How Wide, How Integrated

    Channel design is three decisions. Length is how many intermediaries sit between manufacturer and buyer. Intensity is how many outlets the manufacturer authorizes. Integration is who in the chain owns or controls whom. They move together because they answer the same trade-off, which is reach for control.

    Channel length: zero through three tiers

    4. Japan is the textbook outlier on channel length. Food distribution there runs to six tiers because urban density supports the small neighborhood retailer. Walmart withdrew from Japan in 2020 in part because it could never reconcile Supercenter logistics with the premium Japanese buyers paid for the ritual of the small shop.

    The first design choice is how many handoffs sit between the factory and the buyer. Channel length is counted in tiers, where each tier is one independent business that takes ownership of the goods along the way. A shorter channel means the manufacturer touches more of the journey itself and keeps more of the margin and the customer relationship. A longer channel means more partners share the work, and each one takes a slice of the price in exchange.

    The shortest version is the zero-tier channel. A zero-tier channel moves the unit from the manufacturer to the buyer with nobody else in between. Apple’s 500-plus retail stores fit here, and so does the Tesla showroom, the L.L.Bean catalog that lands in a mailbox, and the Patagonia.com order shipped from a warehouse the brand itself runs. The company that designs the product also takes the buyer’s money and answers her questions when something goes wrong. The advantage is total control over the experience and the full retail margin, while the cost is that the manufacturer funds every storefront, shipment, and service call out of its own pocket.

    One handoff in, the math changes. A one-tier channel inserts a single retailer. Levi’s makes the jeans, Macy’s or Target stocks them, and the shopper buys from the store. The retailer earns a margin (typically 30 to 50 percent of the shelf price) for carrying inventory, paying rent, training staff, and accepting returns, work the manufacturer would otherwise do alone. In exchange, the manufacturer reaches thousands of shopping trips it could never host itself, at the price of letting another company stand between it and the buyer. A two-tier channel and a three-tier channel add further layers wherever the retail base fragments below the size a manufacturer can serve directly, and each added tier shaves a little more margin while reaching a little deeper into ever-smaller corners of the market.

    Channel length trades reach against control. Apple Stores launched in 2001 against a BusinessWeek “Sorry Steve, here’s why Apple Stores won’t work” headline and grew to 500-plus stores with sales-per-square-foot exceeding Tiffany, Coach, and Best Buy. Apple absorbs a vertically integrated footprint because the product carries the margin and the pull to fill a store that stocks no one else’s computers, whereas a snack-food brand trying the same thing would burn cash before the second city.

    Reach-versus-control stops short of the diagnostic question, which is what the buyer needs the channel to do. The methods literature names six service outputs the channel exists to produce: bulk-breaking, spatial convenience, waiting and delivery time, assortment and variety, customer service, and information provision. The marketer asks which outputs each segment requires and derives the channel that delivers them at minimum cost. Built from scratch as if no infrastructure existed, this is a zero-based channel, then compared back against the actual one to expose service-output gaps.

    5. The unit of competition is supply chains, not companies. Costco’s dominance is its supplier base, its 3PL partners, and its dock-to-stock cadence against Sam’s Club’s. Three postures fit different demand patterns. A lean supply chain is optimized for cost. An agile supply chain is optimized for the opposite. A leagile supply chain runs lean upstream and agile downstream. The category that wants agility punishes a lean chain as severely as the category that wants efficiency punishes an agile one.

    Underneath each service output sits a real logistics engine. Five operational components produce the outputs: order management and customer service, inventory management, transportation, warehousing and materials handling, and network design. Bulk-breaking happens in warehousing, spatial convenience is decided in network design, and assortment is funded by inventory policy, because every extra SKU costs cycle stock plus safety stock. One rule runs across all five components, total-cost analysis: cheaper transportation means slower transit, which means more in-transit inventory and larger buffers, so minimizing any single line raises the total. The decision rule is to minimize the sum subject to a service-level constraint.

    Channels also run in reverse. Reverse-flow channels handle returns, refurbishing, recycling, and disposal, and a brand that designs only the forward flow leaves the reverse work to whoever picks it up by default.

    Multichannel: most modern firms run several channel lengths at once

    Multichannel distribution runs two or more arrangements simultaneously to reach different segments or different need-states of the same buyer, the way Hewlett-Packard pairs a direct enterprise sales force with telemarketing, direct mail, online self-service, and authorized retailers. Each segment carries a different cost-to-serve and willingness-to-pay, so a single channel optimized for one mis-serves the others, and the cost of running the spread is conflict. That coverage decision sets up the next one, which is how many outlets to authorize at each tier, the question §11.3 turns into the intensity ladder.

    03 // intensity ladder

    The Intensity Ladder: Exclusive, Selective, Intensive

    5. Hermès limits its leather-goods distribution to about 305 owned stores worldwide and refuses every wholesale request. Coach, the most successful brand to walk back from intensive to selective, closed roughly 25 percent of its U.S. wholesale doors between 2014 and 2017 to restore equity, and the operating-margin recovery took five years to clear pre-walk-back levels.

    The second decision is how many outlets, at any tier, the manufacturer authorizes to carry the product, and three rungs form a ladder. Exclusive distribution holds one end with the fewest outlets, intensive the other with the most, and selective sits in the middle. Because every commercial pressure pushes a brand toward the intensive end, the rung a brand picks is the rung it then has to defend, and the cost of giving up exclusivity is paid in equity rather than revenue. The rung is a real decision the founder has to defend, not a default the channel hands them.

    Exclusive distribution Severely limits intermediaries, often a single dealer per territory. The producer trades coverage for control, used in new automobiles, major appliances, luxury apparel. Gucci ended third-party contracts and opened its own stores in the early 2000s after overexposure tarnished the image; Apple sells direct alongside authorized dealers because no other retailer would protect the brand experience.
    Selective distribution Authorizes some but not all willing intermediaries: more open than exclusive (competing retailers can stock the line), more selective than intensive (the manufacturer chooses which retailers, on what terms). STIHL sells the world’s top-selling chainsaw through six U.S. distributors, six company-owned centers, and 8,000 dealers in 80 countries, but never at Lowe’s or Home Depot, because the product needs in-aisle demonstration, fitted bar-and-chain selection, and lifetime parts service that a self-service big-box cannot deliver without devaluing the warranty.
    Intensive distribution Places the goods in as many outlets as possible. Works for snack foods, soft drinks, newspapers, candy, gum: impulsive decisions, small basket, the buyer substitutes the next brand in the cooler if the first is not there. Coca-Cola is the standing example, “within an arm’s reach of desire.”

    The pressure to drift up the ladder is structural and constant. A salesperson under quota wants the next account approved, a retailer with a private label wants onto the authorized list, and a board with a quarterly comparable-sales target wants distribution growth because it is the easiest revenue lever to pull. Nike pulled its products from Sears in 2004 after Kmart acquired the chain, because placement at a discount big-box would erode positioning at the higher-end specialty stores that carried the margin. The drift only runs one way at full speed, since equity lost to over-distribution is slow and expensive to win back. Two empirical drivers track with holding a lower rung, which are manufacturer brand strength and the level of retailer service the brand requires.

    04 // push and pull

    Push And Pull: Who Is Doing The Work

    6. Trade promotion (slotting fees, retailer co-op ads, manufacturer rebates) accounts for roughly 20 percent of total CPG revenue in the United States, a higher line than the same firms typically spend on consumer advertising. The push budget pays for shelf access; the pull budget pays for the buyer to ask the shelf for it.

    The third structural decision is who in the channel creates demand. A push strategy directs marketing investment at channel members through trade promotion, slotting fees, sales-force incentives, and co-op ad allowances, so the retailer, once paid, pushes the product to the buyer. A pull strategy directs investment at the buyer through advertising, content, social, and search, so the buyer, once primed, asks for the product and the retailer stocks it to meet the demand. The pull side is the mental-availability work from Chapter 6, and the push side is trade marketing. Most brands run both, and the proportions are the decision, because trade-marketing budgets in CPG regularly exceed advertising budgets outside the top three brands, since supermarket buyers reject more than 70 percent of the 150 to 250 new items offered to them weekly.

    Vertical Marketing Systems: When Push Goes Structural

    7. The 70 to 80 percent figure for VMS share of the U.S. consumer marketplace is the canonical Kotler 16e number. When most of the market runs through unified channels, the design of those unifications is most of the channel-strategy work.

    The strongest form of push is a vertical marketing system (VMS), where producer, wholesaler, and retailer act as one system rather than independent firms. It comes in three forms, where corporate VMS is single ownership (Sherwin-Williams owns 4,800-plus paint stores), administered VMS is dominance without ownership (Frito-Lay and P&G command shelf space), and contractual VMS is integration by contract (the McDonald’s and Ace Hardware franchise and cooperative models). The integration runs the catalog backwards too, so a Marvel film opens, the streaming base grows, the parks retheme an attraction within a year, and the merchandise ships into the same Target shelf the film is recommended on. The question for any portfolio brand is which adjacent channel produces a buyer-side benefit a licensed alternative cannot, so that the brand integrates only those.

    Channel Power And Where It Sits

    Once a channel exists, the next question is who sets its terms, and a 1959 taxonomy names five sources of channel power, each with a different lever, cost, and shelf life. Coercive power threatens a worse outcome, the way Walmart can imply a SKU will lose its slot on the next planogram review, which works in the short run and breeds the resentment that becomes withheld information later. Reward power is the mirror image, offering a margin point for a feature display, except rewards become baselines the manufacturer then pays in perpetuity. Legitimate power comes from a contractual right, like a franchise clause requiring approved-list sourcing, and it is predictable inside the contract and worthless outside it. Expert power is leverage from specialized knowledge the partner depends on, such as a manufacturer training a dealer’s mechanics or certifying hospital clinicians on a surgical implant. Referent power is the leverage a brand holds when the partner is proud of the association, like a boutique displaying a Patagonia placard, and it is the most durable of the five because the cost of defection is status rather than money.

    8. Walmart’s U.S. architecture rests on four interlocking innovations: a hub-and-spoke network of regional distribution centers each serving 90 to 170 stores inside a 124-mile drive, cross-docking that reships full inbound truckloads as mixed pallets without touching a shelf, an in-house fleet that scheduled profitable reverse hauls, and a 1983 satellite network that cut a four-day forecasting lag to near-real-time scanner data. Each depends on the other three, and together they let Walmart hold Everyday Low Pricing at a unit cost no competitor of the era could match. Germany inverted every precondition at once, so the four-piece system met a market that broke each of the four pieces, which is the §11.8 failure in one sentence.

    Channel Conflict

    9. Apple Stores absorb full margin yet draw customers away from the authorized resellers Apple still depends on. The conflict is structural, not pathological.

    Channels are systems of interdependent firms, which is the polite way of saying every member earns part of its living off a decision another member controls. The franchisee depends on the franchisor to set the brand standard, and the franchisor depends on the franchisee to hold that standard at the store. The manufacturer depends on the retailer to put product on the shelf, and the retailer depends on the manufacturer to put buyers in the parking lot. That mutual dependence is what makes a channel a channel, and it is also why channel conflict, defined as one member’s actions preventing another from reaching its goal, is structural rather than personal. The conflict sorts into three patterns by where the friction sits, and each pattern calls for a different remedy because the leverage point is in a different place.

    Channels are systems of interdependent firms, so every member earns part of its living off a decision another controls: the manufacturer needs the retailer to stock the product, and the retailer needs the manufacturer to put buyers in the lot. That dependence is why channel conflict, one member’s actions blocking another’s goal, is structural rather than personal, whether it runs sideways between same-level partners, up between maker and retailer, or across two channels of one brand. The question is how to manage it, not eliminate it.

    Horizontal conflict runs sideways between members at the same level, the way Pizza Inn franchisees in the early 1990s skimped on cheese to defend per-unit margin and dropped chain-wide quality perception enough to hurt everyone’s traffic. Each franchisee is locally rational while the brand-level cost is paid by all the others, so the fix has to act at the system level through sourcing clauses, mystery-shopper audits, and territorial protection. Vertical conflict runs between manufacturer and retailer, the way department stores cut Clinique’s shelf space when Estée Lauder launched it online in the late 1990s and read the move as the manufacturer competing with the channel it had asked them to build, so the fix is to design the direct channel so retailer overlap stays small. Multichannel conflict runs across two channels of the same brand, the way Goodyear compressed its independent dealers’ margin by layering Sears, Walmart, and Discount Tire on top of the dealer network, where the textbook fix is channel-specific assortment so the same SKU cannot be price-shopped across retailers. None of the three is caused by malice, so the question is how to manage the conflict, not how to eliminate it.

    Four conflict-management mechanisms do most of the practical work, which are strategic justification (channel-specific SKUs that block identical-product comparison), dual compensation (Allstate paying agents 2 percent on online quotes), superordinate goals (a category-share target the whole chain rallies around), and diplomacy or arbitration. The HBS-tradition research adds MAP policies and territorial protection. Each of these still treats conflict as something that starts between the manufacturer and the reseller, and a 1995 field-research reframing pushed back by relocating the source of recurring trade friction inside the seller’s own walls, reading it as a downstream symptom of internal misalignment between the product, sales, and service functions. So a side-payment to the wholesale partner that never reaches the org chart upstream tends to recur quarter after quarter without removing the structural driver.

    08 // failure modes

    What Goes Wrong

    Five failure modes recur often enough to name, and each one is a channel decision that looked reasonable on the day it was made and broke later under a pressure the original plan never priced. Read each as a diagnostic you can run before the channel goes live, rather than a postmortem you read after it fails.

    Over-extension on intensity The brand climbs the intensity ladder faster than its equity can absorb. Dial-A-Mattress ran a profitable zero-tier model for thirty years, opened about fifty brick-and-mortar stores in the 2000s, and went bankrupt when the secondary locations could not cover their fixed cost. The lesson is to stage each step up the ladder behind real demand evidence, because adding outlets does not multiply demand on its own.
    Poor channel-fit in a new market The brand enters a new geography with its home-market channel design and finds the design does not fit. Walmart Germany, this chapter’s opener, is the canonical case, because the U.S. Supercenter architecture moved into a market Aldi and Lidl already served and broke at every joint. The lesson is to learn the local channel ecosystem before transplanting, and to budget a real horizon for redesign around local conditions rather than assuming the home playbook travels.
    Channel conflict from vertical integration The brand opens a zero-tier channel beside its existing partners, and those partners now treat it as a competitor. When Apple launched its own stores in 2001, the move pulled customers away from authorized resellers, and the conflict had to be managed through deliberate diplomacy. The lesson is to pre-design the mechanism (branded variants, dual compensation, partner-exclusive lines) before the new channel goes live, because a conflict you plan for is one you can manage.
    Tower fragility under partner churn A channel inherited from a decade of bolt-ons looks robust until a single load-bearing block leaves. The Coughlan-Jap channel-resilience tower reframes channel design as the work of finding the load-bearing blocks (a single-source 3PL, a marketplace that controls most online discovery, a wholesaler holding most of the distribution share) and engineering redundancy on the outputs buyers value before the block exits. The lesson is to identify what the channel cannot lose and build the backup before you need it.
    JIT fragility

    Just-in-time inventory improves cash flow because it minimizes the dollars sitting in unsold stock, but it assumes the chain feeding the warehouse never breaks. COVID-19 made the fragility visible in a way decades of theory could not, as port shutdowns, factory closures, and a semiconductor shortage cascaded into nearly every category by 2021. Shelves full on a Friday were empty the following week, and they stayed empty for quarters because there was no buffer to draw down while the upstream gap closed.

    The bullwhip effect makes the fragility worse, and the operations literature answers it with the Triple-A test, taught once in the omni-channel section and reused here. Agility is the chain’s capacity to respond to short-term shifts within hours rather than weeks, built on shared point-of-sale data and safety stock pre-positioned at the choke points. Adaptability is the capacity to redesign the chain when shifts are structural, built on qualified second-source suppliers and a willingness to walk a single-source contract once the concentration looks like a fragility. Alignment is the capacity to write contracts that share risk and gain across tiers, so each partner’s incentive matches the chain’s overall performance. The practical move is to stress-test the chain against the most realistic shock the next year could bring, hold safety stock at the two or three points the chain cannot route around in time, and write the resilience cost into the unit economics rather than treating it as a luxury. A brand that prices resilience as a cost line absorbs the next shock and keeps the buyer, while a brand that treats it as optional absorbs the same shock by losing the buyer to a competitor whose shelf stayed full.

    Key Takeaways

    • A buyer who cannot find you cannot buy you. Being available is a system, not an address, so distribution is the work of putting the brand on the path the buyer is already walking.
    • A channel closes four gaps between production and consumption. Place, time, form, and possession are the four utilities a working channel produces, which makes distribution the physical twin of mental availability.
    • Channel design is one reach-for-control trade-off. Length, intensity, and integration move together, because a longer channel buys reach while a shorter one keeps control and margin.
    • Every commercial pressure pushes a brand toward intensive distribution. Holding the rung you chose is what equity defense looks like, because the cost of over-distributing is paid in equity and is slow to reverse.
    • Push spends on the channel and pull spends on the buyer. Power that depends on belief, the expert and referent kinds, outlasts power that leaves a paper trail, the coercive and reward kinds.
    • Omni-channel is the 2026 default, and the moat sits on the back end. Inventory accuracy, counter location, price consistency, returns parity, and one buyer record decide it, not the front-end website promise.
    • The DTC unit-economics math has to pencil before the launch. A wholesale buffer absorbs the third-party-algorithm shock that broke Casper, Allbirds, and Oddity once acquisition cost crossed gross margin.

    1. Walmart’s German prices were right, its layouts copied U.S. Supercenters, and its supply chain integrated cleanly, yet it lost more than a billion dollars and left in 2006. What did it get wrong, and why is that the chapter’s running lesson?

    The four gaps are place (geography), time (timing), form (configuration), and possession (ownership and payment). Mental availability is what the buyer calls to mind in the moment of need, while physical availability is whether the brand is on the shelf when the buyer gets there. Growing brands grow on both axes at once, and declining brands lose physical availability first, because a delisting is immediate while mental availability decays slowly.

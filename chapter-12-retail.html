    Working Draft

    Retail is where mental availability becomes physical availability.

    1. The Information broke the Just Walk Out story on April 2, 2024, and Bloomberg and Gizmodo amplified it within a day. Amazon disputed the framing of the contractor numbers but did not dispute that the workforce existed. The same reporting put roughly seven hundred of every thousand mid-2022 transactions through human review against an internal target of twenty to fifty per thousand.

    In April 2024 Amazon shut down its Just Walk Out checkout-free system at every full-size Amazon Fresh grocery store in the United States and replaced it with smart carts called Dash Carts. The real story was not the failure of cashier-less retail, because the system had been running on roughly one thousand contractors in India who watched the camera feeds and labeled the transactions the model could not resolve. In 2022 about seven hundred of every thousand transactions still needed a human to review them. The artificial intelligence was a hybrid, and the hybrid did not hold at full-grocery scale.

    The retail decisions a machine cannot yet make are the ones students arrive thinking are obvious. What goes on the shelf and how deep, why Trader Joe's stocks four thousand items where Kroger stocks fifty thousand, why the Apple Store has no checkout counter, and why Aldi keeps adding stores while Macy's closes them: each is one of eight marketing decisions a retailer stacks on top of the others, and every one of them resolves at the moment a buyer reaches for a box at three feet of shelf distance.

    The seven-of-ten review number is the simplest test for any retail decision in this chapter, because it asks whether the format would hold if the human-in-the-loop layer were taken away. The eight decisions, the wheel of retailing, the pricing arithmetic, the omnichannel pivot, and the failure modes named across JCPenney, Casper, and Just Walk Out all come back to that one test. Costco, Trader Joe's, and Patagonia carry the worked cases as the chapter goes, while Liquid Death, Duolingo, and Stanley return in later chapters.

    Retailing includes all the activities involved in selling goods or services directly to final consumers for personal, nonbusiness use. The textbook definition is correct, and it understates the job.

    01 // why retail is its own field

    Why Retail Is Its Own Field

    2. The "moments of truth" language came from A.G. Lafley at Procter and Gamble in 2005. Google added the Zero Moment of Truth in a 2011 e-book to name the pre-purchase research the buyer now does online.

    A manufacturer makes a small set of decisions and sends the product into the channel: what to make, what to charge, how to advertise. The retailer inherits all of that and then makes a much larger set on top of it. The retailer decides where the buyer physically meets the product, what sits next to it on the shelf, who answers a question in the aisle, when the doors open, and whether the lighting flatters the package. Eight decisions stack in any retail operation, and the textbook taxonomy is stable: target market, assortment and procurement, services, store atmosphere, pricing, incentives, communications, and channels. Get any one of the eight wrong and the others lose leverage, because each decision answers a question the one before it raised. Choose the wrong target market and the assortment cannot be right, and price the assortment wrong and the atmospherics cannot rescue it.

    A second axis runs underneath the eight, and it asks whether each decision resolves the same way for everyone or whether it differs by segment inside one store under one brand promise. A Walmart could greet a young single shopper, a parent with three kids in the cart, and a retired couple all the same way, or it could read those differences in its own file and shape eight decisions so that each segment meets a store built for them under the same Walmart promise. The McDonald's 2002 to 2006 turnaround develops that second axis in full in Section 12.2, so the diagnostic for now is simply whether each of the eight decisions is made in one shape for the whole base or in segment-specific shapes that all resolve to the same brand promise.

    The eight-decision stack matters because most of the buyer's brand decision is not made before she walks in the door. It is made at the shelf, in the seconds while she is looking at the package, and the retailer controls that moment. Walk into a Target meaning to grab shampoo. The choice between Pantene, Garnier, and Mielle Organics was not settled on the drive in, since it is a fast match against whichever bottle the eye sees first, and the retailer controls which bottle the eye sees first. That is why the eight decisions add up to a structural advantage the brand on the shelf cannot match by spending more on television.

    That in-store moment is the First Moment of Truth, almost always shortened to FMOT. In that three-to-seven-second window the buyer's eye crosses the shelf, registers a package, and either reaches or moves on, weighted toward whichever package it recognized fastest. The empirical anchor is the POPAI 2014 Shopper Engagement Study, which stopped shoppers leaving the store and reconstructed when their brand choice had actually been made. Roughly seventy-six percent of category and brand choices resolved at the shelf, which is the number behind why a major consumer-goods firm runs a merchandising operation as large as its advertising one.

    Three other moments bracket the FMOT, and naming them in order is what makes the buyer's whole path through a retailer legible. The earliest is ZMOT, the research the buyer does before any trip. After the FMOT and the purchase comes SMOT, the buyer's first use at home. Last is TMOT, the reaction she shares with peers afterward.

    FMOT / SMOT / TMOT / ZMOT Four moments anchor a buyer's relationship with a product. ZMOT is pre-purchase research online (search, reviews, social). FMOT is the three-to-seven-second window at the shelf when the buyer sees the package and decides whether to buy. SMOT is first use at home. TMOT is the reaction shared with peers afterward. The retailer owns FMOT outright, partly owns ZMOT (their search, app, reviews page), shapes SMOT through service quality, and feeds TMOT through experience.

    The practical reading follows from the sequence. The retailer owns the FMOT outright, the moment that decides about three of every four category and brand choices, and partly owns the ZMOT where the buyer researches before she arrives. The retailer shapes the SMOT and feeds the TMOT but controls neither. So the eight decisions in this chapter have to resolve at least for the FMOT window, and most of them should account for ZMOT as well. To audit a real shelf, the question to ask is whether a given decision was made for the moment the retailer actually controls, or whether the team quietly assumed the buyer arrived already decided, because that second assumption is almost never true.

    2a. The standard six-step retail-strategy cycle (situation, objectives, consumer, strategy, activities, control) reads the Just Walk Out reversal as a planning lesson rather than a sensational story, because step six feeds back into step one. Just Walk Out cleared the first five steps cleanly: the situation (rising cashier labor, checkout friction), the objectives (remove the checkout step), the consumer (a shopper who values a fast exit), the strategy (cameras and weight sensors reconstructing the basket), and the activities (build, train, and deploy to thirty-plus stores). The cycle broke at the control step, where a twenty-three-item Whole Foods basket with a child reaching for produce overran the human labeling pipeline. Amazon ran the control step again in January 2026, closing all fifty-eight U.S. Amazon Fresh and fourteen remaining Amazon Go stores while pivoting toward more than one hundred Whole Foods locations, even as Just Walk Out survived in more than three hundred sixty third-party venues such as stadiums, hospitals, and campuses where the baskets stay small and the review pipeline keeps up. Read the closure as the planning cycle working, since step six fed back into step one.

    A last cut sorts the eight decisions along an axis that matters for any retailer reading its own dashboard. Some variables are controllable by the retailer, such as where to put the store and what to stock. Others are uncontrollable, including shifts in consumer taste, a competitor's price, and the platforms shoppers spend their time on. Confusing the two is the most common budgeting error in retail, because a manager who burns budget trying to move TikTok's algorithm is treating an uncontrollable as a controllable. The same budget would have produced more lift inside the platform the algorithm already serves.

    2b. Retailers add value four ways a manufacturer cannot match alone, which is why the channel exists rather than disappearing into a direct pass-through. They provide assortment by aggregating many manufacturers' SKUs into one basket, break bulk from pallet to household-pack, hold inventory between production and purchase, and provide services (returns, fitting rooms, advisors, repair, in-aisle problem-solving) that turn a wandering shopper into a basket. Read any format through the four: Costco buys assortment economy at the cost of household-pack convenience, Aldi minimizes services to push price, and the Apple Store inverts the ratio by spending heavily on services to justify the inventory. The Kotler definition turns on who buys (final consumers); this value-add cut turns on what retailers do, and it is the sharper one for explaining why the disintermediation theories of the 1990s did not eliminate retail.

    The eight decisions answer to the moment at the shelf, and they only make sense once you can name the kind of store they add up to. That is the next step, because ten formats sort on how wide the assortment runs and at what price, and the wheel of retailing then turns each one from low-cost entrant toward decline.

    02 // retail formats

    Retail Formats

    3. Kotler 16e Chapter 16 lists ten major retail formats; Levy, Weitz, and Grewal's Retailing Management groups them similarly with one or two added sub-categories (warehouse versus members'-only club, for instance). The taxonomy is stable across textbooks, and the boundaries between formats blur in practice.

    Ten retail formats anchor most of the literature, sorted by what they sell, how deep, and at what margin. A department store (Macy's, Bloomingdale's) carries several product lines under one roof. A specialty store (Sephora, Apple) carries one line deeply. A supermarket is a large self-service grocery, while a convenience store (7-Eleven) sells a small high-turnover set at premium prices. A mass merchandiser (Walmart, Target) sells routine goods at low margin and high volume, and a category killer (Home Depot, PetSmart) goes narrow but very deep inside one category. An extreme-value or hard-discount store (Aldi, Lidl) sells a limited mix at heavy discount, mostly private label. An off-price retailer (TJ Maxx) sells brand-name overruns at a discount, and a warehouse club (Costco) sells mega-pack quantities to fee-paying members. Drugstores add a regulated pharmacy at the back, and automatic vending reaches the buyer where a clerked store cannot.

    The wheel of retailing

    4. McNair proposed the wheel in a 1957 speech at the University of Pittsburgh; Hollander formalized it in a 1960 Journal of Marketing article. The four-stage version (innovation, trade-up, vulnerability, decline) is Hollander's refinement.

    Retail formats do not stay still. The format that launches as a low-price upstart in one decade is often the one that closes a quarter of its stores in the next, and the pattern is regular enough to have a name. The wheel turns through four stages, and it is the lens that explains why Macy's is closing stores while Aldi is opening them. Innovation comes first, when a new format enters at low status, low margin, and low price, because the unglamorous position is what gives it a beachhead against incumbents grown comfortable at higher margins. Aldi entered the United States in 1976 with about fourteen hundred items per store and plain shelves stocked from the pallet, and TikTok Shop entered in 2023 with a creator-affiliate channel and a shop tab on a video app. Both looked small and odd at entry, which is the predicted shape.

    Trade-up comes second, when the successful entrant adds facilities, services, broader inventory, and gradually higher prices. Walmart in the 1990s is the textbook case, since the rural discounter added supercenters, a grocery aisle, and brand-name electronics until the average store doubled in size. The trade-up lifts revenue, but it also lifts the cost base, which sets up the third stage. Vulnerability arrives when that higher cost base drifts above what the next low-cost entrant has to support, so the original entrant becomes the high-cost incumbent the next entrant attacks. U.S. department stores reached this point across the 2000s, because a 200,000-square-foot full-line store with a commissioned sales force no longer matched what the suburban shopper would pay against Walmart, Target, and Amazon. Decline is the fourth stage, when the new entrant fills the position and the incumbent cannot cut fast enough to defend it. Macy's announced 150 closures in February 2024, a quarter of its footprint that generated under a tenth of its sales.

    5. Levy, Grewal, Peterson, and Connolly's 2005 Big Middle paper in the Journal of Retailing argued that Walmart, Target, and Costco found a sustainable equilibrium of low price plus broad assortment plus acceptable quality. The wheel does not predict their staying power.

    The wheel is a useful default and an incomplete prediction, because the data holds at least two regularities the four-stage cycle is silent on. The first was named in a 2005 paper. Walmart, Target, and Costco occupy a position called the Big Middle, where low price, broad assortment, and acceptable quality coexist as a stable equilibrium rather than a passing stage. The Big Middle retailer expands inside the position instead of trading up out of it. Aldi is the live counter-example: at roughly 2,677 U.S. stores by May 2026, on a $9-billion capital plan toward 3,200 by 2028, the discounter has not traded up. The mechanism is direct. Every new store amortizes the chain's distribution network across more square feet, and every new private-label item concentrates more volume on suppliers Aldi already has under contract, so the per-unit cost of running the format keeps falling. A discounter does not have to trade up if it scales the original format faster than its cost structure rises.

    5a. Freshness anchor (May 2026): Aldi US passed 2,677 stores by early May 2026 (up from 2,614 on December 31, 2025) on the $9B capital plan, and is now the third-largest US grocer by store count behind Walmart and Kroger. By Numerator's late-2025 ranking the chain reached roughly 2.8 percent of national grocery share, and across the 2022-to-2025 inflation cycle it was the only mid-sized US grocer outside Walmart and Costco to gain share. The principle (a discounter scaling faster than its cost structure rises) is sharpening, not weakening.

    The second regularity is where the wheel's decline forecast misfires most often, because a mature format can re-energize from inside without trading up and without ceding the low-cost position. The clearest case is McDonald's between 2002 and 2006. The setup was bleak, since the fourth quarter of 2002 closed with a $343.8 million net loss, the chain's first quarterly loss as a public company, and the obvious move was to chase Wendy's and Burger King down the value-menu curve. Instead, under a strategy codified as the Plan to Win, McDonald's read the differences inside its own customer base and built a different store for each segment under one brand promise. Families saw refreshed Happy Meal merchandising, young-adult moms saw the chicken-and-salad refresh and a redesigned dining room, and young-adult males saw the McCafé build-out and late-night drive-through hours. Each segment saw a different McDonald's, yet each segment saw the same McDonald's. From the 2002 trough the chain ran thirty-two consecutive months of positive comparable-store sales, and the share price recovered from about $12 in early 2003 to roughly $45 by the end of 2006. A mature format renewed its relevance one segment at a time rather than chasing the entrant down the price curve, and whether Macy's will do the same is the open question the chapter leaves to the next decade of department-store data.

    5b. Off-price retail is the wheel's other standing counter-example, and the scale is larger than most courses credit. TJX, Ross, and Burlington together posted roughly $88 billion in fiscal 2025 revenue across nearly seven thousand US stores, and each ran three straight years of positive comps while Macy's and Kohl's declined. The format resists the wheel by a different route than Aldi: it runs an arbitrage operation across other chains' surplus inventory, buying closeouts and overruns at a target cost and paying for the treasure-hunt inconsistency with the price advantage rather than building owned supply. Both routes show that a coherent low-cost format can hold for decades without trading up.

    The wheel sorts formats from the outside. The decision that actually separates Aldi from a supermarket sits one level down, in what each one chooses to put on the shelf.

    03 // assortment

    Assortment Is The Strategy

    6. Kotler 16e frames assortment as a two-dimensional choice: breadth (how many product lines) and depth (how many variants per line). A delicatessen is narrow-deep; a cafeteria is broad-shallow; a Walmart Supercenter is broad-deep.

    The clearest place to see retail decisions at work is the assortment. Product assortment has two dimensions, breadth (distinct lines) and depth (variants per line). A conventional supermarket carries about fifty thousand items across hundreds of categories, Trader Joe's about four thousand, Costco about four thousand, and Aldi about fourteen hundred. The narrow-assortment grocers are not short on shelf space. They are choosing what not to carry, which is a marketing decision and not a logistics one.

    Trader Joe's resolved its assortment decision in the opposite direction from Walmart, and the field calls the answer curated grocery. The reasoning runs in three steps, each one making the next possible. Concentrating every basket on about four thousand items rather than fifty thousand pushes the per-item velocity roughly an order of magnitude higher than a broad-line competitor sees. That volume buys supplier leverage, because each item is now a meaningful sales line for the manufacturer, so Trader Joe's negotiates directly and extracts a unit cost the broad-line grocer cannot match. The supplier relationship then makes the private label decision operational, since roughly eighty percent of the assortment sells under the chain's own brand, which lets Trader Joe's capture both the manufacturer's margin and the retailer's margin on the same unit.

    The four-thousand-item count holds steady through a replacement cadence rather than a hard cap. Roughly twenty new items launch each week and roughly the same number of slow sellers come off, so the assortment refreshes without the count rising. The turnover means a shopper meets a genuinely different store on each visit, which is part of the chain's deliberate treasure-hunt design. She does not arrive with a fixed list, because the list would be stale by the next trip, so she arrives expecting to discover what is new, and that expectation lifts both trip frequency and basket size.

    The most counter-intuitive part of the format is what Trader Joe's has chosen not to do. The chain has refused the omnichannel shift other grocers treat as existential, with no app, no e-commerce site, and no delivery partnership of consequence. The refusal is structural, not nostalgic. An online channel would push the chain to widen the assortment to match what searching shoppers expect to find, which would dilute the per-SKU volume the supplier-leverage math depends on, and picking online baskets from a small store would raise the cost structure faster than the new revenue would lift the top line. Refusing omnichannel protects the eight-decision design. The chain reads the same 2026 environment Walmart and Kroger read and arrives at the opposite answer, because its format makes a different bet about which trip is worth winning.

    Private label A brand owned by the retailer rather than a manufacturer (also: store brand, house brand, reseller brand). Higher margin per unit (the retailer captures the manufacturer's margin too) and a defensible point of difference. Costco's Kirkland Signature, Trader Joe's full assortment, Aldi's full assortment, Target's Good & Gather. Generics are unbranded plainly-packaged versions sold ten to forty percent cheaper.
    7. If Costco's Kirkland Signature were a standalone company, its 2024 revenue of about $86 billion would exceed Nike ($51B) plus Coca-Cola ($46B) combined. Kirkland is roughly thirty percent of Costco's revenue.

    Costco's private-label decision is the same Trader Joe's choice at warehouse-club scale. Kirkland Signature ran about eighty-six billion dollars in 2024, roughly thirty percent of Costco's revenue, on the same narrow four-thousand-item chassis. The chain reached that scale without a Super Bowl spot and without a separate Kirkland marketing program, because the label rides on the warehouse traffic the membership format already produces. The economics explain the format-by-format pattern. Private-label margin is structurally higher, since the retailer keeps both the manufacturer's slice and its own and skips the national brand's marketing cost, so discounters and warehouse clubs lean on it where their cost structure needs the help. Department stores moved the other way, letting private-label share slip toward half its 1990s level, which is part of the margin pressure they report.

    Walmart's fifty-thousand-item count is not assortment laziness. It is the broad-low position, where anything a household routinely buys is in stock, low-priced, and available in one trip. The math holds because Walmart fulfills more than half of its online orders from local stores, using the broad-low footprint as both store and warehouse. Walmart and Trader Joe's are two formats with different assumptions about the trip, not two points on one spectrum.

    7a. Kotler 16e frames the cost side of the assortment decision through Direct Product Profitability: a SKU's true profit is gross margin minus the costs of receiving, moving to storage, paperwork, selecting, checking, loading, and the linear-foot share of rent it consumes on the shelf. The DPP-positive list and the gross-margin-positive list are not the same list.

    The cost side makes broad-low harder than it looks. Direct Product Profitability (DPP) is the true per-SKU profit left after handling costs and the linear-foot share of shelf rent. A long-tail flavor can post a healthy gross margin and still come out negative on DPP, because a slow twelve-pack ties up thirty-six inches of cooler door for two weeks against a fast mover's three days. The cut runs on DPP, not on gross margin, which is why a buyer pulls last quarter's sell-through and shelf-feet by item, charges handling and rent at the standard rate, and drops the bottom DPP quintile at the next planogram cycle.

    7b. The 1990s-2000s PathTracker deployments joined cart-RFID paths to checkout baskets across forty-thousand-square-foot supermarkets, and the per-item revenue distribution from those joins is the empirical complement to Direct Product Profitability: the cull a retailer makes on cost grounds and the cull the shopper data implies often point at the same shelves.

    The DPP cut runs on cost, and it has an empirical twin running on revenue. When cart-path data is joined to checkout baskets across grocery stores, the per-item revenue distribution is a Pareto pattern stable enough to plan against. Roughly twenty percent of the items in an aisle bring in roughly eighty percent of the dollars, and the long boring middle, the bottom half by velocity, eats about a third of the linear feet while contributing under five percent of revenue. In a hundred-item cereal aisle, the top twenty bring in about eighty dollars of every hundred and the bottom fifty bring in five. The distribution recurs because the shopper's mental shortlist of acceptable cereals runs three to five brands deep, her hand reaches for the box she bought last time, and the rest of the aisle is rent paid on inventory no one is asking for.

    So the DPP cull and the Pareto cull point at the same shelves rather than competing ones, because an item in the long boring middle is flagged twice. It consumes the linear-foot rent the DPP formula charges against its margin, and it sits outside the top-quintile traffic the velocity data shows the aisle runs on. The cull is not a leap of faith, since the cost report and the velocity report agree on which shelves to free, and the freed feet flow most cleanly to extending facings on the top quintile until the shelf saturates.

    8. Iyengar and Lepper's 2000 jam study (Columbia) is the canonical academic source for choice overload in retail: shoppers offered a 24-flavor display tasted more but bought less than shoppers offered a 6-flavor display. The effect generalizes to grocery, electronics, and online assortment.

    The narrow-assortment camp has backing on the buyer side too. The 2000 Columbia jam study set a six-flavor table next to a twenty-four-flavor one, and the bigger table drew more shoppers but produced almost no sales, while the smaller drew fewer and produced about ten times the conversion. Choice past a threshold becomes paralyzing, which is why Trader Joe's, Aldi, and Costco sit on the four-thousand-item side on purpose. The Pareto revenue distribution and the choice-overload finding say the same thing from opposite ends, because the long middle costs the retailer linear feet and costs the shopper attention, and the two costs land on the same shelves.

    04 // atmospherics + observational

    Store Atmospherics And Observational Research

    9. Envirosell, founded in 1977, pioneered video-camera ethnography in stores. The 2008 update of Why We Buy consolidates thirty years of cross-category findings, and it is the standard practitioner reference for in-store observational research.

    The textbook on atmospherics is Why We Buy. From the late 1980s onward, video cameras placed inside retailers' stores tracked more than 900 aspects of shopper behavior, from entry path to dwell time to price-tag glances. What the cameras recorded often contradicted what the same shoppers reported in surveys, because the buyer cannot self-report a habit she is not aware of having.

    Three effects show up most often in the design literature, and each one names a piece of behavior the layout has to plan around. The first is the butt-brush effect. Shoppers leave a display the moment another customer bumps them from behind, and widening the Bloomingdale's aisle lifted sales on merchandise the chain was already selling. The fix is geometric rather than aesthetic. Pull the high-engagement merchandise into a wide aisle with about six feet of clearance, because the floor geometry decides whether the shopper stays long enough to buy.

    The second effect is the transition zone. The shopper crosses it while her eyes are still adjusting and her body is still slowing, so any merchandise placed there is functionally invisible. Experienced retailers leave the strip empty and stage the high-value goods just beyond it. The third effect is right-side bias. About ninety percent of shoppers turn right on entering, which makes the right-hand power wall the most valuable square footage in the store, because the first conscious shopping decision of the trip happens within reach of it.

    Beyond the three geometric findings sits the boomerang rate, a per-fixture signal that separates considered buying from impulse buying. The shopper who circles back is comparing the fixture against something nearby, while the shopper who picks an item up on the first pass is making an impulse pickup or a destination buy. Tracking the rate per fixture tells the retailer which displays work as decision aids and which work as billboards, because the two roles need different merchandising.

    The 2009 Inside the Mind of the Shopper program ran in parallel with RFID tags on shopping carts, mapping shopper paths through grocery stores. The findings stack on the camera evidence. Shoppers spend most of a visit in transit between fixtures rather than stopped at them, a small set of destination categories drives most of the traffic, and eighty-twenty distributions dominate the aisle. A planogram reviewer checking facing counts therefore asks two questions first, where the item sits on the shopper's actual path and where it falls in its category's engagement order, because facing space granted to an item past the transition zone but before a destination category's draw is invisible at any facing count.

    The shopper-path data also exposes a decorrelation a retailer cannot see from sales alone. Some items sell well without being looked at, which is the impulse pickup, and others draw long looks but rarely make the cart, which is the considered candidate the buyer rejects. Ranking a shelf by dwell time alone misranks about half of it, which is why the assortment audit reads sell-through and attention together.

    Atmospherics The deliberate use of physical space, lighting, sound, scent, and layout to shape shopper behavior. An Apple Store feels different from a Walmart not because the products differ but because every atmospheric variable was chosen for a different effect: an open floor, no checkout counter, every product demonstrable, and music quiet enough to let the demo hardware be heard.
    10. The first Apple Store opened in May 2001 in Tysons Corner, Virginia. By 2010 it was the highest-productivity U.S. retailer measured in sales per square foot. CoStar's most recent published figure puts Apple at $5,546 per square foot against an industry average of about $325.

    The clearest U.S. case in atmospherics-as-strategy is the Apple Store, and the productivity gap shows why the format itself is the marketing. The Apple Store runs roughly $5,546 in sales per square foot against an industry average near $325, about seventeen times the typical productivity on the same leased square foot. The store sells the same iPhone the buyer could order from amazon.com, through a format whose every decision was made to lift that number.

    The format choices behind the lift are what make the case worth teaching. There is no checkout counter, because a Specialist runs the transaction on an iPad anywhere on the floor, which removes the register line most formats accept as inevitable. Every product is demonstrable rather than display-only, so the buyer types on the keyboard and feels the hinge before deciding. The Genius Bar sits on the floor rather than in a back room, which signals that service is part of the same buying experience. The flagship locations operate as architectural advertising on top of selling product, because the buildings are billboards that brand-name architects designed.

    10a. IKEA carries the same atmospherics-as-strategy logic at warehouse scale, across 374 stores and roughly 1.7 billion visits a year, a footprint about two thousand times the size of one Apple Store doing the same brand-construction work in the aisle. The IKEA Place augmented-reality app extends the decision past the store walls, letting the buyer preview true-to-scale furniture in her own room, so the format's atmospherics now begin in the apartment.

    The floor geometry decides where the shopper stops, but the price she reads at the fixture decides the habit she brings back, which is the pricing choice the next section takes up.

    05 // pricing

    Pricing In Retail

    11. One framework names two anchoring strategies, and another breaks them into three with a hybrid. The older turns and earns pair (turns is inventory turnover, earns is gross-margin percent) names the trade-off, where specialty stores run high-earns and low-turns and warehouse clubs run the reverse. EDLP versus HiLo is how each operating model communicates that trade-off.

    Two pricing strategies anchor most U.S. retail, and the choice between them is the most consequential pricing decision a retailer makes. Both answer the same question, which is whether the shopper should be trained to trust the everyday price or to wait for the promotion. The two strategies produce two different shopper habits, two different cost structures behind the scenes, and two different shapes for the same week of POS data.

    The first strategy is everyday low pricing. EDLP (Everyday Low Pricing) charges a constant low price with little promotional cycling, and the result is that the shopper stops comparison-shopping. A Costco member knows the rotisserie chicken is $4.99 this week because it was $4.99 last week and the week before. Her mental model is that Costco is cheap, and the cost of checking is too high to bother for a few cents of variance.

    The opposing strategy is high-low pricing. HiLo (High-Low pricing) charges higher prices day-to-day but runs frequent promotions that drop selected items temporarily to or below the everyday-low equivalent. The shopper here learns the opposite habit, that it pays to read the circular, check the app coupons, and time the trip to the week her favorites go on sale. The promotion calendar is the format, and the everyday shelf price is the placeholder.

    The two strategies are not interchangeable, and the case most often cited is JCPenney in 2012. The chain switched from HiLo to EDLP under a new chief executive who reasoned that promotional pricing confused the shopper and that a clean everyday price would be more honest. The reasoning was internally coherent, and the result was severe. JCPenney lost roughly one billion dollars inside a year and reversed course. The shopper's habit was the promotion, not the everyday price. She had been trained for years to wait for the coupon, so removing the coupon removed the trigger that pulled her to the store. The format is not a freestanding rational choice but an attachment the shopper builds to a particular interaction pattern, and changing the format breaks the attachment.

    The empirical anchor for that warning is a 1994 Journal of Marketing field experiment on the Dominick's Finer Foods chain in Chicago. The study used the chain's scanner data to run controlled price changes across twenty-six categories, and the finding that broke the EDLP-everywhere thesis was the asymmetry of response. A ten-percent EDLP cut lifted volume by only about three percent, which means the retailer gave up thirteen cents of margin for every three cents of added volume, a losing trade. The symmetric experiment, raising prices ten percent under a HiLo regime, produced a fifteen-percent profit improvement, because the deal-prone shoppers stayed loyal to the promotions while the planned shoppers absorbed the higher everyday price without re-shopping the basket. EDLP is a margin sacrifice that has to be paid for somewhere else in the operating model.

    Paying for it elsewhere is the structural test the Dominick's paper proposes, because EDLP repays its margin sacrifice only where the retailer can call on at least one of three offsets. The first offset is scale-driven supplier leverage: Walmart and Home Depot are large enough that suppliers absorb the sacrifice as a cost of shelf access, so the EDLP price holds without the retailer eating the loss. The second offset is SKU concentration paired with private label: Costco, Aldi, and Trader Joe's carry a fraction of a conventional grocer's SKUs and put eighty percent or more under their own label, so each item moves enormous volume at a margin the retailer captures end to end. The third offset is a second revenue stream that subsidizes thin merchandise margins, and the two textbook examples are Costco's five-billion-dollar-plus membership-fee profit and Walmart Connect's retail-media revenue. Without one of the three, a retailer attempting EDLP gets the JCPenney result.

    The hybrid most practitioners reach for runs on the same asymmetry the study exposed. Category-level demand runs inelastic, while brand-within-category demand runs elastic. So the hybrid holds EDLP at the category level, where the cereal aisle's overall price stays steady, and runs HiLo at the brand-within-category level, where specific brands rotate through promotions. The hybrid beat either pure strategy on contribution margin in the Dominick's data, because it captured category traffic without surrendering brand elasticity. To run this on a real shelf, a category buyer pulls twelve months of SKU-store-week sales, finds the three or four known-value items that anchor price image in each trade area, sets EDLP only on those, and runs HiLo on the long tail where the shopper does not track price closely enough to notice the cycle.

    Price image The buyer's general perception of how expensive a retailer is. It is qualitative ("Walmart is cheap"), distinct from actual price, since a Walmart and a Target may carry the same item at the same dollar number. Its drivers include the average price level, the prices on known-value items, the breadth of the price range, the frequency and depth of promotions, the communication style, the atmospherics, and the service level.
    12. A loss leader is a single low-priced item used to build store traffic. Costco's $4.99 rotisserie chicken is the standard example, reliably below cost and unmoved since 2009, because it pulls the buyer past the higher-margin departments at the back of the warehouse.

    Most retailers shape price image with a small set of known-value items rather than every line. The buyer knows the price of milk, bananas, and a 12-pack of Coke but not imported anchovies, so KVIs priced aggressively cheap with the long tail priced to value produce a low price image at a higher blended margin than EDLP-on-everything. Loss leaders are the extreme version, priced below cost to pull the buyer in.

    Retail Media Networks: Pricing As Ad Inventory

    13. Walmart Connect went from $0 in 2018 to $4.4B in 2024 to $6.4B in 2026 (+46% year over year). The growth rate is nearly five times Walmart's e-commerce growth and accounts for a meaningful share of the chain's incremental operating profit.

    The newest pricing decision a retailer makes in 2026 is not the price on the shelf but the ad inventory it sells inside its own footprint, bought by the same brands whose products sit on those shelves. Retail media networks (RMNs) sell sponsored search results, product-page placements, in-app banners, and in-store screens. Walmart Connect ran about $4.4 billion in 2024 and is on track for $6.4 billion in 2026, Amazon Ads cleared $56 billion in 2024 (the third-largest digital-ad business behind Google and Meta), and total retail-media spend is forecast at $233.9 billion by 2027.

    Retail media network (RMN) Ad inventory the retailer sells inside its own digital and physical properties: sponsored placements on the website, in-app placements, and screens at shelf and checkout. Retailers care for three reasons that the prose names in sequence: the ad revenue runs at near-100-percent gross margin, the data is first-party because the retailer sees the conversion, and the buyer is already in shopping mode. Walmart Connect, Amazon Ads, and Instacart Advertising are the U.S. examples.
    13a. Walmart's FY2024 operating income was about $27B on $648B revenue. Walmart Connect contributed $4.4B at analyst-estimated ~70 percent gross margin, against three-percent operating margin on merchandise, so roughly 0.7 percent of revenue produced ten to twenty percent of operating income.

    The margin gap is what makes the network the most consequential pricing decision a 2026 grocer faces. A chain at a three-percent merchandise margin keeps a dollar of profit for every thirty-three at the register, while the same chain selling ad inventory runs near seventy-percent gross margin. That high-margin line cross-subsidizes a shelf-price elasticity a competitor without an RMN cannot match. The cross-subsidy works only if the retailer holds the asset that makes the inventory valuable, which is a first-party identity graph deep enough that advertisers will pay for closed-loop attribution. Kroger's file spans roughly sixty million households linked to basket, cart, and app behavior. Aldi runs no RMN because its operating model carries no loyalty card, and Macy's 2022 launch found limited traction because department-store visit frequency is too thin to populate a useful file.

    13b. Freshness anchor (May 2026): about fifty-two percent of U.S. marketers now run incrementality tests, only fifteen percent strongly trust their retail-media measurement, and sixty-two percent cite the lack of standards as a top challenge. Google cut the minimum geo-experiment budget from $100,000 to $5,000 using Bayesian models, putting causal testing inside the mid-market budget for the first time. Albertsons Media Collective reported a $2.41 matched-market incremental ROAS and a fourteen-percent in-store lift in early 2026. The landscape is fragmented across six major networks, projected to reach about eleven by year-end.

    The price the shopper reads trains a habit, and the ad inventory beside it pays for the price. Both decisions still assume the shopper walks into the store, yet the same footprint that the 2015 market priced for managed decline became the chain's moat by 2026, which is the reversal the next section takes up.

    06 // omnichannel

    Omnichannel: Store As Fulfillment Node

    14. "Omnichannel" should be distinguished from "multichannel." Multichannel means parallel channels, a website plus a store designed independently. Omnichannel means unified channels: the website knows what the local store has on the shelf, and the store can fulfill an online order. The shopper experiences one retailer.
    Omnichannel vs. multichannel Multichannel: the retailer runs parallel channels (website plus store, designed independently); the website does not know what the local store has on the shelf, and the store cannot fulfill an online order. Omnichannel: channels are unified (one inventory ledger, one customer record, one promise), and the shopper experiences a single retailer regardless of where the trip starts. The 2026 omnichannel retailer treats the store as a fulfillment node, which is why a 4,600-store chain can promise same-day delivery an online-only competitor cannot match.

    The "e-commerce will kill stores" narrative drove most retail journalism through the 2010s, and the post-pandemic data tells a different story. BOPIS reached 97.2 million U.S. users in 2024, and eighty-five percent of pickup visits produce at least one extra in-store purchase. The store turns into a fulfillment node rather than a casualty, because the buyer who comes to collect an online order keeps shopping once she is through the door. The footprint that the 2015 e-commerce thesis priced as a liability now does the picking, the staging, and the last-mile handoff at a cost an online-only rival cannot reach.

    Walmart is the canonical worked example, and two snapshots ten years apart tell the moat-flip story more clearly than any framework would. The 2015 Walmart ran roughly 4,500 U.S. stores with online sales at two percent of revenue, and investors were pricing the chain for managed decline against Amazon. The 2026 Walmart runs more than 4,600 stores, but the stores now operate as warehouses as much as shopping destinations: more than half of online orders are fulfilled from a local store rather than a regional center, global e-commerce volume cleared $100 billion, and the chain can credibly promise same-day delivery from a local node. The reversal was not that Walmart out-coded Amazon. Walmart already owned the physical asset that makes a same-day promise cheap to keep, so the footprint that looked like a high fixed cost in 2015 flipped into the chain's moat once the dominant promise compressed from two-day to two-hour delivery.

    The reason that asset matters is in Location is (Still) Everything: online demand per household runs highest where offline supply is sparse, so a dense store network is infrastructure an online-only competitor cannot synthesize. A Stillwater undergrad already has the intuition. If your nearest decent option for organic diapers is a forty-minute drive, you order them online, and you order more often than the family with a Whole Foods six blocks away. The planning grid that names these levers is called GRAVITY, and for a BOPIS rollout three of its letters do most of the work.

    Isolation is the gap between the goods a household wants and the goods the local stores carry. A high-isolation zip code is not remote; it is one where local supply does not match household preferences. The Diapers.com data is the worked case: per-household revenue ran highest in low grocery-density suburban zip codes, because the dense urban centers already had decent grocery and the suburbs did not. Adjacency says the most likely next online customer is a neighbor of the last one, through correlated tastes, visible delivery trucks, and word-of-mouth at the school pickup line. Vicinity is the catchment math behind every BOPIS rollout: which stores sit close enough to which buyers to make a two-hour promise deliverable. Put the three together and a planner gets a sequencing rule. Rank the metro's zip codes by online demand per household over local supply per household, then start the rollout where that ratio is highest and a store already underwrites the vicinity.

    14a. Whole Foods applies the same demand-grid logic at urban density. The first Daily Shop opened in September 2024 on Manhattan's Upper East Side, where another 40,000-square-foot flagship would have cannibalized the existing stores. At about 9,000 square feet it carries roughly seventy-five percent of the parent SKU mix off a Queens commissary and targets the fill-in trip, the five-to-fifteen-item occasion at higher frequency than the weekly stock-up. Sited between two flagships, it adds frequency without cannibalizing the weekly basket: the chain extends its catchment along time rather than geography.
    15. U.S. TikTok Shop GMV reached $15.1 billion in 2025, up about sixty-eight percent year over year, on roughly seventy-one million U.S. shoppers (about forty-six percent of U.S. TikTok users).

    The omnichannel turn has a second half running in parallel, and the dollars in it have moved fast enough that a 2026 planner has to take it seriously. Social commerce is the half. TikTok Shop, the in-app marketplace that lets shoppers buy directly from short videos and livestreams, processed roughly $33.2 billion in gross merchandise volume in 2024 and reached roughly $64.3 billion in 2025, nearly a doubling in twelve months, with the U.S. share at $15.1 billion across about seventy-one million shoppers. The structural feature that sets it apart is how much of the volume runs through the creator-affiliate channel rather than through brands' own storefronts. Roughly sixty percent of the platform's volume runs through creator-affiliate posts, where a creator earns a commission for featuring a product and the brand's storefront fulfills the order. That sixty-percent share is the empirical version of the claim that on social commerce the creator is the new shelf, because more than half of the volume runs through inventory the creator chose to feature. Whether TikTok Shop becomes a durable format or turns out to be the innovation stage of another turn of the wheel remains an open question, though the size of the dollars moving through it is settled.

    15a. Underneath the BOPIS and social-commerce shifts sits a quieter set of in-the-act levers. Geofencing targets a device as it crosses a defined radius around a store, and the Walmart app's parking-lot trigger flips the home screen into in-store mode. Shopper marketing bundles those levers into one campaign the manufacturer and retailer split. The trade-press headline that in-store decisions drive seventy-six percent of brand choices comes from the 2014 Shopper Engagement Study, but a 2009 Journal of Marketing field study showed the rate is strongly conditional: planned-list shoppers in destination categories run below thirty percent, impulse-prone shoppers in snack categories above eighty percent, with the planned-but-flexible majority at forty-to-sixty percent. A category manager sizing next quarter's shopper-marketing budget should size spend by category times shopper type, which usually means cutting destination-category spend by roughly half and doubling impulse-category spend.

    Five Brand Applications

    Each of the five brands below resolves the eight retail decisions differently, and each resolution holds because the decisions reinforce one promise rather than sitting beside each other as separate choices.

    Costco: The Membership Gate Is The Marketing Model

    16. Costco's actual marketing metric is the membership renewal rate, not advertising spend. Acquisition runs through member-get-member and the format itself.
    16a. Freshness anchor (May 2026): Costco's Q2 FY2026 results (period ending February 2026) report 147.2 million total cardholders and 82.1 million paying member households, with North American renewal at 92.1 percent and worldwide renewal at 89.7 percent. The ten-basis-point renewal slip traces to digital sign-ups becoming a larger share of the base, and Q2 FY2026 digital sales grew 22.6 percent; the membership-fee engine logic is unchanged.

    Costco runs 147.2 million cardholders at a North American renewal rate near 92 percent, and inside that number sits the actual marketing model. Roughly $5 billion of operating profit comes from membership fees alone, while the merchandise runs on a margin near eleven percent and the chain spends close to nothing on advertising. The fee revenue is the engine, and the thin merchandise margin is the cost the engine pays to pull buyers through the door. Run the eight decisions through the chain and it reads as one design. Target market is the fee-paying member household, which pre-filters price-shopping traffic. Assortment is roughly 4,000 SKUs against a Walmart Supercenter's 100,000, with about thirty percent of dollars through Kirkland Signature private label. Services are minimal in the aisle but generous at the gate. Atmospherics are deliberately warehouse, because concrete floors and pallet displays signal that the savings are real. Pricing runs EDLP with the $4.99 rotisserie chicken as the loss leader. Loyalty is the membership renewal itself. Communications run through roadshow demos and the Costco Connection magazine at near-zero broadcast spend. Channels are the warehouses plus a small e-commerce site and an Instacart partnership. Subtract any one and the rest lose leverage, because the chain only works when all eight resolve to the same promise.

    The Strategic Profit Model decomposes return on assets into net profit margin times asset turnover. Costco runs net margin in the one-to-three percent band on asset turnover four to eight times a year, winning on velocity where an Apple Store wins on margin per transaction, yet both reach a respectable return on assets. The membership fee is the third lever that lets the merchandise line run at a margin a non-membership club could not sustain.

    16b. What a ninety-two-percent renewal rate buys shows up in the loyalty literature with a magnitude attached. A 1990 Harvard Business Review study found a five-percentage-point retention lift moved profits twenty-five to ninety-five percent across nine service industries, through baseline repeat profit, growth in annual spend, lower cost-to-serve, referrals, and the price premium long-tenure customers pay. The amplification is largest in categories with high margins, long tenures, and strong referral channels, which is Costco's profile exactly. Retention is not uniform across the file: an Executive member (higher fee, 2% rewards) runs a larger basket and refers more family than a Gold Star member at the same renewal rate, so a chain should read the renewal rate alongside basket and tier rather than in isolation.
    16c. The same eight decisions a $270 billion chain resolves at scale a student team can resolve over a weekend. A pop-up booth at a campus admit weekend resolves the identical stack at a 700-square-foot footprint: a captive admitted-students-plus-parents segment, thirty to fifty SKUs narrowed by the same shelf-foot economics Costco runs on a warehouse aisle, a private-label item on a coral-lit endcap by the door (the Kirkland role), a low-priced sticker as the loss leader (the $4.99-rotisserie role), and one cashier holding the warehouse-club minimum-service decision at small scale. The point is not that the booth matches Costco's revenue but that the format DNA is the same, so practicing the eight decisions on a three-day operation builds the muscle the warehouse-club marketer uses at scale.

    Trader Joe's: The Deliberate Retail Anomaly

    15a. Freshness anchor (May 2026): Trader Joe's count crossed 637 by April 2026, and a count tracker put it at 647 by April 28; the chain disclosed a 2026 plan for stores in twelve additional states, sustaining the thirty-plus-openings cadence that began in 2024. The 608 figure in earlier reporting reflects late-2025; the principle (deliberate growth without abandoning the no-app, no-loyalty rule) is unchanged.

    Trader Joe's runs about 637 stores across forty-two states as of April 2026, up from 608 at the end of 2025, and ranks first among U.S. supermarkets on the American Customer Satisfaction Index. The number that locates the chain against the category is what it does not do: no app, no loyalty program, no customer-level data file. A chain without that data does not pay to run the infrastructure a 45-million-member program would require, and a format that has already pre-selected an attitudinally loyal segment through narrow assortment and private-label depth has done the segmentation work a tiered program would otherwise recover after the fact. Under the private-label headline runs a more specific choice, the supplier concentration model, where Trader Joe's sources each SKU from a single supplier and pushes per-SKU volume past what conventional grocers can match. The Numerator dollar-share data for the twelve months ending June 2024 put private-label share at sixty-nine percent, against Aldi's eighty and Costco's thirty-four. Narrow assortment, supplier concentration, and labor tenure are the same decision rotated three ways, because a smaller assortment lets each clerk learn every item, which raises the value of an experienced clerk, which justifies the wage that produces the tenure.

    Trader Joe's resolves the same eight decisions from the opposite end, since the narrow-assortment, private-label format developed in the assortment section doubles as its loyalty strategy: by pre-selecting an attitudinally loyal segment through the format itself, the chain runs no app, no loyalty program, and no customer-data file at all, and treats that absence as a virtue rather than a gap.

    15b. Read through the Strategic Profit Model, Trader Joe's holds an unusual corner. Estimated net margin sits in the four-to-six percent band (above Costco's one-to-three and roughly half Apple Store's eight-to-fifteen), and estimated asset turnover runs six to eight times a year (well above Apple Store's specialty pace). Supplier concentration plus private label explain the margin lift over Costco; narrow assortment plus no e-commerce explain the turnover lift over Apple. The combination is an off-diagonal corner the equation says should be hard to occupy, and Trader Joe's holds it only because all three decisions are made together. A specialty grocer copying one decision in isolation resolves back onto the warehouse-club path or the conventional-grocer path.

    Sephora: Omnichannel Done Right

    17. Beauty Insider's 2025 crossing of 45 million North American members was up from roughly 40 million in 2024 and traces a continuous build from launch in 2007. The Kohl's shop-in-shop is on a 2025 sales target above $2 billion.

    Sephora sits at the opposite end of the trade-off, because where Trader Joe's pre-selects its customer through the format and treats the absence of data as a virtue, Sephora runs one of the largest instrumented loyalty programs in U.S. retail and lets the program do the segmentation the format alone cannot. Beauty Insider reached 45 million North American members in 2025, and those members generate about eighty percent of Sephora's North American sales, so roughly four of every five dollars at the register pass through a customer the company can identify by name, basket, and tenure. The program runs three tiers set by annual spend: Insider ($0–$349), VIB ($350–$999), and Rouge ($1,000+). Only about six percent of members cross the thousand-dollar threshold in a year, and that six percent produces a far larger share of revenue and visits than its head-count share. The cutoffs implement the loyalty tier mix that the 1995 Marketing Science paper on RFM-based selection formalized: files are heavy-tailed, so the right move concentrates marketing investment where retention and frequency are already strongest rather than spreading perks evenly.

    Sephora's design bet runs against the default U.S. playbook of discount triggers by weighting Rouge perks toward access and recognition rather than dollars off. The annual Rouge Celebration event and rising average order value among those households show the recognition perks were the cheaper input and produced the larger output. The 2020 Kohl's shop-in-shop partnership extends the format the same way: by 2024 it covered more than 1,100 Kohl's stores and added roughly $1.8 billion in incremental sales, because Kohl's suburban locations reach the mid-market beauty buyer who was not driving to a flagship. Every dollar at a Kohl's Sephora counts toward the same ladder, so the file expands without the cost of a thousand full-format leases. Swapping access-and-recognition perks for deeper point multipliers would lift visit count short-term but erode attitudinal commitment, because dollar-off perks attach the customer to the discount rather than the brand, and the next competitor coupon would walk the Rouge household.

    17b. Rouge Celebration, the program's annual in-store event for the top tier, drew about 300,000 attendees in 2024 and roughly 600,000 in 2025, and average order value across those Rouge households rose roughly forty percent year over year (re-verify at build). The doubling of attendance says Rouge households want to be in the room with the brand, and the rise in spend per visit says they buy more once they are there.

    The Rouge threshold of $1,000 marks where two curves diverge. Revenue concentration in a heavy-tailed base is monotonic, so the top six percent always produce a disproportionate share of spend, and the diagnostic question is where the retention curve breaks. Above the break the household has crossed a behavioral threshold (visit cadence, basket-attach, category breadth) where a perk pays back; below it, perks redistribute margin without buying frequency.     Apple Store: The Format Is The Marketing

    The Apple Store's $5,546 sales per square foot has led U.S. retail for more than a decade against an industry average closer to $325, a seventeen-times uplift on the same leased square foot. The format choices that produce it were documented in Section 12.4, the open-table layout, the absent checkout counter, and the Genius Bar on the floor, so the flagships read as billboards that also sell phones. Apple is the inverse of Costco on the Strategic Profit Model, winning on fat margin and slow turnover where Costco wins on thin margin and fast turnover. The no-checkout layout extends time in store, which lifts turnover without conceding margin because the extra minutes are spent looking at AirPods on the next table rather than waiting at a register. A specialty retailer should identify which of the two ratios its format actually drives and stop spending operating dollars on the ratio it cannot move.

    Patagonia: The Repair Counter As Format-Defining Choice

    Patagonia's fiscal-2025 numbers contradict the assumption running through most specialty-retail textbooks, that a specialty retailer's only job is to move the next unit off the rack. The chain repaired 174,799 garments across more than 110 repair centers worldwide in fiscal 2025, a count large enough that the repair operation reads as a distinct business line rather than a brand gesture. Run the eight decisions through the Worn Wear program and the conventional specialty format inverts. Atmospherics move first, because at the Ventura and Boulder flagships the workbench with the sewing machine sits at the front of the store, where a conventional retailer puts the sale rack, so the repair operation is what the buyer sees first. Pricing reads as a published flat repair-rate card on top of the lifetime guarantee Patagonia has held since 1985. Communications favor athlete repair stories, where a sponsored climber walks through the patches of a fifteen-year-old shell, over the seasonal-launch advertising that drives the rest of the category. A specialty retailer reading its own format should ask what in-store moment proves the positioning, and whether that moment sits within ten feet of the front door, because a format that hides its argument behind the merchandise is leading with the wrong thing.

    18b. The Worn Wear program, which Patagonia launched in 2017 to formalize the repair-and-resale side of the brand, generated about thirteen million dollars in revenue in fiscal 2025 on roughly forty percent year-over-year growth (re-verify at build). The Worn Wear customer runs about ten years younger than the chain's full-price customer, so the repair counter acts as the brand's primary acquisition channel for the next generation of buyers.

    Across the five cases the question for a new brand is the same: which format fits the buyer, which atmospherics principles apply, how the assortment logic sets the cull, which trip type to target, and what the team can change next quarter. The same eight decisions that produce a Costco or a Sephora also produce the failures, which is where the chapter turns next.

    08 // failure modes

    What Goes Wrong

    The same eight decisions that produce Costco also produce JCPenney 2012, Walmart Germany, and Just Walk Out at grocery scale, and five recurring failure modes show up across the retail post-mortems.

    Pure-tech without a human fallback Just Walk Out at full grocery scale. Roughly 1,000 contractors in India watched camera feeds, and about seven hundred of every thousand transactions routed through human review against a target of twenty to fifty, so the format failed at supermarket scale. The same technology survives in roughly 360 third-party venues (stadiums, hospitals, universities) where the labeling pipeline keeps up. The fix is to give every AI deployment an instrumented human fallback before launch.
    DTC retail crossover without the unit economics Casper opened about 70 mattress stores by 2020, sold to private equity in 2021, and was acquired by Carpenter in October 2024. Allbirds expanded to about 60 stores by 2023, closed 14 in 2024 with revenue down 25.3 percent, and is closing all U.S. full-price stores by February 2026. Unit economics that work for direct-shipping one SKU did not survive SoHo-flagship rent. The fix is to test physical retail with shop-in-shops, partnerships, or pop-ups before committing to long leases.
    Mid-market department-store stagnation Macy's announced 150 closures in February 2024 (twenty-five percent of footprint, under ten percent of sales) while the higher-margin Bloomingdale's and Bluemercury banners expand. What is dying is the mid-market price point, which Walmart and Target eat from below and Bloomingdale's eats from above, because a department store stuck in the middle has no buyer it serves better than a rival.
    Generic-format-fit A retailer adopts a format because it is industry-standard, not because the format suits the buyer in front of it. JCPenney's 2012 HiLo-to-EDLP switch cost about a billion dollars before reversal. Walmart's 1997 to 2006 Germany expansion transplanted the U.S. supercenter into a market where Aldi and Lidl already ran EDLP, and it ended in a 2006 sale to Metro AG. The fix is to design retail decisions for the buyer in front of you, because the home-market playbook does not travel on its own.
    AI without a measured failure mode Just Walk Out is the case most often cited. Any AI retail-tech claim ("smart shelves know what is in stock," "computer vision counts foot traffic," "the planogram optimizer reorders the shelf nightly") needs a counter-example: what happens the day the model is wrong, what the human-fallback path is, and what a false positive costs at scale. When the answer is "we do not know yet," the right deployment is a small pilot with instrumentation on both paths, before any chain rollout.
    18. Retail shrink (inventory loss from theft, fraud, error, damage) ran $112.1B in 2022 per the NRF and is estimated near $132B for 2024. Theft accounts for about 65% of shrink (29% internal, 37% external, 1% organized crime). NRF discontinued the survey after 2024 over methodology concerns.

    One structural issue runs through every retail format in the chapter, and it is heavy enough at scale that the technology stack a 2026 chain runs has reshaped itself around it. The issue is retail shrink, the inventory that was supposed to be on the shelf when the quarter opened and is gone when the count is run. The 2022 figure from the National Retail Federation came in near one hundred twelve billion dollars across U.S. retail, and the 2024 estimate runs closer to one hundred thirty-two billion, with theft about sixty-five percent of the total. The operational response has been blunt, because twenty-eight percent of retailers closed a location in 2023 or 2024 specifically over shrink, forty-five percent cut operating hours in shrink-affected stores, and thirty percent pulled high-theft categories behind the counter. A regional grocer running a three-percent operating margin cannot absorb a one-and-a-half percent shrink rate quietly, since that loss is half the profit the chain reports and the loss has been climbing. So the working question is which technology stack actually changes the count.

    The modern in-store stack runs three layers. Item-level RFID tagging is the foundation, because it tells the chain what is actually on the shelf, and an accuracy lift from the low sixties to the high nineties cuts stockouts and adds a sales lift before it ever touches shrink. Computer-vision monitoring sits at the self-checkout lane, where it catches the non-scan a register cannot see and flags it to a clerk while the shopper is still there. The electronic shelf labels (ESLs) free the labor that used to re-paper paper tags, and the chain redeploys it to inventory walks and self-checkout attendants.

    The harder lesson runs underneath all three layers. None eliminates shrink on its own, because shrink is partly a measurement problem RFID solves, partly a transaction-time problem computer vision solves at the lane, and partly a staffing problem the freed ESL labor and the Section 12.4 atmospherics walk solve together. A chain that runs all three on top of the Section 12.4 management responses (staffing, fixturing, SKU-mix tightening, behind-the-counter relocation) builds on the gain of each, while a chain that runs one buys only partial protection. Vendor pitches usually frame a single technology as the complete answer, so the standing recommendation of this section holds here too: any AI retail-tech claim needs a measured failure mode. To read your own shrink line next quarter, rank the trailing-twelve-month shrink dollars by category, then ask store operations three things in order, namely the category's current inventory-accuracy percentage and RFID tier, the share of self-checkout transactions and whether computer vision is live on those lanes, and the ESL coverage and whether the freed labor reached the shrink-prevention surface. Whatever gap the answers name is the lever the next review pulls.

    09 // looking ahead

    Looking Ahead

    The eight retail decisions give an AI agent a watching job. A scheduled model audits a planogram against the day's POS data, flags SKUs trending toward stockout, suggests endcap rotations, drafts RFP language for a private-label supplier, and codes customer-review themes, and a human reads each draft before it becomes a decision. The first decisions in this chapter, target market and assortment and format, are the set the agent inherits from the people who own the brand. Three 2025 deployments give that division of labor its first real test. Amazon's Rufus reached roughly 250 million users by late 2025 and is reported on pace for about $10 billion in attributed incremental sales, Walmart's Sparky ran about thirty-five percent higher average order value among engaged members, and Target reported in-store app users with baskets about fifty percent above the chain average. Read those numbers with care, because the same confounds sit under all three: heavier shoppers self-select into the assistant, a conversational interface surfaces complementary products the trip would have missed, and friction reduction rescues a session that would otherwise have bounced. So a careful read credits the assistant for a measurable share and attributes the rest to selection, discovery, and recommendation effects a good system would have produced anyway.

    19a. Freshness anchor (May 2026): Walmart pivoted the OpenAI partnership in March 2026, replacing ChatGPT Instant Checkout with a Sparky-in-ChatGPT app experience after Instant Checkout's conversion ran roughly one-third of Walmart.com's. Sparky-in-ChatGPT pilots converted at about seventy percent of Walmart.com, with Gemini integration the following month. The lesson is the eight-decision stack at the AI layer: a retailer cannot let a third-party agent own the cart without owning the discovery, account, and loyalty layer the format depends on.

    The March 2026 Walmart-OpenAI pivot is the live worked example. Instant Checkout launched in October 2025 with OpenAI taking the cart and Walmart taking the fulfillment, and six months later Walmart pulled the cart back inside Sparky, embedding it as a first-party app inside ChatGPT and then Gemini while keeping discovery, account, and loyalty on its own surface. Run the eight decisions through the move and the logic reads cleanly, because Walmart will let an agent be the entry point while keeping the format in its own hands, since surrendering target market, pricing, and loyalty to a partner it does not own would repeat JCPenney's 2012 generic-format-fit failure at the AI layer.

    Chapter 13 takes up the inverse of the retail-format question. When a brand owns its own storefront and the buyer arrives through a Shopify checkout instead of a Target endcap, the eight decisions this chapter walked compress into a CAC-and-LTV math the brand reads in real time, a Klaviyo lifecycle layer the chain-retail brand never had to build, and the agentic-shopping surface where ChatGPT or Perplexity reads the product page on the buyer's behalf. The DTC operator inherits the discovery and account layers Walmart spent decades building, along with the unit-economics discipline the venture-backed cohort of 2014 to 2020 learned the expensive way.

    Retail is a field people spend whole careers inside, in store operations, merchandising, category management, and the new agentic-commerce layer that is still being built. What you hold now is the working machinery, enough to name a format, read an assortment, diagnose a price image, and tell which retail-tech claim has a measured failure mode behind it.

    Job Aid. The eight-decision retail audit. This worksheet is the chapter’s operating sequence pulled out of the spine so you can run it on any store, your own or a competitor’s. Work top to bottom. Each block is a move the chapter taught.

    1. Place the format on the map and the wheel.

    Put the store on the width-by-price map: is the assortment narrow or broad, and is the price level high or low. Then place it on the wheel of retailing as a low-cost innovator, a chain trading up, a vulnerable incumbent, a declining format, or a stable Big-Middle player. A format that scales faster than its cost structure rises (Aldi, Costco) can resist the wheel, while a format adopted because it is industry-standard (the generic-format-fit failure) usually cannot.

    2. Read the assortment and the private-label share.

    Decide whether the assortment is narrow or broad, then read the private-label share as the tell. A high share means the chain concentrated per-SKU velocity to win supplier leverage and then funded its own label, capturing both margins. Walk one aisle and mark the long boring middle, the slow SKUs the velocity data and the linear-foot rent both flag, and decide where the freed shelf feet should go (usually more facings on the top quintile).

    3. Walk the floor for the geometry.

    Stand at the door and check the transition zone, the first five to fifteen feet where merchandise is functionally invisible. Find the right-hand power wall five to fifteen paces in, the highest-value display spot. Check the high-engagement fixtures for the butt-brush risk, the narrow spots where a shopper bumped from behind abandons the display. The floor geometry decides where the buyer stops, so a layout that looks symmetrical can still leave money on the floor.

    4. Read the pricing and the price image.

    Decide whether the store runs EDLP (everyday low price, training a one-trip habit) or HiLo (high-low promotions, training a wait-for-the-deal habit). Name the known-value items that anchor the price image, the few products whose price the shopper actually remembers (the gallon of milk, the rotisserie chicken). If the store runs EDLP, ask which structural offset pays for the margin sacrifice: scale supplier leverage, SKU concentration plus private label, or a second revenue stream such as a retail-media network.

    5. Check the retail-tech and the AI layer for a measured failure mode.

    For any in-store technology or AI claim, ask the question the chapter ends on: what happens the day the model is wrong, what the human-fallback path is, and what a false positive costs at scale. A scheduled agent can audit a planogram, flag stockouts, and recommend a format, but the target market, assortment, and format stay with the people who own the brand. A tech claim that names no failure mode is still a pilot, whatever the vendor calls it.

    Key Takeaways

    • The retailer inherits the maker's decisions and makes eight more, all resolved at the shelf. Roughly three of every four brand choices happen in the first few seconds in front of the shelf (the first moment of truth), so the retail decisions are where mental availability turns into a sale.
    • Ten formats sort on assortment width by price level, and the wheel turns unless a coherent format resists it. A low-cost entrant usually trades up, grows vulnerable, and declines, but Aldi and the Big-Middle players hold the low-cost position by scaling a coherent format faster than its cost structure rises.
    • Assortment is a marketing decision, not logistics. A narrow assortment forces per-SKU velocity, which buys supplier leverage, which funds private label, while the long boring middle of an aisle costs linear feet and shopper attention at the same time.
    • Atmospherics is the productivity model, not decoration. The floor geometry decides where the shopper stops and what she sees, through the butt-brush effect, the transition zone at the entrance, and the right-side power wall.

    Check Your Understanding

    Answer each in your head, then click to check.

    1. Just Walk Out shut at full-size Amazon Fresh stores in 2024 but survives in 360-plus stadiums, hospitals, and campuses. What single test explains both outcomes?

    Ask whether the format would hold if the human-in-the-loop layer were removed. At grocery scale about seven hundred of every thousand transactions needed a human reviewer against a target of twenty to fifty, so the review pipeline could not keep up and the format failed. At small-basket third-party venues the baskets are simple enough that the review pipeline keeps pace, so it survives. The technology was always a hybrid, and the hybrid only works where the baskets stay small.

    2. The wheel of retailing predicts a low-cost entrant must trade up to survive. Aldi has not. Why is Aldi the counter-example, and what does it share with the Big Middle?

    Aldi scales the original format, narrow assortment, high private label, and deep distribution, faster than its cost structure rises, so each new store amortizes the existing network without diluting the format that made it cheap. Walmart, Target, and Costco occupy the same stable Big-Middle position of low price, broad assortment, and acceptable quality, and they expand inside that position rather than trading up out of it. A coherent format can resist the wheel as long as growth does not force it to abandon the thing that made it work.

    3. Trader Joe's stocks about 4,000 items where a supermarket stocks 50,000. Walk the three-step logic that makes the narrow assortment the strategy.

    Narrowing the assortment concentrates every basket's volume on each item, so per-SKU velocity runs an order of magnitude higher than a broad-line grocer's. That volume gives Trader Joe's the supplier leverage to negotiate a unit cost the broad grocer cannot match. The supplier relationship in turn makes private label operational, so the chain captures both the maker's and the retailer's margin on the roughly four-fifths of the assortment that carries its own label.

    4. Why does the chapter call the long boring middle of an aisle “twice flagged,” and what does a retailer do with the freed shelf feet?

    A slow SKU is flagged once by Direct Product Profitability, which subtracts handling and linear-foot rent from gross margin and shows the item cannot repay its shelf space, and again by the PathTracker velocity data, which shows the aisle runs on its top quintile. The two reports agree on which shelves to free. The freed feet flow to extending facings on the top-quintile sellers until the shelf saturates, which is where the next dollar of space earns the most.

    5. A junior marketer moves the high-engagement display into a narrow aisle near the front door to catch entering shoppers. Why does the Why We Buy research say this loses sales?

    Two geometric effects work against the spot. The butt-brush effect means shoppers abandon a display the moment another customer bumps them from behind, so a high-engagement fixture needs about six feet of clearance a narrow aisle cannot give. The transition zone is the first five to fifteen feet inside the door where the shopper's eyes and pace are still adjusting, so merchandise there is functionally invisible. The high-value spot is the right-hand power wall five to fifteen paces in, past the transition zone.

    6. JCPenney switched from HiLo to EDLP in 2012 with internally coherent reasoning and lost about a billion dollars. What did the chief executive misread, and what does the Dominick's experiment add?

    The shopper's habit was the promotion itself, not the everyday price, so removing the coupon removed the trigger that pulled her into the store. The Dominick's 1994 field experiment shows why the switch was costly: a ten percent EDLP cut bought only three percent more volume, a losing trade, while a ten percent HiLo price raise lifted profit fifteen percent. EDLP is a margin sacrifice that pays only where a structural offset covers it, whether scale supplier leverage, SKU concentration plus private label, or a second revenue stream, and JCPenney had none of the three.

    7. In 2015 investors priced Walmart's 4,600 stores for managed decline. By 2026 the same footprint is the company's moat. What changed, and what regularity explains it?

    The dominant fulfillment promise compressed from two-day to two-hour delivery, and a two-hour promise is cheap to keep from a local store and expensive from a regional warehouse. So the footprint flipped from a cost into fulfillment infrastructure. The regularity is that online demand per household runs highest where offline supply is sparse, which means a dense store network is an asset an online-only competitor cannot synthesize quickly, no matter how much it spends.

    8. The chapter says an AI agent should watch the eight retail decisions while a human acts on them, and points to Walmart's 2026 reversal of the OpenAI Instant Checkout deal. What is the lesson?

    A scheduled model can audit a planogram, flag stockouts, and recommend a format, but the target market, assortment, and format are decisions the agent inherits, not makes. Walmart let the agent be the channel, embedding Sparky inside ChatGPT, while pulling the cart back inside its own surface. Surrendering target market, pricing, and loyalty to a partner the chain does not own would repeat the generic-format-fit failure of JCPenney 2012, this time at the AI layer instead of the pricing layer.
